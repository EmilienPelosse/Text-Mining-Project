<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#2:1> <h> <bold> ON THE EVE OF A NEW ERA </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#4:1> <it> The 1990 Annual National Exhibition at the National Gallery

of Jamaica brought together just over a hundred works by seventy-two artists

to present a fair cross- section of work done last year by Jamaican artists

and artists from overseas who have lived and worked here. </it> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#5:1> Although the absence of our major artists was regrettably evident,

the Exhibition showed some of the extraordinary artistic energy still surging

in Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#6:1> As might be expected from seventy-two searching individualists, the

exhibits varied in concept, technique and style.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#7:1> The three main media used - Painting, Sculpture and Ceramics - were

vehicles to convey social satire; penetrations into death, despair or the

subconscious; environmental protest; and other aspects of Nature and

humanity.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#8:1> All the works show facets of society and its environs reflected by the

artists, but only after they have passed through the alembic of the

individual mind and have been coloured and shaped by it. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#9:1> In this assessment of some aspects of the Exhibition, works have

been grouped according to theme rather than medium not only because most were

paintings but also because Sculpture and Ceramics were under <l> represented.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#10:1> Works from five of these themes have been selected for commentary:

the human head; the human figure; the dark side of the mind; and Abstraction.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#11:1> Interpretations of the human head formed a strong section of the

Exhibition, ranging from in depth psychological probes through stylized

studies to mockery. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#12:1> Two outstanding representational portraits in bronze were by

Valerie Bloomfield- Ambrose and Gene Pear <l> son.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#13:1> In <it> Portrait of Maurice Facey </it> Bloomfield- Ambrose achieved

not only facial resemblance but characteristic mannerisms conveying the

essence of the sitter.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#14:1> The impressionistic surface of the original malleable medium enlivens

the head and hair; and the features, though more precisely modelled, maintain

the animation.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#15:1> Forms of the lower neck and what is shown of the shirt were only

suggested so there is tension between these and the firmly defined collar

which separates them. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#16:1> Bloomfield-Ambrose is a painter but with her firm grasp of

three-dimensional form she could venture far in sculpture. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#17:1> In contrast, Pearson's stately <it> Mona </it> has a smooth

surface which accentuates features infused with emotion.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#18:1> Aloofness is emphasized by the downcast eyes which seem to conceal

unspeakable hurt.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#19:1> Ridges of hair above the forehead and long diagonals concealing half

the neck frame the emotion of the face and give movement to the composition.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#20:1> In <it> Mona </it> , Pearson has broken a barrier of restraint

and thrusts his art to a high level. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#21:1> Roy Reid and Osmond Watson produced commanding stylized portraits

in oil.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#22:1> In Reid's <it> A Talk with God </it> , flattened, delineated forms

and a head disproportionately enlarged for accent ( oh, that single eye!),

lend simplicity to the trusting worshipper.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#23:1> Hands, cupped in awe, curve towards the upturned head, completing a

warm, heart-shaped centre of interest. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#24:1> Hands are so expressive of character and mood that it is a pity

Reid cut them off abruptly in his charming <it> Tell me all the Lies </it> ,

depicting a flirtatious girl in a dress of glorious yellow. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#25:1> Watson's <it> Girl with Braids </it> was a skilful exercise in

symmetry barely avoided.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#26:1> The forms were segmented analytically in the Cubist manner and though

attenuated were correctly structured.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#27:1> This accomplished artist shaped the eyes and nose as a child does,

but in a sophisticated way, which gives the impression that he <O>

advertisement </O> <O> photograph </O> <O> caption </O> had to become like a

little child in order to identify with his subject and capture the child's

wonder as she crossed from innocence to experience. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#28:1> Laura Facey-Cooper's <it> The Game of Chess </it> , is a

pharaoh-like arrangement of carved fragments set on a black chequerboard of

matt and glossy squares.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#29:1> A hollowed head with crown and receding horizontal sheath lined with

black suggest the pyramid's tomb one enters through a long, dark passage; but

viewed from the back it is as if one were looking into the man's mind to see,

through slits of vacant eye sockets, what little light entered there. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#30:1> <it> The Game of Chess </it> is a highly imaginative construction

of delicacy and subtlety.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#31:1> Enclosed in a transparent case, the fragments seem to be suspended in

timelessness, somewhat removed even from the viewer and from the space the

case inhabits. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#32:1> My personal response to the work was that it made life itself

appear to be a chess game in which men are played and toppled.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#33:1> Nothing remains but empty shells and broken bits on Time's

chequerboard. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#34:1> At the opposite pole, Londale Campbell and Douglas Wallace had

created caricature heads for social ridicule.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#35:1> Campbell's <it> Don Man </it> is a turned earthenware pot with a

twisted lip.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#36:1> The neck and shoulder form a plain dark cap pulled low; the body of

the pot, patterned with polychromed flames, has surface modelling of beady

eyes, button nose and mouth gaping with fear&dotted-line;
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#37:1> The Don has been undone. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#38:1> Wallace's <it> Dada Sammypsycho ( man without no name) </it>

derives from Uncle Sam.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#39:1> Dada Art, which erupted in Zurich during World War I, was anti-war,

anti- establishment and anti-art, with often nonsensical titles. <fnr> ( 1)

</fnr> <footnote> ( 1) Peter and Linda Murray, <it> A Dictionary of Art and

Artists. </it> Harmondsworth: Penguin, 1966, p. 77. </it> </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#40:1> Wallace's clever three dimensional egg-head, bristling with nails,

appears to be a protest against the USA's armed intervention in other

countries. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#41:1> The human figure was also treated diversely.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#42:1> Several artists focused on inwardness and spirituality, or their

lack, and physical reality was modified to gain expressive reality. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#43:1> Single figures by carvers Winston Patrick and Williams <quote> 'Woody'

</quote> Joseph are, respectively, an external image imaginatively translated

and an internal image given symbolic form. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#44:1> Patrick's <it> Seated Man </it> is in the act of rousing himself

from deep meditation.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#45:1> Expressing this, the body and upper arms merge in Oneness but the

head, hands and legs ( alerted areas) emerge into multiplicity with greater

realism.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#46:1> The whole was tenderly portrayed. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#47:1> For Joseph's <it> Andy </it> , a cylinder was enough to suggest

the trunk on which incised ribs give an X-ray effect.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#48:1> The X-ray device has been used by North American and other indigenous

people to show the unseen that is known. <fnr> ( 2) </fnr> <footnote> ( 2)

Leonard Adam, <it> Primitive Art </it> , Harmondsworth: Penguin, 1949, pp.

38, 65. </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#49:1> Yet seeing the inside of an inner image was like entering a deeper

level of the Unknown. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#50:1> On each side is a row of five decorative knobs symbolizing the

arm and fingers.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#51:1> With the two on the breast ( prominent as in a woman) they total

twelve, the mythological number of the universal mother to life. <fnr> ( 3)

</fnr> <footnote> ( 3) Robert Lawlor, <it> Sacred Geometry; Philosophy and

Practice </it> . London: Thames & Hudson, 1982, p.5. </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#52:1> Is <it> Andy </it> a hermaphroditic image representing the masculine

and feminine principles? </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#53:1> The stubby legs have unusual knees, like joints with patellas

dotted externally in another X-ray effect.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#54:1> Above the trunk, a strong neck and head describe an optimistic,

dignified man - Joseph's own qualities, surely, and his general view of

mankind, blended with humour. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#55:1> From what deep levels of mind has this figure ascended? </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#56:1> Michael Parchment and Gloria Escoffery satirized topical events

in Church or State.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#57:1> Parchment's painted relief is an incisive comment on hypocrisy and

immorality in the Church.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#58:1> It ridicules <it> The Spiritual Man </it> who doesn't mind that his

right hand, bearing the Cross, knows what his left hand is doing and proceeds

oblivious of his appalled and scattering flock - a problem spotlighted by the

fall of Jimmy Swaggart. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#59:1> The seemingly careless rendering suits the leader's carelessness;

behind him dark and light tones, polarized like the forces in conflict,

direct attention to him, as does the vibrant red of passion he is clothed in.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#60:1> Figures in Escoffery's gouache diptych <it> Rootsman Series: War

Games, Cherchez the Anti-Christ </it> are small and busy, conflicting with

the ethereal quality of the broadly applied pastel colours which flood them.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#61:1> She derides puny man's antics on the cosmic stage: the Gulf

hostilities, and the Search for the Anti-Christ by the Laymen For Religious

Liberty. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#62:1> Architecture as used by Tina Matkovich-Spiro and Allan <quote> 'Zion'

</quote> Johnson awakens environmental awareness.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#63:1> In Matkovich-Spiro's <it> Beyond </it> , perspective heightens the

realism of an interior with tiled floor.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#64:1> An open central door invites one to walk through to a luminous though

hazy landscape but one stops short, revolted by decay on the left wall and a

large dark splotch at the lower left corner - not on the floor but on the

surface of the painting itself.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#65:1> A crow, symbol of death, hovers over the land. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#66:1> <it> Beyond </it> protests man's despoiling of his environment.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#67:1> With all the dark patches on the sinister left of the painting, the

composition is unbalanced.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#68:1> If only we could set it, and the world, right. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#69:1> On the other hand, Johnson's <it> Half Way Tree ( Woman of

Culture) </it> is a gleaming mosaic of painted <quote> 'tesserae' </quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#70:1> Virtually ignoring perspective, Johnson focused on the exterior

decorative elements of an old house.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#71:1> Bricks, jalousies and shingles pattern the whole. </p> <O> photograph

</O> <O> caption </O>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#72:1> The dual stairway, reduced to an oval, flows upwards to the

portico and downwards to the curved garden kerbs, linking the house to its

setting.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#73:1> Two African goddesses, one at each lower corner, like caryatids

support the composition by filling the space left by the receding sides of

the building.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#74:1> Laden fruit trees patterning the distance tell of a fruitful land.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#75:1> Here Johnson saw only the beauty of his surroundings: order and

neatness prevail.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#76:1> Fantastic, magical, with bright primary colours, green, black and

white, this painting is a song of praise to our environment. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#77:1> The dark side of the mind is a hidden habitat of horrors few

attempt to peer into, but some artists ventured to probe it using animals,

people or spirits to gain entrance.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#78:1> Their works are certainly the most disturbing and among the most

moving in the show. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#79:1> Margaret Chen's triptych <it> Shadow I, II & III </it> reveals

our planet's tragedy through ambiguous shapes of dying creatures in the outer

panels and the horned head of a dead ox in the central panel, the ox being an

ancient symbol for the Creative Force. <fnr> ( 4) </fnr> <footnote> ( 4)

Harold Bayley, <it> The Lost Language of Symbolism </it> . New York: Barnes &

Noble, 1957, vol. 1, 308. </footnote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#80:1> In this web of chiaroscuro, dark red shapes warn of death by

pollution or spilled blood.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#81:1> In Panel II, a prominent crimson circle flows into a shape like

Africa, adding historical dimensions to the work. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#82:1> Here Death remains an enigma and murder, direct or indirect, a

crime against Life which the artist's search brings to our attention. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#83:1> Robert Cookhorne's diptych <it> Two Horses </it> ,

black-shouldered and red- faced, reared from another dark terrain: untameable

primitive drives <fnr> ( 5) </fnr> buried deep within us in what Jung called

<quote> 'original mind' </quote> , and barely held in check by religion,

custom and law. <footnote> ( 5) Carl G. Jung, <it> Man and His Symbols </it> .

New York: Doubleday, 1979, p. 174. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#84:1> The two almost identical dark horses differ in expression: in

one, eyes are askance, the mouth sulky; in the other, the eyes challenge the

viewer's and the mouth is cunning. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#85:1> They come from the same corral as the white horse in Fuseli's

<itThe Nightmare   </it> but Cookhorne's are grotes-que and strike

immediately at a deep level, stirring a dormant part of the psyche. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#86:1> When Prudence Lovell was in Jamaica in the early eighties she

painted deftly patterned landscapes in watercolour.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#87:1> It was astonishing to see her two large charcoal drawings <it> Red

Street III </it> and <it> Interval </it> .
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#88:1> P.D. Ouspensky taught that each of us has a number of I's within

which self-mastery unites in the single I. <fnr> ( 6) </fnr> <footnote> ( 6)

P.D. Ouspensky, <it> The Fourth Way. </it> New York: Vintage, 1971, pp. 3,

16. </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#89:1> In these drawings it is as if one of Lovell's slumbering I's were

roused and given rein. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#90:1> Related to Grosz's prisoners in <it> The Robbers </it> from his

New Objectivity phase, Lovell's lifesize, stolid figures, vacant of will, are

distrubingly real.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#91:1> Use of charcoal emphasizes their colourless existence.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#92:1> The subject is prostitution: degraded womanhood in a man's sterile

world. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-007#93:1> Lovell, looking at life with new eyes, has become a powerful

social commentator.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#94:1> Seen at a distance, Everald Brown's <it> Duppy City </it> is a

fragile, green-webbed landscape but things are not what they seem.
<ICE-JA:W2B-007#95:1> This is a mysterious world of transformation filled with double

images: foliage becomes crowds of spirit beings; cultivated hills, shaped

like the M of spiritual waters, <fnr> ( 7) </fnr> metamorphose into two

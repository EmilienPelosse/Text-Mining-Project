<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-012#1:1> <h> <bold> Morality  A Lost Cause? </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-012#2:1> The Jamaican society now stands at a most critical point in its

self- understanding and in the working out of its own future shape, form and

structure at the most fundamental level.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#3:1> In this respect, it is like many other societies which fall into a

similar relationship with the larger and dominant centres of Western

civilization ( Western Europe, the United Kingdom, North America) and which

share a somewhat similar sociopolitical history and experience and face

similar developmental challenges and issues.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#4:1> This is evident in nearly every area of Jamaican life  social,

political, economic and cultural.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#5:1> There is no area, however, in which it is more evident, than in the

area of morality.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#6:1> Even though it may not be readily acknowledged, the lack of

acknowledge <l> ment itself, which may also represent a lack of interest, is

very much a part of the critical nature of our present situation. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-012#7:1> In this particular situation, the society appears to be at a point

where it is both catching up with and being carried away by the spirit of the

times.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#8:1> On the one hand it seems to be belatedly coming to terms with what for

some time was known as the spirit of the modern age, associ <l> ated with

Western culture and civilization  and origina <l> ting in the eighteenth

century.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#9:1> This represented a new framework of thought and new rational criteria

for reflecting on life, investigating reality, relating to nature and

managing human affairs.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#10:1> It meant that previously unchallenged, and presumably

unchallengeable, sources of knowledge and moral authority were brought under

scrutiny and considered, in some instances, to be found wanting.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#11:1> A new sense of individual liberty and an awareness of the ability of

human beings to think through their problems and provide their own

self-determined and self-sufficient answers became domin <l> ant factors in

Western thought.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#12:1> It has taken some time, but now this is a prevailing spirit in our

midst. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-012#13:1> There are some specific and significant ways in which this spirit

has manifested itself.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#14:1> The following are worthy of note. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-012#15:1> <h> <bold> <it> The Growth of a Secular Outlook </it> </bold>

</h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-012#16:1> Religion, particularly the Christian religion, has long played a

dominant role in our society.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#17:1> It is often claimed that there are more church buildings to the

square mile in Jamaica than anywhere else in the world.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#18:1> Whether this is a really so or not, it is speaking to a perceived

religious saturation of the society.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#19:1> Continued formal and semi-formal gestures of recognition ( not

necessarily appreciation) accorded the church and its represent <l> atives

seem to testify to the continuity of traditional assumptions of power,

authority and influence.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#20:1> The influence of religion on the day to day speech and vocabulary of

the people would seem to bespeak widespread religious allegiance. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-012#21:1> The fact of the matter is, however, that the secular option has

become very real and the spirit of secularism has been having a very decisive

formative influence on general viewpoints, attitudes and policies of action

throughout the society. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-012#22:1> The church, a long-standing source and centre of moral authority

and influence, can no longer take for granted that it has any special right

or privilege to be heard and to have its dictates respected and followed.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#23:1> No longer does it have the almost universally accepted role of being

the predominant shaper of the public moral consciousness and chief guardian

of public morality.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#24:1> It can no longer simply press for legislation and, codes of conduct

that presuppose everybody is religious and, more specifically, Christian or

accepting Christian ethical convictions and codes.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#25:1> In addition, there are today areas of life, social, cultural and

political, in which the church is no longer considered to have the competence

or right to comment on or offer counsel and advice.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#26:1> Above all, there is a greater readiness as well as a sense of freedom

to reject outright any religious opposition which is considered to interfere

with people's individual rights and private lives.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#27:1> Whether the issues is gambling, attitudes to holy days and seasons,

forms of entertainment and revelry, the content of popular local music or the

morality of male-female relationships and the quality of family life, the

church's voice of protest and moral instruction no longer bears the

authoritative significance it once did. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-012#28:1> Frequent vandalism of church property and robbery of churches,

emergent and increasing anti-clericalism and more open questioning and

dismissal of the teachings of the church are recent phenomena that bear out

evidence of a new attitude that does not accord a certain respect and regard

to religion and its representative institutions, persons and traditions.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#29:1> Much greater freedom is being displayed, on more than one level, in

rejecting or ignoring religious protests made on presumed moral grounds

against certain socioeconomic policies and lifestyle pursuits. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-012#30:1> All of this undoubtedly is part of the general sidelining of the

religious influence with its strong claims to moral knowledge and authority.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#31:1> Whereas opinion polls at the present moment would still show that a

majority of persons in our society share some form of religious beliefs and

conviction, there is no doubt that the secular spirit is growing. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-012#32:1> So much of the continued acknowledgement the church receives is

more the relic of a perception of past signifi <l> cance than an appreciation

of current relevance.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#33:1> The church is no longer an almost universally accepted source of

moral authority.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#34:1> It has lost its power, and even its confidence, to define and

dominate areas of public debate concerning, among other things, the

community's morals.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#35:1> There is now in evidence a falling away from the belief that the

society is a Christian society along with the corresponding moral

implications. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-012#36:1> <h> <it> a) Multiplicity of Moral Options </it> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-012#37:1> The emergence of the secular option and the onset of the spirit

of secularism also mark the growing recognition of the existence of a

multiplicity of religious perspectives, world views, moral frames of

reference and preferences of lifestyles.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#38:1> Not only has the hegemony of mainline Christian denominations been

broken by a proliferation of other Christian and religious groups, including

our very own home-grown Rastafarianism, but other viewpoints, philosophies,

world-views  borrowed, copied, adopted and adapted  have become, and are

increasingly becoming, part of daily life.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#39:1> Controversies, disagreements, competing claims, differences,

variation and variety rather than agreement and consensus, or even dialogue

and conversation, are becoming more and more the order of the day.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#40:1> The opinion and letter <w> writers' </w> columns in the press,

radio-talk shows, <quote> 'Vox Pop' </quote> , sermons, public speeches, and

street talk all convey this. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-012#41:1> Indeed, even in terms of the once dominant Christian moral

perspective, obvious internal divisions appear.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#42:1> In the recent debate over the reintroduction of a lottery, we

the church. <fnr> 1 </fnr> <footnote>

church groups and their members to consider curtailing their business or

cease doing business with the Bank of Nova Scotia Jamaica Ltd, which is the

custodian, wholesaler and retailer of the lottery tickets. <fnr> 2 </fnr>

<footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#45:1> 2. Press release issued by the National Executive of the New

<ICE-JA:W2B-012#46:1> There were also constant queries as to the right of the churches to

dictate to people on such a matter.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#47:1> It was taken for granted that the church was overstepping its bounds

in seeking to dictate in such an area. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-012#48:1> Both within the ranks of the church and outside, a plurality of

viewpoints manifested itself. This is but a recent dramatic example, but it

reflects what is taking place at several levels, on an ongoing basis.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#49:1> It is a growing trend.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#50:1> No single voice of morality is recognized as having any absolute

right to dictate proper moral behaviour.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#51:1> In this we can see an indication of an abandonment of any universally

accepted source of moral authority.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#52:1> The society is becoming one of open options.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#53:1> A variety of moral frameworks is being recognized, providing

alternative options for the individual.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#54:1> Indeed, there are even some individuals who might not recognize any

of the existing options as the norm, but establish their own.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#55:1> Such a situation now prevails in our present social context. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-012#56:1> <h> <it> b) Individual Moral Autonomy </it> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-012#57:1> There are those who can recall a time when, generally speaking,

most, if not all, persons accepted or acknow <l> ledged the same rules or

codes of conduct in their society.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#58:1> Whether everybody was faithful to them or not, everybody regarded

them as important and warranting being observed and obeyed.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#59:1> There were rules and codes of conduct sanctioned by the traditional

centres of moral authority, for example, the church, the school and the home,

which were themselves, for the most part, mutually supportive in this regard.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#60:1> It cannot be said that such a situation obtains today in our society.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#61:1> With burgeoning pluralism there is the relativizing of morality and a

corresponding individualizing of the moral decision making process.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#62:1> There is what Mervyn Alleyne regards as a confrontation of cultures,

the traditional collectivism of the Jamaican culture being met head-on by

European individualism. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-012#63:1> In the case of the latter, emphasis is placed on individuals

being responsible for their own personal welfare.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#64:1> They should be free from all restraint as long as they remain within

the constraints of the law and, to a lesser extent, also within the bounds of

morality.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#65:1> The goal of life is the individual's own personal success which

invariably is achieved mainly at the expense of others.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#66:1> Those who do not work to achieve this goal are regarded as lazy and

<quote> 'good for nothing' </quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#67:1> It is a matter of two world views in which there are different

orderings of priorities. <fnr> 3 </fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#68:1> 3. Alleyne, Mervyn. <it> Roots of Jamaican Culture, </it> Pluto

Press, London. 1988. pp.192-3 </footnote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-012#69:1> Increasingly, people feel that they are confronted with a variety

of moral options with no overarching moral consensus or absolute moral

<ICE-JA:W2B-012#70:1> The autonomous self assumes the ascendancy  each person chooses his

or her own moral frame of reference and preference as seen fit.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#71:1> There is really no right or wrong lifestyle.
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#72:1> It is essentially a matter of personal opinion, personal wishes,

<ICE-JA:W2B-012#73:1> There is an absence of what Peter Berger refers to as a <quote> 'plausibility

<ICE-JA:W2B-012#74:1> 4. Berger, Peter. <it> The Heretical Imperative </it> , Anchor

Press/Doubleday, Garden City, N.Y: 1979. pp. 7ff. </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2B-012#75:1> And so, whether it is a matter of what one does in terms of business

formed personal convictions. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-012#76:1> The assumption here is that people should not be brought under

<ICE-JA:W2B-012#77:1> It is simply not the business of society how individuals exercise

<ICE-JA:W2B-012#79:1> As far as he is concerned: <quote> 'The really important ethical

<ICE-JA:W2B-012#80:1> 5. Friedman, Milton. <it> Capitalism and Freedom </it> , University

<ICE-JA:W2B-012#81:1> Interestingly it would seem that Friedman's economic ideology, which

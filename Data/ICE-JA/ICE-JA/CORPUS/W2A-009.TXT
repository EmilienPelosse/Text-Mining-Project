<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-009#X38:1> <h> <it> Lifestyle of Maroons in Mexico </it> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-009#X39:1> If the circumstances and location of runaways were varied, so

too were the life <l> styles resulting from maronage.
<ICE-JA:W2A-009#X40:1> There were isolated runaways, many of whom sought to lose themselves

in towns or areas of free coloureds or Indians.
<ICE-JA:W2A-009#X41:1> There were many others, who joined together ( or with other racial

groups) in gangs of vagabonds and bandits, living off the proceeds of robbery

and general pillage, while operating mainly along main roadways. <fnr> <sp>

10 </sp> </fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-009#X42:1> 10 Martin, <foreign> <it> Los vagabundos </it> </foreign> , 95.

</footnote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-009#X43:1> There were others who set up their <foreign> palenques

such as the Sierra de Zongolica, Veracruz, or the Sierras de Guerrero.
<ICE-JA:W2A-009#X44:1> While many of these communities developed an agricultural lifestyle,

others supplemented their farming activities with proceeds from robbing the

colonists. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-009#X45:1> Equally varied was the relationship between Maroons and other

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-009#X73:1> <h> <it> Reactions of Spanish colonists </it> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-009#X74:1> In trying to curb the phenomenon of maronage, the Spaniards used

a variety of measures, chief among which were punitive ones.
<ICE-JA:W2A-009#X75:1> Severe punishments were legis <l> lated against runaways, including

castration and, particularly for leaders, death.
<ICE-JA:W2A-009#X76:1> At times, the authorities were reluctant to return runaways to their

<quote> " owners" </quote> , and preferred to keep them imprisoned.
<ICE-JA:W2A-009#X77:1> In most cases, a runaway was valued at much lower than normal fees

because of the economic risk of their repeating the act.
<ICE-JA:W2A-009#X78:1> In some areas, runaways were sent to work in the mines. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-009#X79:1> And yet, throughout the entire period of slavery, the most

virulently puni <l> tive measures proved incapable of stopping the process of

maronage.
<ICE-JA:W2A-009#X80:1> Other measures were equally ineffective.
<ICE-JA:W2A-009#X81:1> For example, the establishing of the Spanish town of C&oacute;rdoba

in 1617 as a sort of garrison town along the Puebla-Veracruz highway, to

control maronage and slaves in the area, did not prevent them from running

away and damaging colonial property. <fnr> <sp> 20 </sp> </fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-009#X82:1> 20 See Enrique Herrera Moreno, <foreign> <it> El cant&oacute;n de

C&oacute;rdoba. </foreign> Vol. 1 </it> ( M&eacute;xico: Ed Citlaltepetl,

1959). </footnote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-009#X83:1> Armed expeditions against the <foreign> palenques </foreign> met

with some success in the Ver <l> acruz area, but failed in the more remote

and difficult terrain of Guerrero.
<ICE-JA:W2A-009#X84:1> But these military campaigns proved to be very expensive and also

diverted the mi <l> litia members from productive work on their farms.
<ICE-JA:W2A-009#X85:1> The Catholic Church was trying to convince the Maroons that

Christianity and pacifism were the correct paths to follow, teachings which

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-009#X86:1> Where the path of violence was inconclusive in controlling the

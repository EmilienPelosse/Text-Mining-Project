<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#1:1> <h> <bold> The Effect of Kingston Harbour Outflow on the

Zooplankton Populations of Hellshire, South-east Coast, Jamaica </bold> </h>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#2:1> <bold> Mona K. Lindo </bold> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#5:1> <bold> Keywords: </bold> zooplankton populations; spatial

distribution; eutrophication; Kingston Harbour; Hellshire coastline </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#6:1> Zooplantkon sampling was conducted at 16 stations located at the

mouth of Kingston Harbour and throughout the Hellshire area from November

1985 to March 1987.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#7:1> Parameters examined included total biomass, total numbers and numbers

of numerically important zooplankton species.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#8:1> Maximum values were recorded west of the Harbour mouth ( station 1)

and these gradually decreased with distance from the harbour especially at

the <quote> 'offshore' </quote> stations, producing a gradient effect in this

area.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#9:1> Mean biomass and abundance for the period sampled ranged from 14 gm

<sp> - 3 </sp> and 16 313 individuals m <sp> - 3 </sp> at the western side of

the Harbour mouth to 0.4 gm <sp> - 3 </sp> and 172 individuals m <sp> - 3

</sp> at the Wreck Reef.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#10:1> Stations within the bays of Hellshire occasionally had values similar

to those recorded at the mouth of Kingston Harbour and here there was less

evidence of a gradual decline.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#11:1> Considerable monthly fluctuation occurred in these parameters but

there was no discernible seasonal pattern.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#12:1> Copepods dominated the population at most stations and the sergestid

<it> Lucifer faxoni </it> also proved an important member at the western

Harbour mouth station. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#13:1> <h> <bold> Introduction </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#14:1> Tropical coastal zooplankton communities have been employed in

the identification of water quality and as an index of eutrophication in

numerous investigations ( Cluter, 1973; Desai, 1977; Youngbluth, 1976; Wear,

1984).
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#15:1> Usually the pollutant reduces species diversity, increases population

size and causes episodic pulses of the zooplankton community. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#16:1> In Jamaica, Kingston Harbour has been the focus of numerous

ecological studies which primarily sought to monitor the levels of pollution

and its impact on the flora and fauna of the area.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#17:1> Wade ( 1971) reported that the Harbour was polluted and that the most

severe form of pollution was eutrophication.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#18:1> Successive studies revealed the rate of deterioration of the benthic

communities was such that total destruction was imminent if the increasing

eutrophication remained unchecked ( Wade <it> et al. </it> , 1972).
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#19:1> This increasing organic pollution was also reported to have

significant effects on the planktonic communities within the Harbour (

Grahame, 1976; Moore & Sander, 1979), causing increases in phytoplankton

production and zooplankton community size.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#20:1> By 1982, Moore and Sander described Kingston Harbour as a highly

eutrophic body of water. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#21:1> Previous zooplankton investigations on the southern shelf of

Jamaica have compared the eutrophic Harbour to the coastal shelf and oceanic

waters ( Moore, 1967; Moore & Sander, 1979).
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#22:1> However, there is no comprehensive investigation of the zooplankton

of the Hellshire area which lies immediately south-west of Kingston Harbour.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#23:1> It has been suggested that outflow from the harbour, which is

normally via a weak, southerly-flowing surface current ( Sherwin & Deeming,

1980), impacts the Hellshire coastline.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#24:1> This polluted outflow is thought to produce a gradient of

eutrophication from the north-eastern sector of Hellshire which decreases

towards the south-west.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#25:1> Investigations along Hellshire have also reported rapid, deleterious

changes in the coral reef and seagrass communities, coupled with observations

of severe beach erosion in the bays closest to Kingston Harbour ( Head &

Hendry, 1985; Bacon & Head, 1985).
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#26:1> The observed degra <l> dation along Hellshire and the proposed

further development of the area as a major urban centre created the need for

intensive baseline studies and the identification of sources of

eutrophication to the area. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#27:1> The above information suggests that there is one major source of

eutrophication to the Hellshire area, Kingston Harbour.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#28:1> These polluted waters are advected through the area undergoing

progressive dilution by oceanic waters as the distance form the harbour

increases.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#29:1> Alternatively there may be other sources of eutrophication located in

the Hellshire area which would have immediate and large-scale effects on the

area. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#30:1> The objectives of this study were therefore to provide basic data

on the zooplankton populations of Hellshire, identify zooplankton indices

which may be useful monitors of increasing organic pollution (

eutrophication) and identify the source or sources of eutrophication stress

to the Hellshire area. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#31:1> <h> <bold> Site description </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#32:1> The Hellshire area ( Figure 1), which lies south-west of

Kingston, consists of approxi <l> mately 10 000 acres of arid karst limestone

hills, coastal lagoons, beaches and rocky head <l> lands.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#33:1> The 27-km stretch of coastline consists of a variety of highly

productive coastal habitats which include coral reefs, seagrass beds and

mangals.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#34:1> The major lagoonal system in the area, the Great Salt Pond, is a

shallow mangrove lagoon of 17.2 ha ( Reeson, 1971).
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#35:1> It receives land runoff and partially treated sewage from the Breaton

area of St Catherine ( P. Bacon, pers. comm.)
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#36:1> The Salt Pond opens into the eastern end of Hellshire Bay via a 10-m

wide artificial channel. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#37:1> The north-eastern sector of the Hellshire coastline which is

delimited by Small Point in the north-east and Wreck Point in the south-west

is 15 km long and consists of seven major bays.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#38:1> These bays are all relatively shallow ( 1-5 m) and are protected by

reefs and sand banks to varying degrees.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#39:1> Kingston Harbour will not be described here as this has been

extensively done by Goodbody ( 1961) and Moore and Sander ( 1982). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#40:1> <h> <bold> Methods </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#41:1> The 16 stations selected ( Figure 1) were sampled at monthly

intervals for a 17- month period.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#42:1> Three stations ( 1, 2 and 3) were located across the mouth of

Kingston Harbour, six ( stations 4, 6, 7, 8, 9 and 10) within the bays found

along the Hellshire coastline and one ( station 5) inside the Great Salt

Pond.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#43:1> Five stations ( 11 to 15) were located approximately <O> map </O> <O>

caption </O> 3 km from the shoreline ( called <quote> 'offshore' </quote>

stations).
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#44:1> An additional station ( 16) was located at South-east Cay, the most

easterly of the Port Royal Cays, in an area which would not come under the

influence of Kingston Harbour or be affected by land runoff from Hellshire.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#45:1> Zooplankton collections were made with a 200-&mu; m mesh plankton

net with a hoop diameter of 0.5 m ( SCOR, WP2 pattern; UNESCO, 1968). Surface

tows were conducted in a circular path ( circle diameter 30-40 m) at a speed

of 0.5 ms <sp> - 1 </sp> and lasted 5 min.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#46:1> Sampling coefficient of variation, given by the equation, C.V. = (

standard deviaton x 100)/mean involving repeated tows at the same station (

n=5), was determined for all stations.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#47:1> This yielded an average sampling coefficient of variation for the

area of 15.6% ( range 2.29-25.99).
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#48:1> Salinity and temperature measurements were also done at each station

contemporaneously with faunal collections.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#49:1> The filtering efficiency of the net used was determined at each

station using two general oceanic digital flow meters with low-velocity

rotors.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#50:1> This ranged from 50% at the Harbour mouth to 80% at Wreck Reef. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#51:1> Identification and counting were performed on an aliquot of the

sample preserved in 10% formalin.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#52:1> A subsample was taken using a 60-ml graduated syringe while keeping

the sample agitated ( Tremblay & Roff, 1983).
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#53:1> All taxonomic groups were subsampled in this manner with the

exception of the larger medusae and ctenophores which were too large to be

picked up by the syringe and were counted separately.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#54:1> Each subsample was transferred to the circular tray of a modified

James Ward Plankton Counter ( Bacon, 1971; Ferraris, 1982) and viewed under a

Wolfe dissecting microscope.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#55:1> The precision of this technique was tested by enumerating the total

numbers in five subsamples from each station and calculating the coefficient

of variation.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#56:1> These yielded an average subsampling coefficient of variation of 7.5%

( range between 2.07 and 16.28).
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#57:1> For biomass determinations, a portion of each sample was washed with

distilled water and dried to constant weight at 60 &degree; C. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#58:1> Two communities index tests were applied to the data using the

species composition.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#59:1> These were the Jaccard Community Coefficient ( Whittaker & Fairbanks,

1958) and the Percent Similarity Coefficient ( e.g. Kwiatkowski & Roff,

1976).
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#60:1> The Jaccard Community Coefficient is given by the formula <O> formula

</O> where <it> a </it> is the number of species present at both stations,

<it> b </it> is the number of species present at station 1 but not at station

2 and <it> c </it> is the number of species present at station 2 but not at

station 1.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#61:1> This coefficient thus neglects conjoint absences and is based on

presence or absence, not the number of times present of the quantity.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#62:1> Thus single rare occurrences will carry as much weight as those that

are regular and in large amounts.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#63:1> The Percent Similarity Coefficient is given by the equation <O>

formula </O> where <it> a' </it> and <it> b' </it> are the respective

percentage of animals ( for each species) at each pair of stations.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#64:1> This coefficient neglects single rare occurrences and thus may

compensate for the weakness of the Jaccard Community Coefficient.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#65:1> Both coefficients were calculated com <l> paring the community at

stations 1, 6 and 15 to that at all other stations.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#66:1> Stations 1 ( Harbour mouth) and 6 ( Great Salt Pond) were the

suspected sources to the area and 15 was thought to represent the extreme

pristine end of the system. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#67:1> <h> <bold> Results and discussion </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#68:1> <h> <it> Physical parameters </it> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#69:1> Salinity and temperature with means of 33.5 and 28.5 &degree; C,

respectively, were found to not vary significantly spatially or temporally (

MANOVA test with <it> F </it> value probabilities of 0.9670 and 0.9136).
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#70:1> The lack of spatial and temporal variation in these variables is

typical of this area ( Moore & Sander, 1979, 1982) and thus cannot account

for spatial variation of the biota.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#71:1> The salinity in Kingston Harbour and along Hellshire changes

significantly only under conditions of extreme flooding ( Goodbody, 1961).

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#72:1> <h> <it> Sampling variability </it> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#73:1> The coefficient of variation associated with subsamples and

repeated plankton tows at each station indicated the degree of variability

associated with the techniques.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#74:1> It must be noted that the variation inherent in the methodology (

average 7.5 and 15.6% C.V.) was low compared with that obtained from monthly

variation in the parameters, which in some cases exceeded 300% C.V. ( Figure

3). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#75:1> <h> <it> Zooplankton biomass and abundance </it> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#76:1> It can be postulated that high mean biomass or numbers coupled

with high monthly fluctuations are indicative of an area subject to periodic

large-scale enrichment ( land <l> derived nutrients); high mean values with

low fluctuations indicate regular large-scale enrichment.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#77:1> Similarly, low mean values with high monthly fluctuations are

indicative of an area subject to infrequent enrichment and areas where both

mean values and monthly fluctuations are low constantly receive little or no

enrichment. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#78:1> Biomass and numbers m <sp> - 3 </sp> of the zooplankton

population at each station ( Figure 2) varied significantly throughout the

area ( Table 1).
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#79:1> Maximum mean values were recorded at station 1, at the west of the

Harbour mouth, usually followed by the other Harbour mouth stations ( 2 and

3).
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#80:1> Occasionally the population size at station 5 at the mouth of the

Great Salt Pond and station 7 in Hellshire Bay exceeded those at the harbour

mouth stations, and the monthly fluctuations ( % C.V.) calculated for these

stations usually exceeded that at the Harbour or any other station throughout

the area.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#81:1> The degree of monthly fluctuation ( % C.V.) in total numbers and

biomass was greater than 50% at most stations and was as high as 200% at

stations 5 and 9.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#82:1> High monthly fluctuation or episodic behaviour is not uncommon in

tropical coastal zooplankton communities, but such large values suggest

community instability usually resulting from enrichment. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-022#83:1> There was a pattern of gradual decline in total numbers at

<quote> 'offshore' </quote> stations ( 11 to 15) with distance from the

Harbour.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#84:1> There was no similar gradual decline along the bays due to the large

values recorded at stations 5 and 7.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#85:1> Station 4 ( Green Bay) is the closest bay to the Harbour and would

therefore be expected to have the greatest values of all bay stations.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#86:1> Stations within Hellshire Bay however had the greatest populations of

all the bay stations.
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#87:1> Thus the distribution within the bays cannot be explained by gradual

dilution with distance from a source of enrichment ( Kingston Harbour).
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#88:1> Simple linear regression revealed that a greater per cent of the

variation in biomass and, to a lesser extent, total numbers at the <quote> 'offshore'

</quote > stations was attributable to distance from the harbour, more so

than at the bays ( Table 2).
<ICE-JA:W2A-022#89:1> This successful regression model suggests a gradual decline with

distance along Hellshire which is strongest along the <quote> 'offshore'

</quote> area. </p>

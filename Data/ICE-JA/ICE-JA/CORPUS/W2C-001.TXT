<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#1:1> <h> Florists could lose Valentine sales boom </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#2:1> By Mark Dawes </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#3:1> Florists could lose out on sales opportunities this Valentine's

Day, because of a decision by the Government to ban cut flowers originating

from cholera-infested countries of South America. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#4:1> But local spokesmen for the floral industry have criticized the

government's decision, arguing that flowers come to Jamaica after satisfying

quarantine requirements of the United States. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#5:1> Florists claim that cholera cannot be transmitted by a flower,

unless it is eaten. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#6:1> This decision to stop the importation of flowers originating in

South America was made by a committee comprised of florists and officials

from the Ministry of Agriculture, sources told the <it> Gleaner </it> .
<ICE-JA:W2C-001#7:1> That decision was made about two years ago when the deadly disease was

diagnosed in Peru and began to sweep across countries of Latin and South

America. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#8:1> Some florists say that the fear of the Government seems misplaced,

as major entities such as the World Health Organisation ( WHO), the Pan

American Health Organisation ( PAHO), and the United States Department of

Agriculture have all conducted tests and have found that the disease is not

transmitted by flowers. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#9:1> Most flowers which came to the island in the past, did so after

satisfying quarantine and other health standards of the United States,

sources told the <it> Gleaner </it> . </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#10:1> Maurice Facey of Jamaica Floral Exports said that with South

American flowers satisfying the high health standards of the United States

and Holland, the Jamaican Government, perhaps, needed to rethink its position

regarding the importation of flowers from South America. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#11:1> Dr. Barry Wint, Chief Medical Officer in the Ministry of Health,

said that contrary to information reaching the <it> Gleaner </it> , the MOH

was not part of the decision to stop the importation of flowers from South

America.
<ICE-JA:W2C-001#12:1> He said that the transmission of cholera via flowers was possible,

but an unlikely occurrence.
<ICE-JA:W2C-001#13:1> It would become possible, he said, if a person on handling a plant

which harbours the germ, then proceeds to handle food just before partaking

of it. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#14:1> Florists told the <it> Gleaner </it> that Jamaica grew most of

the flowers it needed for the local market, as well as for the export market.
<ICE-JA:W2C-001#15:1> However, a problem exists with regards to carnations. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#16:1> Carnations, according to Geoffrey Shields, Managing Director of

Shields and Shields Ltd, is an indispensable filler for floral arrangements

in Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2C-001#17:1> Florists have no alternative but to import carnation as it requires a

temperature cooler than typical Jamaican climate, in order to thrive. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#18:1> Mr. Shields and a prominent New Kingston flower dealer told the

<it> Gleaner </it> that the absence of carnation in the stores this year is

likely to reduce their potential sales for Valentine's Day. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#19:1> <quote> " It is pathetic that they ( the Government) have taken

that decision," </quote> said the New Kingston florist.
<ICE-JA:W2C-001#20:1> He claims to have lost about US$3,000 from a shipment of carnation

four months ago, which the Government burnt, rather than allowing it in the

country. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#21:1> Both Mr. Shields and Mr. Facey acknowledged that it was cheaper

to import carnation than to attempt to grow it locally. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#22:1> The inability to import carnation has resulted in a number of

florists importing silk flowers, cologne, stuffed toys and chocolates to fill

gift orders that arise during peak seasons such as Valentine's Day, Mr.

Shields said. </p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#23:2> <h> Appalling conditions at Kingston Abattoir </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#24:2> UNTREATED water, failure to collect garbage and offal have left

the Municipal Abattoir in Kingston in an unsanitary condition. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#25:2> The <it> Gleaner </it> understands that meat at the abattoir is

being washed with unpurified water from a nearby well.
<ICE-JA:W2C-001#26:2> At present, there are two sources of water at the abattoir, the

regular National Water Commission ( NWC) pipes and from the well. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#27:2> It is said that attempts to get chlorine tablets from the Public

Health Department since last year to treat the water from the well have gone

unheeded for over six months. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#28:2> A Ministry of Health official explained that the main reason why

meat was washed was that during the slaughtering process parts of the

entrails, which could be contaminated, come in contact with the meat.
<ICE-JA:W2C-001#29:2> She said the chlorine tablets were expensive and had to be used every

day to treat water, and that it is the responsibility of the authority in

charge of the well to acquire the chlorine tablets. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#30:2> Added to the problem, there is an irregular collection of offal -

the entrails and internal organs - from the abattoir.
<ICE-JA:W2C-001#31:2> Stanley Shaw, Acting Manager of the abattoir confirmed to the <it>

Gleaner </it> yesterday, that the collection of offal from the abattoir was

irregular. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#32:2> A KSAC statement noted that since Metropolitan Parks and Market (

MPM) stopped removing garbage September last year, a private contract was

signed between the abattoir and Garbage Disposal Limited, a private company

to do removal of garbage twice a week.
<ICE-JA:W2C-001#33:2> In a letter to the KSAC, Mr. Shaw states that <quote> " the service

is not altogether satisfactory as on occasion offals are piled up for days

before removal, due to motor vehicular problems." </quote> </p>

<p> The <it> Gleaner </it> spoke with the Manager of Garbage Disposals and

Sanitation, who said that her company was supposed to collect offals from the

abattoir on Tuesdays and Fridays, but sometimes <quote> " they weren't

collected on these days." </quote>
<ICE-JA:W2C-001#34:2> Asked why the collection was not done on the prescribed days, she

said <quote> " just like other vehicles sometimes they ( vehicles) break

down." </quote>
<ICE-JA:W2C-001#35:2> However, she denied signing any written contract to collect garbage.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#36:2> The Public Health Department has been blamed for its tardiness in

ensuring proper sanitation at the abattoir, as citizens on premises adjacent

to the abattoir, have been rearing pigs which attracts flies and exudes an

offensive odour.
<ICE-JA:W2C-001#37:2> Repeated attempts by the KSAC to get the Public Health Department to

serve immediate notice on the citizens failed. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#38:2> Between November and December last year, 2,588 animals were

slaughtered at the abattoir and 1,196 animals were slaughtered last month (

January).
<ICE-JA:W2C-001#39:2> Revenues collected during the November to December period was

$161, 926.75 and last month collection stood at

$72,364.50. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#40:2> Apart from the irregular collection of offals, there is a large

pool of water at the entrance to the abattoir on Darling Street, because when

it rains it takes days before the water recedes.
<ICE-JA:W2C-001#41:2> Butchers and motorists have been complaining about this and as one

KSAC Councillor puts it: <quote> " It's like driving through the sea."

</quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#42:2> Mr. Shaw in making his report to the KSAC said <quote> " with

limited resources in hand, management is trying its best to keep the abattoir

at a reasonable and acceptable public health standard." </quote>
<ICE-JA:W2C-001#43:2> The lack of telephone facilities at the abattoir was also causing

grave inconveniences and hardships. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#44:2> The matter concerning the abattoir came up for mention at a KSAC

Trust meeting yesterday.
<ICE-JA:W2C-001#45:2> Mayor of Kingston, Marie Aitkins, spoke out against the bad condition

of the abattoir, citing the bad condition of the entrance which she said

<quote> " had become a permanent problem." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#46:2> The state of the animal pound was also mentioned and it was said

that the truck have been having mechanical problems since last month.
<ICE-JA:W2C-001#47:2> Revenues collected between November and December from poundage fees

stood at

$31,044. It could not however, be ascertained, how much revenue was collected

last month. </p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#48:3> <h> 400 MoBay vendors to be shifted </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#49:3> <h> Western Bureau: </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#50:3> EXPANSION work, soon to begin on the Creek in Montego Bay, will

be displacing over 400 vendors at the Dry Goods and Harbour Street markets.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#51:3> The Creek, which runs along both markets, is a shallow, narrow

gully which overflows whenever there is heavy rainfall, affecting the flow of

traffic and operations of businesses nearby. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#52:3> The leasee of the Harbour Street Market, O. Dave Allen told the

<it> Gleaner </it> yesterday that the St. James Parish Council had given

notices last month to vendors at both markets to quit the premises by March

31.
<ICE-JA:W2C-001#53:3> Some 260 of the vendors occupy the Harbour Street Market. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#54:3> <quote> " Some of these vendors are thrown in a state of

confusion concerning their future.
<ICE-JA:W2C-001#55:3> They have constructed their shops - some at costs of up to

$20,000 - and now they have nowhere to relocate or store them.
<ICE-JA:W2C-001#56:3> What will these people do?," </quote> Mr Allen asked. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#57:3> In a letter to the Council, Mr Allen described the notification

as a suggestion of the Council's <quote> " insensitivity and callousness.

&dotted-line;
<ICE-JA:W2C-001#58:3> You offer no hope at all to the former street vendors who have

conformed to the directives of the St James Parish Council to leave the

sidewalks of the city." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#59:3> Mr. Allen stated that the <quote> 'drastic action' </quote> in

dislocating the vendors in both markets without an alternative could serve to

undermine the efforts of the Council to keep vendors from the streets and

sidewalks. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#60:3> When contacted, Mr O'Meally told the <it> Gleaner </it> that

there was no truth in Mr Allen's claim that they were being dislocated.
<ICE-JA:W2C-001#61:3> He said the letter to the vendors served only to advise them that the

move would be temporary and that a date was not yet set for work to begin.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#62:3> <quote> " We are trying to avoid a situation in which the vendors

could later claim that they had not been informed of the project.
<ICE-JA:W2C-001#63:3> A date has not been set, but the Parish Council has asked Government

to construct concrete slabs over the areas where it affects both markets, so

that the vendors can re-occupy the premises as soon as work is completed.
<ICE-JA:W2C-001#64:3> We have not yet received a reply to our request from Government.

</quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-001#65:3> <quote> " We are now in discussions with representatives of the

Dry Goods Market ( who contacted us) with a view to identifying and working

out a temporary arrangement for their relocation.
<ICE-JA:W2C-001#66:3> I do not have a problem with Mr Allen coming in and doing the same,"

</quote> Mr. O'Meally said. </p> </I>

Marwick. </p>

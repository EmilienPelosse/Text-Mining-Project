<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-036#3:1> <h> <bold> Abstract </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-036#4:1> <it> This paper examines the developmental efficacy of the

export-oriented clothing industry in Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#5:1> The objective is to assess the attainability as well as the

desirability of the goals of export-oriented industrialization in terms of ex

<l> port and employment growth, foreign exchange earnings, local value-added,

<ICE-JA:W2A-036#6:1> The findings of the study suggest that this new mode of peripheral

opportunities. </it> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-036#7:1> <h> <bold> INTRODUCTION </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-036#8:1> This paper focuses on the specific case of Jamaica in the

export-oriented clothing industry.
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#9:1> The objective is to assess the developmental efficacy of the

export-oriented industrialization ( EOI) strategy given the emerging transfor

<l> mations in the international political economy and the Caribbean's

history of extraverted and unequal specialization under plantation

agriculture, resource-based industrialization and import-substitution

industrialization.
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#10:1> The paper thus evaluates the attainability as well as the

desirability of the goals of ex <l> port-processing zones ( EPZs) and world

market factories ( WMFs) <fnr> <sp> 1 </sp> </fnr> in a pe <l> ripheral

capitalist economy. <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#11:1> The terms export processing zone ( EPZ) and world market factory (

WMF) are taken from the seminal study of Frobel et al. [ 18].
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#12:1> EPZs are geographic enclaves in which a <quote> " new" </quote> type

of factory  the world market factory ( WMF) which can be located within or

<ICE-JA:W2A-036#13:1> The WMF concept in <l> volves integrated and complementary production

between various affiliates in different countries or regions.
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#14:1> It differs from the affiliate under import substitution where there

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-036#15:1> The paper will examine the performance of EOI and EPZ investments

<l> ing of personnel, and in general, the promotion of industrial

<ICE-JA:W2A-036#16:1> The objective here is to go beyond the sterility of cost-benefit

<ICE-JA:W2A-036#17:1> The paper begins with some reflection on the global expe <l> rience

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-036#18:1> <h> <bold> THE CASE STUDY </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-036#19:1> Jamaica provides an interesting case study because during the

1980s the neo-liberal model of structural adjustment was embraced, first, by

Seaga, as an economic and foreign policy strategy, then, continued by Manley

after the 1989 elections for lack of a political and economic alternative [

Huber and Stephens, 29].
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#20:1> The strategy gained strong financial and intellectual support from

the Bretton Woods institutions and the U.S. administration, especially in the

early 1980s under the Reagan administration. <fnr> <sp> 2 </sp> </fnr>

<footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#21:1> In the period 1980 to 1988, Jamaica received US$1,035 million from

the United States, making it one of the five largest aid beneficiaries per

capita [ See Huber and Stephens 29, p. 68]. </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#22:1> The promotion of EOI was one of the major planks of this strategy.
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#23:1> EPZs and WMFs were estab <l> lished and they eventually made a real

impact on the domestic economy, especially in terms of employment generation

( mostly young women) and the growth of non-traditional exports ( mostly

clothing). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-036#24:1> The export-oriented clothing industry is the dominant sector in

Jamaica's <quote> " new" </quote> export thrust. <fnr> <sp> 3 </sp> </fnr>

<footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#25:1> There are four EPZs in Jamaica, but only three were operational in

December 1990.
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#26:1> Of the operational zones the Kingston Free Zone is the largest  peak

employment of 11,000 in 1987.
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#27:1> The main activity is apparel manufacturing.
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#28:1> The same applies to the Garmex Free Zone.
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#29:1> The Montego Free Zone has some apparel manufacturing and houses a

Digiport system which facilitates information processing.
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#30:1> There is also some export-oriented activity ( apparel and electronic

assembly) in WMFs outside of the EPZs. </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#31:1> It is also the most transnationalized industry, and, despite the

trend towards redeployment to the industrialized countries [ Junne 33], firms

continue to migrate to low wage areas, especially for labour-inten <l> sive,

assembly stage production [ Hoffman 27, Mody and Wheeler 45, Mytelka 46].
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#32:1> Exemplifying this trend is the locational shift of U.S. and Asian

clothing manufacturers into the region, since the early 1980s, to exploit the

low levels of coverage of Caribbean countries under the Multi-Fibre

Arrangement ( MFA), their relative ease of access to the U.S. market through

re-import schemes like the Caribbean Basin Initiative ( CBI) <quote> " super

807", </quote> <fnr> <sp> 4 </sp> </fnr> their cheap labour, and their

geographic proximity to the U.S. [ Steele 55]. <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#33:1> <quote> " Super 807" </quote> is a preferential trading programme

introduced in 1986 that applies to only CBI-eligible countries with whom the

U.S. has negotiated bilateral agreements.
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#34:1> It guarantees access for clothing assembled in the Caribbean without

regard to quota levels providing the clothing is made entirely from U.S.

materials [ See Steele 55]. </footnote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-036#35:1> The study of the export-oriented clothing industry in Jamaica is

also timely because of the establishment of the North American Free Trade

Agree <l> ment ( NAFTA) between the United States, Canada and Mexico.
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#36:1> NAFTA is essentially an economic strategy to enhance the

competitiveness and market share of U.S. firms in the Americas in response to

the high level of import penetration from Japan, Europe and Asia during the

<ICE-JA:W2A-036#37:1> It is envisioned that NAFTA will erode the preferential market access

<ICE-JA:W2A-036#38:1> The argument being put for <l> ward suggests that the trade access

advantage that NAFTA gives to Mexico will result in some trade and investment

diversion from the Caribbean Basin. <fnr> <sp> 5 </sp> </fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#39:1> This sentiment was expressed in a communique at the 17th annual Miami

Conference on the Caribbean.
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#40:1> The conference was sponsored by Caribbean/Latin American Action, a

Washington-based, non-profit advocacy organization for private

</footnote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-036#41:1> These impulses in the world economy behove us to assess the

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-036#44:1> <h> <bold> REFLECTIONS ON THE GLOBAL EXPERIENCE </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-036#45:1> Since the early 1970s, with the onset of the world-wide economic

crisis, many developing countries have experienced worsening economic

conditions ( e.g. declining earnings in commodity exports, balance of

payments crises, debt overhang, chronic unemployment).
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#46:1> As a response, many countries have opted to promote EOI, inviting the

establishment of EPZs and WMFs.
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#47:1> This strategy has enjoyed much favour in international development

policy and praxis, especially among the multilateral financial agencies and

has become the ma <l> jor policy prescription in the structural adjustment

package. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-036#48:1> The confidence in this strategy is derived largely from the

<quote> " success sto <l> ries" </quote> of the newly industrializing

countries ( NICs) of East Asia, particularly when compared with the

performance of the industrially advanced Latin American countries in terms of

growth rates, non-traditional exports, and manufacturing employment.
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#49:1> The Asian success, it is argued, proves that the developmental gains

possible from EOI are superior to the previously favoured strategy of

import-substitution industrialization ( ISI) [ Balassa 2, Krueger 37,

Harberger 25, Lin 41, Fishlow 16].
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#50:1> These authors also argue that the success of the Asian NICs is

attributable to <quote> " getting prices right" </quote> through liberalizing

their economies and reducing the role of the state. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-036#51:1> The <quote> " new" </quote> orthodoxy of EOI has not gone

unchallenged, however.
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#52:1> Sev <l> eral critiques have emerged that question not only the

universality of the EOI prescriptions for developing countries [ Cline10,

Griffith 22, Hamilton 24, Broad and Cavanagh 6], but also the validity of the

orthodoxy's interpre <l> tation of East Asian history and their conception of

the Asian NICs as free trade, laissez-faire economies [ Cummings 13,

Luedde-Neurath 43, Bienefeld 4, Jenkins 32].
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#53:1> For example, Jenkins [ 32] in an article dealing with the les <l>

sons Latin America can learn from the East Asian experience concludes that:

</p> <X>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-036#X54:1> <quote> Attempts to impose neoclassical orthodoxy on the Latin

American countries, in the guise of learning from the East Asian NICs, will

do nothing to resolve the fundamental economic problems of the

re<l>gion&dotted-line;
<ICE-JA:W2A-036#X55:1> Many of the policies currently in vogue in Latin America are based

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-036#X56:1> Additionally, there are those authors that argue that EOI should

<ICE-JA:W2A-036#X57:1> Their argument is based on the view that EOI is the outcome of the

<ICE-JA:W2A-036#X60:1> These transactions accommodate highly centralized decision-making,

<ICE-JA:W2A-036#X61:1> W.W. Blumenthal [ 5], the former U.S. Secretary of the Treasury,

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-036#X62:1> <quote> The enhanced interdependence in world trade is changing

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-036#X63:1> Despite the utility of these critiques, there continues to be

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-036#X68:1> In the Caribbean and Central American region ( excluding the

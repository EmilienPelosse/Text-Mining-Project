<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-006#5:1> <it> Sevilla la Nueva, sometimes referred to as Sevilla de Oro, is

situated in the parish of St Ann, Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#6:1> The Amerinidian village of Maima, a Taino Arawak settle <l> ment, was

once on this site.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#7:1> It was here that Christopher Columbus on his fourth and last voyage

spent one year, June 1503 to June 1504, stranded on the island with his two

worm- eaten ships; beached on the north coast of Jamaica which he had claimed

for his family and which his son, Diego, asserted was without dispute indeed

his father's <quote> 'discovery' </quote> . </it> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-006#8:1> Just four years after Columbus's death in 1505, the settle <l>

ment of Seville was started by Juan de Esquivel, who had been appointed

governor by the Columbus family.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#9:1> Here, in 1509, a town was laid out.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#10:1> In an island described by Columbus as <quote> 'the fairest island

eyes have beheld' </quote> , it is not surprising that those who desired to

build the town of Seville wished it also to be the fairest town eyes had

beheld. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-006#11:1> The layout for Seville, the third Spanish town founded in the

<quote> 'New World' </quote> , was similar to that of the two earlier towns,

Santo Domingo in Hispaniola and Caparra in Puerto Rico.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#12:1> They were all built on the pattern of the Royal grid plan first laid

down for Santa Fe in Spain, the town built outside Granada by <quote> 'Los

Reyes Catolicos' </quote> during their siege of the last Islamic region of

Spain. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-006#13:1> On the Seville property was the quarry which supplied the stone

for the building of this town.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#14:1> Edward Long writing in 1774 said that <quote> 'close adjoining to the

</quote>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-006#18:1> Hans Sloane who was in Jamaica earlier than Long - between 1687

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-006#20:1> Riding over the Seville property in 1937, in the area known as

<ICE-JA:W2B-006#22:1> Five years ago I had the opportunity of speaking with Jack Ritchie,

<ICE-JA:W2B-006#25:1> He recalls seeing the pieces of masonry, wrapped in crocus bag (

burlap) lying in the pimento storehouse of the great house; they were being

<ICE-JA:W2B-006#26:1> Were they stored or dumped?
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#27:1> And when did this take place?
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#28:1> Were they put there for safe <l> keeping when the settlement was

<ICE-JA:W2B-006#29:1> Or were they dumped after 1655, when the English had seized the

<ICE-JA:W2B-006#30:1> Did an irate Captain Hemming, anxious to plant sugar cane on his new

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-006#33:1> <h> <bold> The Builders of New Seville </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-006#34:1> Who were the people who built this town?
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#35:1> Francisco de Garay, who assumed the office of Governor of Jamaica on

behalf of the Spanish Crown in 1515, was an important pioneer. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-006#36:1> An excellent administrator, he established farms and ranches in

the island which, in turn, attracted more settlers to Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#37:1> Charles I ( Emperor Charles V) made him responsible for the

conversion and good treatment of <quote> 'the Indians' </quote> in his

charge.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#38:1> Eventually, hopes of reaching the American mainland lured this Basque

adventurer from the island.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#39:1> A rival of Cortes, he died in Mexico in 1523.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#40:1> The carving of a coat of arms shown in figure 8 is reminiscent of a

simpler one in the Basque region of Spain, where the name <it> de Garay </it>

is to be found.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#41:1> It shows a tree, a wolf and a castle.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#42:1> Was this stone piece part of the castle in which we know Garay lived

before leaving for the mainland?
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#43:1> Another builder, Juan de Mendegurren, succeeded Francisco de Garay as

governor. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-006#44:1> <h> <bold> Friars and Prelates </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-006#45:1> We are aware of the presence of the Franciscan order at Seville

during the period of early settlement but what of the Jeronomites? <fnr> ( 4)

</fnr> <footnote> ( 4)Baxter, Ivy. <it> The Arts of an Island </it> .

N.J.:The Scarecrow Press, Inc., McLuchen, p. 31. 1970. </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#46:1> In their blue habits, this Order, which was much beloved by Charles I

( Emperor Charles V), <fnr> ( 5) </fnr> had arrived in the islands of the

Greater Antilles in the year 1517. <footnote> ( 5)Bridges, George Wilson.

<it> The Annals of Jamaica </it> , London: 1827 ( Frank Cass & Co. Ltd.

1968). p. 532. </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#47:1> Originally a fourteenth century order of hermits under the Rule of St

Augustine, in the late fifteenth and early sixteenth centuries it was used by

the Holy See to reform other religious orders which had lost their first

zeal.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#48:1> In Jamaica the order would have sought to right the wrongs inflicted

under the Encomineda system, originally designed to protect the Amerindians.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-006#49:1> The first four abbots of Jamaica were all absentees: Don Sancho

Matienzo, Don Luis de Figueroa, Don Andres Lopes Frias and Don Pedro Martyr.

<fnr> ( 6) </fnr> <footnote> ( 6) Osborne, S.J. Francis J. <it> History of

the Catholic Church in Jamaica. Chicago: Loyola University Press, 1988. pp

36-38 </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#50:1> There appears to have been a strong Jeronimite connection with both

Figueroa and Peter Martyr.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#51:1> Was the Church a Jeronimite one? </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-006#52:1> Don Pedro Martyr who was born in 1457 at Arona, near Anghiera,

Italy, appears to have been the most influential of these four men.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#53:1> In 1489 he went to the University of Salamanca, Spain, by special

invitation.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#54:1> Later he was to go to the Spanish court where he taught nobles and

prominent Spanish citizens.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#55:1> He became the official Chronicler in 1511, having access to all

documents which passed through the powerful Council of the Indies.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#56:1> It should also be remembered that Abbot Peter Martyr was personally

acquainted with Christopher Columbus.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#57:1> Writer of the book entitled <it> The Decades </it> , there was no one

to equal him in humanistic studies and knowledge of the Indies.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#58:1> In fact, his appointment to the Abbacy of Jamaica in 1524 indicated

that Jamaica ranked high in the eyes of both Spain and Rome. <fnr> ( 7)

</fnr> <footnote> ( 7) Ibid pp 35-36. </footnote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-006#59:1> The career of Peter Martyr, Abbot of Jamaica, coincides with the

flowering of late Gothic art in Spain, his adopted country, and it is

therefore essential for us to look briefly at art in the Spain of Peter

Martyr. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-006#60:1> <h> <bold> Spanish Art 1480-1530 </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-006#61:1> The decorative style of the Late Gothic ( <it> estile florido

</it> ) reached its zenith in that kingdom in the late fifteenth century.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#62:1> The splendour of decorative elements now outweighed the architectural

elements and was expressed in a style called <it> plateresque </it> ( meaning

silversmith-like) which was often extravagant and florid, conveying great

animation and energy. </p> <O> four-photographs </O> <O> four-captions </O>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-006#63:1> During this period, innumerable foreign artists were finding

employment in Spain.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#64:1> The leading ones came from the Netherlands, and among those ingenious

craftsmen was Gil de Siloe.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#65:1> There were artists who were sufficiently talented to be able to solve

any task in a truly creative manner.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#66:1> The Spanish environment, however, with its strong tinge of Moorish

culture, had in turn an immense influence on these foreigners whose work now

reflected certain features of Spanish art. <fnr> ( 8) </fnr> <footnote> (

8)Matejech, Antonin. <it> Art and Architecture in Europe </it> . Batchworth

Press, London: 1960. </footnote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-006#67:1> Sculpture flourished under this decorative dominance.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#68:1> Commissions for portals, choir benches, tombstones, and other pieces

attracted stone masons and wood carvers who introduced every conceivable

variation of style into their work.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#69:1> Shortly before 1520, great national artists, who have sometimes been

referred to as the Spanish Eagles, emerged: among the foremost were Bartolome

Ordonez and Diego de Siloe ( believed to be the son of Gil de Siloe). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-006#70:1> Ordonez's early death robbed the Spanish Renaissance of a great

talent but his association with the younger sculptor Diego de Siloe of Burgos

who had done his apprenticeship in Florence, meant that there was yet another

Spanish artist who could express space and compose with clarity and force.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#71:1> In San Miguel at Onate, Guipuzcoa, is a large monument commissioned

by the Bishop of Granada.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#72:1> At the Cathedral of Granada, Siloe's main sculptural works are four

portals done in the 1530s.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#73:1> The most elaborate is the <it> Puerta del Perdon </it> ( 1537).
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#74:1> Faith and Justice are amid the artist's usual motifs - grotesque

friezes and panels enlivened by monsters, nude athletes, sprouting bird and

flower forms.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#75:1> Although this distinguished architect and sculptor travelled to many

cities in Spain where he was much in demand, it is in Granada that Siloe did

most of his work and where his influence was felt well into the seventeenth

century.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#76:1> Both in Burgos and in Granada he left numerous pupils. <fnr> ( 9)

</fnr> <footnote> ( 9)Kubler, George and Soria, Martini, <it> Art and

Architecture in Spain and Portugal and their American Dominions 1500-1800.

</it> London: Penguin Books, pp. 1-42. 1959. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-006#77:1> <h> <bold> The Church in New Seville </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-006#78:1> In this artistic atmosphere, it is not surprising that Abbot

Peter Martyr would wish that the most beautiful church should be his own in

Seville in Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#79:1> His church would rival any church in Europe.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#80:1> Construction began in 1524.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#81:1> By 1526 it was well on its way but in October of that year, Peter

Martyr died. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-006#82:1> Upon the death of Martyr, a disgruntled and troublesome official

by the name of Pedro Mazuelo disrupted the building of the church; although

Charles I gave instructions that the work should continue, complaints in 1533

that the stone church was unfinished support Sloane's observation that the

church was never completed. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-006#83:1> Judging from what both Sloane and Long have told us, several

fragments of carved work in stone such as mouldings, festoons and cherubs

that were intended for the unfinished church <quote> 'would be thought no

mean ornaments in a European church' </quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#84:1> Long mentioned the ruins of two buildings still standing in 1774: a

castle and a church.
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#85:1> Both had walls several feet thick and were cemented with a hard

substance made into lime from <quote> 'shell marble' </quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2B-006#86:1> Sloane had described the west gate of the church earlier: <quote> 'Over

the door, in the centre, is our Saviour's head with a crown of thorns between

two angels, on the right side a small, round figure of some saint, the left,

a madonna, her arm tied in three places, after the Spanish fashion.' </quote>

</p>

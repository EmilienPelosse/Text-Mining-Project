<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#1:1> <h> <bold> Threats To Family Continuity </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#2:1> The continuity of family life is based upon the transmission of

positive attitudes and values from one generation to the next.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#3:1> In Jamaica today, this process of socialization is affected by threats

which arise from various sources.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#4:1> These threats can result in change affecting not only the physical

well-being of family members, but also the psychological climate of the home.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#5:1> This has implications for the quality of life enjoyed by the family

members, the continuity and stability they are able to establish and

maintain, and the progress of communities and the nation.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#6:1> Five of these threats will be examined here: </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#7:1> 1. The shrinking economic resource base of the family. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#8:1> 2. Inefficiency of the educational system. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#9:1> 3. Adolescent parenting. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#10:1> 4. Family violence. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#11:1> 5. Inappropriate societal images/role models. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#12:1> <h> <bold> <it> The shrinking economic base of the family </it>

</bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#13:1> The economic state of the country and the high un-em <l> ployment

rates of both men and women have reduced the financial support available to

families at a time when the cost of living is rising dramatically.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#14:1> External fund <l> ing arrangements with the World Bank and the

International Monetary Fund under the Structural Adjustment Programme have

resulted in a large na <l> tional debt, and a substantial proportion of

Jamaica's exports of goods and services is used to repay this debt.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#15:1> In 1989, this proportion was 26.4% compared with 2.8% in 1970.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#16:1> The resulting cutbacks in public expenditure have adversely affected

the availability of social services for the poor.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#17:1> Derrick Boyd reporting for the United Nations Children's Fund (

UNICEF) in 1988 indicated a reduction of 44% in real expenditure on

education, health and social security between 1981-82 and 1985-86.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#18:1> Other areas of cutback include transportation, commu <l> nication,

roads, agriculture and industrial services.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#19:1> Many government employees, most of them responsible for households,

have been laid off their jobs.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#20:1> The infla <l> tion resulting from a series of currency devaluations

over the past fifteen years has increased the cost of food, fuel, and public

utilities as well as a host of other items.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#21:1> Food prices have also risen significantly as a result of the removal

of subsidies. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#22:1> The high rate of unemployment among men, partic <l> ularly in

urban areas, means that, even when willing, they are unable to provide

financial support for their families.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#23:1> In 1990, the Family Court, which is composed of judicial and social

sections providing family support services to the public, heard 2,690 cases

where fathers were being sued for the maintenance of their children.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#24:1> The decline in male earning capacity makes it difficult for women to

rely on men  whether their baby-fathers or new sexual partners  for

financial support; they have had, therefore, to be more reliant on their own

income and wages. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#25:1> Jamaican women have a high labour force participa <l> tion rate.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#26:1> Among them are many professional women for whom work involves using

skills in which an educa <l> tional investment has been made, and whose

capabili <l> ties have been developed because of ability, interest, op <l>

portunity and commitment.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#27:1> The proportion of women in this category increased from 4% in 1943 to

over 37% in 1984 [ Gordon, 1989].
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#28:1> A large percentage are in the service professions  nursing and

teaching  which are the low <l> est paid, but where the working hours allow

a measure of flexibility and permit greater involvement in child <l> rearing.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#29:1> Many professional women rely on domestic helpers to assume

responsibility for day- to-day house <l> hold chores, and child care.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#30:1> These helpers come from the large majority of Jamaican working women

who provide a source of cheap unskilled and semi-skilled labour and who work

in areas which reflect the sexual division of labour  in handicraft, garment

making, domestic service, growing and trading in food.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#31:1> A number are employed in labour-intensive industries where they do

monotonous, repetitive tasks for low wages, and where there is limited

opportunity for skill training or promotion.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#32:1> Others have become risk takers and have established themselves as

Informal Commercial Importers ( ICIs), traders in consumer goods, who travel

abroad and purchase goods for sale locally.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#33:1> Many also become street vendors. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#34:1> The work done by women in maintaining the family unit is

essential to the functioning of society and of the economy, yet has little

recognized economic value, and no legal or social status.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#35:1> Hilary Phillips noted in 1987 that a Jamaican wife can make no claim

to the division of matrimonial property if her contribution to the home was

limited to cooking, cleaning and looking after the children.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#36:1> She can only substantiate a claim if her name is on the registered

title, if she makes direct financial contribution to the purchase of the

property or if she pays some of household expenses.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#37:1> Phillips pointed out that this situation differs from that in

Barbados and Trinidad where the wife's non-financial contribution to the

maintenance of the household is recognized and quantified in the division of

matrimonial property. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#38:1> It has been estimated that 42% of all households in Jamaica are

headed by women, with the percentage in the Kingston Metropolitan Area rising

to 45%.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#39:1> These women are often the only source of financial support for their

families, but they earn lower wages than men, and are more likely to be

unemployed.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#40:1> In 1990, the unemployment/job-seeking rate for men was 13.7% while

that for women was 33.2%. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#41:1> In July 1984, the Food Aid Programme was introduced, designed to

cushion the effect of the removal of subsidies on basic food items to the

most vulnerable in the society: school children, the poor, the needy and the

elderly.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#42:1> The objectives of the Programme are: </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#43:1> - to maintain existing nutritional levels of persons at risk of

malnourishment because of changes in purchasing power. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#44:1> - to provide minimal nutrition intake levels for persons with no

or little visible income. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#45:1> - to improve the nutritional levels of school children through

the School Feeding Programme. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#46:1> Reports suggest that this programme is inadequate in meeting the

demand which exists among the target groups.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#47:1> No significant difference has been found, in participation in the

Food Stamp and School Feeding Programmes, between female-headed households

and male-headed households nor in indicators such as health, nutritional

status, and learning progress in school.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#48:1> Louat, Grosh and van der Gaag [ 1992] advise targeting rural

households for social programmes directed at alleviating conditions of the

poor, as such programmes will reach 87% of the poor; targeting female-headed

households will succeed in reaching only 50% of the poor. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#49:1> Coping strategies adopted by poor families involve the one

traditionally employed by women: use of the kin <l> ship network.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#50:1> Kinship links allow them to respond to hardship and crises in

creative ways.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#51:1> Deere <it> et al </it> [ 1990] report household exchange networks

which increase the flow of food, services and cash among relatives, friends

and neighbours.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#52:1> One example given is that of the exchange of fresh vegetables from

rural relatives for manufactured items such as flashlight batteries from

urban network members.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#53:1> Other strategies include co- operative measures such as <quote> 'partner'

</quote> savings, and increasing the size of the household by bringing in

additional income earners and persons who can assist with household chores

and child care, while those who can obtain employment do so. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#54:1> Housing is a major problem.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#55:1> In urban areas it is unavailable and unaffordable and this results in

over <l> crowding in small, badly-ventilated homes with poor sanitation which

leads to unhealthy personal habits and creates an environment in which

disease is readily spread.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#56:1> D.R.B. Grant [ 1980] reported that children of varying ages, and both

sexes, often sleep together on the same bed because there is no space for

them to have a bed each.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#57:1> There have also been reports of children sleeping in shifts. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#58:1> The Survey of Living Conditions in Jamaica reports a growth in

the number of households since 1985 of ap <l> proximately 13,000 per annum.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#59:1> This creates a strong po <l> tential demand for new housing,

particularly in urban areas.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#60:1> The effective demand is, however, low, for although the need is

there, the price-to- income ratio is high and is expected to continue rising.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#61:1> The housing available is therefore out of reach for most persons in a

wide range of income groups.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#62:1> As a result, many families are forced to live in conditions which are

inadequate and uncomfortable. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#63:1> The overcrowding and resultant lack of privacy create tired,

frustrated, anxious adults and children, who also have to endure the

indignities and discomfort of an inadequate transportation system.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#64:1> In a <it> Gleaner </it> article, Eleanor Wint condemns the conditions

under which transportation is made available to the public, and the hazards

which attend its use, particularly for children.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#65:1> She notes: </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#66:1> <quote> Many of us have witnessed small girls being handed

through a bus window to unknown male arms inside a crowded bus where she is

squeezed into any available space.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#67:1> Or where bus conductors curse children to get up and give their seat

to an adult and go and be squeezed into some space with a bottom in their

face. </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#68:1> [ <quote> 'Urban Bus System Hazards' </quote> . <it> Daily

Gleaner </it> 18.11.1992] </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#69:1> Such everyday experiences adversely affect the social, emotional

and psychological well being of individuals, and deny family members the

feeling of security and refuge which are so necessary for healthy interaction

and relationships.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#70:1> They also result in large tenement yards and squatter communities.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#71:1> In some situations, these function like an extended family but in

others the conditions encourage truancy and delinquency among children and

gradually lead to criminal activity. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#72:1> Most families struggle to provide basic needs for their members

but there are affluent homes where the family members enjoy luxurious life

styles.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#73:1> Problems can arise there too when the children suffer from

over-indulgence and over-gratification.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#74:1> Many feel that their <w> parents' </w> wealth will constantly be

there to support them, and so they lack motivation and see no need to exert

themselves unduly in school or to prepare themselves for the world of work.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#75:1> In several instances, the children are deprived of their <w> parents'

</w> time, attention and interest, but have large sums of money to spend on

clothing, shoes, restau <l> rant meals and, unfortunately, also on drugs.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#76:1> Children in this situation spend a great deal of time sampling the

entertainment provided by the satellite dish, or in shop <l> ping malls and

cinemas.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#77:1> Many suffer from boredom  by the time they are sixteen or seventeen,

they have seen and done it all!
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#78:1> The old concept of parties as family gatherings where friends get

together to share and enjoy each <w> others' </w> company, where a modest

meal is provided and where parents can meet their children's friends, has

given way to <quote> 'pay parties' </quote> for thirteen, fourteen and fif

<l> teen-year-olds.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#79:1> They pay for food, drink, and disco mu <l> sic, and the adult

<ICE-JA:W2B-011#80:1> One wonders at the values of the parents who tolerate pay parties in

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#81:1> Money assumes great importance in the lives of these young

people.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#82:1> They assess other <w> individuals' </w> worth and status on the basis

<ICE-JA:W2B-011#83:1> They often fail to develop an appreciation for, and do not

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-011#84:1> One of the survival strategies used over the years to supplement

the economic base of the family is migration.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#85:1> This occurs locally and there is considerable movement of young

people from rural areas to urban centres, generated by the need for

employment.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#86:1> International migration, particularly of men, is con <l> sidered to

be one of the major factors contributing to the large number of female-headed

households in the Caribbean.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#87:1> At the turn of the century, many men migrated to find work  in

Panama to help build the Canal, and to Cuba to harvest sugar.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#88:1> In the 1950s, whole families migrated to Britain, and since 1962,

there has been a steady stream of migrants to North America.
<ICE-JA:W2B-011#89:1> Chaney observed in 1985 that the Caribbean region as a whole sends

out a greater percentage of its population than does any other world region.

</p>

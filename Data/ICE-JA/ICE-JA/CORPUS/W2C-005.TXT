<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#1:1> <h> Disasters send reinsurers reeling </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#2:1> By Claire Clarke </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#3:1> PROPERTY insurance against natural catastrophes, such as

hurricanes, earthquakes and floods, may soon be history in countries like

Jamaica if the high frequency of worldwide natural disasters continues. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#4:1> Reinsurers, those agencies which insure insurance companies, have

been reeling under the impact of a spate of natural disasters worldwide over

the past five years. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#5:1> In 1989, Lloyds of London, one of the most prominent names

internationally in the insurance business, registered a loss of 2,06 billion

pounds sterling because of massive pay- outs. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#6:1> Joe O'Connell, a re-insurance intermediary from IOC Re-insurance

Brokers Limited in Toronto, Canada, said that in terms of losses and natural

disasters in 1988 Hurricane Gilbert, <quote> " sort of started it." </quote>

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#7:1> Gilbert, the Piper Alpha oil platform disaster in the North Sea,

earthquakes in Australia and California, winter storms in Europe, Hurricane

Hugo and Hurricane Andrew all brought to the fore the real risks faced by

re-insurers, Mr. O'Connell said. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#8:1> Nick Doak, public relations manager for Lloyds in London, told the

<it> Sunday Gleaner </it> that <quote> " the re-insurance market has

contracted in the wake of windfall losses." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#9:1> Mr. Doak said last week in London the impact of climatic changes

caused by global warming was being discussed by re-insurers.
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#10:1> Natural disasters have been happening in places which were thought to

be relatively safe, such as hurricanes in England. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#11:1> Some re-insurers have cracked under the pressure of frequent and

large claims.
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#12:1> Withdrawals started three years ago, there has been a 40 per cent

reduction in the number of re-insurers in the market compared to two years

ago, Mr. O'Connell said. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#13:1> Neville Henry, vice-president of the Jamaica Association of

General Insurance Companies ( JAGIC) said <quote> " since 1992 we ( JAGIC)

succumbed to the insistence of re- insurance companies to the existence of a

draconian excess." </quote>
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#14:1> This is one of the steps re-insurers are taking to protect

themselves. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#15:1> The <quote> " draconian excess," </quote> which is two per cent

on the sum insured, came into effect in Jamaica in January 1992 and is

brought into play in the event of a catastrophe.
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#16:1> The excess means that in the event of a catastrophe <quote> " any

loss which falls below an amount equivalent to two per cent of the sum

insured would not be borne by the insurance company," </quote> Mr. Henry

said. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#17:1> Prior to Hurricane Gilbert the excess was a flat sum of

$300. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#18:1> Other protective measures are in the form of restricting the

amount of insurance being offered, Mr. Doak said. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#19:1> Mr. Henry speculated that should the frequency of natural

disasters continue along its present trend more re-insurers will <quote> "

come out of the business of catastrophe insurance." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#20:1> This would mean that many insurance companies, especially in

countries like Jamaica, could not write insurance.
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#21:1> It may also reach the point where <quote> " earthquake insurance may

have to be shared," </quote> by the company and its client, Mr. Henry said.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#22:1> The increased number of natural disasters could also affect the

ability of people to buy insurance as premiums would become too expensive.
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#23:1> If the frequency of disasters in the Caribbean continues it may reach

the point where <quote> " not more than about 10 per cent of the population

would have insurance," </quote> Mr. O'Connell said. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#24:1> Mr. Doak, however, thinks that the re-insurance industry is just

experiencing one of its usual cycles of high claims which it has from time to

time. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#25:1> Prior to these disasters the insurance market was doing so well

that everybody was rushing in.
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#26:1> The result was that <quote> " increased competition drove down

insurance rates," </quote> Mr. O'Connell said.
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#27:1> Rates in the past 10 to 15 years have been under priced <&> sic! </&>

, he said. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#28:1> In fact, Mr. O'Connell said it is this very competition that

allowed regions like the Caribbean to be able to get full insurance coverage.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#29:1> Jamaica's recent brush with an earthquake got many people

thinking of the worth of their home.
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#30:1> So much so that Mr. Henry said on the very evening after the

earthquake people came in to find out about insurance coverage. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#31:1> An estimated

$5 million of damage to residential properties reported to the Office of

Disaster Preparedness are from persons who have no insurance coverage on

their property. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#32:1> At NEM, Mr. Henry said, since the earthquake there appears to

have been a <quote> " significant increase in applications for insurance."

</quote> </p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#35:2> By Gordon Williams </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#36:2> A CRACKDOWN on unlicenced buses in the Corporate Area could leave

thousands of commuters stranded without public transportation starting

tomorrow. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#37:2> The Transport Authority, Government's public transport monitoring

agency, plans to seize buses belonging to owners who failed to renew

operating licences and pay franchise fees by the January 31 deadline. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#38:2> Members of the newly formed National Transportation Co-operative

Society Ltd. ( NTC), who reportedly own the majority of the buses, could be

the hardest hit. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#39:2> Through late Thursday afternoon, the TA received 183 applications

for licences, which includes franchise fee payments, from operators in the

Kingston Metropolitan Region ( KMR).
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#40:2> But according to the Government agency, none of the applications came

from the NTC, which claims to have over 480 of the estimated 531 Corporate

Area bus owners as members.
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#41:2> The KMR, which is served by 835 buses providing 22,627 seats to

transport well over 500,000 commuters each day, is already hampered by a

shortage of available seats. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#42:2> Beginning tomorrow, the TA plans to remove unlicenced buses from

the road. </p>

<p> <quote>
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#43:2> " We will start seizing buses for non-payment of franchise fees as of

next week," </quote> newly appointed TA managing director Commander John

McFarlane said Thursday. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#44:2> When we asked if the NTC was aware that the deadline had passed,

he replied: </p>

<p> <quote> " We have advised the Co-operative that the grace period has

expired." </quote> </p>

$210 per seat:

$200 per seat for Portmore buses. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#46:2> But the NTC on Friday denied that all of its members had failed

to file licence applications and paid franchise fees before the deadline.

</p>

<p> <quote>
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#47:2> " We have submitted applications," </quote> said NTC general manager

Tony Smith. <quote>
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#48:2> " Applications are being processed as it relates to the renewal of

franchise fees as it applies to members." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#49:2> On Friday morning Mr. Smith promised to supply the <it> Sunday

Gleaner </it> with a list of operators who had submitted applications to the

NTC and the TA.
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#50:2> He acknowledged that NTC members were aware of the January 31

deadline. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#51:2> The <it> Sunday Gleaner </it> did not receive the information.
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#52:2> The NTC claims to have issued several reminders to members, including

a letter dated January 25, 1993, which stated: <quote> " Franchise fees are

now due" </quote> and <quote> " the deadline for payment is January 31,

1993." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#53:2> The NTC made special arrangements to accommodate tardy bus owners

by extending office hours to January 30 and 31.
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#54:2> But according to the TA, no applications came in from NTC members

through Thursday. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#55:2> In recent weeks the TA has seized over 160 buses for various

violations, including operators not issuing tickets and failure to complete

routes.
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#56:2> Most of the buses have since been returned to the operators. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#57:2> According to Mr. Smith, many of the bus owners could not afford

to pay franchise fees before the deadline.
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#58:2> However, he said the TA was entitled to carry out its duty under law.

</p>

<p> <quote>
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#59:2> " In light of the economic strain some operators find it difficult to

pay," </quote> he said. <quote>
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#60:2> " But if our members fall out of line the penalties are there."

</quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#61:2> NTC members file licence renewal application through the NTC,

with fee payments made out to the NTC and submitted to the Co-operative's

account.
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#62:2> Payment also includes a

$500 processing fee for each application.
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#63:2> The NTC processes each licence application before <}> <-> transfering

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#66:3> <h> Motorcar disappears from police station </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#67:3> By Gary Spaulding </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#68:3> THE disappearance of a white 1991 Toyota Starlet motorcar owned

by Circular Car Rentals of Ocho Rios, from the Rockfort Police Station, is

shrouded in mystery. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#69:3> The motorcar which is said to be valued in the region of

$500,000, was stolen from a client at River Sea Inn in the tourist resort

area at 3 a.m., recovered at 4 p.m., taken to the Rockfort Police Station and

has not been seen since. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#70:3> Repeated efforts by the <it> Sunday Gleaner </it> to get the

police side of the story failed as all the stations contacted have disclaimed

knowledge of the theft. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#71:3> Owner of Circular Car Rentals, Byron Williams, told the <it>

Sunday Gleaner </it> that investigations disclosed that the vehicle was

stolen from the client by a woman, abandoned on Mountain View Avenue, and

found by the police. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#72:3> As they did not have the keys for the vehicle, the police towed

the car to the Rockfort station and made contact with Williams. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#73:3> According to Mr. Williams, <quote> " I received a call from the

Elletson Road Patrol Division at about 6:30 p.m. that they had recovered the

car on December 29 and that it was at the Rockfort Police Station." </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#74:3> " About 10:30 on December 30 ( the following day) I received a

call from the Rockfort Police Station querying whether I had collected the

car." </quote>
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#75:3> On his response in the negative, Mr. Williams was told that the car

was missing from the station. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#76:3> Shocked, Mr. Williams hurried to the Rockfort Station but

stumbled into a stonewall. <quote>
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#77:3> " No one seems to know anything about how the car was missing from

the station compound," </quote> he said. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#78:3> An Acting Corporal Grant, who was said to be the station guard at

the time the car made its mysterious disappearance, is unable to shed any

light on the matter which was later reported to Detective Inspector Rowe of

the Elletson Road Police Station. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#79:3> When contacted an officer of the Rockfort Police Station told the

<it> Gleaner </it> that Acting Corporal Grant was out and openly expressed

his refusal to divulge any information on the double theft. </p>

<p> <quote>
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#80:3> " I have no knowledge of it ( the disappearance of the car)."

</quote>
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#81:3> Pressed, he added <quote> " I am unable to give you any information.
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#82:3> You have to call the Police Information Centre ( PIC)." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#83:3> The PIC disclaimed knowledge of the incident and claimed that it

was trying to get touch with the Rockfort Station to get details. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#84:3> The Stolen Car Unit has on record that a white Toyota Starlet

licenced RR 3063 and owned by Circular Car Rentals was stolen from the

Dacosta Drive in Ocho Rios, but none that it was recovered by the police and

subsequently stolen from the station. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#85:3> This is one in a series of assorted items which have been

mysteriously disappearing from police stations in recent months.
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#86:3> A report in the <it> Sunday Gleaner </it> of January 3, indicated

that four fire arms and other items mysteriously disappeared from the Newport

West Police Station in Kingston.
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#87:3> Firearms were also reported to be missing from the Elletson Road

Police Station some months ago. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-005#88:3> It was revealed at Wednesday sitting of the Public Accounts

Committee ( PAC) that last year, Government had to pay

$4.3 million of taxpayers money in damages filed against the police, which

include theft and abuse.
<ICE-JA:W2C-005#89:3> This represents a big increase over the

$1.5 million paid out in 1991. </p> </I>

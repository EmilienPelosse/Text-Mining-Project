<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#1:1> <h> Uproar at celebratory function for Garvey </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#2:1> BY DEVON YETMAN </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#3:1> An evening of powerful African history at the Cultural Training

Centre, on Monday, almost ended in a fracas when the guest speaker mentioned

that whites had come out of Africa. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#4:1> The event was a seminar on <quote> " African Continuity in the

21st Century" </quote> with particular interest in the relationship between

Garvey and the Lion of Judah.
<ICE-JA:W2C-007#5:1> The chairman was Dr. Julius Garvey, son of the late national hero and

guest speaker was Professor James Small, lecturer in the Department of Black

Studies, City College of New York. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#6:1> Panellists included Nigerian High Commissioner, Professor Emannuel

Ogochukwu; Dr. Rupert Lewis, Reader in Political Thought, University of the

West Indies; Andrea Williams, announcer from Irie FM, and Garth White,

researcher at the Afro Caribbean Institute of Jamaica. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#7:1> After many of the preliminaries and discussions about the wealth

of African Literature and descriptions of the roots of black culture, the

mood of the evening soured. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#8:1> During the question and answer session, a member of the audience,

one of a group of persons, apparently belonging to the Black Muslim group,

asked Professor Small where his locks were and his reason for cutting them.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#9:1> When Mr. Small tried to avoid the question, saying that he had

already explained this and that it could be discussed elsewhere, the young

man, who sported locks, became abusive.
<ICE-JA:W2C-007#10:1> Persons in the audience were openly upset with the young man's

attitude.
<ICE-JA:W2C-007#11:1> What threatened to become open hostility from several sides was

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#12:1> The whole discussion came to a virtual chaotic standstill,

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#14:1> In his attempts to explain, the speaker was shouted down, even as

<ICE-JA:W2C-007#15:1> As he made each point, the greater portion of the audience sided with

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#51:2> <h> Teens taking a forward LEAP downtown </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#52:2> Everyday, between 80 and 90 teenagers, some of whom just walk in

off the street, give visitors to the LEAP Centre in downtown Kingston an

insight into the great possibilities of the human spirit. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#53:2> Working under the guidance of the Centre, these teenagers are

turning out work in shoe-making and repair, ceramics, garment making and art

and craft, surpassing the quality of many commercial establishments and

creating a demand that they cannot yet fill. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#54:2> The Learning for Earning Activity Programme ( LEAP) Centre was

established by government in 1986 to provide an education that would help

correct the learning problems of 'special children' and enable them to

re-enter the normal school system.
<ICE-JA:W2C-007#55:2> It is also geared at providing vocational education to equip them

with marketable skills, as well as to give them real life experience in

handling an income. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#56:2> Since then, the Centre, located at Duke Street in downtown

Kingston, has been laying the foundation for street children, slow learners

and mentally retarded children - 126 are now enrolled - to lead useful,

productive lives. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#57:2> Co-ordinator of the Centre, Lenworth Black says LEAP has been a

success story, with the work done by the youngsters winning frequent awards

and commendations at major exhibitions. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#58:2> Among its most recent credits were the top prize in the craft

category at the Jamaica Manufacturers Association/Jamaica Exporters

Association Exposition at the National Arena and the July 18-19 Craft Fair at

Devon House. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#59:2> Sale of the items provide a source of income for the students who

are given guidance in money management and encouragement in the habit of

saving.
<ICE-JA:W2C-007#60:2> Individual bank accounts are established for students. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#61:2> The Government provides a stipend of

$37,50 a week to each student, in addition to meals, clothes, and

accommodation for the mix of residential and non-residential students. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#62:2> Noting that some of the students are orphans, the Coordinator

Veda Pascoe, is full of praise for those parents who take an active interest

in their children's progress.
<ICE-JA:W2C-007#63:2> To promote such interaction, parenting workshops and open days are

held. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#64:2> However, she is sharply critical of parents who abandoned or

neglect their children who are in the programme, but turn up to claim them as

soon as they hear that these children have bank accounts.
<ICE-JA:W2C-007#65:2> She says they then abandon the children as soon as the bank accounts

are exhausted. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#66:2> She said the centre was pushing for protective legislation to

guard against such abuse and exploitation of children. </p>
<ICE-JA:W2C-007#67:2> <h> Apprenticeship </h>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#68:2> Mrs. Pascoe says that among the most significant developments is

the Centre's organisation of an apprenticeship programme for the students and

private sector companies.
<ICE-JA:W2C-007#69:2> Such programmes, she explains, in addition to giving the students the

practical hands-on experience, helped to ease them into the working

environment before they became fully employed. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#70:2> LEAP maintains a three-month liaison with the employer while the

students are apprentices, to deal with possible problems.
<ICE-JA:W2C-007#71:2> To date, the programme has been working well, she reports. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#72:2> The next major item on the LEAP agenda, Mrs Pascoe says, is a

plan to venture into the repair of small appliances.
<ICE-JA:W2C-007#73:2> She noted that there is a large market in that area and individuals

trained in such skills are always in high demand. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#74:2> Future plans also include expanding the Centre into the rural

areas, Mrs Pascoe says, to serve those areas which were neglected in terms of

services for such children. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#75:2> This move, however, will depend on increased funding from

non-governmental sources to implement the contributions from the Friends of

LEAP and some private sector contributors, she emphasises. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#77:3> <h> Drug smugglers now targeting airports </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#78:3> THE Government's seaport security programme is so effective that

drug smugglers have now shifted their attention to the airports.
<ICE-JA:W2C-007#79:3> Last year 70% of the drugs detected were found at airports. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#80:3> The amount of drugs seized both in Jamaica and the United States

had been declining since 1986 but it went up again last year, though only 30%

was discovered at seaports. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#81:3> This was revealed yesterday by the Parliamentary Secretary in the

Ministry of Production, Mining and Commerce, Horace Dalley, as he opened a

course for port security officers at the Finance and Accounts College of

Training ( FACT). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#82:3> <quote> " Now that the smugglers have shifted their attention to

the airports, we have been putting more emphasis on security work there,

hence, one of the key objectives of this course is update you on the most

effective techniques for detecting drugs in the air freight," </quote> Mr.

Dalley told the participants. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#83:3> Giving a breakdown of the amount of drugs detected at the ports

since 1986, he disclosed that during that year 96,000 pounds of ganja was

discovered.
<ICE-JA:W2C-007#84:3> In 1987 the figure went down to 51,000 pounds and in 1988 to 38,000

pounds.
<ICE-JA:W2C-007#85:3> The amount of ganja discovered in 1989 was even less and by 1990 it

had reached a low of 13,000.
<ICE-JA:W2C-007#86:3> Last year, however, it climbed to 21,000 pounds, 70% of which was

consigned to air freight. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#87:3> <quote> " We cannot let up in our war against drug trafficking,

and we will fight it on every front," </quote> he vowed. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#88:3> He said the Government could not allow its over US$1.1 billion

trade with the United States to be threatened because of criminal elements.
<ICE-JA:W2C-007#89:3> It made very little sense for workers and managers to put out their

best, only to have the country's exports jeopardized because of narcotics

trafficking.
<ICE-JA:W2C-007#90:3> He said the only way the poor could get better medical care and

educational facilities was for the country to earn more foreign exchange.
<ICE-JA:W2C-007#91:3> This could not be done if narcotics were frequently found in export

shipments. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#92:3> Mr. Dalley said government recognised that the integrity of

Jamaica's exports was vital and thus took the decision to institute the now

over 700-strong port security officer corps, which had made a significant

impact in discouraging drug shipments. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-007#93:3> He said although the press had not given it much attention, the

port security programme was an outstanding success. </p> </I>

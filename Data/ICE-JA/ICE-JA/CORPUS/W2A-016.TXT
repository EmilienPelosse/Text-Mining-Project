<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#1:1> <h> I. INTRODUCTION </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#2:1> Natural disasters destabilize the economy.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#3:1> The more wide-scale is the impact of the disaster, the greater is the

degree of instability which the system is likely to experience.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#4:1> The disaster may be sufficiently extensive and severe, to give rise to

changes in both the composition and levels of investment, as well as the flow

and level of exports. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#5:1> The resumption of economic activities and the restoration of

macro- economic stability at the level of economic activity which is

warranted by prevailing conditions during the post disaster period, are among

the important tasks of the economic and other managers, during the post

disaster period. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#6:1> This paper examines in brief, the impact of natural disasters on

macro- economic performance and on the essential steps required for the

restoration of normalcy in the operations of the economy.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#7:1> The steps which may be taken on an ongoing basis to moderate the

economic impact of natural disasters are identified and the link between

these measures and the performance of the economy during the post disaster

period are examined. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#8:1> Some of the steps which may be taken during the pre-disaster

period to assist the post-disaster adjustment process and to return the

economy to normalcy and stability within the shortest possible time-frame are

reviewed in brief in the final segment of the document. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#9:1> <h> II. <ul> MACRO ECONOMIC STABILITY </ul> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#10:1> There exists a condition of macro-economic stability where there

is internal and external balance.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#11:1> There is internal balance where total desired consumption and

investment expenditures plus desired exports are equal to the maximum output

level which is sustainable plus the total imports of goods and services. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#12:1> Price increases may be moderate under conditions of stability

since total desirable expenditure is equal to total realizable output and

there is no excess demand in goods, services and factor markets. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#13:1> For those economies which can meet only a small part of their

requirements of investment goods, there is a state of internal balance and

stability where realizable output plus the external current account deficit

for which there is adequate funding, is equal to total desirable expenditure

( consumption plus investment). Many developing countries find themselves in

this position. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#14:1> The economy enjoys a state of external balance where over an

extended period, the total demand for foreign exchange is equal to the total

supply of foreign exchange.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#15:1> For those economies which are not in the fortunate position of being

holders of embarrassingly huge international reserves, the definition of

external balance envisages the build-up of international reserves, in as much

as the supply of foreign exchange exceeds the demand for foreign exchange

during the period of reserve accumulation.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#16:1> The build-up of international reserves on the basis of capital

inflows ( public and private) in the face of current account deficits, is

least painful and most beneficial where long-term private capital inflows

constitute the major component of total capital inflows. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#17:1> The achievement and maintenance of internal and external balance

at the highest sustainable levels of economic activity or with high and

sustainable rates of growth of gross domestic product and high employment

levels, is currently the focus of most economic stabilization programmes.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#18:1> Sustainable output growth rates accompanied by stable prices and

improvements in the economy's international reserves are currently the main

features of most stabilization programmes. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#19:1> Natural disasters do give rise to internal instability which is

manifested in extraordinary rates of inflation.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#20:1> More often than not, they create imbalances between desirable

expenditure and realizable output which, in the very short-term, cannot be

bridged by inducing steep increases in imports even where the availability of

financial resources is not the principal inhibiting factor.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#21:1> It may be a situation in which the physical movement of goods and

services cannot be undertaken before shortages within the system manifest

themselves in upward movements in prices. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#22:1> Natural disasters such as hurricanes, which effect severe damage

to agriculture and disrupt domestic food supplies, do impact strongly and

speedily on the general price level.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#23:1> Cost inflation which may have been a minor concern prior to the

disaster, may turn out to be a major contributor to domestic inflation after

the disaster.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#24:1> With higher levels of demand for labour as restoration works are

mounted, labour rates may be paid at levels which are far in excess of

established market rates, as individuals and firms hasten to rebuild and

re-establish dwellings, office buildings, factory buildings, and other

business establishments.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#25:1> Furthermore, with business operations made more difficult by damages

to plant, machinery and general infra-structure, productivity levels do fall

below the norm for at least the initial phase of the restoration period. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#26:1> The Jamaican experience of 1988 is worth noting.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#27:1> Just prior to the 1988 hurricane ( Hurricane Gilbert), the year to

date inflation rate as measured by the Consumer Price Index was 3.05 per

cent.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#28:1> This inflation rate compared very favourably with the inflation

target of 7 per cent which was programmed for that year.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#29:1> There was an acceleration of inflation during the immediate

post-hurricane months.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#30:1> The year to date inflation rate increased from the 3.05 per cent just

prior to the hurricane, to 8.3 per cent in November 1988. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#31:1> The upward movements in the inflation rate were attributable to:

the dislocation in the domestic production and marketing of foods and the

inadequacy of supply of imported food products.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#32:1> High levels of rebuilding activities during the post- hurricane

period accompanied by supply shortfalls in respect of building materials,

gave rise to steep increases in the prices of materials.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#33:1> There were shortages of skilled labour, which was acute in the case

of certain skills.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#34:1> These factors combined, produced the acceleration in inflation

discussed above. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#35:1> The pace of inflation slowed considerably before the end of the

1988/89 fiscal year as food and other product supplies were normalized.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#36:1> Domestic inflation which at November 1988, had been projected at some

14 per cent for the fiscal year, reached 9.1 per cent for the fiscal year,

2.1 percentage points above the programmed rate of 7.0 per cent. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#37:1> <h> II.1 <ul> Restoration of Stability </ul> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#38:1> The country's capacity to re-establish internal and external

balance and stability within the shortest period, is dependent upon the size

of its international reserves, the country's capacity to organize external

financial aid flows, and the speed with which normal flows can be

re-established within the goods and services markets. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#39:1> The Jamaican experience of 1988 and the immediate post-1988

period, strongly support the position set out in the foregoing paragraphs.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#40:1> If the full impact of Hurricane Gilbert which struck in September

1988, were reflected in the country's balance of trade deficit in the 1988/89

fiscal year, the trade balance for that fiscal year would have increased by

some 119.5 per cent.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#41:1> This compares very unfavourably with a pre-hurricane projection of an

increase in the trade deficit of some 7.0 per cent.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#42:1> The actual increase in the deficit that year turned out to be 48.4

per cent.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#43:1> With the surge in imports of building materials, machinery and food,

coupled with a marked decline in the value of exports of agro-based products

and a wide range of other products, the dramatic increase in the size of the

trade deficit was unavoidable. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#44:1> Despite this 48.4 per cent or US$243.1 million increase in the

country's trade deficit during 1988/89, the external current account position

improved dramatically.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#45:1> Re- insurance inflows linked to the disaster, amounting to some

US$413.0 million and incremental flows of grant funds from official sources

of some US$104.0 million, were the principal contributors to an external

current account out-turn of minus US$38.3 million.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#46:1> In the absence of these huge flows, the full impact of the

deterioration in the balance of trade would have been reflected in an

external current account deficit of US$253.0 million. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#47:1> If the country's stock of free international reserves at that

point in time were in excess of US$1.0 billion, an external current account

deficit increase of US$0.5 billion which is implied by a trade deficit of

US$1.1 billion and a fall-off of some US$104.0 million in tourism earnings,

could have been financed in that fiscal year without significant external

assistance, by pulling down the stock of international reserves. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#48:1> As a draw-down of international reserves of this magnitude would

have been unsustainable, the balance of payments position for that year would

have been as reflective of external instability as a major build-up of

external payments arrears, which would have come about if the stock of

reserves did not permit the magnitude of drawn-down warranted by the size of

the external current account deficit set out above. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#49:1> It is clear, therefore, where the country's stock of

international reserves is relatively small, the level of external financial

inflows, the capability to secure commitments for such inflows and to

mobilize them, are extremely important determinants of the speed with which

macro-economic stability may be restored and maintained during the

post-disaster period. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#50:1> This fact is illustrated by Jamaica's experience during the

immediate post Hurricane Gilbert period when, on account of added foreign

exchange inflows of over US$500 million, a 48.4 per cent increase in the

external trade balance did not give rise to a deterioration in the external

current account position of the country.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#51:1> There was, instead, - a significant improvement in the external

current account position. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#52:1> Conditions of macro economic instability may not be removed from

the system during the early phase of the post disaster period.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#53:1> In the absence of careful and effective management of the economy,

conditions of macro economic instability may persist for several years.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#54:1> The authorities must ensure, therefore, that the post disaster

restoration programme takes fully into account, the entire scope of the

restoration programme, the full foreign exchange and other cost of the

programme and most importantly, the time-frame over which work will be

undertaken. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#55:1> The policies which underpin the programme must be formulated on

the basis of reasonably good judgement's about lags in the direct and

indirect impact of the programme.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#56:1> Such issues as the multiplier impact of the expenditure programme,

direct and indirect foreign exchange demand as well as imbalances which may

arise in the flows of foreign exchange are critical. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#57:1> The need for the foregoing approaches is illustrated by the

experience of the Jamaican economy during the 1989/90 and 1990/91 fiscal

years.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#58:1> The economy moved into a period of acute instability in 1989/90.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#59:1> This unstable condition intensified during the 1990/91 and 1991/92

fiscal years.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#60:1> The external current account deficit deteriorated to minus US$297.6

million in 1989/90, US$262.1 billion in 1990/91 and US$111.9 million in

1991/92.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#61:1> There was marginal growth in exports during the period, yet

merchandise imports rose from US$1.6 billion in 1988/89 to US$1.9 billion in

1989/90 and 1990/91.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#62:1> The net international reserves position of the country deteriorated

by some US$182.9 million during the 1988/89 and 1989/90 fiscal years. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#63:1> Inflation, as measured by the Consumer Price Index increased at

annual average rates of 16.3 per cent in 1989/90, 24.8 per cent in 1990/91

and 68.6 per cent in 1991/92. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#64:1> The inflation rate represented by the change in the GDP deflator,

rose to 15.9 per cent in 1989/90, 24.2 per cent in 1990/91, 60.0 per cent in

1991/92 and some 50.0 per cent in 1992/93. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#65:1> Output growth rate rose to 3.7 per cent and 4.2 per cent in

1989/90 and 1990/91 respectively, then fell to 1.2 per cent in 1991/92 and

0.8 per cent in 1992/93. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#66:1> <h> II.2 <ul> Specific Actions for the Restoration or

Establishment of Stability </ul> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#67:1> The restoration of internal and external stability during the

post disaster period, or the attainment of macro-economic stability, requires

appropriate assessments during the very early phase of the post disaster

period in order to build a sound foundation for the establishment of

appropriate monetary and fiscal parameters accompanied by an appropriate role

for exchange rate policy. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-016#68:1> The best possible assessment must be made of the level of

spending which is financeable during the first year of the post disaster

period, taking into account the phasing of such expenditures ( public sector

private sector).
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#69:1> A careful assessment of the growth rate of output, as well as the

likely inflationary impact of the restoration programme is required.
<ICE-JA:W2A-016#70:1> The assessments of output growth, inflation and other such

macro-economic outcomes are critical inputs in the adjustments of monetary

and fiscal policies, which must be undertaken during the period. </p> </I>

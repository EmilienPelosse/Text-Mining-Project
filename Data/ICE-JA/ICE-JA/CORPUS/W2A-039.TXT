<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#6:1> <h> <bold> ABSTRACT </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#7:1> Polluted water issuing from Kingston Harbour results in a offshore

pollution gradient.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#8:1> This study sought to determine whether coral tissues were enriched in

the heavy isotope of nitrogen ( <sp> 15 </sp> N) according to their position

on this gradient.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#9:1> Four coral communities, characterised by a progressive increase in

water transparency and a decrease in abundance of free-living algae, were

selected.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#10:1> <it> Montastr &aeligature; a annularis </it> samples from each of the

four sites had significantly different mean levels of <sp> 15 </sp> N in

their tissues.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#11:1> Inshore, closer to Kingston Harbour they are enriched in <sp> 15

</sp> N , but progressively less so offshore, yielding an apparent index of

their exposure to terrestrial sources of nitrogen.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#12:1> This could be a valuable tool for analysis of patterns of pollution

in shallow tropical coastal waters. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#13:1> <h> <bold> INTRODUCTION </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#14:1> Polluted water issuing from Kingston Harbour, Jamaica, and

neighbouring sources results in an offshore pollution gradient ( Goodbody

1989).
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#15:1> Elevated levels of nitrogen and phoshorus were detected to the south

and west of the Harbour enterance, carried there by the estuarine outflow and

the westward flowing Caribbean Current.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#16:1> The nutrients have resulted in blooms of plankton and of benthic

algae along the Hellshire coast, where coral reefs are severely degraded.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#17:1> Degradation is less in the Port Royal Cays to the south and east of

the Harbour ( Head and Hendry 1985).
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#18:1> Living corals are found along this gradient of pollution. </p> <O>

figure </O> <O> caption </O>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#19:1> One feature of nitrogen isotope dynamics in both marine and

terrestrial food webs is a stepwise enrichment of &delta; <sp> 15 </sp> N of

about 3.5 &per-thousand; at higher trophic levels within a food web ( DeNiro

and Epstein 1981, Minegawa and Wada 1984).
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#20:1> This is due to excretion of nitrogenous wastes that are isotopically

depleted relative to the diet.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#21:1> In the open ocean, both particulate and dissolved nitrogen show

approximately zero enrichment over atmospheric values. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#22:1> Corals are mixotrophs, capable of obtaining their food from a

variety of dissolved and particulate sources, both marine and terrestrial.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#23:1> They obtain their nitrogen by capture of suspended living and dead

particles, and through direct uptake of dissolved organic and inorganic

compounds.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#24:1> Some of the exogenous nitrogen is then metabolised and translocated

to the zooxanthellae: the nitrogen cycle in corals, as monitored by <sp> 15

</sp> N tracers, is described more completely in Heikoop et al. ( in review

b). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#25:1> If <sp> 15 </sp> N levels in coral tissue reflect that in their

sources of nitrogen, coral have the potential ability to trace isotopically

enriched nitrogen near sources of terrestrial input.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#26:1> However, caution must be used in interpreting <sp> 15 </sp> N values,

because <sp> 15 </sp> N values are dependant on light as well as on exogenous

<sp> 15 </sp> N levels ( Muscatine and Kaplan 1994, Heikoop et al. in review

a). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#27:1> Studies by Risk et al. ( 1989, 1994) have indicated the potential

of <sp> 15 </sp> N in coral tissue to trace terrestrial sources of nitrogen,

while recent unpublished studies from Indonesia ( Dunn 1995, Jompa 1996,

Limmon 1996) have shown trends in <sp> 15 </sp> N in coral tissue with

pollution gradients. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#28:1> This paper seeks to determine if coral tissues are enriched in

<sp> 15 </sp> N according to their position along a known pollution gradient

in Jamaica. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#29:1> <h> <bold> METHODS </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#30:1> <h> <bold> Site </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#31:1> Four coral communities were selected along the pollution gradient

( Figure 1), characterised by a progressive increase in water transparency

and a decrease in abundance of free-living algae between the sites.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#32:1> They were, in order of most to least exposure to effluent from

Kingston Harbour: </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#33:1> Harbour Shoal, at the mouth of Kingston Harbour; Drunkenmans Cay,

south and down current of Kingston Harbour; South East Cay, east and

upcurrent of Kingston Harbour; and Middle Cay, about 50 miles offshore on the

Pedro Bank. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#34:1> <h> <bold> Nitrogen </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#35:1> Coral samples were collected between January and April 1993.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#36:1> At each site, at a depth of 5-8 metres, six colonies of morphotype 2

( Knowlton et al. 1992) of the reef building coral <it> Montastr &aeligature;

a annularis </it> ( = <it> Montastr &aeligature; a faveolata </it> , Weil &

Knowlton 1994) were sampled.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#37:1> All samples were chiselled from the base of the colony and were

approximately 20cm <sp> 2 </sp> .
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#38:1> The samples were frozen before transportation to Canada for analysis.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#39:1> The samples were prepared for <sp> 15 </sp> N determinations

according to Le Blanc et al. ( 1989).
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#40:1> First, the aragonite skeletons were dissolved in 10% hydrochloric

acid.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#41:1> Following dissolution of the skeleton, endolithic algae were cleared

from beneath the coral tissue.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#42:1> The coral tissue was then rinsed in distilled water, centrifuged and

freeze dried.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#43:1> For each sample, approximately 15mg of the freeze dried tissue was

combusted with an excess of cupric oxide at 550 &degree; C for two hours in

vacuum sealed pyrex tubes.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#44:1> The gases produced by combustion were cryogenically separated to

yield pure N <sb> 2 <sb> .
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#45:1> Nitrogen isotope ratios were determined in a VG SIRA mass

spectrometer.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#46:1> Organic standards ( gelatin, valine and glutamic acid) as well as

atmospheric standards for nitrogen were analysed to estimate precision of the

sample nitrogen isotope ratios. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#47:1> <sp> 15 </sp> N results are reported in standard delta notation

relative to the atmospheric standard in &per-thousand; units.

<p> <O> formula </O>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#48:1> <h> <bold> Light </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#49:1> Light was measured at 3 of the 4 sites in May 1995 using Hobo

Light Intensity Miniature data loggers.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#50:1> These were deployed simultaneously at a depth of 8 metres at each

site for 4 days.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#51:1> The data loggers were calibrated against a Li-Cor integrating quantum

radiometer/photometer ( Model no. LI 188B) fitted with a sperical underwater

sensor.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#52:1> This allowed the miniature data logger readings to be converted to

micro- einsteins. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#53:1> For each site the mean maximum daily reading was calculated as an

indication of relative available light between sites. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#54:1> <h> <bold> RESULTS </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#55:1> Corals from the four sites had significantly different mean

levels of &delta; <sp> 15 </sp> N in their tissues ( Table 1, Figure 2): </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#56:1> at Harbour Shoal, 6.47 &per-thousand; &plus-minus; 0.7,

Drunkenmans Cay, 4.99 &per-thousand; &plus-minus; 0.3, South East Cay, 3.05

&per-thousand; &plus-minus; 0.6, and Middle Cay, 1.51 &per- thousand;

&plus-minus; 0.3.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#57:1> These &delta; <sp> 15 N values show a clear trend.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#58:1> Inshore, closer to Kingston Harbour, they are enriched in <sp> 15

</sp> N, but progressively less so offshore.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#59:1> The Middle Cay samples show &delta; <sp> 15 </sp> N values in the

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#60:1> The Coefficients of Variation ( CV: Standard deviation/Mean) are

<ICE-JA:W2A-039#61:1> This can be compared with the high variability of direct measurements

of water pollution.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#62:1> CV for the data on suspended particulate organic nitrogen ( Figure 1)

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#64:1> <h> <bold> DISCUSSION </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#65:1> Previous studies of <sp> 15 </sp> N variations in coral tissue (

Muscatine and Kaplan 1994, Heikoop et al. in review) have shown that coral

tissue becomes more depleted ( lighter) with decreasing light levels due to

decreased fractionation by photosynthesis.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#66:1> In this study, light levels are lower nearshore, where the tissues

are more enriched.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#67:1> The gradient in &delta; <sp> 15 </sp> N values is the inverse of that

which would be due to differences in light, and much more pronounced,

suggesting that enrichment from terrestrial sources is the dominant factor.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#68:1> Previous measures of terrestrial pollution in this area have been

based on direct water quality measurements ( Goodbody 1989) or benthic

surveys of indirect indicators such as benthic algae ( Head and Hendry 1985).
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#69:1> Water quality measurements must be collected over an extended period

due to high variability. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#70:1> In the present study, a single sampling occasion yielded an

apparent index of exposure to terrestrial sources of nitrogen.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#71:1> Analysis of Nitrogen isotopes could be a valuable tool for

determination of patterns of pollution in shallow, clear tropical coastal

waters, as the method is objective and highly cost effective, requiring

little field work. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#75:2> <h> <bold> Neutron activation analysis of cadmium in Jamaican

soils </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#76:2> <bold> C. N. Grant, G. C. Lalor, <sp> * </sp> M. K. Vutchkov </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#77:2> <it> International Centre for Environmental and Nuclear Sciences

University of the West Indies, Kingston, Jamaica </it> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#78:2> ( Received March 19, 1998) </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#79:2> A procedure for the instrumental neutron activation analysis of

Cd in soils with relatively high Cd content and possible interferences is

reported.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#80:2> Cadmium concentrations in Jamaican soils above 4 mg·kg <sp> - 1 </sp>

can now be reliably determined by multielemental instrumental activation

analysis with an accuracy &plus-minus; 10% and the reasonably high throughput

of 30 samples per day.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#81:2> Over 600 geochemical survey samples were analysed for Cd along with

some 20 other long-lived elements. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#82:2> <h> <bold> Introduction </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#83:2> Recent work <sp> 1 </sp> has shown that, compared with world

levels Jamaican soils are enriched in many heavy metals.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#84:2> The concentrations of the potentially hazardous element cadmium are

particularly interesting; these range from less than the detection limit (

0.3 mg·kg <sp> - 1 </sp> ) for the flame atomic absorption spectrophotometric

( AAS) method, used previously, to over 400 mg·kg <sp> - 1 </sp> and average

19 mg·kg <sp> - 1 </sp> .
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#85:2> These values are very high compared with the world average <sp> 2

</sp> being below 1 mg·kg <sp> - 1 </sp> and ranges from 0.005 to 0.9 mg·kg

<sp> - 1 </sp> .
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#86:2> The higher cadmium concentrations are generally found in the terra

rossa soils and are, therefore, as shown in Table 1, associated with high

concentrations of aluminium, iron and manganese.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#87:2> There are also significant concentrations of uranium in these soils.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#88:2> Because cadmium is considered to be an extremely toxic element

these findings indicate the need for a detailed study which requires the

analysis of very large numbers of samples of rocks, sediments, and other

materials.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#89:2> AAS is a standard method for the determination of cadmium but neutron

activation analysis has the great advantages of multi- elemental capability

and minimum sample preparation.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#90:2> However, its application to the routine analysis of Cd has some

limitations.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#91:2> V <smallcaps> ostokova </smallcaps> et al. <sp> 3 </sp> noted that

the multielement INAA scheme used for 35 elements is unsatisfactory for Cd,

Mg, Ti and Ni.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#92:2> W <smallcaps> u </smallcaps> and L <smallcaps> andsberger

</smallcaps> <sp> 4 </sp> used epithermal irradiation and Compton suppression

for INAA of Cd in new soil CRMs.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#93:2> S <smallcaps  <teinnes  </smallcaps> <sp> 5 </sp> used 2 days

irradiation in a thermal neutron flux of 1.5·10 <sp> 13 </sp> n·cm <sp> - 2

</sp> ·s <sp> - 1 </sp> and radiochemical separation to determine Cd levels

in soils.
<ICE-JA:W2A-039#94:2> These irradiation and counting conditions are beyond the capabilities

of the SLOWPOKE <sp> 6 </sp> and radiochemical separation for large-scale

geochemical surveys is time consuming. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-039#95:2> This paper reports an INAA procedure for cadmium in Jamaican

soils which also allows multi-elemental analysis with good accuracy and

reasonably high throughput, and is therefore suitable for geochemical

surveys. </p>

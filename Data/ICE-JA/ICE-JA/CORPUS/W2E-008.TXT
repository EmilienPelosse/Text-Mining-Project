<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-008#1:1> <h> Domestic budgeting in the region </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-008#2:1> By Lisa McGregor </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-008#3:1> MEXICO has the second largest economy in the Latin American region

with an average gross domestic product of US$3,412 per capita.
<ICE-JA:W2E-008#4:1> Nonetheless, the country does not escape the problem of rampant

poverty.
<ICE-JA:W2E-008#5:1> According to 1991 figures of the United Nations Economic Commission

for Latin America and the Caribbean, 48.8% of Mexico's 82 million inhabitants

live below the poverty level.
<ICE-JA:W2E-008#6:1> This factor has forced the Mexican government to create and implement

a social programme to alleviate the plight of the under <?> - </?> class.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-008#7:1> Social programmes as a general rule are costly to governments.
<ICE-JA:W2E-008#8:1> However, when President Carlos Salinas de Gortari took office on

December 1, 1988, he made a brazen promise to address Mexico's urgent social

needs before waiting for the national economy to recover as a whole.
<ICE-JA:W2E-008#9:1> The following day, President Salinas's first act in office was to

create the National Solidarity Programme, a do-it-yourself type anti-poverty

plan aimed at providing and/or improving basic facilities for the country's

poor addressing areas which include job creation, health, education, and

nutrition. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-008#10:1> Since President Salinas has set out to embark on a tight economic

policy upon entering office, which included balancing the budget, controlling

inflation, and exercising strict fiscal discipline, Solidarity was precluded

from being financed by printing more money or increasing public expenditure.
<ICE-JA:W2E-008#11:1> Instead, funds have been sourced from renegotiation of the foreign

debt, privatisation of public enterprises, and to a lesser extent from funds

contributed by the private sector.
<ICE-JA:W2E-008#12:1> The initial amount available for the Solidarity fund was US$820

million in 1989 and this figure has increased to US$2.2 billion in 1992.
<ICE-JA:W2E-008#13:1> In fact, between 1988 and 1992, general spending in social welfare in

Mexico has increased by 60%. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-008#14:1> Unlike our Solidarity programme here in Jamaica, its Mexican

namesake does much more than provide loans through a venture capital fund to

start small businesses.
<ICE-JA:W2E-008#15:1> As some of its main thrusts, it has sought to provide medical

services in rural areas to improve the distribution of basic foodstuffs in

low-income areas; to increase material infrastructure for education and to

improve infrastructure especially, but not exclusively, in poverty stricken

urban areas. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-008#16:1> The two latter thrusts should be of great interest to countries

like ours with scarce economic resources and much need for improvement of

infrastructure.
<ICE-JA:W2E-008#17:1> To begin, the Solidarity Programme's priorities in each community are

determined by the beneficiaries.
<ICE-JA:W2E-008#18:1> Members of communities in need of improvement must organize

themselves and apply for the funds to carry out a selected project.
<ICE-JA:W2E-008#19:1> When it is approved, they are provided with the funds to purchase the

necessary materials and a civil engineer to coordinate the project.
<ICE-JA:W2E-008#20:1> The difference lies in the fact that completion of the project is

left up to the beneficiaries. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-008#21:1> When the plan was first proposed it was met with much skepticism,

built.
<ICE-JA:W2E-008#22:1> Not to mention a number of other improvements including the creation

of recreational areas, the installation of street lighting and running water

in neighbourhoods, and the refurbishing of schools.
<ICE-JA:W2E-008#23:1> The programme is cost-effective because citizen participation

drastically cuts labour cost.
<ICE-JA:W2E-008#24:1> It is estimated that Solidarity road building projects cost 30% less

than those undertaken by government contractors, and school refurbishing

costs are cut by an average of 40%. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-008#25:1> <h> Problems </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-008#26:1> As is expected, these community projects have their share of

problems mainly because participants know little about the work on which they

are embarking.
<ICE-JA:W2E-008#27:1> Materials are often wasted in the initial stages and lessons are

learned through trial and error.
<ICE-JA:W2E-008#28:1> Conversely, however, when the project is completed much pride is

taken in the final result and more care is taken to maintain it. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-008#29:1> President <w> Salinas' </w> brainchild has attracted the

attention not only of many Latin American countries, but also China and

India, which have all sent delegations to observe and learn from the Mexican

<ICE-JA:W2E-008#30:1> In fact, Argentina has already begun implementing an anti-poverty

programme with a self-help component based on the Mexican model. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-008#31:1> Jamaica would do well to follow suit, be it by expanding on our

existing Solidarity programme or creating a new anti-poverty scheme.
<ICE-JA:W2E-008#32:1> If this were done, instead of schools which are in much need of

repair waiting to be 'adopted' by a private company, parents, teachers, and

students could organize themselves and carry out the necessary repairs after

receiving the financial assistance to purchase the inputs.
<ICE-JA:W2E-008#33:1> Sidewalks could be built in certain communities to put a stop to

children playing in the streets.
<ICE-JA:W2E-008#34:1> Much needed toilet facilities or common washing areas could be

<ICE-JA:W2E-008#35:1> The list of possible and very necessary improvements goes on and on.
<ICE-JA:W2E-008#36:1> More importantly, these projects could be carried out on an ongoing

basis, and not just a token once per year on Labour Day which is simply not

enough.
<ICE-JA:W2E-008#37:1> It is understood that the Mexican government's budget far exceeds

that of Jamaica's, but in Jamaica the project could start on a small scale

and gradually increase without putting too much of a strain on government

coffers.
<ICE-JA:W2E-008#38:1> Furthermore, in Jamaica this type of programme would more easily

attract non-governmental contributions as results of contributions would be

visible. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-008#39:1> If this type of programme were to be implemented by the <}> <->

governmet </-> <+> government </+> </}> - and it should be - it would

<ICE-JA:W2E-008#40:1> But if good public relations are carried out, once the programme <}>

<-> bagan </-> <+> began </+> </}> people would realize the benefits which

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-008#41:1> It is a known fact of human nature that persons will take better

care of that which they have had to attain, than something that has been

handed to them.
<ICE-JA:W2E-008#42:1> The recently renewed effort in Kingston toward beautifying

<ICE-JA:W2E-008#43:1> We now need to look beyond cosmetic improvements however, to those

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-008#50:2> Is the capacity to spell correctly a universal trait among girls

<ICE-JA:W2E-008#51:2> It would be interesting to know. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-008#52:2> I suspect that it may be a special Jamaican quality:
<ICE-JA:W2E-008#53:2> for our women are formidable.

<ICE-JA:W2E-008#57:2> As someone pointed out to me the other day there is only one case in

<ICE-JA:W2E-008#58:2> They carry chips on both shoulders. </p>

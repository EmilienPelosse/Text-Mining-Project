<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#1:1> <h> <bold> TO MARKET, TO MARKET </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#2:1> Mention vegetables and most Jamaicans think of the vegetables they

cook in their one pot soup.
<ICE-JA:W2D-011#3:1> Ital ( no salt) foods which the Rastafarians cook  also the

ever-growing army of vegetarians are changing the green taste of Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2D-011#4:1> Callaloo, which we use to make our pepperpot soup and team with

seafood to make our Florentine dishes, flourishes everywhere.
<ICE-JA:W2D-011#5:1> Barbers plant it between haircuts, some even switched and all they now

cut is callaloo, or greens as they prefer to call it. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#6:1> We are full of beans.
<ICE-JA:W2D-011#7:1> The world may know it as kidney beans but we call it red peas;

susumber we rename gully beans and pigeon peas we call gungo peas.
<ICE-JA:W2D-011#8:1> The fact is we use any lentil in combination with whatever is

available, but the trio of trotters, tripe and oxtail is definitely married

to broad beans. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#9:1> Although we do have unusual and interesting vegetables, we are

still in the process of learning to use them innovatively. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#10:1> <h> <bold> Rice and Peas </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#11:1> Rice and peas is usually rice and red kidney beans, Jamaica's

<quote> 'Coat of Arms' </quote> in cooking.
<ICE-JA:W2D-011#12:1> ( Dried gungo peas, or green, when in season, are also used.)
<ICE-JA:W2D-011#13:1> The proportions vary from household to household, but a 1/4 pt. of

dried peas or a 1/2 pt. of freshly picked red peas or gungo to 1lb. rice is

adequate.
<ICE-JA:W2D-011#14:1> Dried peas are put on in cold water, green peas are put on in boiling

water. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#15:1> <it> 1/4pt. dried red peas </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#16:1> 1lb. rice </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#17:1> 1/2 pt. green gungo peas </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#18:1> 2 stalks escallion </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#19:1> thyme </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#20:1> 2 tsp. salt </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#21:1> 1 tbsp. sugar </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#22:1> 1 dry coconut </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#23:1> milk from 1 dry coconut </it> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#24:1> <bold> 1 </bold> If dried peas are used, wash well before soaking

and boil in the same water until cooked enough, but <bold> not too much

</bold> water should be used as coconut milk is also to be added.
<ICE-JA:W2D-011#25:1> ( If dried peas are used soak for 2 hours, if peas are freshly picked

( green) no soaking is necessary.). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#26:1> <bold> 2 </bold> When peas are nearly cooked, add rich milk from

very dry coconut ( p.148), bringing liquid to about twice or 2 1/2 times the

quantity of rice to be used.
<ICE-JA:W2D-011#27:1> See that pea grains are not broken. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#28:1> <bold> 3 </bold> When peas are cooked, add seasoning.
<ICE-JA:W2D-011#29:1> Wash rice and add to peas.
<ICE-JA:W2D-011#30:1> When water boils again, reduce heat and cook on slow fire.
<ICE-JA:W2D-011#31:1> Rice should be tender and all the liquid absorbed.
<ICE-JA:W2D-011#32:1> ( Near the end of cooking time, if the grains are not quite cooked,

and it is not advisable to add more water  place a piece of Saran wrap

instead of foil on top of rice and cover.
<ICE-JA:W2D-011#33:1> This usually finishes the cooking satisfactorily.) </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#34:1> <bold> 4 </bold> When cooked, stir with a fork before serving.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#35:1> <bold> <it> Serves 6 </it> </bold> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#36:1> <h> <bold> Cook's Tip </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#37:1> If preferred use margarine or butter and cow's milk, powdered

skim milk or evaporated milk instead of coconut milk.
<ICE-JA:W2D-011#38:1> If desired, fry a few strips of bacon, cut into small pieces and add.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#64:1> <h> <bold> Dove Peas </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#65:1> Green gungo peas are usually harvested in December.
<ICE-JA:W2D-011#66:1> Higglers spend their time between serving customers, shelling the

gungo or pigeon peas, as they are called.
<ICE-JA:W2D-011#67:1> Jamaicans on the whole, use much of them green, in soups, stews, with

rice, and in rice and peas, so much so, that precious little survives to see

the sun and get dried.
<ICE-JA:W2D-011#68:1> Lucille Jackman serves this dish with flair, from her adopted

Barbados.
<ICE-JA:W2D-011#69:1> Once I had some repairs done to my house and Mrs. Sylvia Hendricks

arranged a party, at which she cooked the Jamaican version of Doved peas.
<ICE-JA:W2D-011#70:1> She mixed the cooked gungo peas with creole saltfish, topped by

crisp, fried corn pork and served with roasted, yellow heart breadfruit.
<ICE-JA:W2D-011#71:1> Everyone was impressed with how delicious this simple mixture could

be. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#72:1> It is difficult to state quantities because it is such a

favourite whenever cooked, but allow at least: </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#73:1> <it> 6 cups green gungo peas </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#74:1> 1 tsp. salt </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#75:1> 1 large onion, minced </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#76:1> 1 red sweet pepper, cut up </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#77:1> 1 green sweet pepper, cut up </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#78:1> 2 tbsp. butter </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#79:1> bacon cuttings and bits of ham ( optional) </it> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#80:1> <bold> 1 </bold> Boil 6 cups green gungo in enough water to

cover. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#81:1> <bold> 2 </bold> Add 1 teaspoon salt ( if you have bacon cuttings

available and bits of ham, use some in the water boiling the peas.) </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#82:1> <bold> 3 </bold> Put the remainder of the bacon and ham with some

minced onion, and red and green pepper in a frying pan and fry in butter

until cooked. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#83:1> <bold> 4 </bold> Add the cooked gungo and stir mixture in frying

pan until cooked. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#84:1> <bold> 5 </bold> Remove from heat and stir in a little butter.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#85:1> <bold> <it> Serves 6-8 </it> </bold> </p>

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#225:1> <bold> 6 </bold> Press down with the bottom of a bottle or

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#226:1> <bold> 7 </bold> Place the pan over a moderate heat.

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#228:1> <bold> 8 </bold> When the edges shrink slightly from the sides

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#229:1> <bold> 9 </bold> The process should take approximately 10

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#230:1> <bold> 10 </bold> Repeat the process until all the cassava is

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#232:1> <bold> 11 </bold> When ready to use the bammies, soak them in

<ICE-JA:W2D-011#234:1> They can be buttered before placing on grill or immediately they are

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#237:1> <h> <bold> Golden Bammy Slices </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#238:1> <it> 2 six inch bammies </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#239:1> 3 cups cold water </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#240:1> 1/2 tsp. salt </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#241:1> 1 oz. soft margarine </it> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#242:1> <bold> 1 </bold> Cut each bammy in 4 pieces. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#243:1> <bold> 2 </bold> Soak in water for 5 minutes.
<ICE-JA:W2D-011#244:1> ( Slices should be completely submerged.)
<ICE-JA:W2D-011#245:1> Check bammy, it should not be too soft, as some bammies have very

little starch to hold them together.
<ICE-JA:W2D-011#246:1> Soak longer if bammy is still hard. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#247:1> <bold> 3 </bold> Pour off water and spread with margarine. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#248:1> <bold> 4 </bold> Grill about 8 minutes on one side and 5 minutes

on the other. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#249:1> <bold> 5 </bold> Steam in a steamer until cooked. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#250:1> <bold> 6 </bold> Fry in deep fat until golden brown or fry in

fat about 1/2 inch deep. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#251:1> <bold> <it> Serves 4 </it> </bold> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#252:1> <h> <bold> Potato Cheese Casserole </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#253:1> <it> 1 cup milk </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#254:1> 3 eggs </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#255:1> 1 tsp. salt </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#256:1> 1/2 tsp. pepper ( optional) </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#257:1> 1 cup cubed cheddar cheese </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#259:1> 1 onion, quartered </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#261:1> <bold> 1 </bold> Preheat oven to 350 &degree; F. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#262:1> <bold> 2 </bold> Grease ovenproof casserole dish. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2D-011#263:1> <bold> 3 </bold> Put all ingredients in blender in order listed.

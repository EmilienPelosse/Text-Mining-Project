<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#1:1> <h> <bold> INTRODUCTION </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#2:1> It is difficult to explain the significant dimensions of the

intellectual and administrative movement in the post World War 2 society

without reflecting on the contributions of Gladstone Mills.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#3:1> <it> Grist for the Mills </it> embodies a wider universe, offering

important insights and lessons for anyone interested in the interaction of

people, movements and ideas in Jamaica and beyond. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#4:1> This book is more than an autobiography of a brilliant

administrator, distinguished scholar, and a moral exemplar.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#5:1> It is an historical essay without losing its autobiographical

momentum.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#6:1> It is a refreshing symposium of memoirs, ideas and analyses resulting

in an authoritative portrait of his time.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#7:1> He gives us fresh insights into the nature of political organization

and practice in Jamaica, and captures the fluctuating relationships and

tensions between impulses to change and to preserve a society still searching

for itself. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#8:1> <it> Grist for the Mills </it> is a study in character.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#9:1> There are sharp, shrewd and clear characterizations of family members,

friends, colleagues and public figures.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#10:1> Constructed unconsciously is a fine portrait of the author.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#11:1> He emerges as a gentleman of unruffled calm, caring much for good

government and little for partisan engagement.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#12:1> He is exceptionally intelligent, witty and a good listener.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#13:1> A man of robust integrity, he would flatter no one, great or small,

always speaking his thoughts. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#14:1> It is out of this crucible that <it> Grist for the Mills </it>

has been constructed.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#15:1> It is an immensely readable and compelling book.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#16:1> Its triumph is that it enlightens the Jamaican society about the main

concepts of the national political sociology of over sixty years. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#17:1> Skillfully integrated into this are the beliefs and ideals,

feelings and actions of a patriot who has himself helped to positively shape

that profile. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#18:1> <it> Grist for the Mills </it> is a model of what reflections

should be: factual, vivid and contemporary. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#19:1> Gladstone Mills has had two outstanding careers spanning over 50

years of public service.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#20:1> Strong scholarly orientations and a brilliant career in the Jamaica

Civil Service ( 1938-1960) facilitated easy transition to the University of

the West Indies as Director of Training in Public Administration, and shortly

thereafter, to a chair in that discipline. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#21:1> In both career streams, he offered consistently purposeful

professional leadership.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#22:1> In the civil service he managed several strategic desks at home and

overseas.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#23:1> Thus, he has been instrumental in shaping path-breaking policies and

institutions in the public domain via chairmanship of over a dozen official

Commissions/Committees.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#24:1> At the UWI he offered high quality academic and administrative

leadership, functioning as teacher, departmental head, Dean of the Faculty of

Social Sciences and respected representative of academic colleagues,

University and country. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#25:1> All his professional work within those two career streams won the

international reputation that assured him seats on the editorial advisory

boards of major disciplinary academic journals; membership in the premier

professional organizations, and the status of an expert on numerous

international missions, studying and advising on academic and civic

development matters. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#26:1> Prof. Mills is also among the most sensitive students of West

Indies cricket, chronicling its movement, and assisting its administration.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#27:1> For his enormous contributions, a grateful nation has conferred

on him several high honours, including the Order of Jamaica and the UWI has

retained his services as an Emeritus Professor. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#31:1> <h> <bold> JAMAICA: THE TURN OF THE 1920s-1930s </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#32:1> A profile of Jamaica at the turn of the 1930s reveals an island

of approximately one million people, less than half of its present

population.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#33:1> The Kingston and St. Andrew ( KSAC) conurbation was not as dominant

in population proportions as it was to become by mid-century.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#34:1> The majority of residents of that area, including the middle and

upper classes, lived below Half-Way-Tree - the upper income strata not yet

having migrated to the upper reaches of St. Andrew, nor taken to the hills.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#35:1> Cargill and Seymour Lands, bearing the names of prominent earlier

proprietor families, and Mona were extensive estates accomodating a mere few

residents.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#36:1> The less isolated <quote> 'Pens' </quote> , formerly cattle-grazing

areas, such as Jones, Rollington, Vineyard and Admiral would not be elevated

to the euphemistic status of towns until the early 1940s. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#37:1> Jamaica would suffer from the social and economic consequences of

the Great Depression which hit the great industrial countries between 1929

and 1933; and later from the impact of the emergence of Hitler and nascent

Nazism in Germany and the election of Franklin D. Roosevelt to the Presidency

of the USA. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#38:1> It was a time when agriculture and agricultural products still

formed the bedrock of the Jamaican economy.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#39:1> Sugar and bananas were the primary assets, followed by citrus and

coffee.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#40:1> The manufacturing industry was confined to the centuries-old

agro-industry of sugar processing. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#41:1> Tourism was in its infancy, centered on Port Antonio, in

association with the United Fruit Company's Boston banana trade and Montego

Bay, on the basis of the reportedly curative powers of the water of Doctor's

Cave.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#42:1> Kingston boasted a quintet of famous hotels: Constant Spring, Mona

Great House, Manor House, Myrtle Bank, South Camp Road, none alas, still in

existence.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#43:1> Ochos Rios was still a charming, quiet and unspoilt village with a

profusion of murmuring, soporific streams and less silent cataracts. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#44:1> A few miles away, even then a focus of outings for the few

natives who could afford the trip, were the picturesque Dunn's River and more

dramatic Roaring River Falls, the latter taken out of service since 1946 as a

venue for visitors and transformed into a source of hydro- electric power.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#45:1> Negril was primarily swamp land and would not become an objective of

government activity for another quarter of a century when Chief Minister and

Minister of Development Norman Manley challenged the two-year old Central

Planning Unit to initiate ideas for a framework for the development of this

far western outpost. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#46:1> Bauxite was already being mined and processed at MacKenzie in

British Guiana, and a geological survey, undertaken by J.G. Sawkins 60 years

before, had indicated the presence of large deposits of that mineral in

Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#47:1> It was not until the early 1940s when Sir Alfred D'Costa rediscovered

the cause that Jamaicans acknowledged that the red dirt in which children

played games and the red mud which encased the wheels and mudguards of cars

in the parishes of Manchester, St. Elizabeth, and St. Ann was an important

potential resource.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#48:1> No one imagined at that time that bauxite would replace sugar and

bananas as the country's leading revenue and foreign exchange earner within

two decades. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#49:1> In the depression years at the turn of the thirties the sugar

industry was in a state of crisis, suffering from a disastrous slump in world

prices.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#50:1> Demand fell short of supply; the latter stimulated by European,

including the British government's policy of providing subsidies for their

beet-based production.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#51:1> At that time 40 sugar factories were in operation compared to the 9

which are in operation today.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#52:1> Other agricultural products such as citrus and coffee also faced

adverse international market conditions.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#53:1> These factors and the attendant social conditions, rising

unemployment and atrociously low wages were setting the stage for the riots

of 1938, the emergence of Alexander Bustamante and Norman Manley, and the

Moyne Commission. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#54:1> Despite falling prices, the banana industry had come to the

rescue of the economy, especially in St. Mary and Portland, where <it> green

gold </it> formed the foundation of fortunes.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#55:1> In 1929 banana growers led by Charlie Johnson created their

cooperatively owned marketing and shipping company, the Jamaica Banana

Producers Association Ltd.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#56:1> By 1931 the company's ships, with passengers as second class <quote> 'cargo'

</quote> had begun to ply between Jamaica and London in competition with the

United Fruit Company Ltd. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#57:1> During the 1930s, banana exports were to represent almost 60% of

the total value of Jamaica's export trade.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#58:1> By 1937 more than 360,000 tons were exported, representing 87% of the

United Kingdom's total imports of bananas.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#59:1> These figures contrast dramatically and depressingly with 1993

exports of 76,770 tons or about 16% of UK imports.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#60:1> The considerable decline is a consequence of the disappearance of

shipping and markets during World War II, the impact of Panama disease on

production, and more recently, competition from Central America and the

Windward Islands. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#61:1> At the turn of the thirties social, economic and political life

was dominated by landed proprietors and managers of the sugar and banana

plantations, and merchant- businessmen of downtown Kingston whose shops

engaged in a thriving trade on the still prosperous and busy King, Harbour

and Port Royal Streets.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#62:1> Incoming ships unloaded their cargo on the <it> finger </it> piers

jutting out on the waterfront at King, East and Hanover Streets and George's

Lane.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#63:1> Even then, the cargo traffic on drays and trucks had begun to clutter

and congest the rectangular grid of narrow streets and lanes behind the

piers.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#64:1> Thirty years later, the port would be moved westward to Newport West

and the piers replaced by alongside quays.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#65:1> In those days King Street was the mecca of business activity as it

was to remain until the 1960s, dying gradually with the development of New

Kingston and the proliferation of uptown shopping plazas. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#66:1> Looking down towards the harbour from the Kingston Parish Church

and Parade, one saw a scene of shops on both sides of the street: L.A.

Henriques, of jewellery and delicate chinaware, established on the same spot

in 1918, and lower down, Nathan's at the corner of Barry Street, Hanna's, and

members of the various Issa family enterprises.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#67:1> Interspersed among the dry goods and hardware emporia, were

government buildings: the Treasury, Supreme Court and Post Office

headquarters.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#68:1> The banks too were well represented: Nova Scotia, Barclays, Canadian

Imperial and the local Government Savings Bank.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#69:1> At a time when racial and <quote> 'shade' </quote> discrimination was

at its zenith, the banks and other commercial houses, even at the lowest

clerical levels, were staffed exclusively by persons of a white or high

colour/complexion. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#70:1> The late Hon. Amy Bailey observed that when other commercial

houses began to hire black girls, they were not to be seen by customers at

the counter, but were <quote> 'hidden away' </quote> upstairs doing the

books.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#71:1> Another two decades would pass before Miss Bailey and <quote> 'Fighting

Barrister' </quote> Ethelred Erasmus Adolphus Campbell, and later, Mayor

Walker ( father of insuranceman <quote> 'Molo' </quote> ) , would break down

these barriers and open the way ultimately for the recruitment and rise to

the top of bright black natives of ability such as Douglas Folkes, Don Banks

and Delroy Lindsay in the banking world. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#72:1> At Christmas, King Street presented a spectacular sight of

brilliantly sparkling <it> starlight </it> , and of shops lit up and

decorated in a kaleidoscope of colours, competing with countless balloons

clutched by children.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#73:1> The crowded street reverberated with cries of merriment from

children, sounds from horns, tin whistles and the chirping of toy birds.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#74:1> The air was disturbed intermittently by the rifle-like <it> rat-a-tat

</it> of the inevitable firecrackers, and the occasional thunderbolt. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#75:1> Throughout the years, in parts of Kingston <it> Bag-an-pan </it>

would roam the streets and higglers, not yet dignified with the euphemistic

designation <quote> 'Informal Commercial Importers' </quote> ( ICIs), walked

up and down from early morning, warbling their waves: <quote> 'Buy yuh

coconut oil! Fresh Feesh! Grater cake!" </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#76:1> Commercial air travel did not reach Jamaica until December 1930

with PANAM sea-planes conveying their cargo of passengers and mail.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#77:1> The few who could afford to go overseas to the USA and the UK on

holiday, students and businessmen, and those migrants in search of work in

the USA, Cuba and Central America, travelled by steamer or cargo boat,

sharing their voyage with export bananas. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#78:1> There were no airports.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#79:1> The Palisadoes peninsula was a deserted swamp, known olny for the

pirates of Port Royal and for Lewis Galdy's miraculous survival during the

1692 earthquake and tidal wave. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-001#80:1> Outside of Kingston, the ports such as Port Antonio, Lucea and

Montego Bay were actively involved in transporting incoming and outgoing

freight, while the 90-year old railway was still managed as a government

department and not yet converted into a statutory corporation.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#81:1> Its steam engines transported passengers and freight through the

pock-marked Cockpits and lush hinterland to the west and northeast coasts and

to inland Frankfield.
<ICE-JA:W2B-001#82:1> The new dimensions of dominance by overland lorries and bustling

buses remained a distant dream of a rare few. </p> </I>

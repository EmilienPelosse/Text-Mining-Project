<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-011#1:1> <h> <bold> Middle Class Domination, External Dependency, and

Internal Clientelism, 1962-1972 </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-011#2:1> This chapter analyzes the linkages between the interests of inter

<l> national actors and the noncapitalist state-controlling middle class.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#3:1> The Colonial Development and Welfare Act of 1940 legitimized both

statist expansion for welfare purposes and lower-class mobilization into

labor and party organizations.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#4:1> State expansion gave the middle class tremendous access to the

resources needed to finance its clientelistic structure of power maintenance

( see Chapter 2).
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#5:1> That structure has been largely maintained by extracting resources

from international capitalist actors.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#6:1> The latter includes international business, international

organizations, international financial agencies, foreign governments, and

international sectors within the local economy. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-011#7:1> The terms and conditions of the relationship between these inter

<l> national actors and the middle class were determined by the latter's

willingness to maintain the existing structures of international depen <l>

dency.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#8:1> Both JLP and PNP governments of the 1950- 1972 period gave foreign

capital the most favourable economic conditions possible to pursue its

interests ( see Chapter 2).
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#9:1> Additionally, international actors were protected from the

sociopolitical crises of potential private-sector mobilization and

lower-class rebellions.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#10:1> The state mediated class conflicts in the domestic economy to ensure

continued middle-class access to external resources.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#11:1> External resources were used to constrict and restrict the power of

the domestic capitalist class and simultaneously demobilized the lower class

through clientelism, intimidation, and violence.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#12:1> The interests of the middle class, the domestic capitalist class, and

inter <l> national capitalist actors converged in the 1962-1972 postinde <l>

pendence Jamaican political economy.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#13:1> As all these interests were mediated by the state, it became very

powerful relative to other domestic actors and had room to maneuver in its

relationship with international actors. <fnr> <sp> 1 </sp> </fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#14:1> For the past two decades, debate among radical neo-Marxists has

focused on the <quote> relative autonomy </quote> of the postcolonial

state.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#15:1> Some argue that the postcolonial state cannot be independent of

foreign interests and cannot therefore be used in the interests of the nation

involved.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#16:1> Others argue that the state is <quote> relatively autonomous

</quote> with some power to set limits to the power of foreign capital and

bargain with it.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#17:1> The first coherent advocate of this position was Hamza Alavi ( 1972).
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#18:1> Dozens of articles have been written on the subject.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#19:1> For example, see Leys ( 1976), Von Freyhold ( 1977), Shivji ( 1976),

and Stone ( 1977a). </footnote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-011#20:1> <h> THE STATE AND TRANSNATIONALS: </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-011#21:1> THE BAUXITE INDUSTRY </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-011#22:1> Foreign ownership of business in the postindependence period in

developing nations has become an issue of growing sensitivity.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#23:1> In many instances, foreign companies were allowed to purchase

controlling interests in key industries without giving domestic purchasers

some type of preference.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#24:1> Insofar as acquisitions were concerned, the local private sector was

at an economic disadvantage vis-&agrave;-vis their foreign competitors in the

area of tax treatment, technology, skilled personnel, and capital resources.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#25:1> The dominant view within Jamaica was that foreign direct investment

was desirable.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#26:1> Foreign control, on the scale on which it took place during the

1950-1967 period, was inimical to the national interest as it involved

selling permanent assets and created a foreign claim on the Jamaican economy.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#27:1> Backed by the emerging capitalist classes, the political leaders made

a decision to allow unlimited foreign control of strategic domestic

industries.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#28:1> This decision appeared to have been based on Jamaica's overdependence

on foreign capital and the political <w> leaders' </w> fear of frightening

that capital away. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-011#29:1> Perhaps the most significant economic event in the postwar period

was the arrival of North American-based TNCs in the bauxite, tourist, and

manufacturing industries.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#30:1> Demand for aluminium had been stimulated by the Korean War and the

space exploration program.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#31:1> Companies participating included Aluminium Company of America (

ALCOA), Aluminium Company of Canada ( ALCAN), Kaiser, and Reynolds.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#32:1> Massive inflows of capital also led to the establishment of

branch-plant manufacturing.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#33:1> Many TNCs set up manufacturing subsidiaries in Jamaica, often in

joint ventures with the state or the local private sector.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#34:1> Familiar names such as Firestone, Exxon, Colgate Palmolive, and Del

Monte set up alongside existing British companies, such as Bata Shoes, which

chose to remain.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#35:1> Sheraton, Hilton, among others, scrambled control of the hotel

industry.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#36:1> First National City Bank, First Chicago, and Citizen's Bank arrived

in the same period to join Barclays, Bank of Nova Scotia, and Royal Bank of

Canada  British and Canadian banks that had been around for a long time.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-011#37:1> Transnational corporate capital was invited into the Caribbean

because the needs of the state-controllers and the foreign investors

coincided.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#38:1> The former was committed to capitalist accumulation and the latter

was interested in accumulation in the form of access to the resources of the

state.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#39:1> The middle class's desire to expand the state sector and the TNC's

interests in gaining cheap labor and making huge profits coincided to create

the basis for alliance. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-011#40:1> Underlying these seemingly apparent common interests are two

contradictory interests.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#41:1> First, the TNC bourgeoisie wanted to bring its technology, skills,

and expertise to Jamaica in exchange for cheap labor and an increase in its

global profits.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#42:1> Second, the Jamaican middle-class rulers desired state resources to

satisfy developmental functions and to employ state control of the economy to

maintain dominance over other social classes.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#43:1> On the surface, these two goals do not appear contradictory.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#44:1> The contradiction lies in the structure of the exchange relationship

between the TNCs and the political directorate whereby TNCs not only

expropriate surpluses created in developing nations, but also eliminate

potential bases for local capital accumulation through the international

political and economic structures they monopolize and control ( Girvan 1978).
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#45:1> The global interests of the TNCs were often in conflict with the

political interests of the state in developing nations. <fnr> <sp> 2 </sp>

</fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#46:1> For different perspectives on the bauxite TNCs in Jamaica, see Stone

( 1977a), Stephens and Stephens ( 1985), and Manley ( 1987). </footnote> To

head off conflict, the Jamaican political elite accepted the terms dictated

by the bauxite TNCs, as it feared that retaliation could lead to withdrawal

of investments. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-011#47:1> <h> <it> The Politics of Accommodation </it> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-011#48:1> Capital investments made by the four largest North American

bauxite companies  Kaiser, Reynolds, ALCAN, and ALCOA  have been the

largest single source of investment and capital flow into the postwar

economy.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#49:1> This import of capital was accompanied by the purchasing of more than

600,000 acres of Jamaican farmland to carry out bauxite mining.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#50:1> The aluminium industry quickly grew to one of the wealthiest

international industries and became the largest export industry in Jamaica,

accounting for 47 percent of its exports in 1968 ( Girvan 1971a; Jefferson

1972) and one-tenth of its GDP.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#51:1> Bauxite TNCs controlled 100 percent of Jamaican bauxite alumina

production and were the largest single source of taxes paid to the Jamaican

government. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-011#52:1> The U.S. government made a decision after World War II that

national reserves of strategic minerals should be stockpiled.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#53:1> With the decision to depend on imported bauxite, U.S. aluminium

companies turned their attention to Jamaica. The island soon became a key

link in the <w> companies' </w> strategy to internationalize their

operations, and became the major supplier of bauxite to North America. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-011#54:1> Labor was cheap and abundant in Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#55:1> In 1953, the unskilled bauxite worker earned an average of about 13

pounds a week, just slightly more than the sugar cane cutter working

full-time during the harvest ( Manley 1975:115).
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#56:1> Because there was relatively little skilled labor in Jamaica, the

companies could control the training and set wage scales for skilled workers.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#57:1> But worker militancy soon forced the companies to raise wages well

above the Jamaican average.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#58:1> The companies still had an economic advantage, however, as even with

the wage increases they were paying the bauxite workers in Canada and the

United States significantly more than their Jamaican counterparts.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#59:1> At that time there was not a single union specifically for Jamaican

bauxite workers. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-011#60:1> During the period between World War II and 1972, the aluminium

companies dominated the bargaining situation in Jamaica ( Huggins 1965).
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#61:1> The strength of the companies was fortified by the existence of many

enterprises in the extraction of bauxite and the production of alumina.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#62:1> Agreements linking Kaiser with Reynolds, Pechiney with Kaiser,

Alusuisse with Pechiney, and other such pairing characterized the structure

of the aluminium industry. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-011#63:1> From the point of view of the bauxite TNCs, Jamaica gained

technological and managerial skills, much needed capital, government

revenues, and foreign exchange.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#64:1> The problem was that the bauxite industry was capital intensive,

which meant high labor productivity and hence a high demand for skilled

personnel.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#65:1> The bauxite industry employed approximately 1 percent of the Jamaican

labor force in 1976  approximately 6,000 workers out of a total employed

labor force of 883,600 ( Department of Statistics 1976:15).
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#66:1> At the level of the highly skilled occupations, the industry imported

its own expatriate staff.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#67:1> Thus it displaced more agricultural labor than it employed because

the costs of extracting the ore was extremely low relative to the price

received for the final product. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-011#68:1> Revenues accrued to the government were a <quote> drop in the

bucket </quote> in comparison to the billions of dollars of profits returned

from the Jamaican subsidiaries.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#69:1> In 1968, the Jamaican government received 18 percent of its tax

revenues from the bauxite industry.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#70:1> In the same year, taxation was based on a price of J$7.50 per ton,

whereas the U.S. companies reported a value of US$15.00 per ton ( 1J$ =

US$1.10) to U.S. customs authorities ( <it> Jamaica Bauxite Institute Digest

</it> 1, 3 ( 1976):16).
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#71:1> The companies consistently undervalued the price of Jamaican ore,

which lowered their tax payments to the government.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#72:1> The government could have developed fiscal policies to combat this

problem, but it opted to acquiesce to TNC demands, as it feared retaliation

that would cripple an economy experiencing incipient industrialization. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-011#73:1> National policy during the period was geared toward satisfying

the claims of the TNCs while hoping to secure enough revenues for the

state-controlling elite to exercise control and maintain its dominance over

other social groups.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#74:1> The state opted for a strategy of cooperation and conciliation.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#75:1> Its acquiescence was based on its perception that any negative action

on its part might lead to a severe economic crisis because Jamaica had now

become dependent on bauxite exports. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-011#76:1> Jamaica's bauxite was important to North America only in the

short run, as alternate sources could be found ( although initially

inconvenient and expensive) in Brazil, Guinea, and Australia.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#77:1> Jamaica's ore was not as unique as the calcined ore found in Guyana,

nor was Jamaica as central to the global operations of the TNCs as was

Guyanese bauxite to ALCAN.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#78:1> The political climate within Jamaica was one focused on political

sovereignty and independence from Britain.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#79:1> Economic nationalism was not yet a salient issue in local politics.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#80:1> Furthermore, because the economy was experiencing unprecedented

growth, the middle classes were expanding and the government appeared to have

sufficient revenues for clientelist patronage to the poor.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#81:1> The government was not yet pushed to seek additional revenues to

accommodate the disenchanted and disaffected classes.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#82:1> The state had the option to pursue a policy of conciliation and

collaboration with the TNCs. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-011#83:1> In an alliance with the emerging local capitalist classes,

successive PNP and JLP governments passed legislation ( e.g., the Bauxite and

Aluminium Industries Encouragement Law), granting incentives to foreign

investors as well as to local private capital.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#84:1> Governmental power was used to stimulate industrial growth with the

formation of state agencies such as the Jamaica Industrial Development

Corporation ( JIDC), whereby foreign investors, the local private sector, and

the state were all actively involved in economic policymaking. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-011#85:1> Bauxite industry directors were placed on various advisory boards

and government committees, as the political directorate had very little

knowledge of the industry.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#86:1> The politicians relied exclusively on the information given to them

by the officials of the aluminium industry, although there were competing

information sources available to them among economists at the University of

the West Indies.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#87:1> This close relationship between the political leaders of both JLP and

PNP governments and the bauxite companies ensured the latter's dominance in

matters of bauxite policy during the 1950-1972 period. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-011#88:1> If a developing nation produces a strategic proportion of a

commodity, in whose absence from the international community crisis is sure

to ensue, then that nation is in a position to exercise leverage

vis-&agrave;-vis powerful TNCs.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#89:1> In 1971, the Guyanese Peoples National Congress was able to dictate

the terms of the bauxite <w> TNCs' </w> involvement in its economy.
<ICE-JA:W2A-011#90:1> This was successfully done because at the time Guyana produced 80

percent of the world's calcined bauxite, and it had the management and

organizational resources required to run its nationalized operations (

Hintzen 1989). </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-006#1:1> <h> Financial institutions should consider a secondary mortgage

market - official </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-006#2:1> At a recent conference of the Caribbean Association of Housing

Finance Institutions ( CASHFI) in Kingston, James Carr, vice president in

charge of housing policy research at Fannie Mae, Washington D.C., had some

incisive comments on the possibilities for the development of a secondary

mortgage market in the Caribbean. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-006#3:1> The following summarizes some of the general issues that he

suggests should be considered:- </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-006#4:1> &dot; The presence of some common basis or coordination of

economic and financial policies is a necessary pre-condition for the

development of a secondary market, especially if it is to flourish and if

financial securities are to follow more easily across national boundaries.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-006#5:1> &dot; There has been a widely divergent economic structure and

performance across the Caribbean countries.
<ICE-JA:W2C-006#6:1> Some have strong economies and others have weak and very unstable

economies.
<ICE-JA:W2C-006#7:1> This will make pricing of financial securities based on mortgages in

these countries very difficult.
<ICE-JA:W2C-006#8:1> There is also the problem of co-ordination of foreign exchange rates

to varying degrees, the extent to which these are subsidised or capitalized

into the price of financial securities and will have to be investigated

before a common secondary market can be developed. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-006#9:1> &dot; The institutional structures of the financial systems in

these countries are also different.
<ICE-JA:W2C-006#10:1> Some of the countries like Trinidad and Tobago have large banks that

dominate the banking system.
<ICE-JA:W2C-006#11:1> These banks serve as the secondary market and function in a manner

that can enhance the development of their primary market.
<ICE-JA:W2C-006#12:1> Some of the other countries have fragmented banking structures and

many may require some consolidation of such institutions to be competitive

with strong and big banks from other countries. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-006#13:1> &dot; The secondary markets function to redress the regional

imbalance in spatial allocation of mortgage credit.
<ICE-JA:W2C-006#14:1> This system works as long as there are capital-rich and capital-poor

regions.
<ICE-JA:W2C-006#15:1> In the case of Caribbean countries, most of the countries have such

similar growth profiles that it is not clear which of the countries want to

be exporters of capital as opposed to importers of capital.
<ICE-JA:W2C-006#16:1> If all, ( not most countries) want to be importers, a question is

raised as to where the capital will come from. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-006#17:1> &dot; The secondary market has also evolved to take advantage of

the scale of economies directly.
<ICE-JA:W2C-006#18:1> An integrated banking system is needed to take advantage of such

economies of scale.
<ICE-JA:W2C-006#19:1> The securities in the US are fairly complex and the advent of the

Savings and Loans network allows easy evaluation of underwriting practices

across a wide range of communities in the US.
<ICE-JA:W2C-006#20:1> This makes it relatively easy to determine the characteristics of

mortgage pools, including payoff, characteristics that were put together from

activities of the primary lenders all across the country.
<ICE-JA:W2C-006#21:1> The underwriting procedures might vary widely across Caribbean

countries, and it is not clear whether risk characteristics of mortgage loans

in these countries can be easily compared and pooled as they are in the US.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-006#22:1> &dot; The issue of credit quality is very important for

securitization of loans in the secondary market.
<ICE-JA:W2C-006#23:1> Part of standardizing credit quality is standardizing collateral

quality.
<ICE-JA:W2C-006#24:1> In the US, the FHA played a huge role to ensure that not only

underwriting rules are standardized, but also construction standards. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-006#25:1> There is a need to ensure that a system is in place to assess the

collateral quality of houses in these countries before prompting secondary

markets. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-006#26:1> &dot; One feature of the US secondary market system that is worth

looking at for possible adaptation in the Caribbean is the extent to which

geographic diversification of risk could be achieved.
<ICE-JA:W2C-006#27:1> There is also a need for some vehicle to hold mortgages in order to

pump liquidity into the system. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-006#28:1> &dot; There must be institutions either private or public willing

to accept a lot of credit risk for the secondary market to work.
<ICE-JA:W2C-006#29:1> This is necessary, within the context of a secondary market, because

one is managing risk from afar.
<ICE-JA:W2C-006#30:1> Under such circumstances, it is very difficult to figure risk

accurately.
<ICE-JA:W2C-006#31:1> In the US, the government accepted this burden with the establishment

of federally-insured mortgages.
<ICE-JA:W2C-006#32:1> The willingness of the Federal government to accept the default risk

allowed the market to develop and flourish.
<ICE-JA:W2C-006#33:1> Within the Caribbean countries, there must be an institution either

private or public that is willing to bear the default risk. </p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-006#X81:4> Data released by the Homicide Division show that dead bodies

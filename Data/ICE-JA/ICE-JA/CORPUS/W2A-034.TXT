<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-034#6:1> ABSTRACT
<ICE-JA:W2A-034#7:1> Experiments were carried out to develop the process parameters for the

production of sorghum syrup.
<ICE-JA:W2A-034#8:1> A number of parameters including pH, temperature and acid/enzyme

inversion were evaluated in order to produce a syrup with the desired market

specifications.
<ICE-JA:W2A-034#9:1> This resulted in a process flow in which the major unit operations

were enzyme inversion of sucrose at 55-60 &degree; C, hot clarification and

starch elimination at 95 &degree; C and pH 7.2. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-034#10:1> <h> <bold> INTRODUCTION </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-034#11:1> Sorghum, <it> Sorghum bicolor </it> , is a member of the grass

family and is closely related to broomcorn, sudangrass and milo.
<ICE-JA:W2A-034#12:1> It is more distantly related to sugarcane, <it> Saccharum officinarum

</it> , which like sor <l> ghum is used to produce syrup.
<ICE-JA:W2A-034#13:1> <quote> 'Sweet Sorghum' </quote> describes the varieties that are

particularly sweet and juicy for example, Wray, Sart and Dale, and these are

of particular interest in syrup production. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-034#14:1> Sweet sorghum has one of the highest photosyn <l> thetic rates

among terrestrial plants and its total sugar content parallels that of

sugarcane.
<ICE-JA:W2A-034#15:1> It is cultivated principally in South Africa and in sections of the

United States.
<ICE-JA:W2A-034#16:1> Since its introduction in the United States in 1857, it has been

grown for syrup produc <l> tion and eight south- eastern states are

responsible for 90% of the sweet sorghum produced <fnr> <sp> 1 </sp> </fnr> .

<footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-034#17:1> 1. Kipps, N. <quote> " Production of Field Crops" </quote> . 6th ed.

New York: McCraw-Hill, 1970. </footnote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-034#18:1> In recent times, sorghum syrup production in the United States

has declined and this syrup has become a specialty product.
<ICE-JA:W2A-034#19:1> An economically feasible proc <l> ess for the production of sorghum

syrup from locally grown sweet sorghum was identified and estab <l> lished.
<ICE-JA:W2A-034#20:1> One important aspect of ensuring economic feasibility was the use of

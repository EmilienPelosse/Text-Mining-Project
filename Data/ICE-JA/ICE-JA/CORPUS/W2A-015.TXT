<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-015#3:1> <h> <bold> 1. INTRODUCTION </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-015#4:1> The standard advice now offered to low-growth, developing coun <l>

tries with protracted international payments problems is that policies should

be pursued which lead to a more efficient allocation of re <l> sources

internally and that a shift into the production of exportable goods should be

encouraged.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#5:1> With financial assistance tied to the adoption of this advice, what

usually follows is a series of measures to increase public savings, constrain

<ICE-JA:W2A-015#6:1> A transparent system of prices, it is argued, supported by adequate

information and freedom of action, leads inevitably to a more efficient and

standards.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#7:1> The free market and its political coun <l> terpart, democracy, now

represent the prevailing tenet in develop <l> ment thinking. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-015#8:1> This was not always the case, even among industrialised coun <l>

tries.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#9:1> A strong role for the state in the ownership of key natural re <l>

sources and industries, in planning and in safeguarding the welfare of the

ordinary citizen was for many years the accepted norm in Europe, with the

state becoming more pervasive as one moved eastwards.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#10:1> The North American version of this thinking was manifested in an

increasing tax rate and growing public sector deficits which were used to

support subsidies on income, medical care, food and housing for the less

fortunate.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#11:1> Studies in benevolent state intervention spawned think tanks and

civil rights groups and became a strong branch of the literature during the

decades of the 1960s and 1970s when consider <l> ations of social welfare and

protection guided the invisible hand of private economic endeavour. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-015#12:1> These broad swings in international politico-economic thinking

have been reflected in the perceived role of the state in Caribbean economies

and perhaps most intensely in Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#13:1> Following politi <l> cal independence with the champions of the

<ICE-JA:W2A-015#14:1> The generous incentives to foreign investors which had begun in the

<ICE-JA:W2A-015#15:1> But these en <l> claves of industrial wealth were relatively small

and isolated and stood in stark contrast with a much lower average per capita

<ICE-JA:W2A-015#16:1> These gaps in income distribution amid the mood of liberalism and

self actualisation sweeping North America heralded a change of government in

the early 1970s and a shift in the perceived role of government in a popular

democracy. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-015#17:1> During that decade, the governing party joined the ranks of the

international socialist movement.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#18:1> The thinking of the government was guided by notions of equity,

social justice and the collective good of the community.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#19:1> This philosophy found its most important expres <l> sion in the

nationalisation of the <quote> " commanding heights" </quote> of the economy

 the land, mineral deposits, major export industries, fi <l> nancial

significantly increased the share of national income accruing to labour.

<ICE-JA:W2A-015#22:1> Economic strategy was focused inwards for self reliance in basic

<ICE-JA:W2A-015#23:1> While income distribution <quote> " improved" </quote> , output,

<ICE-JA:W2A-015#24:1> Buoyed by an overval <l> ued exchange rate, trade restrictions and

price controls, a large mar <l> ket for illegal imports emerged which, when

<ICE-JA:W2A-015#25:1> By 1980, the inherent contradictions in the economy constituted a

<ICE-JA:W2A-015#26:1> In economic terms, the leaders of this movement advocated lower

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-015#27:1> Structural Adjustment in Jamaica is the body of policies pur <l>

</}> was to be progressively reduced to its bare essen <l> tials  that of a

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-015#32:1> The review which forms the remainder of this paper is arranged as

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-015#37:1> <h> 2. THE ECONOMY PRIOR TO ADJUSTMENT </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-015#38:1> From political independence in 1962 up to 1972 Jamaica enjoyed

steady growth, with GDP expanding at a real average annual rate of 5.3%.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#39:1> Direct foreign investment in bauxite, alumina and tourism, combined

with domestic financial stability, contributed to this per <l> formance.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#40:1> But overall employment grew at a much lower rate, re <l> flecting in

part a shift away from labour-intensive agriculture to baux <l> ite/alumina

and import- substitution manufacturing.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#41:1> In 1974 the economy started a sustained contraction, with real GDP

declining by about 3% per annum and unemployment rising to almost 28% in

1980.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#42:1> This prolonged crisis was triggered by a combination of politi <l>

cal division, inappropriate sector policies and macroeconomic man <l>

agement, compounded by adverse external developments. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-015#43:1> Despite the prosperity of the late 1960s there was growing socio

<l> economic dissatisfaction and a clamour for reform.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#44:1> Income dispari <l> ties were marked.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#45:1> In all the major productive sectors  agriculture, mining,

manufacturing  as well as the utilities and financial institu <l> tions,

ownership and wealth were concentrated in very few hands, many of them

foreign.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#46:1> The thrust of Government policy in most of these areas was to acquire

land, enterprises and equity participation in most areas of the economy on

behalf of the public and to widen the availability of social services to the

general population.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#47:1> The redis <l> tributive stance of the government created unease

between the pri <l> vate sector and itself which translated in short order

into considerable flight of capital, <-> <sp> 2 </sp> </-> closing of

enterprises, and tax evasion.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#48:1> As a result, and although public investment grew as a share of total

investment, total investment declined abruptly. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-015#49:1> Overall agricultural production declined from 1972 to 1980, with

export agriculture falling sharply, while domestic food crop produc <l> tion

increased.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#50:1> The decline of export agriculture was related to pric <l> ing

policies.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#51:1> Farmgate prices for export crops were regulated by the Marketing

Boards and they lagged behind the unregulated farmgate prices for domestic

crops.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#52:1> Export crops were produced in an increas <l> ingly regulated

environment and suffered from under-investment and an incentive system which

favoured the production of domestic crops. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-015#53:1> One goal of the government was to extend Jamaican control over

the bauxite/alumina industry.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#54:1> In 1975 a government holding company was formed that took a 51%

equity position in Kaiser and Reynolds and a minority position in Alcoa and

Alcan and engaged in external bauxite/alumina trading on its own account.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#55:1> An attempt was made to emulate OPEC by promoting the establishment of

<ICE-JA:W2A-015#56:1> In April 1974 Jamaica introduced a controversial 7.5% production

levy, and government revenue from bauxite/alumina production rose seven fold.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#57:1> Imposition of the bauxite levy was followed by the start of the

decline in the Jamaican bauxite/alumina industry.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#58:1> This decline was subsequently aggravated by the inflation of energy

prices and fluc <l> tuations in world demand and sourcing of bauxite. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-015#59:1> Manufacturing production peaked in 1973; by 1980, it was 30%

lower.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#60:1> This result was partly caused by shortages of foreign exchange,

<ICE-JA:W2A-015#61:1> The small domestic market was reaching its saturation point while the

<ICE-JA:W2A-015#62:1> The import- substituting oriented industries set up in the 1950s and

1960s were ill-prepared to face world markets.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#63:1> Be <l> sides, the protective system based on quantitative

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-015#64:1> After 1973, the growth in public resources was not commensu <l>

<ICE-JA:W2A-015#65:1> Financing this defi <l> cit resulted in a crowding out of the private

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-015#67:1> There were other contributors to the balance of payments crisis.
<ICE-JA:W2A-015#68:1> The maintenance of an overvalued exchange rate, the deterioration in

<ICE-JA:W2A-015#69:1> Many properties were acquired by Government in the process.

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-015#73:1> The decline of the Jamaican economy during the 1972-80 period

<ICE-JA:W2A-015#74:1> Terms of trade movements were slightly favourable and only a part of

</p>

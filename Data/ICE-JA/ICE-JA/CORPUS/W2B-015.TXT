<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-015#9:1> In his relentless quest to understand his surroundings, man has

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-015#13:1> In considering the possibility of world peace, we find that the

<ICE-JA:W2B-015#14:1> Develop <l> ment refers to a country's ability to satisfy its

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-015#19:1> The intent of this essay is to provide a working under <l>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-015#21:1> <h> <bold> Peace and its Enemies </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-015#22:1> <bold> F </bold> or analytical purposes, there are two types of

peace: negative peace and positive peace. <fnr> 2 </fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#23:1> 2. UNESCO. <it> Thinking Head - UNESCO and the challenges of today

and tomorrow. </it> Paris, 1977. </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#24:1> Negative peace exists when there is the absence of violence or

hostilities, while positive peace prevails when there is not only an absence

of tension and violence, but also a community of interest among people,

impelling them to cooperate in an atmosphere of kinship, justice and mutual

respect.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#25:1> Peace among nations must go beyond mere political diplomacy.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#26:1> It must include social, cultural and economic relations based on

voluntary cooperation which is, in turn, founded on equity and justice.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#27:1> Understanding, friendship and mutual enrichment will surface in

positive peace so that intellectual and moral fellowship will naturally

follow.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#28:1> Military security will then be replaced by economic security, the

foremost security of all. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-015#29:1> Both war and peace start in the mind of man.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#30:1> For true peace to exist, a new psychological awareness must embrace

peace as the overriding precondition for human progress.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#31:1> Peace must replace war as the power-base for man if he is to harness

his true potential. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-015#32:1> Observations over the years have led to the identification of

certain elements which promote tension, sporadic violence and, ultimately,

war.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#33:1> Chief among these influences are the great material disparities

within and among societies.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#34:1> In both developed and underdeveloped countries, these disparities

often reach extreme proportions when the basic needs of large sections of a

population are denied as the direct result of exorbitant self-interest and

rugged individualism in socio-political systems.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#35:1> Jamaica is a prime example of these sorts of divisions.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#36:1> Because of the small size of the island and the extremes in economic

disparities, which are among the worst in the world, excessive need and

over-whelming greed rub shoulders daily. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-015#37:1> The gap between the well-being of a few and the vital needs of

the majority of mankind is steadily growing.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#38:1> Appalling conditions, distantly associated with early capitalism, now

exist in many underdeveloped countries as a consequence of misguided attempts

to cater to an absurd international economic order, based on the ideology of

a so-called free market system.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#39:1> The results are islets of wealth in a sea of poverty and despair.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#40:1> In Jamaica the results are internecine violence and murderous

assaults on the established order and those who are symbols of oppression.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-015#41:1> Starvation, disease and premature death are the products of such

backward social systems.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#42:1> Underdevelopment is not ordained; it is simply the result of an

organized system of domination and exploitation.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#43:1> Among the most dan <l> gerous consequences to the world of this

man-made poverty is that, each second, legions of children are being added to

the pool of despair, without the mental or physical strength to cope.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#44:1> Over-population looms in these circum <l> stances, outstripping all

attempts to ameliorate conditions. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-015#45:1> Against this background of ignor <l> ance, squalor and early

death, a few indulge in scandalous excesses of over-consumption and

ostentation.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#46:1> Scarce resources are squandered worldwide in maintaining military

establishments which now swallow over one thousand billion dollars a year.

<ICE-JA:W2B-015#53:1> This is surely a monstrous waste of human capacity, tending to

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-015#54:1> Our planet is now under tremendous pressure from the

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-015#85:1> <h> <bold> The Way Toward Peace </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-015#86:1> <bold> T </bold> he lack of peace in the world arises essentially

from the bankrupt striving of man to maintain dominance over others and to

create material wealth far beyond the practical needs of individuals and

societies.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#87:1> The way toward peace, then, must begin with the amelioration, and

where possible elimination, of these destructive courses.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#88:1> The impediments to peace which can be identified nationally

frequently mirror the problems at the international level.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#89:1> Strictly national problems are often the result of super <l> stition,

ignorance or insecurity. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-015#90:1> In a general sense, it has to be recognized that for peace to

become firmly entrenched, it must be actively pursued at the highest levels

of leadership.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#91:1> The mere absence of war, tension or conflict, in itself, is not

enough.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#92:1> For peace to break out, all mechanisms of violence and confron <l>

tation must be removed, and in their place, positive initiatives for peace

constructed.

must be abolished, and the sale of armaments rejected as suicidal.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#94:1> Doctrines of spheres of influence, vital interests and surrogate

states must be abandoned.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#95:1> Military security must be seen as a contradiction in terms.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#96:1> In its place economic security must be sought for each human being.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#97:1> This is not as visionary as it may seem.
<ICE-JA:W2B-015#98:1> In 1980 it was estimated that seventeen billion US dollars were

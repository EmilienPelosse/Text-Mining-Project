<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#1:1> <h> <bold> Reynolds Metals Company ( 1928-2000) </bold> </h> </p>

<O> photograph </O>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#2:1> <bold> Bauxite mining in St. Elizabeth, viewed in 1980 by David

<ICE-JA:W2B-040#3:1> He toured the Alpart plant as part of his visit for talks with the

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#4:1> Carlton Davis, Contributor </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#5:1> The announcement on May 3, 2000 that the United States Department

of Justice and the European Union had approved the acquisition of <w>

Reynolds' </w> assets by Alcoa, and the de-listing of the former from the New

York Stock Exchange marked the demise of what had hitherto been one of the

six great transnational aluminium companies for most of the period since the

Second World War, and which through two of its subsidiaries, was a

significant producer of bauxite and aluminia in Jamaica. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#6:1> As I have written in detail elsewhere, the predecessor to the

Reynolds Metals Company was the United Foil Company which Richard Reynolds

founded in December 1919 at Louisville, Kentucky.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#7:1> United Foil's principal activity was the production of lead and tin

foil for the packaging of tobacco, an industry in which Richard Reynolds's

uncle, R.T. Reynolds was established.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#8:1> The company began to produce aluminium foil in 1926, reportedly

because it was finer and of better appearance than the tin and lead foil.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#9:1> Its name was changed in 1928 to Reynolds Metals Company which embarked

upon a decade of growth.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#10:1> The headquarters which was transferred from Louisville to New York in

1936, was transferred permanently to Richmond, Virginia in 1938. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#11:1> The Second World War and, in particular, the U.S.'s role in it

used to have a major impact on, among other things, increased demand for

aluminium. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#12:1> Richard Reynolds foresaw the entry of the U. S. in the conflict

and the role which the metal would play in it, and along with others, worked

ceaselessly to ensure a greater role for Reynolds in the aluminium industry.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#13:1> The War was to stimulate great demand for aluminium for aircraft

manufacture, tanks and automotive parts, ordinance, packaging, barracks,

bridges, etc.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#14:1> The United States for example, produced 278,000 military aircraft

using some 1.5 million tonnes of aluminium between 1942 and 1945.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#15:1> By way of comparison, in 1939, the year when the war in Europe

started, production was just over 2,000 aircraft. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#16:1> <h> <bold> Beginning </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#17:1> The beginning of this mobilisation was the call by President F.D.

Roosevelt, in August 1940, for the production of some 50,000 planes per year.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#18:1> Following this call, he created the National Defence Advisory

Commission ( NDAC) the first of several control agencies established between

1940 and 1945 to direct the country's production effort.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#19:1> In respect of aluminium, it invested some US$739 million ( of course,

worth several times that amount in today's terms) in a number of facilities,

including two alumina plants ( which were subsequently to be supplied, wholly

or in part by bauxite from Jamaica); nine aluminium smelters; and a number of

fabrication facilities. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#20:1> Despite this huge investment by the U.S. Government, Alcoa, one

of the founders of the modern aluminium industry ( as the Pittsburgh

Reduction Company in 1888) by virtue of being holders of the patent for

reduction of the metal from its oxide, still dominated aluminium production

in the United States, as almost all the plants constructed by the Government

were operated by that company. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#21:1> Richard Reynolds tried to break Alcoa's dominance by seeking the

sponsorship of the NDAC to build a primary aluminium facility, but was

unsuccessful.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#22:1> However, he persisted, and through the intervention of Senator Lister

Hill of Alabama, the company obtained a loan of US$20 million in August 1940

to construct smelters at Listerhill ( named after the Senator, it was said,

as a <quote> " thank you" </quote> ) Alabama, and Longview, Washington for

US$15.8 million and US$4.2 million respectively. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#23:1> Following the War, the U.S. Government's Surplus Property Board (

authorised by the Surplus Property Act of 1944) was charged with the disposal

of Government-owned property.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#24:1> A number of influences were brought to bear in the formulation of the

Act and subsequently, the composition, operation and focus of the Board.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#25:1> The U.S. House of Representatives and Senate held somewhat divergent

views on this matter.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#26:1> The House favoured a bill that would result in a speedy disposal of

surplus property with the highest possible cash return to the Government,

while the Senate favoured a bill that would emphasise the fostering of

competition. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#27:1> The view of the Senate, ( and that of the Bonneville Power

Administration which supplied electricity to aluminium plants, other

industrial facilities, and individual homes in the Pacific Northwest) more or

less prevailed.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#28:1> The result was that, in respect of the aluminium industry, the policy

adopted by the Board required that: </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#29:1> &dot; Prospective competitors of Alcoa were to have first choice

of plant and equipment; </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#30:1> &dot; Preference was to be given to bidders with the organisation

experience and financial resources that afforded the greatest prospects for

successful survival and maximum production in the industry; </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#31:1> &dot; Alcoa was to have an opportunity of taking over certain

facilities subject to the approval of the attorney general and only under

conditions which would give it no competitive advantage. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#32:1> Reynolds ( as did Kaiser) eventually benefited from this

arrangement.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#33:1> It was able to lease or purchase the Hurricane Creek, Arkansas,

alumina plant, the Listerhill, Alabama, sheet mill, and the Troutdale,

Oregon, smelter.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#34:1> Reynolds also got the right to purchase bauxite from the U.S

Government's stockpile for the Hurricane Creek, Arkansas, alumina plant, and

the right to the use of Alcoa's patents royalty-free at the plant.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#35:1> In addition, both Kaiser and itself benefited from rental terms

varying from 4 per cent to 8 per cent of the fair value of the plant's

capacity used over a five-year lease period and the granting of lease renewal

and purchase options. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#36:1> <h> <bold> Advances </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#37:1> Despite these advances by Reynolds, an important link was

missing: access to bauxite reserves.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#38:1> Alcoa and Alcan ( which at the time were closely related), had

<quote> 'shared' </quote> the bauxite reserves in the Guianas - the then

known main source of economic reserves in the Western Hemisphere - Alcoa, in

Dutch Guiana ( later Suriname) and Alcan, in British Guiana ( later Guyana).

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#39:1> Reynolds clearly recognised the strategic importance of access of

bauxite reserves and accordingly, from as early as November 1942, through its

vice-president, Walter Rice, wrote the US Secretary of State Cordell Hull,

seeking the State Department's support by linking the access by the company

to Jamaican bauxite ( which was positively identified by 1942, and over which

Alcan especially, and Billiton of the Netherlands, had begun preliminary

investigations) to the United <w> States' </w> strategic interests.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#40:1> Rice wrote, in part: </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#41:1> <quote> 'The combination of these huge deposits ( Jamaica's) with

low-cost power and the billion pound aluminium plant in Saguenay River in

Canada would enable the British Empire to dominate the international

aluminium markets for perhaps a hundred years, and would stifle the

development of a large competitive aluminium industry in the United States...
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#42:1> The problem is much larger than our company.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#43:1> Since we know of no ore discovery in recent years, of comparable

strategic importance, we believe the freedom to mine and ship bauxite from

Jamaica to the United States is vitally important to the industrial future of

our country and our national security, and the issue cannot await the

termination of the war.' </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#44:1> This letter was followed by one to the U.S. Bureau of Mines, in

January 1943, seeking information on the bauxite in Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#45:1> The Bureau responded by giving it some information of where it

believed some deposits were located, the size of the ore bodies, and the

chemical characteristics. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#46:1> In the very same month, Reynolds wrote yet another letter, this

time to John Huggins ( later Sir John, Governor of Jamaica from 1943 to

1950), who was then with the Anglo-American Commission in Washington,

formally requesting the co-operation of the Jamaican Government in exploring,

acquiring and mining ore. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#47:1> This was followed by a visit to Jamaica, in February 1943, by Mr.

Rice and the chief geologist O.C. Schmedeman.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#48:1> They met with the Governor, Sir Arthur Richards during this visit,

and requested equal treatment with Alcan and Billiton. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#49:1> Among other things, the Reynolds team stressed the significance

of exploiting the ore for the war effort: </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#50:1> <h> <bold> Urgent </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#51:1> Since it is urgent to produce high-grade bauxite in order to

obtain maximum output of aluminium required for military aircraft, we would

like to have permission to proceed at the earliest date. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#52:1> Sir Arthur wrote the British Secretary of State for the Colonies

in March 1943 passing on <w> Reynolds' </w> requests, and supported the

company on the grounds that it was undesirable to give Alcan a concession

over the whole island.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#53:1> ( It was clear that there was never any intention to allow Billiton

to become seriously involved in the Jamaican industry, and, as it turned out,

the company soon left Jamaica for good).
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#54:1> Support was also given by U.S consular and other representatives.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#55:1> In the event, the Secretary of State for the Colonies, wired the

Governor to say that the matter was referred to the Aluminium Committee of

the Combined Raw Materials Board
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#56:1> ( this Board was set up after Japan's attack on Pearl Harbour on

December 7, 1941, by the U.S. and U.K. Govern-ments to co-ordinate their

policies in purchasing and allocating raw materials).
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#57:1> The Board, comprised Sir Richard Fairy of the British Aluminium

Company, Mr. G.C. Bateman, the Canadian Metals Controller, and Mr. C.E.

Wilson, of the US War Production Board. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#58:1> Apparently, the Board gave Reynolds the <quote> 'thumbs down'

</quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#59:1> This decision was regarded as likely by Jamaican officials, because

British Aluminium was closely allied with Alcan, and the Canadian Metals

Controller would be hard put to go against his country's aluminium flagship,

Alcan. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#60:1> Reynolds learned from U.S. Government officials in May 1943, that

tests of bauxite shipped by Alcan to Alcoa's East St. Louis, Illinois,

alumina plant, could not be processed by the technology that had been used to

process bauxite from the United States and the Guianas.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#61:1> It also learned from the State Department that the Aluminium

Committee of the Combined Raw Materials Board had rescinded its earlier

decision in regard to their application to explore and mine bauxite in

Jamaica. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#62:1> The inability to process the ore, and, in the event, the <quote> 'turning

of the tide' </quote> in favour of the Allies resulted in Jamaican bauxite

not being required for the war effort.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#63:1> So, by late 1944, all hurdles to <w> Reynolds' </w> participation in

the Jamaican industry had been removed.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#64:1> Clearance was given to begin exploration, and, in November of that

year, the Company secured options on 15,000 acres of land.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#65:1> It later purchased lands ( ultimately some 60,000 acres) and

undertook research on the economic processing of Jamaican ore. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#66:1> It moved to commercial operations on the bases of:
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#67:1> ( a) its ability to develop an appropriate processing technology;
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#68:1> ( b) the receipt of " Marshall Plan" Funds ( as did Alcan) from the

United States Economic Co-operation Administration;
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#69:1> ( c) attractive taxation regimes, both in the United States and

Jamaica;
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#70:1> ( d) the stockpiling of bauxite by the U.S. Government;
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#71:1> ( e) increase in the demand for aluminium for peace time uses as well

as a result of the War in Korea. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#72:1> The round of expansion to meet the Korean War effort as well as

the stockpile of the metal by the U.S. Government were particularly

significant in creating the need for more bauxite, alumina, and alumina

capacity.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#73:1> One outcome of this need was the construction of the Corpus Christi,

Texas alumina plant, by Reynolds to process Jamaican bauxite. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#74:1> The company established its mining facility at Lydford, St. Ann,

and a port at Ocho Rios.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#75:1> It had the distinction of being the first exporter of commercial

quantities of bauxite from Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2B-040#76:1> This happened on June 5, 1952. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#77:1> <h> <bold> Major player </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#78:1> It became a major player in Jamaica's economic life in three

distinct respects: </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#79:1> &dot; Mining and shipping bauxite for thirty-two unbroken years

largely to Corpus Christi, Texas, until the changing circumstances in the

world aluminium industry, including the worst recession in its history,

resulted in the closure of the Lydford operations in December 1984; </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-040#80:1> &dot; Through the Alpart plant with which it shared joint

ownership ( first with Kaiser and Anaconda, and later when Anaconda divested

its share, with Kaiser only) between 1969 and 1985. </p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#1:1> <h> <bold> Introduction </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#2:1> Music and dance forms have always been integral elements of a

people's culture and in Jamaica it is no different.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#3:1> Through our music and dance we find creative and artistic expressions

of our joys, sorrows, tragedies and triumphs.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#4:1> Over the years, our music and dance forms have evolved from the

traditional forms of our ancestors to their present forms. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#5:1> Our music and dance are rich, powerful and vibrant.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#6:1> We have gained international acclaim through our many artistes and

dancers such as Bob Marley, Willard White, and the National Dance Theatre

Company among others.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#7:1> These are aspects of Jamaican life of which we can be truly proud.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#8:1> <h> <bold> Music and Dance in Jamaica </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#9:1> <h> <bold> <it> Mento </it> </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#10:1> Mento is the original popular music form in Jamaica, developing

during the plantation period and holding sway up to the 1950s.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#11:1> It was born out of the fusion of African and British influences.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#12:1> Its performance mode, rhythmic impulse, as well as its call and

response type singing are African in origin, while the scale patterns,

harmonic concepts, verse and chorus song types are British. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#13:1> Mento was one of the vehicles by which aspects of life considered

taboo, were addressed.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#14:1> Often done with a touch of humour, the words of the accompanying

songs addressed our social and economic struggles.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#15:1> A mento band could be found in most villages throughout the country.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#16:1> Mento is regarded in some circles as the Jamaican equivalent to

calypso.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#17:1> While some of the songs were aired regularly, others were banned as

they were thought to be too sexually explicit. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#18:1> Mento was first recorded by artistes such as Lord Flea and Lord

Fly and later by Harold Richardson and <quote> 'Sugar Belly' </quote> Walker.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#19:1> Popular mento recordings include <it> <quote> 'Run Mongoose', 'Rukumbine'

</quote> </it> and <it> <quote> 'Peel Head John Crow.' </quote> </it> <O>

photograph </O> <O> caption </O>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#20:1> Today, Mento is played mainly by bands in North Coast hotels and

as accompaniment for dances such as the traditional Quadrille. </p>

<p> <h> <bold> <it> Quadrille </it> </bold> </h>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#22:1> Quadrille originated from the popular dances of the French and

English in the 18th and 19th centuries.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#23:1> It highlighted the elegance and mannerisms of the elite of these

societies.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#24:1> It is however believed that Quadrille began in France, as it was the

leading country in cultural development, especially in the dances of that

period. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#25:1> Quadrille is performed in four distinct movements, called

figures.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#26:1> A fifth or <quote> " brawta" </quote> figure was later added to the

dance - the Mento. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#27:1> The Square and Long Way Set formation of Quadrille was introduced

in the early 19th century.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#28:1> However as the dance developed the names were changed to Ballroom

Quadrille and Campstyle. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#29:1> The Long Way Set formation ( Campstyle) was performed mostly in

the rural areas.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#30:1> In this dance, four couples stand in two lines facing each other, all

of whom move at the same time. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#31:1> The Square Quadrille ( Ballroom) was performed mostly in the

towns where the elite resided.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#32:1> They tried to maintain an atmosphere of elegance and grace as their

counterparts in their home country.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#33:1> In the Ballroom, four couples stand in the cardinal position where

the head and tail couples move first followed by the side couples. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#34:1> Both the Campstyle and Ballroom Quadrille dance we know today

include African elements, but this is more evident in the Campstyle version.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#35:1> There are five African features in Campstyle Quadrille.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#36:1> These are: the spectacular footwork by the men, the <quote> " bent

knee" </quote> quality, the <quote> " throw back" </quote> , practice by the

blacks to mimic the whites, the use of the hips and the incorporation of

steps from other European social dances. </p> <O> photograph </O> <O> caption

</O>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#37:1> Although the Campstyle Quadrille includes African elements, it

still retains original elements such as Advancing and Retreating, Crossing (

pass through), Promenade, Figure of Eight made by Ladies, Star Formation,

Grand Chain, Wheels and Balancing. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#38:1> Quadrille was a popular social dance after Emancipation.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#39:1> It was performed at tea parties, house openings and family

celebrations, especially weddings.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#40:1> It was also performed at community functions such as the 1st of

August celebrations, fairs held by church groups, 4H Club Day celebrations,

Boxing Day and New Year's Day celebrations. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#41:1> On these occasions, music was provided by Mento Bands, which

played a variety of traditional European tunes.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#42:1> The instruments included violin, fife, piccolo, saxophone, trombone,

clarinet, trumpet, banjo, guitar, drum, marimba or rhumba, triangles, pieces

of irons, sardine cans, forks or graters or double bass. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#43:1> Of particular interest is a bass instrument constructed from a

piece of thick bamboo about 13.13 m ( 4 ft.) long.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#44:1> A string which is attached is pulled against a hole as the musician

blows through it.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#45:1> Musicians sometimes used improvised instruments made from discarded

material such as pieces of iron and empty sardine tins.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#46:1> The women <w> dancers' </w> dress was rustic with headties while the

men wore bright shirts. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#47:1> The popularity of Quadrille declined after the introduction of

American music and calypso in the 1950s.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#48:1> Today it is mostly performed as part of the annual Festival

celebrations. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#49:1> <h> <bold> <it> Myal </it> </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#50:1> Myal, one of the oldest dances in Jamaica, is associated with a

type of religious observance.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#51:1> This dance was mostly performed in the parish of St. Elizabeth.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#52:1> It has also been erroneously associated with Obeah.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#53:1> The difference, however, is the belief that while Obeah uses evil

powers, Myal uses good powers. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#54:1> Myalists captured the ghosts of dead people in order to use and

control them.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#55:1> Myal was declared illegal by the authorities who regarded it as a

great evil. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#56:1> In the 1840s Myal was revived when missionary activities began to

get stronger.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#57:1> It was recorded in the Moravian and Presbyterian records that this

action distressed the ministers. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#58:1> The Myal cult is now a relic of the past but elements still

exist.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#59:1> The dance shows a wide range of body movements, extensive use of

space and violence of action.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#60:1> These are done by throwing the body on the ground and by acrobatic

feats, as well as a vibrating movement brought about by a succession of rapid

sideway shifts from foot to foot, on the toes and with knees bent.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#61:1> These elements can also be found in the Gumbay dance. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#62:1> Myal ceremonies took place in open fields, or under cotton trees,

and often people would sing, drum and dance all night. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#63:1> <h> <bold> <it> Gumbay </it> </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#64:1> The Gumbay dance, an element of the cult of healing of the same

name, was also performed in the parish of St. Elizabeth and is derived from

Myal. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#65:1> The dance consists of a series of long steps followed by

vibrating sideways body movements and by wheeling turns and sudden stops with

pelvic forward tilt. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#66:1> At healing ceremonies, which lasted most of the night, various

feats such as back bending, rolling over in a succession of somersaults and

climbing high coconut trees, would take place in the height of possession.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#67:1> The square drum was used in this dance.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#68:1> It had four feet like a stool and was beaten with two sticks. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#69:1> This dance is also known among some members of the Maroon

population. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#70:1> <h> <bold> <it> Ettu </it> </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#71:1> The Ettu dance is performed in the parish of Hanover and is a

social dance from Africa.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#72:1> It is believed that Ettu is a corruption of the word Edo, the name of

a West African Yoruba Tribe. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#73:1> The dance involves the lifting and dropping of elbows and

shoulders, with the feet doing a sideways shuffling step. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#74:1> The musical instruments consist of a double headed goatskin drum

- the irre and a kerosene tin referred to as the achaka or katta.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#75:1> The dance metre is of two types, simple duple and compound duple,

known as the blues.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#76:1> The songs are short and repetitive, built on four notes only and sung

in a Yoruban dialect. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#77:1> <h> <bold> <it> Maypole </it> </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#78:1> The Maypole dance originated in the 15th century and is now the

national dance of St. Vincent, performed on May 1 ( May Day).
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#79:1> It celebrates the coming of spring and new growth. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#80:1> The dance was introduced to the island by slaves and was usually

performed on May 27, Queen Victoria's Birthday. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#81:1> Maypole, also referred to as the Long Ribbon Pole in rural areas,

was a part of outdoor social festivities of old England and Jamaica and was

performed at fairs, garden parties or picnics. </p> <O> photograph </O> <O>

caption </O>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#82:1> There are various styles in Maypole, more popular being the

Spider Web, Flair, Dome and Umbrella. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#83:1> Maypole is still performed in the rural areas at village fairs

and on other celebratory occasions. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#84:1> <h> <bold> <it> Dinki Mini/Gerreh </it> </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#85:1> Dinki Mini originates from the Congolese word <mention> 'ndingi'

</mention> which means lamentation or funeral song.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#86:1> Dinkies are celebratory occasions.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#87:1> Although associated with death, the music is lively, joyous and

exciting, intending to cheer the family and friends of the dead person.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#88:1> Dinki Mini was practiced openly throughout slavery but is now done

mainly during our annual Festival celebrations. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#89:1> However, it is still performed in the parishes of St. Mary, St.

Ann, St. Andrew and Portland, while Gerreh is found in the parishes of

Hanover, Westmoreland and St. James.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#90:1> Its popularity came about from the death of Tacky, a hero of the

Maroons, as it was performed during his funeral celebrations. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#91:1> Popular at set-ups or nine-nights, the first few nights consist

of singing and dancing to mento music.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#92:1> The sixth to the eighth night is dominated by ring games, role

playing, riddles and anancy stories.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#93:1> On the ninth night, a ritual to send off the <quote> 'mature'

</quote> spirit to begin its journey <quote> 'home' </quote> , is performed.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#94:1> The family of the deceased will <quote> 'turn out' </quote> the

spirit by turning over mattresses and rearranging rooms. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#95:1> The aim of the ritual is to properly send the spirit on its

journey.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#96:1> Hymns such as <quote> " Rock of Ages" </quote> are sung. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#97:1> Included in the activities, is the feeding of the dancers and

singers who will not hesitate to remind the householders of this duty. This

is done in song, for example: <O> lyrics </O>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#98:1> While refreshment is provided at the set-up, a mini feast is

prepared for the Ninth Night.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#99:1> This con <l> sists of fried fish, coffee, or chocolate tea, crackers

and bread.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#100:1> In some parishes curried goat and rice with mannish water ( i.e.

goat head soup) is served. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#101:1> Instruments associated with the Dinki Mini are shakas, katta

sticks, condensed milk tins, grater, the tamboo ( cylindrical shaped drums)

and the benta.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#102:1> The benta is an ancient stringed instrument - a fret board made of

bamboo and a gourd resonator. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#103:1> The Dinki Mini dance focuses on the pelvic region, as it is

performed in defiance of the death that has occurred.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#104:1> The dancers, male and female together, make suggestive rotations

with the pelvis in an attempt to prove that they are stronger than death, as

they have the means to reproduce. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#105:1> The lyrics of the songs associated with Gerreh are also

suggestive.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#106:1> Gerreh however has another dimension - the bamboo dance - that is

dancing on elevated bamboo poles and between four bamboo poles brought

together and pulled back by four crouching players. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#107:1> <h> <bold> <it> Revivalism </it> </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#108:1> Revivalism began in Jamaica between 1860 and 1861 as part of a

religious movement called the Great Revival.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#109:1> It is a combination of elements from African pagan beliefs and

Christianity and has several forms.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#110:1> The two major ones however, are Pocomania & Revival Zion ( Zion).

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#111:1> Revivalists believe that the temporal and spiritual worlds are

indivisible, therefore the living can be possessed and influenced by the

spirits of the dead. </p> <O> photograph </O> <O> caption </O>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#112:1> Once a worshipper is possessed, the spirit becomes his protector

and consultant, in return for food which is usually offered. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#113:1> A ritual which involves singing, drumming, dancing, clapping,

groaning and the use of prayers is done to invite possession.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#114:1> Some of the music and songs come from orthodox religion.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#115:1> Evidence of possession is wheeling and dancing, falling into a

trance and speaking in unknown tongues. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#116:1> Revival groups or bands have leaders.-
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#117:1> The male leader is called the <quote> " Shepherd" </quote> or

<quote> " Captain" </quote> , while the female leader is referred to as

<quote> " Mother" </quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#118:1> The shepherd or leader controls and uses several spirits.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#119:1> As the distinctions between the branches of revivalism become less

defined and a certain <quote> 'respectability' </quote> is acquired, leaders

are often referred to as Bishop.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#120:1> The group consists of many officers who have titles and specific

functions.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#121:1> Meetings are held in a <quote> " mission ground" </quote> where a

temporary structure is erected. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-010#122:1> Today bands erect concrete structures.
<ICE-JA:W2B-010#123:1> The <quote> " Mission Ground" </quote> is usually a part of the

leader's yard and is marked by a tall pole on which a flag is flown. </p>

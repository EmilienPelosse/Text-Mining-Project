<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#1:1> <h> Norman Manley </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#2:1> <h> <bold> SPORTING HERO and More </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#4:1> <it> In a very real sense, the fact that Norman Manley first

attracted wide attention in Jamaica as a sporting hero had a great deal to do

with his entry into public life, a public life which culminated, after his

death, in his elevation to the Order of National Hero in September, 1969.

</it> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#5:1> No one should pretend that it was not Norman Manley's great

intellect which was to serve as his chief recom <l> mendation from his very

early days.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#6:1> However, there can be little doubt that but for his great sporting

prowess he might not have secured the Rhodes Scholarship which took him to

Oxford and the study of law. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#7:1> As we celebrate Norman Manley's centenary, it is fitting for us to

be aware that the wide recognition of his sporting prowess coincided with the

first three years of the Inter-Secondary Schools Championship Sports, known

over the years as Championships, Champs, <w> Boys' </w> Championships and now

<w> Boys' </w> Champs.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#8:1> The first of these events was in the year 1910, which was also

Manley's first year in Class 1, the senior class, where any marked success

was sure to attract attention. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#9:1> In those early days, only six schools entered the championships.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#10:1> Those institutions were Wolmer's Boys, the first winners and one of

the oldest schools in the island, Manley's Jamaica College, St George's

College and Potsdam School, all of which are still very much with us.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#11:1> Potsdam was the original name of what is now Munro College.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#12:1> After the outbreak of the First World War in 1914, German names

became highly unpopular in Britain and the colonies, and so the Germanic

<mention> 'Potsdam' </mention> was changed to the acceptable <mention> 'Munro'

</mention> </p> .

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#13:1> The best summary of Manley's schoolboy career in track and field

athletics still remains that of the late Sir Herbert Macdonald in the

Foreword to his <it> History of the Inter-Secondary Schools Championship

Sports - Champs 1910-1970 </it> , a rare but indispensable volume.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#14:1> Sir Herbert, a man who knew a thing or two about sport, wrote of

Manley: <quote> 'He was the first schoolboy in Jamaica to take training

seriously </quote> ( and do note that Macdonald did not write <quote> 'to be

trained seriously' </quote> )
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#15:1> <quote> He took unto himself the job of imposing discipline into the

athletic life of the College.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#16:1> He ordered all boarders to run two laps before breakfast each

morning.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#17:1> He stood at the top of the steps with a strap and corked every boy

who disobeyed his order.' </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#18:1> Even if that account contains some hyperbole, it is clear that

certain aspects of the schoolboy sporting leader pre <l> figured certain

aspects of the national professional, social and political leader - social

encompassing both sports and the arts. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#19:1> In his Norman Manley Memorial Lecture in 1986, the Hon. Viv Blake

told a nice story which illustrates Manley's speed, athleticism and

discipline.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#20:1> To quote from the lecture: </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#21:1> <quote> Manley's contemporaries record the fact that as Captain

of the Jamaica College cricket eleven that was competing for the Sunlight

Cricket Cup, it dawned on him that the team was not likely to succeed without

the assistance of at least one good fast bowler.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#22:1> After a futile search for such talent in the upper school, he decided

that he had to do the job himself.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#23:1> He had the speed and stride for the run up to the wicket so as to

create the necessary momentum for the delivery, and soon mastered the art of

bowling the inswinger, the away-swinger and the yorker.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#24:1> It is said that he even learnt to bowl the occasional bouncer.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#25:1> After six weeks of intensive practice, carried out in the early

morning hours before the boarders at Jamaica College had shaken off their

slumbers, he was satisfied that he was capable of becoming a devastating

opening bowler.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#26:1> And his commitment and efforts were rewarded.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#27:1> That season, he was the most successful fast bowler in Sunlight Cup

cricket, and Jamaica College duly won the competition. </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#28:1> Sir Herbert Macdonald provides the best summary of Manley's

schoolboy efforts - some of which are worthy of close examination.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#29:1> <quote> 'For the three years he ran in Class 1 for Jamaica College,

this is his remarkable record which has never been equalled and never can

be.' </quote>
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#30:1> These words were first published twenty-two years ago and no doubt

were written before that time.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#31:1> They remain as true as ever.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#32:1> Some may wonder about the last three words, <quote> 'never can be'

</quote> , but they are true.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#33:1> First of all, there are now some restrictions in the rules about the

number and combination of individual and relay events that an athlete can run

( and one must remember that during Manley's time there were no relay events.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#34:1> Had there been, then his record would probably have been even more

remarkable.)
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#35:1> The other thing is that today, whatever the rules might be, a

valuable athletic <quote> 'property' </quote> like Manley - especially in

Class 1 - would never be worked as he was then.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#36:1> Macdonald summarized the details: </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#37:1> <quote> He ( Manley) took part in seventeen events ( over three

years). He won twelve of these. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#38:1> He finished second the remaining five times. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#39:1> He never finished third or worse at any time. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#40:1> He won the Class 1 ( individual) Championships for three years in

a row, 1910: 11 points, 1911: 17 points, 1912: 18 points. </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#41:1> In the last two of the three years, the Jamaica College team won

the overall event with 35 points in 1911 and 33 points in 1912.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#42:1> Manley thus scored very nearly 50% of the points in the first year

and rather more than 50% of the points in the second. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#43:1> The details make fascinating reading.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#44:1> In 1910 Manley finished second in the 100 yards to J M ( later Dr

<quote> 'Fire' </quote> Hall), the father of the well-known television and

radio personality, Dennis Hall.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#45:1> He then finished with three more seconds: in the 440 and Open 880

events to M O Marsh, and in the Long Jump to J R Lewis.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#46:1> His sole victory was in the 120 yards Hurdles, an event in which he

was to do well when we went to Oxford.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#47:1> This all-round performance in a short sprint, the hundred yards, a

long sprint, the quarter mile, and a middle distance event, the half mile, as

well as <O> photograph </O> <O> caption </O> his efforts in the Hurdles and a

field event, the Long Jump, earned him his First Class Championship and made

him a man to watch. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#48:1> Indeed, in the next year there was, in a sense, hardly anything

to watch but Manley.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#49:1> He won five events and finished second in one, including his most

famous performance: 10 seconds in the 100 yards, or what was known in the

Mother Country as an even time.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#50:1> <quote> 'Even time' </quote> was for many a year the ambition of any

good-class adult athlete and here it had been achieved by a schoolboy.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#51:1> Manley also won the 220 yards, the 440 yards and the 880 yards as

well as the 120 yards Hurdles for the second year in a row.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#52:1> He was beaten only in the Long Jump by Hall, who had won both the 100

and 220 yards in the previous year.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#53:1> Manley's 17 points would actually have placed him higher than the

fourth placed school.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#54:1> The combined points for Manley and Hall totalled 24, above St

George's College with 22 points. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#55:1> Manley's 100 yards record was to last an incredible 41 years

until 1952 when it was beaten by Frank Hall, now a well known housing

developer in Jamaica and other parts of the Caribbean.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#56:1> Among the athletes who were to equal this record was, interestingly

enough, his older son, Douglas, who accomplished the feat in 1941 while at

Munro College. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#57:1> The field in Douglas's year was to be extremely interesting.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#58:1> The runners included the great athlete, Herb McKenley, who finished

third, L <quote> 'Coco' </quote> Brown, who was McKenley's Jamaican nemesis

at school and after, and George Prescod, well known in Jamaican cricketing

and football circles.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#59:1> S W Seymour James of Jamaica College, later the Librarian at the

United Nations, was an injured non-starter. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#60:1> Yet it was Norman Manley's 220 yards record which really

demonstrated that he was one of the fastest school <l> boys in the world.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#61:1> He ran the event in 23 seconds flat.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#62:1> Just three years before, over the slightly shorter 200 metres, worth

about one-tenth of a second less, Robert Kerr of Canada had won the gold

medal at the London Olympics in 22.6 seconds, or four-tenths of a second

faster.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#63:1> One year later in the Stockholm Olympics, fourth, fifth and sixth

places were to be all within eight-tenths of a second of Manley's 1911

effort. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#64:1> Although his times in the sprints were not as imposing in the

1912 championships, Manley was to win all six events in which he entered.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#65:1> His 18 points were one more than those of Potsdam School in fourth

position.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#66:1> In 1920 Potsdam School, renamed Munro, was to win the <}> <->

Championahips </-> <+> Championships </+> </}> . </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#67:1> Manley's 1912 victories were in the 100, 220, 440, Hurdles, Long

Jump and High Jump.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#68:1> In the latter he did not attempt to clear the bar until he was the

only competitor left and he cleared 5 feet, 7 inches to win.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#69:1> Had he failed at the height he would, of course, not have placed at

all.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#70:1> Second in this event was Lambert Lewis of St George's who was to

become famous as one of the best footballers ever produced in Jamaica. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#71:1> Manley's most remarkable performances were in the Hurdles where

he ran 14.6 seconds in the heats and 14.8 in the finals.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#72:1> It was felt that these extraordinary times indicated that something

was wrong and that perhaps only nine instead of ten hurdles had been set up.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#73:1> However, the height of the hurdles was only three feet, much less

than the Olympic height of three feet, six inches, therefore no one should be

too surprised that an athlete with Manley's speed could run faster than the

Olympic times, given the lower height.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#74:1> The fact that O J Lecesne's time in the other heat was 16 seconds

suggests that there was not very much wrong. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#75:1> So ended Manley's schoolboy track and field career.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#76:1> It would have been very interesting to see just what he might have

done if the First World War had not prevented the Berlin Olympics planned for

1916 from being held.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#77:1> The times of the 100 and 200 metres winners at Antwerp in 1920

suggest that he could have been a medal contender.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#78:1> Charlie Paddock, the famous American runner, won the 100 in 10.8 and

the other two medals went in 10.8 and 11 seconds.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#79:1> Allen Woodring won the 200 in 22.0 seconds and the other two

medallists finished at 22.1 and 22.2.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#80:1> In the Hurdles, Earl Thomson set a world record at 14.8 followed by

15.1 and 15.2.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#81:1> The 110 metres distance was, however, one foot longer than 120 yards.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#82:1> Manley himself writing about those early days said, <quote> 'Work

and I worked very hard and Sports and I did a bit of everything - Track,

Athletics, Cricket, Football, Rifle Shooting, filled my life.' </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#83:1> It was not fashionable then for Jamaican boys of Manley's social

class and abilities to go to American universities, not even Ivy League ones,

but one can imagine what he might have done even at an Ivy league school had

he specialized in Track and Field Athletics.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#84:1> He might well have beaten Herb McKenley, as his son was to do in that

famous 1941 race, as the first English-speaking Caribbean man to secure a

time or distance-measured world record in sport. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-009#85:1> Twenty years after his schoolboy career had ended, Manley was

still remembered for his feats by his school <l> mates and peers.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#86:1> H O A ( Harry) Dayes was one of the few legal men who was thought to

be worthy of intellectual comparison with Manley and he himself was a

<ICE-JA:W2B-009#87:1> In 1932 he spoke at the Jamaica College Old Boys dinner when Manley

was honoured for becoming King's Counsel - a very rare distinction in those

days.
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#88:1> Said Dayes, <quote> 'When one went to the sports of J.C., when one

turned the programme, one saw what appeared to be only the name of Manley,

<ICE-JA:W2B-009#89:1> They always regarded him as a demon of speed - a belief still shared

by many of the cops and speed cops on the island.' </quote>
<ICE-JA:W2B-009#90:1> The aside referred to Manley's lifelong reputation as a fast driver.

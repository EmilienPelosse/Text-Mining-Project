<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#1:1> <h> <bold> 1. DESCRIPTION OF THE AREA </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#2:1> <h> <bold> 1.1 Overview </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#3:1> Jamaica in 1988 had a population of approximately 2.43 million and

Gross Domestic Product of US$2,858 million.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#4:1> The agricultural GDP in 1987 was US$174 million with fisheries

accounting for approximately 1% of this amount.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#5:1> In 1987 the unemployment rate in Jamaica stood at 21% of the labour

force ( PIOJ, 1988). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#6:1> Jamaica is located in the Greater Antilles, in the Caribbean Sea,

approximately 145 km south of Cuba and 161 km west of Haiti ( Fig.1).
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#7:1> The island is 236 km long, between 35 and 82 km wide, with a total

land area of 10,940 km <sp> 2 </sp> and a coastline of approximately 885 km.

</p>

<p> The topography consists of an interior backbone of mountain ranges

running almost the length of the island from roughly east to west, surrounded

by flat coastal plains.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#8:1> The Blue Mountains in the east have the highest peak at 2,256 m.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#9:1> The interior of the central and western two-thirds of the island is

dominated by a limestone plateaux, the majority of which lies between 350-610

m ( NRCD, 1987). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#10:1> The irregular coastline is punctuated by numerous coastal

features such as harbours, bays, beaches, estuaries, mangrove swamps, rocky

shores, cays, coral reefs and lagoons.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#11:1> These natural features provide a resource base that contributes

significantly to the economic well-being of the country, chiefly through

tourism. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#12:1> Jamaica has a tropical maritime climate which is modified by

northeast trade winds and land-sea breezes.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#13:1> Temperature is fairly uniform in the coastal areas.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#14:1> The average temperature is 27 &degree; C, ranging from a low of 23

&degree; C, in the winter ( January and February) to 28 &degree; C in the

summer ( June-August).
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#15:1> Humidity is usually above 60% and is generally highest in the morning

( 85%), falling slightly by mid afternoon.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#16:1> Rainfall in Jamaica is marked by monthly, annual, and spatial

variability.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#17:1> The average annual rainfall for the entire island is 195.8 cm and

greatest rainfall occurs form October to November with a secondary peak in

May. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#18:1> <h> <bold> 1.2 Oceanographic features </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#19:1> The Island lies within the path of north-easterly trade winds and

wind speeds exceeding 15m/sec with associated choppy seas are common.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#20:1> A season of relative calm usually occurs between October and February

each year ( Munro, 1983).
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#21:1> There is <O> map </O> <O> caption </O> a hurricane season in the

Caribbean from July to October each year during which unstable weather

systems, including violent storms, occur. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#22:1> The branch of the North Equatorial current which flows westwards

and enters the south-eastern Caribbean Basin is the dominant surface current

system affecting Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#23:1> It exits the Caribbean Sea via the Yucatan Channel and then passes

between Florida and the Bahamas as the Florida current before rejoining the

eastern branch of the North Equatorial current to become the Gulf Stream, and

eventually the upper portion of the North Atlantic Gyre ( Lyons, 1980; Apel,

1987).
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#24:1> The presence of east flowing surface and sub-surface counter currents

and gyres on both sides of the main current has been reported by Emilson ( in

Munro, 1983). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#25:1> Munro ( 1983) reported that tidal effect on the Jamaican Coast is

slight, the range varying between 20 cm and 36 cm.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#26:1> Tidal currents on Pedro Bank are reported to be sometimes sufficient

to stop or reverse the flow of the prevailing westerly Caribbean current.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#27:1> The annual temperature range of the surface waters south of

Jamaica down to a depth of about 75 m is reported to be between 26.5 &degree;

C and 29.5 &degree; C ( Munro, 1983).
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#28:1> Goodbody and Munro ( 1971) reported fairly constant salinity of 35.2

to 35.8% in the waters south of Jamaica. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#29:1> <h> <bold> 2. MARINE HABITAT </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#30:1> Jamaica has a 12 mile territorial zone around the main Island as

well as around the offshore Morant and Pedro Cays.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#31:1> In keeping with the provisions of the International Convention on the

Law of the Sea, Jamaica is presently in the process of drafting a bill for

the establishment of a 200 mile Exclusive Economic Zone ( EEZ), which would

include the existing 12 miles territorial limit.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#32:1> The matter, however, is a complex one and may take some time to be

successfully implemented. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#33:1> <h> <bold> 2.1 Island Shelf </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#34:1> The Island shelf and nine proximal banks ( Fig. 1) have a total

area of 4,170 km <sp> 2 </sp> .
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#35:1> The South Shelf is relatively wide whilst the North Shelf is very

narrow and its width does not exceed 1.5 km at any point.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#36:1> The North Shelf is characterized by fringing coral reefs with few

gaps, from Morant Point in the east to Negril in the west.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#37:1> The fringing reef and the sill reef are almost contiguous along the

north coast.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#38:1> The South Shelf attains a maximum width of 24 km south of the

parishes of St. Catherine and Clarendon ( Fig.2).
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#39:1> Large reefs are restricted to the eastern portion of the South Shelf,

small patches of corals, gorgonians and seaweeds, interspersed with sandy

patches are found in most other areas of the South Shelf.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#40:1> Well developed sill reefs occur along most of the edge of the South

Shelf.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#41:1> The sill reef and drop-off are generally characterized by massive

corals. </p> <O> map </O> <O> caption </O>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#42:1> <h> <bold> 2.2 Offshore Banks </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#43:1> The Morant and Pedro Banks rise abruptly from depths exceeding

500 m to form submerged plateaux with average depths from 20 to 30 m.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#44:1> Pedro Bank is relatively large with a total area of 8,040 km <sp> 2

</sp> whilst the Morant Bank is relatively small being approximately 100 km

<sp> 2 </sp> in area ( Munro and Thompson, 1983). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#45:1> The Pedro Bank may be divided into various depth zones as

follows: </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#46:1> 0 - 10 m <space> 171 km <sp> 2 </sp> ( 2.1%) </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#47:1> 10 - 30 m <space> 5917 km <sp> 2 </sp> ( 73.7%) </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#48:1> 30 - 50 m <space> 1948 km <sp> 2 </sp> ( 23.3%) </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#49:1> The top of the Pedro Bank is relatively flat whereas the edges

are characterized by sill reefs of various sizes.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#50:1> The shallowest, south-eastern, southern and south-western portions of

the Bank, which face into the Caribbean Current, have the best developed

reefs.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#51:1> The middle of Pedro Bank is dominated by silt and sand with sparsely

dispersed patches of sea grass, algae, and isolated coral heads.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#52:1> In contrast to the edge, the middle of the Bank is reported to be

relatively unproductive ( Munro and Thompson, 1983).
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#53:1> Beyond the sill reefs at the edge of the Bank the depth increases

abruptly sometimes by near vertical drops to more than 500 m.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#54:1> At the eastern section of Pedro Bank there are three small cays two

of which are inhabited by fishermen.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#55:1> Small cays also occur on the Morant Bank, and are occupied by

fishermen. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#56:1> <h> <bold> 3. MARINE FISHERY RESOURCES </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#57:1> <h> <bold> 3.1 Organization of the Fisheries </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#58:1> In order to fully understand the different types of resources,

their locations and rates of exploitation in the Jamaican fishery, it is

first necessary to examine briefly the organization of the fishery itself.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#59:1> The fishery of Jamaica is made up largely of artisanal fishermen

operating from open canoe type boats powered by either outboard motors or

oars.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#60:1> There are approximately 12,000 registered persons who fish using

approximately 4,000 boats from 168 fishing beaches scattered around the

Jamaican coastline.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#61:1> The artisanal fishery may be sub-divided into two main sectors, the

inshore fishery and the offshore fishery. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#62:1> <h> <bold> 3.2 The Inshore Fishery </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#63:1> The inshore fishery takes place in the coastal waters of the

Island shelf and its nine proximal banks ( Fig.1).
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#64:1> Historically, the shelf and proximal banks have supported the bulk of

fishery activities in terms of manpower and vessels.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#65:1> The major fishing gear used in the artisanal fishery is the Z-shaped

antillean fish trap.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#66:1> Other common gear include gill nets, seine nets, hook-and-line, and

spearguns.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#67:1> There is some collection of crustaceans, molluscs and algae by

divers. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#68:1> <h> <bold> 3.3 The Offshore Fishery </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#69:1> This includes fishing operations that are based on the offshore

cays, as well as deep- sea fishing conducted by vessels operating

independently of any land base for extended periods.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#70:1> There is at present a fleet of approximately 250 mechanized canoe

type boats and approximately 700 fishermen based on the Morant and Pedro Cays

( Fig.1).
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#71:1> They are serviced by carrier boats in the 10-26 m size range which

ply between the Cays and Kingson, transporting supplies to the fishermen and

returning with fish in ice.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#72:1> During the 1980s there was a significant change in the fleet of

carrier vessels with smaller, faster, open hull vessels replacing the larger

decked boats.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#73:1> In addition to the Cay based operation, there are about five vessels

ranging in size from 15-30 m operating on either the Pedro Bank or in

extra-territorial waters.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#74:1> The Morant Bank supports about 120 fishermen using between 40 to 50

canoe type vessels ( Anon., 1989). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#75:1> The fisheries of Jamaica are based primarily on multispecies

coral reef fish resources.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#76:1> Families of major economic importance are Lutjanidae, Serranidae,

Carangidae, Mullidae, Scaridae, Haemulidae, Balistidae, Acanthuridae,

Holocentridae, Chaetodontidae, Palinuridae ( lobsters).
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#77:1> Among the coastal pelagic fishes the most important families are

Clupeidae, Engraulidae, Mugilidae, Scombridae, and Hemiramphidae. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#79:1> There is not as yet a permanent system for the collection of

<ICE-JA:W2A-023#80:1> The available estimates have come from Government surveys conducted

on irregular basis, as well as from research projects.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#81:1> Limited data are also obtained from the Fisheries Division through

various monitoring programmes.

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#87:1> It is important to note that prior to 1955 there was no offshore

fishery and the total catch came from the inshore fishing grounds.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#88:1> In 1955 a boat mechanization scheme was launched by the Fisheries

Division and by 1981 approximately 54% of the fishing boats in the Island

were mechanized. Soon after the mechanization programme started some boats

from beaches along the South Jamaican Shelf travelled to Pedro Bank to fish

and returned to their home base with the catch.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#89:1> The sample surveys conducted before 1981 assumed that the fish landed

on the South Shelf beaches were captured solely from the South Shelf.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#90:1> The mixing of the catch from Pedro Bank with that from the South <O>

caption </O> <O> table </O> <O> caption </O> <O> table </O> Shelf means that

the productivity of the South Shelf may have been overestimated. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#91:1> <h> <bold> 3.5 Status of the Fisheries </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#92:1> Aiken and Haughton ( 1987) analyzed the available statistical

data on the inshore fishery and found that although the total catch and the

total number of boats in operation changed very little between 1968 and 1981,

very significant changes in fishing effort and fishing pattern were taking

place as a consequence of the process of mechanization.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#93:1> Between 1968 and 1981 fishing effort was estimated to have doubled

whereas the catch remained at about the same level. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#94:1> An examination of the process of mechanization of the fishing

fleet reveals that very significant changes in both fishing effort and

fishing patterns were taking place.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#95:1> The mechanized canoes which are replacing the unmechanized canoes are

capable of carrying more fishermen, going farther offshore, spending longer

periods in the fishing grounds and hauling more fish traps than the

unmechanized canoes.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#96:1> As a consequence, they harvest more fish per unit of time. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#97:1> Haughton ( 1988) developed a standardized measure of fishing

effort which was termed <quote> " effective canoe effort" </quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#98:1> Since 1968 the catch per effective canoe has been steadily declining

and by 1981 it was 59% of the catch rate in 1968 ( Fig.3).
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#99:1> A logarithmic surplus production model of the inshore fishery in

Jamaica suggests that maximum yields of around 2.2 t/km <sp> 2 </sp> / year

are possible with a fishing intensity of between 1.0 to 1.2 mechanized canoes

per km <sp> 2 </sp> of shelf ( Fig.4). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#100:1> <h> <bold> 3.6 Comparative Yield Levels </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-023#101:1> The available figures published by FAO ( 1987) indicate that for

the entire Caribbean region, the average yield from shelf areas around the

Caribbean islands is around 0.5-1.5 t/km <sp> 2 </sp> year.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#102:1> This is less than some earlier estimates, which tended not to take

into account the proportion of relatively unproductive sandy bottom.
<ICE-JA:W2A-023#103:1> In Jamaica on the other hand the average yield from the island shelf

and proximal bank, computed from the Government sample surveys, was 1.7 t/km

<sp> 2 </sp> / year between 1968-1981. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#1:1> <h> Rocky road for transport system </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#2:1> By Gordon Williams </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#3:1> <h> Inside Transport </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#4:1> This is the second in a series on the public transportation

system.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#5:1> Today's article looks at the history of Jamaica's public transport,

especially in the Corporate Area, leading up to the present situation. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#6:1> Much like the common sight of speeding mini-buses, recollections

of how Jamaica's public transportation system slipped into today's muddled

state are often blurred. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#7:1> But rolled back, the route covered by transport systems over

decades bears striking resemblance to the present.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#8:1> And if the old cliche of history repeating itself unfolds, today's

system of public transport could be in for a prolonged bumpy ride. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#9:1> In November 1982, not long before Government's decision to turn

over Jamaica's public transport almost exclusively to individual operators,

then Minister of Transport, Pearnel Charles called the system <quote> "

complete nonsense" </quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#10:1> He also echoed Government's opposition to the <quote> " harum scarum"

</quote> way urban transport was operated.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#11:1> That system paired the Jamaica Omnibus Service ( JOS) and mini- buses

to serve the Corporate Area. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#12:1> But over 10 years and a switch of Government later, the transport

tag has changed little.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#13:1> In 1992, with the JOS long gone, Government called public

transportation <quote> " poor" </quote> and Minister of Transport Robert

Pickersgill stoutly defended a decision not to grant fare increases, first

requested at the beginning of the year, until there is <quote> " a visible

improvement in the system." </quote>
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#14:1> Even long-time bus operators, such as Arthur Chin, admit that the

system is tilting precariously close to collapse. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#15:1> <quote> " It ( transport system) has crashed," </quote> Mr. Chin

said last week, <quote> " but not yet written off." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#16:1> More than 50 years ago, any crash would have made far less noise.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#17:1> Demands on the public transport system were significantly lower.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#18:1> In 1943, the population of Kingston and St. Andrew was 238,000.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#19:1> By 1991 it rose to almost 650,000.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#20:1> The population climb overall in Jamaica brought additional problems

for public transport, but in the Corporate Area, including rapidly developing

St. Catherine, the intensity of those problems magnified. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#21:1> Still, the increase of motorized public transportation in Jamaica

after the turn of this century, gave few indications of the problems to come.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#22:1> Then the tram car, controlled by the light and power company,

occupied the main routes running on roadways through Kingston and lower St.

Andrew.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#23:1> Ad hoc bus lines filled out supplementary routes.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#24:1> But soon the demands changed.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#25:1> Additional motor vehicles increased the need for road use.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#26:1> The tram car eventually departed, and by the late 1940s privately

contracted bus service had already rolled into the Corporate Area. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#27:1> However, the fickle footing of the business side of public

transport had already shown.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#28:1> Lack of profits forced early private operators to give up the urban

franchise, and the JOS Company Ltd., controlled mostly by England-based

investors wooed by local interest, took over responsibility for Corporate

Area public transportation in 1953. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#29:1> For many years the JOS steadied Jamaica's public transport

system.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#30:1> But the stint proved an extended lull before a turbulent storm.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#31:1> For what transport experts now say was literally being <quote> "

penny wise and pound foolish" </quote> efficient public transport was lost,

possibly forever. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#32:1> The JOS had kept the growing urban population moving with

adequate scheduling and maintenance.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#33:1> For example, no bus was kept on the road after 10 years.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#34:1> Regardless of its condition, it was replaced.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#35:1> At its peak, the privately-owned JOS had more than 550 buses on the

road each day.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#36:1> But the company needed additional income to remain profitable.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#37:1> A fare increase, reportedly <quote> " one penny" </quote> , was

requested but not approved by the Government.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#38:1> As time passed the company cut back on maintenance costs.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#39:1> Buses stayed on the roads longer - up to 16 years.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#40:1> The standard of public transportation deteriorated, but remained

effective. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#41:1> <quote> " The service didn't fall apart," </quote> said Mr. Chin,

a 40-year veteran of the transport system <-> remembers </-> .
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#42:1> <quote> " But it wasn't as good as the beginning." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#43:1> The Government, meanwhile, showed interest in acquiring the JOS,

although word was already out that the company was willing to put more buses

into the system if the fare increase was granted.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#44:1> It never came.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#45:1> By 1974, the JOS was acquired by the Government, keen on controlling

another essential service, at a price related to a net asset value of

$3,5 million. </p>
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#46:1> <h> Major upheaval </h>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#47:1> While rural transportation trudged along mainly unaltered, the

urban system was about to undergo a major upheaval.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#48:1> Although the Government had refused to grant a fare increase to the

privately-owned JOS, it managed to subsidise its new acquisition handsomely,

by some reports up to

$12 million per year.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#49:1> But the JOS had already been plagued by relentless industrial

disputes.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#50:1> Work stoppages, mainly strikes or sick-outs, consistently disrupted

the service.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#51:1> Commuters buckled under the disruptions. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#52:1> <quote> " The public, as has been the case of previous JOS

strikes, was the greatest loser," </quote> the Jamaica Employers Federation

stated during a 1972 strike. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#53:1> But JOS work stoppages continued, averaging about one each year

for several years.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#54:1> The strikes took their toll, and eventually led to the legitimisation

of a rapidly growing phenomenon - the mini-bus service.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#55:1> Mini-buses rode routes the weakened JOS could not.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#56:1> Reports of thefts, especially spare parts, also cost the JOS dearly.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#57:1> The strikes also released a number of JOS workers into mini-bus

operations.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#58:1> More crushing to the public, some mini-buses charged fares higher

than those authorised for the JOS. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#59:1> By 1982, the Ministry of Public Utilities and Transport reported

that when the JLP Government took over in 1980, the deficit for the JOS was

$30 million, and the JOS was seen as a national disgrace.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#60:1> Newspapers spotlighted the JOS's <quote> " checkered history of

labour troubles and general inefficiency." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#61:1> In December 1983, the Government divested the JOS.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#62:1> The buses were eventually sold, and the franchise for providing

Kingston Metropolitan Region ( KMR) with public transportation was split

among several private operators who were allotted packages of bus routes.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#63:1> A total of 589 JOS employees were laid off. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#64:1> But since 1979, many of the routes had already been

sub-franchised, with mini-buses carrying about 80 per cent of the passenger

journeys, and the JOS 20 per cent.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#65:1> The JOS lost big, and in the year of divestment, 1983, the company's

income totalled

$15,3 million while expenditure hovered just above

$42 million.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#66:1> At that time only about 140 JOS buses serviced routes each day.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#67:1> The JOS no longer worked. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#68:1> <quote> " The mini-bus system was born out of the inefficiency of

the JOS as operated by Government," </quote> said Dennis Harris another

long-time bus operator. </p>
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#69:1> <h> Dominant force </h>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#70:1> The service in Montego Bay, the country's second largest urban

centre, also declined.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#71:1> In 1983 only nine buses operated by the Montego Bay Municipal Bus

Company ( MBMBC) worked each day, down from 12 in 1982 and 17 in 1981.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#72:1> The number of routes covered dropped from 10 to eight.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#73:1> Except for the Jamaica Railway Corporation ( JRC), which managed to

reduce operating expenses by 3 per cent below 1982, and increase revenues by

14 per cent, the transport sector unearthed few good signs. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#74:1> By now the mini-buses had emerged as the dominant force of urban

public transportation.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#75:1> The numbers grew steadily.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#76:1> By 1988 the public passenger transport fleet was 3,340 buses,

totalling 65,586 seats.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#77:1> In 1988 the KMR was served by 1,220 legal buses, providing 27,133

seats.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#78:1> An estimated 600 operated illegally. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#79:1> The public embraced the new force in public transport.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#80:1> The mini-buses stopped anywhere to pick up or let off passengers,

when the JOS was restricted to assigned bus stops.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#81:1> The mini-buses were swifter than the lumbering JOS buses.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#82:1> People got to destinations quicker.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#83:1> But the honeymoon would soon end. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#85:2> <h> New page in politics </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#86:2> <h> Husband, wife teams to appear on local ballot </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#87:2> By Wyvolyn Gager </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#88:2> The major political parties are set to write a new page in the

island's political history when for the first time, husband and wife teams

will appear on the ballot in a general election. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#89:2> The People's National Party, ( PNP) and the Jamaica Labour Party,

( JLP) are throttling up. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#90:2> Among the candidates readying themselves on the JLP side are Lola

Ramocan, educator, who is running in north west Clarendon and George Ramocan,

former senator, publisher and coffee farmer, who will contest west rural St.

Andrew. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#91:2> On the PNP slate are Karlene Robertson, former councillor in the

KSAC, who is running in north east St. Andrew and Senator Dr. Paul Robertson

in south east St. Catherine. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#92:2> George Ramocan once served as a councillor in the Knockpatrick

division of central Manchester, but this will be his first outing in the big

leagues.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#93:2> Lola is completely new to elective politics. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#94:2> They met in 1962 when they were selected as the island's student

representatives to Expo in Canada. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#95:2> That meeting culminated in marriage.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#96:2> Both 42, they have four children. </p>
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#97:2> <h> Roots </h>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#98:2> Lola is from Ballieston, Clarendon, and returns to familiar turf.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#99:2> She attended Alston School and Frankfield High School.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#100:2> A teacher, she later became an education officer. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#101:2> At one time, Mrs. Ramocan was supervising her opponent Carl

Marshall, when he was headmaster of Kemps Hill and she was education officer.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#102:2> Marshall is the sitting Member of Parliament. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#103:2> George represented the JLP in the Senate between 1983 and 1989

and is now executive director of Jamaica Institute of Political Education,

JIPE. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#104:2> He also returns to his roots as he is from Lawrence Tavern.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#105:2> His opponent is pharmacist Vernon Robinson, a newcomer to

competitive politics. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#106:2> The Ramocans have regular devotions and family conferences to

keep their children abreast of developments. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#107:2> Many will remember the energetic KSAC Councillor Karlene Kirlew

of the 1970's who was at the forefront of the movement to establish the PNP

women's auxiliary.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#108:2> Now as Karlene Robertson, she will be challenging Douglas Vaz.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#109:2> He was named among the gang of five, who fell out of favour with the

leadership of the JLP. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#110:2> Mrs Robertson is a coffee farmer.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#111:2> The Robertsons have an eight year-old daughter. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#112:2> Dr. Robertson has been at the ringside of party politics but he

has never actually entered the ring, now he is donning gloves to challenge

seasoned campaigner - Kingsley Sangster. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#113:2> Dr. Robertson was quick to point out that as general secretary

of the PNP for many years, he was intimately involved in running election

campaigns at all levels. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#114:2> But why has he decided to run now?
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#115:2> He replied: </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#116:2> <h> My life </h> </p>

<p> <quote>
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#117:2> " Politics has been my life.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#118:2> I have given up an academic career to go into politics, so the next

step in terms of completing that career and helping the party that I believe

in and love, is to contest a seat." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#119:2> As an early indication of the turn his life would take, Dr.

Robertson's M Sc. thesis in 1971 was titled <quote> " political behaviour at

the grass roots level" </quote> . </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#120:2> With his wife involved in her own campaign, Dr. Robertson admits

they see very little of each other, <quote> " except late at nights."

</quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#121:2> Husband and wife teams are not new to politics.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#122:2> For example, Valrie McNeil served as a Councillor in the Hughenden

division of the KSAC while her husband Dr. Kenneth McNeil was Member of

Parliament in the newly created north west St. Andrew constituency. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#123:2> And over in St. Ann, Aabuthnott Gallimore was a Member of

Parliament and his wife, Myrtle, a Councillor for the Alexandria division.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#124:2> Meanwhile in Hanover, Cleveland Stanhope was western Hanover Member

of Parliament and his wife, Zena was Councillor and eventually became Mayor

of Lucea. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#125:2> And Madame Rose Leon, who was a pioneer of the 1940's, was

succeeded in the western St. Andrew constituency by her husband Arthur in

1959, when she fell out of favour with the JLP and was barred from running in

the constituency.
<ICE-JA:W2C-010#126:2> She later contested the seat as an independent and eventually

crossed the floor and joined the PNP in 1966. </p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#127:3> <h> Consumer League's call for price reduction ignored </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#128:3> By Claire Clarke </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#129:3> Despite a call by the National Consumers League ( NCL) to lower

prices on six basic food items last month, it appears that nothing has been

done so far. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-010#130:3> On December 30, 1992 the NCL called for a reduction in the

prices of tinned cheese, tinned mackerel in tomato sauce, bulk cornmeal,

counter flour, chicken necks and backs, and skimmed milk powder. </p> </I>

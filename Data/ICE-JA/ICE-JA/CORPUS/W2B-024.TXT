<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-024#2:1> <h> <bold> CONSERVATION AND DEVELOPMENT </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-024#3:1> Sustainable development is the buzzword of the environmentally

conscious nineties.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#4:1> The Jamaica Conservation and Development Trust, a not-for-profit,

non-government organisation has <quote> " working for sustainable

development" </quote> as its motto.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#5:1> One definition of sustainable development is <quote> " development

that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of

future generations to meet their own needs." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-024#6:1> We will all agree that economic development is essential both for

a country and for individuals.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#7:1> Simply put development is an improvement in standards of living,

usually achieved by the production of goods and services for sale.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#8:1> The fact is, that all development is in some way based on the use of

natural resources  tourism is dependent on beautiful beaches, waterfalls and

attractive natural sites; agriculture is dependent on fertile soil and

dependable sources of water and every industry requires water. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-024#9:1> In light of this, conservation or wise use of our resources is

simply good sense.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#10:1> The World Conservation Union sums it up well: - <quote> " For if the

object of development is to provide for social and economic welfare, the

object of conservation is to ensure Earth's capacity to sustain development

and support life". </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-024#11:1> The establishment and management of National and Marine Parks is

one of the major projects of the Jamaica Conservation and Development Trust (

JCDT).
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#12:1> These Parks are large areas of land or sea, in as pristine a state as

possible, protected by law from destructive use for the enjoyment and benefit

of Jamaicans.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#13:1> National Parks have been viewed as a tool or vehicle for sustainable

development as they encour <l> age development that is compatible with the

natural environment and seek to ensure that benefits accrue to local

communities. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-024#14:1> The first two Parks are the Blue and John Crow Mountains National

Park and the Montego Bay Marine Park.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#15:1> They were estab <l> lished through the Protected Areas Resource

Conservation ( PARC) Project, funded by USAID and the Government of Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#16:1> The JCDT played a major role in the planning and implementation of

this successful project - the first time a private sector organisation has

had such an important role in a govern <l> ment project.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#17:1> Two other Parks will be estab <l> lished under phase two of this

project  the Black River National Park and the Cockpit Country National

Park. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-024#18:1> Although the primary reason for selec <l> tion of these sites for

protection of their natu <l> ral resources, many of which are unique to

Jamaica, the importance of these areas for socio-economic benefits to local

communi <l> ties and Jamaica on a whole was paramount.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#19:1> The Blue and John Crow Mountains are the watershed for close to half

of Jamaica's population.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#20:1> The Montego Bay Marine Park protects the coral reefs and other marine

and coastal systems critical to that city's continued success as the islands

foremost tourism dollar earner.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#21:1> The Black River National Park focuses on an area of great cultural

her <l> itage and importance for the fishing industry, particularly for

shrimp.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#22:1> The rugged terrain of the Cockpit Country is the source of the major

rivers in the West and South-west of the island in addition to pro <l> viding

habitat for hundreds of unique plant species. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-024#23:1> Public participation in the planning and management of the

island's first two National Parks has been the key to their success.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#24:1> Local advisory committees meet regularly with Park management to

discuss relevant issues and evolve solutions that meet the goals of the

various interest groups.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#25:1> One such success story is the building of the fording over the

Yallahs River between Mavis Bank and Hagley Gap in 1992.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#26:1> The fording was destroyed during Hurricane Gilbert and had never been

repaired leading to great difficulties in transportation as the road was the

only one into and out of those rural villages.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#27:1> It was through the encouragement and support of the Blue and John

Crow Mountain National Park staff and the Jamaica Conservation and

Development Trust that the community was able to rebuild the fording.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#28:1> Thus, the projects of the JCDT not only seek to protect plants and

animals and the places they live but also to protect the livelihood of

communities.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#29:1> Another project of the JCDT and the Park is the facilitation of

community members in the Hagley Gap and Minto areas in the establishment of a

Tour Guide Company.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#30:1> Financial and technical assistance including training is being

provided over the long term to the Top of Jamaica, Blue Mountain Company.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#31:1> This is a great example of sustainable development, the long-term

success of this tour company is dependent on their wise use of the natu <l>

ral resources in the area. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-024#32:1> Conservation and Development therefore are not incompatible.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#33:1> With careful planning and consid <l> eration of our environment 

both natural and social, the path to sustainable development can be taken and

all the benefits derived. </p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-024#34:2> <h> <bold> WORLD ENVIRONMENT DAY FEATURES </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-024#35:2> BY SHANE CAVE </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-024#36:2> Humans generate more energy by burning wood for cooking and heat

<l> ing than they generate from hydro-electric dams and nuclear fuel plants.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#37:2> But, according to UNEP estimates, the current rate of deforestation

means that, by the year 2000, about 2.7 billion people will be short of the

fuel they need to cook their daily meal. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-024#38:2> Every year, people clear and burn the trees of an area half the

size of The Netherlands  22,000 km squared  so they can cook their food and

heat their homes.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#39:2> Much of the wood is made into charcoal, because charcoal is light,

burns hot but slowly and is easily broken up into manage <l> able, burnable

lumps.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#40:2> But making charcoal wastes 70 percent of the energy of the wood, even

before it has been dug out from the ovens in which it is made.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#41:2> Even when the wood is burnt directly because the wood is in big

lumps, is wet or is burnt inefficiently on an open fire or in an old stove.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-024#42:2> Trees are actually a renewable energy source; they are simply

stores of solar energy, because it is sunlight that allows them to

photosynthesize and grow.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#43:2> But the people who depend on trees for their daily needs are burning

wood faster than it can grow.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#44:2> Between 30 and 40 percent of the world's 5 billion people are depen

<l> dent on wood for cooking and heat <l> ing.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#45:2> In 1980 1.3 billion people were already short of fuel-wood; by the

end of the century, 2.7 billion people will be short of firewood. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-024#46:2> This rate of fuel consumption might, at one time, have been

sustain <l> able for fewer people.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#47:2> Now, the land on which the wood once grew is not left for regrowth,

but is used for building and farming, or is simply too fragile to recover,

for example, in places such as Somalia and Amazonia.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#48:2> But the people who use this wood do not mean to destroy their

homelands; they are simply try <l> ing to survive.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#49:2> They have no choice but to burn the wood. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-024#50:2> Some of these people, those who are on the edge of poverty, can

use wood more efficiently, by using better designed stoves, letting their

wood dry and cutting it into smaller pieces.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#51:2> It is the fact that these people can reduce the wood they use, and so

reduce their impact on their environ <l> ment, that illustrates the cost of

pover <l> ty.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#52:2> Many more people either do not have access to or cannot afford an

efficient stove and, especially in Africa, the women spend so long walking to

find wood that they do not have the slightest chance of building up a reserve

of wood to dry.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#53:2> They are too exhausted to collect more than a few <w> days' </w> wood

at best, and certainly have no time to collect extra wood.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#54:2> So they use what they can, as they find it.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#55:2> They very rarely have the nec <l> essary tools to cut the wood up

into small pieces so that it dries out more quickly and burns more

efficiently. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-024#56:2> <h> <bold> The wood stove option </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-024#57:2> Steven Karekezi, Executive Secretary of the Foundation for Wood

Stove Dissemination, points out that the efficient wood stoves now being used

increasingly throughout Asia and Africa can reduce charcoal use by up to 50

percent.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#58:2> The improvements in efficiency result from: controlling the air flow

to the fuel; insulating the side of the stove to minimize waste heat; and

using small pieces of dry wood or charcoal.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#59:2> Karekezi empha <l> sizes that these are not laboratory fig <l> ures,

but that they are the gains that can be made in everyday use.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#60:2> The stoves use simple designs, and are easily manufactured by trained

local craftsmen throughout Africa and Asia; however, the necessary materi <l>

als are required, and enough people with money to buy them.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#61:2> The basic materials are thin sheet metal and a clay suitable for

firing. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-024#62:2> The idea to develop and use more efficient woodstoves was initiat

<l> ed by Mahatma Gandhi in the 1950s.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#63:2> Gandhi wanted to remove wood smoke from Indian household kitchens,

and so improve the health of women and children.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#64:2> Wood smoke that is trapped in the cooking places of the poor causes

considerable lung damage.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#65:2> Gandhi's improved wood stove movement was reinvigorated and given

global significance with the energy crises of the 1970s, when the

contribution of biomass ( mostly wood, but including dried animal dung) to

national energy consumption began to be quantified.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#66:2> Biomass combustion in Asia and Africa is cur <l> rently estimated to

contribute, on average, 35 percent of the energy used annually, but in some

countries it makes up much more than that. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-024#67:2> With the importance of biomass in these development countries,

the expense of fossil fuels drove people to further.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#68:2> The global significance of deforestation was also recognized; wood

burning contributed to the greenhouse effect, and tree felling reduced the

planet's capacity to reab <l> sorb the carbon dioxide given off by combustion

and also exposed the top solid to greater erosion. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-024#69:2> Furthermore, there has been a growing awareness of the sheer

scale of the health risk posed by wood smoke inhalation.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#70:2> In 1987, the World Health Organization declared that res <l> piratory

diseases were the principle cause of death among children younger than five,

with kitchen wood smoke being one of the major causes of respiratory disease.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-024#71:2> Improving the access of the poor in Africa and Asia to these

simple, but effective, stoves will be a crucial factor in the fight to

improve the qual <l> ity of their lives and their environments. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-024#78:3> <h> Poverty and the Environment: Challenges and Solutions </h>

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-024#79:3> <bold> ONE of the many challenges facing every developing nation

is the inevitable trade-off between economic development and concerns for the

natural environment.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#80:3> Policymakers in these countries have the unenviable task of

formulating and ensuring implementation of development policies which must

take several factors into consideration.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#81:3> It is well recognised that the process of industrialisation is one

route to overcome or reduce poverty.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#82:3> In the past, much emphasis has been placed on the economic aspects,

such as Gross Domestic Product as it was felt these were important indicators

of the level of development and that they would provide the means whereby

social issues such as poverty could be addressed. </bold> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-024#83:3> It has become more and more evident, however, that issues such as

environmental protection, future viability of species, ( including human

beings) and poverty cannot be divorced from the economic development

pursuits. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-024#84:3> Poverty may be defined as lack of resources both material and

spiritual.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#85:3> One of the consequences of poverty is ignorance and this can have

devastating effects on the environment.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#86:3> Many people who are poor are nor aware of the effects of their

actions on the environment and consequently have not made the connection

between degradation of the environment and future problems to be experienced

by them.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#87:3> Indeed, many who are rich are also unaware.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#88:3> This is amply illustrated by the wanton cutting of trees by some

persons, which they burn for charcoal, or as clearance of plots to provide

farm land.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#89:3> These activities are conducted to generate income and to provide a

livelihood for the individual and his family in the short term.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#90:3> The soil erosion which results from the removal of the trees and the

future effect on rainfall are not considered in the immediacy of providing

for short term needs.
<ICE-JA:W2B-024#91:3> This then points out the need for increasing public

awareness/education of the majority of the people so that the consequences of

their action today can be fully understood. </p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-013#1:1> <h> <bold> The aesthetics of <it> N&eacute;gritude </it> : a

metaphor for liberation </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-013#2:1> <it> Rex Nettleford </it> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-013#3:1> <h> <bold> <it> Introduction </it> </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-013#4:1> The very title of this chapter betrays the problem of black

existence as part of the human condition in the lopsided world of today.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#5:1> The notion of black aesthetics, as against a supposedly white

aesthetics, comes out of the con <l> ceptual configurations of a white world

that is used to conquest, domina <l> tion, and superordinate status with

Europe as Conqueror, Overlord and Mother Superior while the rest remain at

the bottom of a power pyramid on the basis of certain laws of nature and the

Law of God. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-013#6:1> The idea of black aesthetics is very much a European invention,

part of a power- apparatus devised to keep large masses of humanity in

subjuga <l> tion and to deny to them any innate sense of order, interior

logic or consistency in all the varied acts of intelligence that are the

product of the collective or individual intellect and imagination of those

people.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#7:1> Such preferred patternings of the black experience as are deliberately

misrepre <l> sented and discounted, have pushed the blacks ( in Africa and

the diaspora) into dialectical defence, for survival and beyond. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-013#8:1> Africans have long turned the weapons of their denigration against

their oppressors and so to many people of African ancestry, black aesthetics

takes on positive meaning in the fight against such denigration.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#9:1> Yet one can be sucked into arguments that are shaped by European

experience about self-worth and about the credit worthiness of this or that

work of art created out of the experience, world-view(s), or ontological

configurations of people of African ancestry. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-013#10:1> The temptation to be sucked in is indeed great especially when

the black man functions in an environment of power over which he has no

control.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#11:1> Hence the adoption of the terms and attitudes of a Eurocentric

tradition of aesthetic description, evaluation and criticism.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#12:1> Thus the search for the irreducible kernel of the African experience,

wherever it was found, was conducted using non-African concepts. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-013#13:1> <h> <bold> <it> The origins of </it> N&eacute;gritude </it>

</bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-013#14:1> It was from an exiled African, Aim&eacute; C&eacute;saire of

Martinique, <fnr> <sp> 1 </sp> </fnr> that the world received the gift of a

new and resonant designation for that quest by blacks for the ideal, form and

purpose they knew to reside in their African heritage. <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#15:1> 1 Aim&eacute; C&eacute;saire was born in Martinique in June 1911, and

educated in Paris, France, where he met L&eacute;on Damas of French Guiana

and L&eacute;opold S&euml;dar Senghor of Senegal.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#16:1> Together they became the <quote> 'legendary founders of the Black

Awareness Movement known as N&eacute;gritude' </quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#17:1> A foremost thinker of the twentieth century's Black Consciousness

Movement, C&eacute;saire is a practising politician in his native Martinique

besides being an acclaimed poet, playwright, literary critic, and

philosopher. </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#18:1> It came at a time when that heritage was facing the threat of

enfeeblement from assimilationism in the case of the Francophones and from

counterpart afflictions which had been the target of assault by men like

Marcus Garvey and William Du Bois among the Anglophones. <fnr> <sp> 2 </sp>

</fnr> <footnote>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-013#19:1> 2 Francophone black awareness had its forerunner in ( a) the

Garvey Movement led by Jamaica visionary and activist, Marcus Mosiah Garvey,

founder of the Universal Negro Improvement Association ( UNIA), who worked

out of Harlem and influenced liberation movements among blacks all over the

Americas and in colonial Africa itself, and ( b) the Harlem Renaissance in

which the scholar and a leading intellectual William Du Bois was a major

figure.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#20:1> Dr Du Bois is considered one of the fathers of Pan- Africanism, a

movement that saw Africans and peoples of African ancestry outside the

African continent joining forces for effective political action to fight

racism and African denigration. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-013#21:1> See Robert Hill ( ed.) <it> The Marcus Garvey ( and UNIA) Papers

</it> ( UCLA Press, 1983); Rupert Lewis, <it> Marcus Garvey: Anti-colonial

Champion </it> ( London, Karia Press, 1987); Rex Nettleford, <quote> 'Garvey's

Legacy: Some Perspectives' </quote> in Rupert Lewis and Patrick Bryan ( eds)

<it> Garvey; His Work and Impact </it> ( Mona, 1988) being proceedings of the

Symposium on Marcus Garvey held at the University of the West Indies in

November 1987; Amy Jacques Garvey, <it> Garvey and Garveyism </it> (

Kingston, A.J. Garvey, 1963); A.J. Garvey ( ed.), <it> Philosophy and

Opinions of Marcus Garvey </it> ( New York, Atheneum, 1969); W.E. Du Bois,

<it> Dusk of Dawn: an essay toward an autobiography of a race concept </it> (

N.Y. Har <l> court, Brace and World, 1940) and <it> Souls of Black Folk </it>

( N.Y. Fawcett, 1965); and Eliot N. Rudwick, <it> W.E.B. Du Bois: a study in

minority  group leadership </it> , ( Philadelphia, University of

Pennsylvania Press, 1960). </p> </footnote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-013#22:1> Then thirty years ago L&eacute;opold Senghor, one of the founding

apostles of this grand attempt at the rejection of black humiliation, offered

a seminal essay entitled <quote> 'African-Negro Aesthetics' </quote> . <fnr>

<sp> 3 </sp> </fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#23:1> 3 Senghor, L&eacute;opold S., <quote> 'African-Negro Aesthetics'

</quote> , in <it> The Ideology of Blackness </it> ed. with introduction by

Rayond Betts ( Lexington, Massachusetts, D.C. Heath & Co., 1971), pp. 110-25

and reprinted from <it> Diogenes </it> No.16 ( Winter 1956) pp. 23- 38.

</footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#24:1> The essay could have been easily dismissed as an example of

Eurocentric aesthetics. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-013#25:1> Indeed, 13 years later a younger generation found a spirited

spokes <l> man in the person of one Stanislas Adotevi <fnr> <sp> 4 </sp>

</fnr> who at the Pan-African Cultural Festival held in Algiers in 1969 took

not just the aesthetics of N&eacute;gritude but all of N&eacute;gritude to

task for being too fey to deal seriously with the harsh reality of armed

struggle and the technological modernisation of a slothful African society.

<footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#26:1> 4 Adotevi, Stanislas, <quote> 'The Strategy of Culture' </quote> in

<it> The Ideology of Blackness </it> ed. with Introduction by Raymond Betts (

Lexington, Massachusetts, D.C. Heath & Co., 1971), pp. 186-96, and reprinted

from <it> The Black Scholar </it> , 1 November 1969. Dr Adotevi, who at the

time of writing his essay was Commissioner General for Culture and Youth in

Dahomey ( later Benin), presented his Paper at the Pan-African Cultural

festival, held in Algiers in the summer of 1969.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#27:1> That festival was reportedly organized as a rejoinder to the Festival

of Negro Arts held in Dakar in 1966, in celebration of N&eacute;gritude.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#28:1> The Adotevi contribution was enthusiastically received as one of the

more forceful interventions in the conference which sought to discredit

N&eacute;gritude as a meaningful ideology for modern Africa.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#29:1> Nathan Hare reported on the Algiers Conference in <it> Black Scholar

</it> 1 ( 1969) pp. 2-10. </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#30:1> Both of these duties had by then become to many the only meaningful

ones in response to the very humiliation that N&eacute;gritude had sought to

address but failed to destroy.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#31:1> Adotevi, with the anguish and anger of youthful impatience and the

thirst for less poetic resistance against an affliction of ages, declared

<quote> 'Today this humiliation is still apparent and the problem posed by

N&eacute;gritude remains.' </quote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#32:1> Literary assertions of <quote> 'the values which distinguish the

Black world from that of the white man,' </quote> Adotevi graciously

acknowledged as <quote> 'the first moment of present-day requirements& one of

the possible forms for the struggle of emancipation, especially in the realm

of artistic creation.' </quote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#33:1> To this young and ardent critic N&eacute;gritude even represented the

<quote> 'courage& needed to dare protest against the humiliation of the

thirties' </quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#34:1> But the new generation, according to Adotevi, found this wanting.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#35:1> And in the spirit of the time when Black Power and the ideas of Fanon

were in the ascendancy, N&eacute;gritude was all but replaced by what the

critic called <quote> 'Melanism' </quote> . <fnr> <sp> 5 </sp> </fnr>

<footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#36:1> 5 The doctrine of Melanism was the alternative offered by Stanislas

Adotevi to N&eacute;gritude.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#37:1> This brand of <quote> 'black consciousness' </quote> ( from Melanism

meaning darkness of colour deemed to result from <quote> 'abnormal

development of black pigment in epidermis' </quote> etc.) was seen by Adotevi

as a source of energy for <quote> 'unceasingly renewed action' </quote> as

against the literary indulgences of N&eacute;gritude.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#38:1> Melanism was indeed to act as a <quote> 'cementing force and thought

which, operating in the perspective of unity, reacts on particular

sensibilities as do Margrabism and Arabism' </quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#39:1> Adotevi elaborates on the meaning of the term as follows: <X> <quote> '

1. &It is not a new racialism but an identification.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#X40:1> It is an affirmation of the plain fact that to be Negro today is

still to live through the violent degradations of the slave trade.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#X41:1> 2. Melanism is the acceptance of a state of war, but with arms other

than prayers and Negro Spirituals ( it being) <quote> " an act of humanity to

take up arms in the defence of a people for whom arms are the only

resource"</quote> [ as Machiavelli said] and 3. Melanism will be the unique

resource of a people who can no longer decorate their torture with trophies

conquered from shame&
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#X42:1> We must give the lie to negation by assigning positive tasks to each

Negro'</quote>.</X> See Adotevi, S: <it> op. cit. </it> ( 1971), page 192.

</footnote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-013#X43:1> At the heart of this stirring missionary statement was a sense

of history, with its chronic humiliation starting with the slave trade to be

avenged by the recognition of a state of war seeking affirmation in positive

action.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#X44:1> For the immediate task, according to the young rebels, was not to

decorate the torture of history <quote> 'with trophies conquered from shame'

</quote> - in reference, no doubt, to N&eacute;gritude's aesthetically

acceptable literary expres <l> sions in the torturer's lexicon and complete

with Eurocentric notions of the perfection of line, harmony, metric logic,

truth and beauty.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#X45:1> The task rather was to address such issues as the methods of

modernization, the liberation of Africa from the old structures of the past,

the rational use of technology, the attainment of equality with African

societies, the allocation of place and human purpose to black women and the

young in society, a realistic approach to tribalism, and getting into

perspective the role of traditional religions in modern African society.

<fnr> <sp> 6 </sp> <fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#X46:1> 6 Adotevi, S: <it> op. cit. </it> ( 1971), page 189. </footnote>

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-013#X47:1> Beside such harsh evidence of existential reality in the late

sixties, the primary involvement of N&eacute;gritude with its literary

concerns, aesthetic sen <l> sibilities, the characteristics of a poem, dance,

song or carving seemed irrelevant and insipid.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#X48:1> <quote> ' N&eacute;gritude, hollow, vague and inefficient is an

ideology' </quote> insisted Adotevi. <quote> 'There is no further place in

Africa for literature other than that of revolutionary combat,' </quote> he

declared. <quote> ' N&eacute;gritude is dead.' </quote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#X49:1> The literary indulgences of N&eacute;gritude were too far divorced

from the ordinary activities of humiliated Africans and from what Adotevi

referred to as <quote> 'the daily problems which must be solved immediately'

</quote> . </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-013#X50:1> <h> <bold> <it> A search for essences </it> </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-013#X51:1> Yet part of Senghor's claims for the aesthetics of

N&eacute;gritude was that <quote> 'literature and art ( to the Africans) are

not separable from men's generic activities, particularly from the artisan's

techniques.' </quote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#X52:1> The functionality of art, with Man at the centre of the process of

creation, affirms Man's <quote> 'individual essence' </quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#X53:1> But African literature and art are also collective, taking on

meaning through the participation of the <quote> 'anonymous masses who sing,

dance, carve and paint' </quote> . By their very nature, functionality and

collectivity in turn commit the persons involved both as individuals and as

members of the community, precisely because, according to Senghor, lit <l>

erature and art for the Africans <quote> 'are techniques of essentialisation'

</quote> . <fnr> <sp> 8 </sp> </fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#X54:1> 8 Senghor, L&eacute;opold, <it> op. cit. </it> ( 1971), page 114-15.

</footnote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-013#X55:1> In this sense N&eacute;gritude is a search for essences.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#X56:1> The now classic dismissal of the movement as an exercise in futility

since the <quote> 'tiger does not concern itself with its tigritude' </quote>

<fnr> <sp> 9 </sp> </fnr> raises questions about the black man's own claim to

full and unfettered membership in the human race. <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#X57:1> 9 The pun is attributed to the poet-playwright and Noble-Laureate

Wole Soyinka of Nigeria. </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#X58:1> For his irresist <l> ible claim lies in the fact that he, as Man, is

able to <it> think </it> and follow through to action on the basis of his

thought(s).
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#X59:1> To equate him with a tiger in any literal sense is to fall prey to

assumptions about the black man's legendary location outside the very species

that sets great store by the gift of culture in the creative exercise of his

imagination and intellect  something report <l> edly not within the reach of

living organisms such as tigers.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#X60:1> The tiger is not concerned about his tigritude indeed.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#X61:1> But that may well by why Man is able to cage him and not he Man.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#X62:1> And when Man attempts to deprive the tiger of his natural habitat,

the tiger fights back, which may well be that animal's way of asserting his

tigritude.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#X63:1> Well, if with the tiger, how much more with the human being!
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#X64:1> Deprivation of self and society has never failed to attract

resistance from, and assertion of self-preservation by, the human being.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#X65:1> Man is by nature concerned with the essence of himself: it makes

sense that men like C&eacute;saire, Senghor and L&eacute;on Damas approached

their liberation from this angle.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#X66:1> The Gallic concerns with things ontological were part of the

apparatus of assimilationist education meted out to French-speaking Africans

to whom the search for essences came more naturally than to their Anglophone

counterparts, whose Anglo-Saxon education often eschews such essays into what

Edmund Burke, the English philosopher, might well have dismissed as

metaphysical nonsenses. <fnr> <sp> 10 </sp> </fnr> </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#X67:1> 10 Attributed to the English philosopher, Edmund Burke, in his

objections to the natural-rights claims made by protagonists of the French

Revolution.
<ICE-JA:W2A-013#X68:1> See Burke's <quote> 'Reflections on the Revolution in France'

</quote> in <it> Durham University Journal </it> , Vol. XIV, No. 3, June

1953, and Charles Parkin <it> The Moral Basis of Burke's Political Thought

</it> ( Cambridge, Cambridge University Press, 1956), Chapter II. </footnote>

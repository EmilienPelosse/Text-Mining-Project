<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-038#1:1> <h> <bold> Glycine is Not Formed through the Amino Transferase

Reaction in Human or Rat Placenta </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-038#2:1> <bold> F. I. Bennett <fnr> <sp> a </sp> </fnr> and A. A. Jackson

<fnr> <sp> b </sp> </fnr> <sp> , c </sp> <footnote>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-038#5:1> Paper accepted 7 November 1997 </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-038#6:1> The fetus has a substantial demand for glycine, which is satisfied

in part by placental formation.
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#7:1> The ability to form glycine through the activity of alanine :

glyoxylate aminotransferase enzyme was measured in placentae from normal term

human pregnancies and placentae from rats at day 20 of gestation.
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#8:1> There was no detectable enzyme activity in either human or rat

placentae, although activity was measured in rat liver.
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#9:1> It is concluded that in the placenta glycine is only formed from

serine through the activity or serine hydroxymethyl transferase enzyme, which

uses folate as a cofactor, because there are no other known metabolic

pathways for endogenous glycine production. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-038#12:1> <h> <bold> INTRODUCTION </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-038#13:1> The fetal demand for amino acids is high for net tissue

deposition and as an oxidizable substrate to provide energy.
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#14:1> The demands for individual amino acids are uneven with the highest

fetal demand being for glycine ( Widdowson, Southgate and Hey, 1979; Jackson,

1991).
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#15:1> Glycine, the simplest amino acid, is a component of most proteins,

but is also needed as a building block for the biosynthesis of a range of

more complex molecules and plays an important role in the development and

function of the central nervous system ( Jackson, 1991).
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#16:1> In adequately nourished individuals on a normal dietary intake, up to

90 per cent of the requirement for glycine might be met by endogenous

formation ( Neuberger, 1981), and therefore it has been considered to be a

dietarily non-essential amino acid.
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#17:1> However, with the increased demands of pregnancy or growth during

neonatal life, the availability of the amino acid is constrained and it

becomes conditionally essential ( Jackson et al., 1997c,d). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-038#18:1> The fetus might obtain its glycine from the mother, by formation

in the placenta, or its own production.
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#19:1> Maternal provision only satisfies about 10 per cent of the need of

the human fetus ( Cetin et al., 1995) and, based upon sheep studies, the

formation of glycine by the placenta appears to play a major role ( Geddie et

al., 1996).
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#20:1> Serine is taken from the maternal circulation, and also from fetal

liver, as the precursor for placental glycine formation and thus, the pathway

for glycine synthesis is presumed to be through the serine hydroxymethyl

transferase reaction ( SHMT), which uses folate as a cofactor ( Neuberger,

1981, Stover and Schirch, 1990).
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#21:1> However, there is a possible alternative pathway for glycine

formation, through the enzyme alanine glyoxylate transaminase ( AGT, <it> EC

2.6.1.44 </it> ) in which glycine is produced by transamination of glyoxylate

with either alanine or glutamic acid ( Noguchi and Takada, 1979).
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#22:1> The placenta has a net consumption of glutamic acid which might

provide the amino group for glycine formation. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-038#23:1> The present study was undertaken to identify the activity of AGT

in human and rat placenta in order to determine whether this pathway could

contribute to the additional glycine required by the fetus. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-038#24:1> <h> <bold> MATERIALS AND METHODS </bold> </h> </p>

research purposes.
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#27:1> A research assistant attended the delivery, collected and processed

the placenta as soon as it was delivered.
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#28:1> A sample was taken and immedialtely placed on ice.
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#29:1> The sample was stored at 4 &degree; C and analyses performed within

24 h.
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#30:1> Rat dams had received a formula diet based on casein ( 120g/kg) from

2 weeks before becoming pregnant and for the duration of pregnancy. <O>

<ICE-JA:W2A-038#33:1> For each tissue a known weight of approximately 10 mg was taken and

<ICE-JA:W2A-038#35:1> The supernatant was analysed for enzyme activity and protein content.

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-038#48:1> <h> <bold> DISCUSSION </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-038#49:1> Glycine is required in large amounts for normal growth ( Jackson,

1991).
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#50:1> Although the fetal liver may make some contribution ( Moores et al.,

1994), delivery of adequate amounts from the placenta is considered to be the

most important source for normal fetal development.
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#51:1> In the human, placental uptake of glycine from the maternal

circulation contributes less than 10 per cent to the flow of glycine in the

umbilical vein and the transporter systems for glycine are much less active

than those for leucine, for example ( Cetin et al., 1995); rather, serine is

taken up by the placenta from the maternal circulation in relatively large

amounts, and most of this is released into the umbilical vein as glycine (

Geddie et al., 1996).
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#52:1> Based on this evidence it has been concluded that the placental

formation of glycine from serine, derived either from the maternal

circulation or the fetal liver itself, is the major source of glycine for the

fetus ( Meschia, 1997).
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#53:1> In the present study we have shown that the alternative pathway for

glycine formation by transamination does not take place in either the rat

placenta at 20 days gestation or the term human placenta.
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#54:1> From this we conclude that the formation of glycine from serine

through the pathway catalysed by SHMT is the most important, if not only,

pathway for glycine formation in the placenta ( Neuberger, 1981). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-038#55:1> SHMT requires folate as a co-factor, and in rats experimen <l>

tal folate deficiency reduces the interconversion of serine and glycine by 50

per cent ( Arnstein and Stankovic, 1956).
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#56:1> There <l> fore, it can be presumed that if maternal folate status is

marginal or inadequate the placental synthesis of glycine will be

constrained.
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#57:1> Periconceptual folate status has been associated with neural tube

defects ( MRC Vitamin Study Research Group, 1991) and is linked directly with

fetal development ( Shojania, 1984).
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#58:1> Poor folate status during pregnancy has been associated with an

increased prevalence of low birth weight babies ( Baumslag, Edelstein and

Metz, 1970, Shojania, 1984, Tamura et al., 1992).
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#59:1> In infants with intrauterine growth retardation, the activity of the

placental system A transporter, which carries glycine and serine, is reduced

so that both direct glycine transfer, and the glycine endogenously produced

from serine are reduced ( Dicke et al., 1993; Mahendran et al., 1993). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-038#60:1> We have used the urinary excretion of 5- <smallcaps> L

</smallcaps> - oxoproline as a marker for glycine status.
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#61:1> In pregnancies where glycine status is poorer, the offspring tend to

be smaller ( Jackson et al., 1997d).
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#62:1> It appears that mothers with poor folate status tend to have children

with poor glycine status ( Jackson et al., 1997a).
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#63:1> As glycine status is itself related to vascular development ( Sherman

et al., 1998), and has been associated directly with the development of

maturity onset diabetes mellitus ( Jackson et al., 1997b), the possibility

emerges that the ability to form adequate amounts of glycine is programmed in

utero in relation to maternal glycine status at the time.
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#64:1> If this were to be so then the factors which determine the ability of

the placenta to provide adequate amounts of glycine to the fetus need to be

explored in much greater detail. </p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-038#71:2> <it>
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#72:2> Activity guided isolation of the crude ethanol extract of the leaves

and stems of <ul> Bontia </ul> <ul> daphnoides </ul> using adult <ul> Cylas

</ul> <ul> formicarius </ul> as the test organism revealed that the most

insecticidally active compound was the sesquiterpene furan, epingaione.
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#73:2> This compound was previously isolated and characterized by Chinnock

<ul> et </ul> <ul> al. </ul> , ( 1987).
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#74:2> The 72 hour Lc <sb> 50 </sb> values ( &mu; g/insect) found for the

furan was 20.80 ( 17.34  24.52), while the values obtained for a nicotine

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-038#75:2> <h> <bold> INTRODUCTION </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-038#76:2> The long term use and misuse of conventional synthetic

insecticides ( organochlorines, organophosphates and carbamates) has created

many ecological and environmental problems ( Brown, 1978; Flickinger <it> et

al. </it> , 1986; Stromborg, 1986).
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#77:2> Chief among these problems include: induction of resistance in

several pest species ( Georghiou and Melon, 1982), toxicity to non-target

organisms including man and the natural enemies of various pest species (

Reid, 1987; Georghiou and Melon, 1982), contamination of several water bodies

( National Research Council of Canada, 1975; Williams and Chow, 1993) and

high levels of residues in agricultural food products ( Thomson and Abbott,

1967; Vettorazzi and Miles-Vettorazzi, 1975; Flickinger <it> et al. </it> ,

1986).
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#78:2> Thus, there is an urgent need to find safer pesticides for use in

present day pest management programmes.
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#79:2> One source of these safer pesticides is the secondary metabolites (

natural products) isolated from plants ( Williams, et al., 1989).
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#80:2> These compounds have demonstrated a diverse array of biological

actions on arthropod species which include disruption cytochrome P450

activities by monoterpenes ( Brattsten, 1982); retardation of corpora allata

development by the precocenes ( Bowers, 1982) and inhibition of

neuroendocrine processes, protein and lipid metabolisms by several limonoids

e.g. the azadirachtins ( Rembold <it> et al. </it> , 1984; Sieber, <it> et

al. </it> , 1984; Bidmon <it> et al. </it> , 1987; Dorn <it> et al. </it> ,

1987; Wilps, <it> et al. </it> , 1992). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-038#81:2> Williams ( 1992) demonstrated that extracts from the leaves and

stems of <it> Bontia daphnoides </it> were effective in killing <it>

Tribolium confusum </it> .
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#82:2> These findings were confirmed in a later study by Williams and

Mansingh ( 1993).
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#83:2> However, the major insecticidally active compound ( s) was not

elucidated from the extract.
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#84:2> Thus, in the present study an attempt was made in isolating and

elucidating the active compound. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-038#85:2> <h> <bold> MATERIALS AND METHODS </bold> </h>

<p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-038#86:2> <h> <bold> Preparation of crude extracts and fraction from B.

daphnoides </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-038#87:2> Leaves and stems of <it> B. daphnoides </it> were air dried in

the laboratory for 5 days.
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#88:2> Dried plant material weighing 400 g was then pulverized and extracted

with 8.0 L of 95 % ethanol for 120 hrs.
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#89:2> The resulting crude extract was then concentrated <it> in vacuo </it>

to 20 mL using a rotary evaporator.
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#90:2> To the concentrated crude extract 200 mL of hexane and 200 mL of a

methanol-water ( 10: 1; v/v) mixture were added for separating the extract

into non-polar ( hexane) and polar ( water-methanol) fractions for bioassay.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-038#91:2> <h> <bold> Separation and isolation of the active compound in the

hexane fraction </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-038#92:2> A twenty gram portion of the hexane fraction was redissolved in

hexane and separated using a solvent system of 12.0 % ethyl acetate in

petroleum ether ( 30 &degree; C to 40 &degree; C) on a flash glass column.
<ICE-JA:W2A-038#93:2> The glass column of 75.0 cm length and dia of 2.5 cm was packed with

kieselgel 60 ( particle size = 0.07 mm) and pressurized with air at 1 bar/min

during the separation process. </p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W1B-021#63:4> <h> <bold> <it> Not considered for

<p> <ICE-JA:W1B-021#64:4> Sir,</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W1B-021#65:4> If Mr. Tom Tavares-Finson had read and properly understood the

Prime Minister's letter to the Leader of the Opposition, proposing the

appointment of the Hon. R. Carl Rattray, Q.C. as President of the Court of

Appeal, he could never have made the claim, as he has, that the Prime

Minister was guilty of <quote> grave and misleading deception</quote> by

drawing an analogy between the post of President of the Court of Appeal and

that of the Lord Chancellor.</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W1B-021#66:4> The point being made quite clearly and correctly was that in the

United Kingdom ( UK) from which we take our jurisprudence and most of our

judicial traditions, previous political involvement does not debar a person

from being appoint<l>ed to high judicial.</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W1B-021#67:4> The highest judicial office in the UK is that of the Lord

Chancellor.
<ICE-JA:W1B-021#68:4> He performs not only judicial but administrative functions and he is

a Cabinet Minister and Speaker of The House of Lords.</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W1B-021#69:4> The statistics as to how often a particular Lord Chancellor sits

as Judge are irrelevant since he has a right to sit and determines how often

he does so.</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W1B-021#70:4> Lord Taylor of Gosforth, the Lord Chief Justice of England, in

the Dimbleby Lecture delivered on the November 29, 1992 stated on the matter

of selection of Judges - <quote> ultimately the Lord Chancellor is the Judge

maker.
<ICE-JA:W1B-021#71:4> He appoints the Circuit Judges and Stipendiary Magistrates; also the

Assistant Recorders and Recorders who are practising lawyers appointed to sit

part-time as Judges in less serious cases.
<ICE-JA:W1B-021#72:4> The higher judiciary is appointed upon the Lord Chancellor's

recommendation.</quote></p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W1B-021#73:4> In relation to the independence of the judiciary, he also stated

<quote> Of course the independence of the judi<l>ciary is a vital principle.
<ICE-JA:W1B-021#74:4> If judges are vulnerable to pressure or influ<l>ence, their

impartiality and therefore the justice of their decision may be at risk.
<ICE-JA:W1B-021#75:4> So security of tenure is crucial.</quote>
<ICE-JA:W1B-021#76:4> We tend to forget that the independence of judges rests not so much

upon how a judge is appoint<l>ed but rather as to how secure a judge is in

terms of protection from dismissal.</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W1B-021#77:4> The appointment of persons who have held political office to be

Judges has been exampled in the UK by the cases of Sir Reginald

Manningham-Buller, later Lord Dilhorn; Lord Hailsham ( Quinton Hogg) and most

recently Lord Havers ( Sir Michael Havers), all of whom were appointed to

high judi<l>cial office from being Attorneys General.</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W1B-021#78:4> In other parts of the Commonwealth, Sir Garfield Barwick was

appointed Chief Justice of Australia from having been Minister of Foreign

Affairs; and Nigel Bowen and Lionel Murphy were appointed to the High Court

of Australia, in each case directly from having been Attorneys General.</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W1B-021#79:4> In Canada, the Hon. Roy McMurtry who was Attorney General and

went on to become Canadian High Commissioner in London, a political

appointment, moved from that post to be appoint<l>ed a Deputy Chief Justice

of the Province of Ontario.</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W1B-021#80:4> In the other great common law jurisdiction the United States of

America, Governor Earl Warren of California moved directly from that position

country's his<l>tory  The Warren Court.</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W1B-021#81:4> In Jamaica, the Hon. Leacroft Robinson, Q.C. as Attorney General

politician.
<ICE-JA:W1B-021#82:4> He served in that office with great distinction.
<ICE-JA:W1B-021#83:4> Let us cease underrating our capacities.</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W1B-021#86:4> We would do well to endorse the requirements in those terms.</p>

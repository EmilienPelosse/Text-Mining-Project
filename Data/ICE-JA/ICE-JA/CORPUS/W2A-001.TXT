<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-001#1:1> <h> <bold> Language as an expression of identity in Dominica

</bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-001#2:1> Pauline Christie </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-001#3:1> <h> <bold> Introduction </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-001#4:1> This paper takes as its starting point the languages Dominicans

consider themselves to speak, although it is recognized that these represent

stereotypes to which actual usage does not necessarily conform.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#5:1> Indeed, Le Page ( Le Page and Tabouret-Keller 1985: 9) has pointed

out, with much justification, that the problem of defining a language is

especially real in the linguistically heterogeneous societies characteristic

of much of the Caribbean.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#6:1> Even in societies like these, however, speakers have some concept of

their language.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#7:1> Such concepts have their uses.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#8:1> They represent one means by which speakers identify themselves and are

identified by others, and they also provide a standard against which actual

usage can be judged.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#9:1> It will be shown here that even where their linguistic behavior

diverges from the stereotypes, Dominican speakers are still using language to

express their identities as members of Domini <l> can society. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-001#10:1> Consideration of contact situations has tended to focus on the

tradi <l> tionally recognized languages seen to be involved.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#11:1> These represent, histori <l> cally, not just different linguistic

structures, but also different cultures.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#12:1> Even from this standpoint, however, the fact is brought out that what

are involved are never really separate systems, since the speech of mono <l>

linguals in the relevant communities has, regardless of which of the

languages they claim to speak, features in common which are not shared with

monolingual speakers of these languages in noncontact situations ( see, for

example Weinreich 1953).
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#13:1> In extreme cases, what has been labeled code switching, defined by

Gumperz and Cook- Gumperz ( 1976: 8) as reliance on <quote> 'the meaningful

juxtaposition of what speakers must process as strings formed according to

the internal syntactic rules of two distinct systems&' </quote> , has become

the norm for informal exchanges be <l> tween bilingual speakers of comparable

social backgrounds.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#14:1> All these phenomena can be observed in Dominica. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-001#15:1> <h> <bold> Language varieties and linguistic history </bold> </h>

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-001#16:1> Traditionally, two languages have been identified as spoken in

Dominica: a lexically French Creole ( Patois) and English.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#17:1> Patois is the more highly focused.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#18:1> Its grammatical structure has been the subject of a number of

descriptions, notably by Taylor ( for example, 1977).
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#19:1> Although even from these descriptions it is evident that the

inevitable variation occurs, identi <l> fication of Patois as a distinct

variety is relatively unproblematic.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#20:1> For one thing, its lexis, being mainly derived from French, sets it

apart, at least in theory, from the other varieties in use.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#21:1> What passes for English in Dominica is relatively heterogeneous,

though less so, on the whole, than lexically English varieties spoken in some

parts of the Caribbean, for example in Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#22:1> The varieties all share with Patois features that are foreign to

generally accepted Standard English.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#23:1> They are nearly all labelled English by Dominicans.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#24:1> The one exception is a variety known to its speakers as Cockoy, which

appears to be confined to the villages of Marigot and Wesley in the northeast

of Dominica.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#25:1> Cockoy shares some of the Creole features of other Dominican

varieties, but it is characterized by the presence of other Caribbean islands

such as Antigua.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#26:1> Speakers of Cockoy all appear to control some other lexically English

variety spoken in Dominica.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#27:1> These other varieties will all be referred to as DEC ( Dominican

English Creole) in this paper. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-001#28:1> Some understanding of the contexts out of which the concept of

each language developed in Dominica is necessary to account for the current

relationship between language and identity, since choices and their impli <l>

cations derive, to a large extent, from historical facts. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-001#29:1> Patois was first brought to Dominica in the seventeenth or

eighteenth century by slaves who arrived there with French settlers from

Martinique and Guadeloupe, where French colonies had been established in

1635.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#30:1> Dominica is sandwiched between these islands, 25 miles south of Guade

<l> loupe and 30 miles north of Martinique.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#31:1> These early French settlers were mainly woodcutters who later became

small farmers, cultivating mainly tobacco and cotton.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#32:1> Coffee was introduced early in the eighteenth century, and it soon

became the most important crop, earning much wealth for the growers.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#33:1> Though denied political power after the coming of the British in

1763, the French planters remained numerically and economically strong until

well into the nineteenth century, and even then, many of the estates were

taken over by mulattos from Martinique and Guadeloupe ( Honeychurch 1984:

77). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-001#34:1> After the British assumed control, some of the slaves belonging

to the French ran away and formed Maroon settlements in the mountains, where

they were soon joined by newly arrived slaves, among others.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#35:1> These settlements became Patois-speaking enclaves.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#36:1> At the same time, on the plantations, those slaves who had stayed

remained openly loyal to their French owners.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#37:1> For them Patois became a symbol of their <quote> 'Frenchness'

</quote> . </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-001#38:1> Patois is the language acquired by the majority of Dominicans,

approximately 97 percent of whom are of African descent.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#39:1> The only significant ethnic minority is made up of descendants of the

Caribs who controlled the island before the coming of the Europeans.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#40:1> Over the centuries, the Caribs gradually abandoned their language for

Patois.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#41:1> Linguistically they are no longer distinguishable as a group.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#42:1> Their ethnic separateness, too, is threatened, since the majority of

those so labeled are of mixed ancestry. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-001#43:1> Patois remains structurally close to the vernaculars of the

neighbouring French islands and St. Lucia.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#44:1> Despite differences, many of which relate to the fact that Patois

coexists with English, whereas French is the official language of Martinique

and Guadeloupe, language provides a bond between all these islands.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#45:1> Although St. Lucia, like Dominica, is <quote> 'An <l> glophone'

</quote> , it is slightly further away than the French islands with which

Dominica has the most direct air links, for example. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-001#46:1> Patois is the traditional vehicle for oral literature, that is,

for the <foreign> <it> kont </it> </foreign> or folktales told at wakes for

the dead, for folksongs, and for proverbs.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#47:1> A link between Patois and national culture has long been recognized

by many Dominicans.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#48:1> This recognition arose out of the nostalgia for a romanticized French

past which Patois evoked.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#49:1> More recently it has become part of the new national consciousness

and has been officially encouraged more and more in recent years.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#50:1> Prizes have been offered for some time to the best tellers of

<foreign> <it> kont </it> </foreign> .
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#51:1> During the annual independence celebrations, a special day is set

aside on which everyone is urged to use only Patois.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#52:1> There are now radio programs in Patois, and the occasional

advertisement may also be heard on the radio.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#53:1> During the early 1980s, an independent orthography for Antillean

Creole, that is, the vernaculars of Martinique, Guadeloupe, St. Lucia, and

Dominica, was agreed to at a workshop held in St. Lucia, but this has not yet

been put to much use.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#54:1> A <quote> 'Creole Desk' </quote> has also been set up as part of the

Cultural Division of the Ministry of Community, Housing and Social Affairs.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#55:1> Last but not least, Dominica's motto, adopted even before its

political independence in 1978, <foreign> <it> Apr &egrave; s Bondi &eacute;

C'est la Ter </it> </foreign> , <quote> 'After God, it's the Land' </quote> ,

is in Patois. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-001#56:1> English was introduced as the official language in 1763.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#57:1> However, in addition to what has been seen as a language barrier

created by Patois, difficulty in getting from place to place in this, the

most mountainous island in the Lesser Antilles, hampered its spread.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#58:1> As late as 1946, census figures indicated that 25 percent of the

population did not speak English.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#59:1> The percentage must now be significantly smaller, but more recent

figures are not available.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#60:1> Many young people nowadays acquire some variety of DEC as a first

language. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-001#61:1> As the language of the colonial rulers and their administration,

as well as the only accepted written medium, English has been regarded as the

language of the elite.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#62:1> This prestige is shared by all lexically English varieties, with the

exception of Cockoy.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#63:1> English is seen as the key to upward social mobility.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#64:1> Parents not only actively encourage their children to speak it, but

they make a special point of addressing the younger ones in their version of

English, even if they customarily use Patois to each other and to other

adults.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#65:1> Ability to speak English is not only seen as a sign that the user is

educated; it is also taken as a mark of sophistication.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#66:1> These correlations are becoming less and less valid, but the

stereotypes persist. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-001#67:1> The origin of Cockoy can be traced to slaves of British planters

who moved to Dominica from the Leeward Islands shortly after the British

assumed control.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#68:1> Most of them settled in the parish of St. Andrew ( then La Soye), in

which the villages of Marigot and Wesley are situated.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#69:1> New ties have been formed over the years.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#70:1> Dominica was administered along with Antigua, Montserrat, and St.

Kitts-Nevis in the Leeward Islands Federation from 1871 to 1939. More

recently, during the 1960s when the airport at Melville Hall, also in the

parish of St. Andrew, was being built, workers were recruited from these

other islands.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#71:1> There is also a link through the Methodist Church, which is strong in

the Leewards and also in the Marigot-Wesley area, whereas the majority of

Dominicans are Roman Catholic. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-001#72:1> Cockoy is rarely heard outside the home nowadays.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#73:1> Many of those who do not speak it hold it up to ridicule, an attitude

which is sometimes transferred to all Dominicans who are known to have been

born in the areas with which the variety is associated.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#74:1> However, like Patois, it is preferred by its speakers for <}> <->

telliing </-> <+> telling </+> </}> jokes, for gossiping, or for exchanging

curses.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#75:1> Like Patois, too, it is a vehicle for folklore.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#76:1> The folktales, whether narrated in Patois or in Cockoy, share the

African heritage of most Dominicans. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-001#77:1> Alongside the negative attitudes to Cockoy, it must be noted

that, despite current efforts to promote Patois as the vehicle of national

culture, those Dominicans who regularly use Patois rather than English in

their daily intercourse through lack of proficiency in English are looked

upon by the population as a whole as uneducated and/or countrified.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#78:1> This negative view of Patois speakers is being reinforced by the fact

that some variety of Dominican English is now the first language of a growing

number of young Dominicans.
<ICE-JA:W2A-001#79:1> It is clear to the outsider, however, that linguistic features

associated with Patois occur more or less frequently in the speech of all

Dominicans. </p>

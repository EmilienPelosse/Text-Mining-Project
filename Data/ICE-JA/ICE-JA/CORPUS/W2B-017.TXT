<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-017#1:1> <h> <bold> Caves and cultural remains </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-017#2:1> The use of caves by the Jamaican and Caribbean Taino as

multi-purpose depositories has been recorded from the sixteenth century to

the present time for burials, for petroglyph sculptures and pictograph

murals, all with corollary mystical and religious connotations.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#3:1> Caves were also used by the Taino as sacrosanct shrines and

sanctuaries wherein were placed <quote> 'images' </quote> of supreme

magico-religious, iconographic, socio-cultural and socio-political

significance accessed only by the <it> nitaino </it> , <quote> 'the noble

ones' </quote> , specifically the <it> ca&ccedille;iques </it> ( chiefs) and

the <it> boyez/bohuti </it> ( the shamen). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-017#4:1> Within Caribbean Taino material culture assemblages of art and

craft objects of various dimensions in bone, clay, stone, ivory, wood, shell,

guannin and dor&eacute; gold ( mined gold and river gold) and other

materials, can be found sculptures and carvings of heads, figures and shapes

in anthropo <l> morphic, zoomorphic or zoological form depicting the

principal and minor deities of the Taino pantheon.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#5:1> Such images are termed generally <it> zemis </it> .
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#6:1> These simple or complex objects are often combined with a more secular

use, can be free- standing or pendant for use and display, individually or as

part of an object arrangement.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#7:1> They include <quote> 'stone collars' <quote> , ceramic containers such

as <quote> 'boat-shaped' </quote> vessels decorated with <it> adornos </it> ,

( clay sculptured images), greenstone petaloid celts, wooden <it> duhos </it>

( ceremonial seats) and stone <it> metates </it> ( elaborate grain-kneading

platforms).
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#8:1> There are also spears and spearheads of wood, stone, shell and bone;

darts, throwing-sticks and stone axes; wooden canoes and oars; wooden bowls,

wooden fire drills; bone, stone shell, wooden, coral and other beads; gold

jewellery; organic fibre containers, baskets of various types, calabash

gourds; three-pointed stones, anthropomorphic/zoomorphic/zoological statues

in multimedia; ceramic cloth- marking stamps and seals, shell masks and

stone, bone, and wooden vomiting spatulae and other objects. </p> <O>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-017#9:1> <h> <bold> Historiography </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-017#10:1> The earliest published probable depiction of a carved Jamaican

Taino wooden image is shown in a cartouche on the 1752 <smallcaps> AD

</smallcaps> West Indian map of Captain John Henry Schroeter.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#11:1> Around the border of the plan are illustrations of zemis, at least

two of which are wooden, described as <quote> 'characteristic of the

aboriginal inhabitants of the Antilles' </quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#12:1> One of these two has a <quote> 'helmeted head' </quote> on a tapered

wooden cylinder, all carved out of one piece, resembling Object A.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#13:1> The exact provenance of this cartouche remains obscure but Schroeter

served as a captain of the garrison at Fort Balcarres, Trelawny, and could

have observed such objects there.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#14:1> The cartouche also bears similarities to the earliest, unequivocably

Jamaican Taino cave-provenanced wooden zemis, described below, found about

the time of the publications of the plan. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-017#15:1> Still not fully provenanced, two wooden zemis were found some

time before May 25, 1757 <smallcaps> AD </smallcaps> , on <quote> 'a large

Jamaican estate, near the entrance of a deep cave' </quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#16:1> One was presented on that date to the British Museum by James

Theobald who described it as having been found by a friend <quote> 'some

years ago' </quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#17:1> This zemi may be the same as a small wooden carving ( 39.5 cm high)

now at the British Museum depicting a pelican and snake entwined at the mouth

under a <it> cohoba </it> bowl.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#18:1> <it> Cohoba </it> is the Arawak word for tobacco, particularly in its

powdered form as snuff.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#19:1> In the <it> cohoba </it> ceremony, the powdered tobacco in the <it>

cohoba </it> bowl was lit and the participants drew the smoke up through a

forked nose pipe called a <it> tabaco </it> .
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#20:1> This was the word the early Spanish colonizers mistakenly applied to

what we now know as tobacco.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#21:1> The second zemi may be that housed at the Museum of Primitive Art in

New York and later at the Rockefeller Foundation, Washington DC.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#22:1> It has been given a Jamaican provenance, and has a grotesque, staring

visage and male torso.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#23:1> This figure holds a <it> cohoba </it> bowl.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#24:1> Irving Rouse and Jos&eacute; Arrom have described this zemi as

depicting the Taino deity <it> Baidrama </it> .
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#25:1> Both objects are believed to be of mahagony. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-017#26:1> Three more wooden carvings were found by a land surveyor in June,

1792, in a natural dry cave near the peak of high hill called <mention> 'Spots'

</mention> in the Carpenter's Mountains of the <O> photograph </O> <O>

caption </O> old Vere parish, southern Manchester.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#27:1> They were facing the east, towards the rising sun and Hispaniola.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#28:1> On April 11, 1799, they were presented to the British Museum, where

they remain today.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#29:1> The three wooden carvings in this find consist <l> ed of a male

figure with a weeping face and outstretched legs, a human torso and the arms

bent at the sides across the lower body; an anthropomorphic figure with an

avian head and the arms and legs outstretched from a human body ( 88.8 cm),

the so-called <mention> 'Bird Man' </mention> ; and <quote> 'a

grotesque-faced weeping figure' </quote> with a flattened canopy above the

head, joined hands and stylized feet ( 37.5 cm). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-017#30:1> In July 1939, the government of Jamaica submitted a request for

the repatriation of these pieces, discovered in 1792.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#31:1> In response, the Trustees of the British Museum presented Jamaica

with plaster casts of the male figure and the Bird Man and, in error, a cast

of <quote> 'the 1757 pelican and snake carving' </quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#32:1> All three have been on display at the White Marl Arawak Museum since

it opened in 1966. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-017#33:1> Since the 1960s an attempt has been made to rediscover the

<quote> 'high hill' </quote> once known as Spots.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#34:1> In 1965 a cavern in the Carpenter's Mountains known locally as the

<quote> 'Image Cave' </quote> yielded to Dr James W. Lee, then President of

the Archaeo <l> logical Society of Jamaica, no wooden carvings, pictographs

or petroglyphs but a wooden spindle or loom weight, the sole example in

Caribbean Taino collections.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#35:1> This discovery proved that the Taino at least made use of the Image

Cave.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#36:1> The cave is located within a twenty-mile radius of the Redware

Ceramic Site Complex at Alligator Pond and the Aboukir Cave findspot itself.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#37:1> The Spots finds, like the Aboukir pieces, were very exceptionally

found all in one place and, in the context of Caribbean Taino, represent a

<O> photograph </O> <O> caption </O> unique combination of images, among the

finest achieve <l> ments known of Taino Art. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-017#38:1> A ninth Jamaican Taino wooden carving was reportedly located

within the same decade as the Aboukir find in a shallow cave immediately in

front of the present New Seville Great House gate, St Ann.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#39:1> I was told in March, 1991, that in the 1940s a child at play fell

into this cave and came out holding a small wooden figure about twelve inches

high, apparently made of lignum vitae and with a <quote> 'hideous doll-face'

</quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#40:1> It was eventually mislaid in the overseer's house at Seville.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#41:1> I had heard this story earlier from Captain Charles Cotter in 1975

and I do not doubt its veracity.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#42:1> The object has not been seen for some fifty years but it appears to

have been similar to the known pieces.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#43:1> It too was found in a cave, also within a twenty-mile radius of

Aboukir, near to the large Taino site of Maima which featured prominently in

Jamaica's proto-historic period. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-017#44:1> Thus, prior to the Aboukir find, six Taino carved wooden objects

had been discovered in Jamaica in three separate caves with heights varying

from one to three feet.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#45:1> They contain anthropomorphic, zoomorphic or zoological images similar

enough and with sufficient parallels to each other to be deemed to relate to

one culture, the Taino Nation.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#46:1> The <quote> 'Aboukir' </quote> objects are similarly linked to these

six and extend the height range from six inches to five feet six inches. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-017#47:1> <h> <bold> The Pantheon </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-017#48:1> The Jamaican ( Caribbean) Taino were pantheists whose mythology

and religion included vibrant magico-religious forces founded upon four

principal deities and a number of minor spirits, all of which were

represented and incarnated in human or semi-human form materially as zemis

made of or depicted on wood or stone.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#49:1> Animistic in manifestation, the foundation was based on ancestral and

creation stories to explain human and elemental origins.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#50:1> The stories provided a conduit to the deities for appeals and

blessings, enabling the individual to achieve a utopian afterlife. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-017#51:1> The primary male deity was god of the heavens, the elements, the

skies, volcanoes, thunder, lightning, storms and hurricanes, which word is

itself derived from the Arawak <mention> 'Huracan' </mention> , an

alternative named for this deity.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#52:1> His counterpart was the female earth goddess, his mother and

sometimes consort who controlled fertility, agriculture, flora and fauna,

valleys and bodies of water.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#53:1> The third deity was the guardian who led the dead Taino to their

heaven, <it> Coyoba </it> , in the islands to the south.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#54:1> His counterpart, also male, was a kind of benevolent uncle and father

benefactor, uncle and brother-in-law of the first deity and brother of the

second who myste <l> riously watched over the living Taino and could dispense

intercessions and blessings.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#55:1> Other important deities evolved from the ancestral and creation

stories with specific names and powers.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#56:1> The pantheon was completed by the one to ten zemis manifested in all

living things and who could be contacted by depiction.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#57:1> These depictions themselves were transformed into zemis and could

provide replacements for the flora and fauna consumed as food. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-017#58:1> Associated with religious duties and their observance were

ceremonies and rituals, incorporating <it> areitos </it> ( songs and dances

in established forms), customs and traditions relative to various illnesses,

famine, drought, prosperity, death, interment, the seasons, lunar and solar

cycles etc. and intended to express supplication or gratification.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#59:1> These included the various forms of the <it> cohoba </it> ceremony.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#60:1> The content of the rituals was related to the purpose and all were

conducted at carefully prescribed venues such as the <it> zemi bohio </it> ,

the abode of the <it> bohuti/boyez </it> , the ca&ccedille;ique's <it> bohio

</it> , the dwelling of the <quote> 'god-king/queen' </quote> who also

functioned as principal shaman.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#61:1> Other venues were particular natural places such as blue or ocean

hole on land and under water and marked boulders or caves, wet or dry,

specially associated with the principal female deity.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#62:1> Within these natural sites, and oriented usually towards the rising

sun, the principal male deity and the ancestral home, could be placed any of

an assemblage of sacred objects including duhos, the bodies of the <it>

nitaino </it> , pictographs, petroglyphs, canoes and oars, and carved

anthropomorphic, zoomorphic and zoological zemis representing the principal

and minor deities. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-017#63:1> The paramount ritual, the <it> cohoba </it> ceremony, would take

place in a venue with preparations, accoutrements and participants

appropriate to the significance of the occasion.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#64:1> The ca&ccedille;ique and <it> bohuti/boyez </it> , the principals,

would abstain from food and other bodily pleasures for days beforehand.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#65:1> Adorning themselves in full regalia, including feathered headdresses,

capes and jewellery, with bodies intricately painted primarily in red, black

and white patterns, they would use vomiting spatulae to void their stomachs.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#66:1> Entering the ceremonial area, they would place the <it> cohoba </it>

in the form of snuff in a receptacle raised above a zemi, and having lit it,

amidst appropriate <it> areitos </it> , would draw the smoke up their

nostrils directly to the brain using a <it> tabaco </it> , the forked nose

pipe.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#67:1> Hallucinogenic trances were thus induced during the ceremony which

could takes hours.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#68:1> Communication with the deities could ensue, often resulting in

prophecy. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-017#69:1> <h> <bold> Iconography </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-017#70:1> Each of the principal deities had a number of names and name

combinations, as well as associated sub-deities, relative to aspects of their

powers.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#71:1> These names have been preserved for us largely through writings in

European languages, and especially English and Spanish, so the orthographies

used here are derived from those early works.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#72:1> The principal male deity was <it> Yocahuna </it> , usually shown with

( at least stylized) male genitalia, with ( at least partially) outstretched

legs, with arms akimbo or laid to the sides or flat across the mid- torso,

often with some kind of headdress, often adorned with some clothing and

jewellery, with exaggerated eye sockets and mouths designed for insets to

seal these body openings, and sometimes also with pigmentation.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#73:1> The figure of Yocahuna could be used to dignify a composite

utilitarian piece, such as Object A, a staff of office, or a vomiting

spatula, a stone collar or a belt.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#74:1> The image could also consist of a head alone, head and torso or full

body, forming more than one image on the sides of the <quote> 'prayer-stones'

</quote> or <quote> 'three-pointed stones' </quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#75:1> Alternate names include <it> Marcoti, Yobanua-Borna, Huracan </it>

and <it> Baidrama </it> . </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-017#76:1> Sometimes Yocahuna lived in <it> Giovovava </it> , the cave of

human creation, in which he at times imprisoned himself.
<ICE-JA:W2B-017#77:1> Yocahuna's main helpers were <it> Guabanex </it> , manipulator of

wind and water, a seducer, represented by a cotton zemi; <it> Boiniael </it>

, a rain and wind god, representing the sun, a son of the master of water,

kept in a cave at Tomboyna from which the sun and moon had emerged, and <it>

Maroio </it> also a rain and wind god, representing the moon and also a son

of the master of water. </p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#1:1> <h> Foreign aid to Jamaica cut </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#2:1> By Misha Lobban </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#3:1> CANADIAN and United States aid to Jamaica is being slashed as

funds are being diverted to assist East European countries as well as

famine-ridden countries such as Bangladesh and Somalia. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#4:1> The word from the Unites States Agency for International

Development ( USAID) is that this year has seen a US$1.8 million cut in the

PL480 programme, a US$1.5 million cut in development assistance programme and

a drastic reduction by US$10 million in the Economic Support Fund. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#5:1> And the word from the Canadian International Development Agency (

CIDA) is that not only is food aid being cut: the Canadian Government is now

studying proposals for future cuts in other aid programmes as well as a

scaling down of balance-of-payments support for Jamaica. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#6:1> The cuts are set to have spiralling effects as they will speed up

the phasing out of Canadian support programmes to Jamaica such as the Bridge

Building Project, the metallic minerals support programme to the Ministry of

Mining and smaller projects. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#7:1> Peter Houliston, Counsellor and Programme Director of Development

at the Canadian High Commission, told the <it> Gleaner </it> that the planned

government development assistance budget for the next two years has been cut

by 10 per cent. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#8:1> <quote> " The economic situation in Canada like most

industrialised countries has been subjected to a recession for the past three

years and the Federal Government has been incurring for a number of years, a

large budgetary deficit which has now accumulated into a debt of Can$450

million, this means that about a third of all tax dollars goes to pay

interest on the debt." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#9:1> <h> Recession </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#10:1> This recession has affected the Government's ability to give more

to international aid and has forced the Government to embark on a series of

expenditure cuts since December.
<ICE-JA:W2C-011#11:1> This means a scaling down of programmes in countries like Jamaica.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#12:1> This is further heightened by the great demand on Canada from

African nations and other famine-ridden countries for food aid as Canada's

food aid to these countries is primarily on a humanitarian basis, whereas

food aid to Jamaica was given for balance of payments support. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#13:1> Jamaica receives balance of payments support under the CIDA

counterpart fund programme.
<ICE-JA:W2C-011#14:1> But reports are that food-aid under the CIDA programme has already

been cut this year and future agreements are uncertain. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#15:1> Mr. Houliston said prior to this, the Jamaican Government along

with the Canadian officials had been having discussions on how to consolidate

programmes into major areas as it was felt that the Canadian programmes were

to disbursed. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#16:1> Major focus would go to education and human resource development,

agriculture and environment while areas to be phased out were the bridge

building programme which CIDA has been involved in for 30 years: projects in

the metallic minerals and small projects such as the twinning of the Society

of Engineers with Canadian counterparts. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#17:1> The results of the budget cuts will be that these programmes will

be phased out more quickly, he said.
<ICE-JA:W2C-011#18:1> The phasing out of these projects is expected to be completed

sometime this year. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#19:1> The budget cuts, he said, will also impact on Jamaica's balance

of payments support from that country.
<ICE-JA:W2C-011#20:1> However, he said the feeling of the Canadian Government was that with

the required policy steps and stabilization of the dollar and an increase in

Jamaica's national reserve meant that there was a decreasing need for balance

of payment support from countries such as Canada. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#21:1> According to an official from the USAID, the development

assistance to Jamaica which has been in the form of grants used for

development projects was down this year to US$13.2 million, one of the lowest

levels compared to a total of US$15.7 last year and a little more than US$14

million in the previous year. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#22:1> The reason for the cuts in US development assistance aid, the

official said, was that countries like Eastern Europe and the newly

independent states in the former Soviet Union were being given priority in

terms of development assistance. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#23:1> This, he said, made it difficult for countries like Jamaica to

get support in this area. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#24:1> Regarding the Economic Support Fund, he added that he did not

expect more than US$5 million to Jamaica this year compared to US$15 million

last year.
<ICE-JA:W2C-011#25:1> Allocations under the ESF, the official said, was tentative as there

was no formal projection for this type of fund. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#26:1> <quote> " International foreign assistance ( from US) has not

declined but other priorities are emerging in Eastern Europe as well as

emergencies in Africa," </quote> the official said. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#27:1> This year, the projected budget for the PL480 programme under

which Jamaica imports agricultural products from the US on a special

concession to be repaid on a long-term basis of 10 years or more is US$30

million.
<ICE-JA:W2C-011#28:1> This is US$1.8 million less than last year's allocation. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#29:1> However, the official said expectations are that the allocation

under the PL480 will be increased by the US Department of Agriculture for

fiscal year 1993/94 based on the availability of funds as well as priorities.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#30:1> He said although bi-lateral aid to Jamaica from the US was

scaling down, the country was <}> <-> benefitting </-> <+> benefiting </+>

</}> from debt relief programmes under the Enterprise for the Americas

Initiative.
<ICE-JA:W2C-011#31:1> In addition, debt relief under the PL480 programme last year was

US$217 million and already the debt reduction from USAID for this year has

been US$94 million. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#32:1> This, he said, decreased the amount of international debt held by

Jamaica and also served to balance the country's need for aid in the areas

that have been slashed. </p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#33:2> <h> Charges of fraud at JADCO </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#34:2> DIRECTOR of Public Prosecutions, Glen Andrade, and

Auditor-General Adrian Strachan, have, in their investigations into the

irregularities of the accounts of the Jamaica Attraction Development Ltd., (

JADCO) found that fraud appeared to have been committed. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#35:2> This disclosure was made at yesterday's sitting of the Public

Accounts Committee when a letter from Mr. Andrade and a Special Report on

JADCO from the Auditor-General were made public. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#36:2> At the centre of the investigations is JADCO's former managing

director, Ossie Seymour, who was a People's National Party ( PNP) candidate

in the 1989 general elections. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#37:2> Appointed to the position of managing director of the company in

May 1989, Mr. Seymour was sent on leave in January 1992 by then Prime

Minister Michael Manley because of irregularities in JADCO's accounting

system and was later asked to resign. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#38:2> In a letter dated February 5, the DPP said: <quote>
<ICE-JA:W2C-011#39:2> " Based on my preliminary assessment of the Auditor-General's report

it would seem that fraud has been committed.
<ICE-JA:W2C-011#40:2> If this is substantiated by the investigations, criminal proceedings

will be instituted." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#41:2> Mr. Andrade extended a request to the PAC not to embark on an

enquiry as it had planned.
<ICE-JA:W2C-011#42:2> <quote> " In the circumstances," </quote> he said, <quote> " I am

clearly of the opinion that even though the matter is not sub judice in the

sense that criminal proceedings in the Courts have not been instituted, it

would not be in the interest of justice for the committee to embark upon an

enquiry which might prejudice the trial." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#43:2> After some debate on the request, particularly between chairman

Bruce Golding and Government member A.J. Nicholson, the Committee took the

decision not to deal with the particular issue but to examine some general

situation in the Ministry of Tourism under which JADCO falls. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#44:2> Some <quote> " irregular" </quote> transactions which came under

<}> <-> scrutinity </-> <+> scrutiny </+> </}> include Mr. Seymour's

involvement in an investment with the Bank of Credit and Commerce

International ( Overseas) ( BCCI). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#45:2> According to the Auditor-General's report, on January 18,

$150,000 was paid by JADCO to the BCCI to be placed on fixed deposit.
<ICE-JA:W2C-011#46:2> Based on correspondence seen by the Auditor-General, this transaction

was made in Mr. Seymour's name. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#47:2> In addition, the Auditor-General reported that a number of

receipts, all dated February 4, 1991 were seen at JADCO with the stamp of

Recreational Development Corporation Ltd. affixed to them and signed by Ossie

Seymour, purporting to acknowledge receipts of

$260,000. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#48:2> Mr. Strachan noted that over the period December 1989 to January

1992, other payments totalling

$369,508 were made by JADCO to Mr. Seymour, purportedly for salaries, fees,

advances, travelling expenses and chairman's expenses. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#49:2> <quote> " My calculation of the amount to which he was entitled

for the same period in respect of those expenses is

$150,700," the Auditor-General reported. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#50:2> In addition,

$150,000 was applied against the purchases of a Ford motor-car sold to JADCO

by a company in which Mr. Seymour was a principal shareholder.
<ICE-JA:W2C-011#51:2> This, it was noted, left what appeared to be an overpaid balance of

$68,808.46 at January 31, 1991. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#52:2> Included in this was an amount

$55,555 paid to Mr. Seymour on August 14 for salary in respect of the period

May 1 to August 31, 1991.
<ICE-JA:W2C-011#53:2> However, the Auditor-General said he was not satisfied that Mr.

Seymour was entitled to payment since, from correspondence seen, a salary was

not payable to him before November 1, 1991.
<ICE-JA:W2C-011#54:2> The payment was authorised by Mr. Seymour himself. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#55:2> The motor car which was purchased by JADCO was assigned full time

to Mr. Seymour and maintained by JADCO. </p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#56:3> <h> Missing guns story slated </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#57:3> ASSISTANT Commissioner Nelson Fogah has described as inaccurate a

<it> Gleaner </it> story carried last week, which said his transfer to Area 2

headquarters resulted from statements he made about missing police guns

recently. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#58:3> Mr. Fogah described the report as <quote> " most misleading"

</quote> and said it was sloppy journalism.
<ICE-JA:W2C-011#59:3> He said he had known about the transfer since the first week of

January and even welcomed it because the post was near to his hometown -

Highgate. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#60:3> On Thursday, January 28, radio reports quoted Fogah as saying

that 153 guns were missing from police stations islandwide.
<ICE-JA:W2C-011#61:3> Fogah was commissioned to conduct a physical audit of the police

firearms islandwide.
<ICE-JA:W2C-011#62:3> The statements, which Fogah said were misunderstood, turned public

attention to the police once again and there were calls for explanations from

the Commissioner of Police, Roy Thompson. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#63:3> Thompson while speaking with the <it> Gleaner </it> on January 28

said that the Assistant Commissioner was misunderstood and attempted an

explanation. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#64:3> One week later on Wednesday February 3, it was reported that

Fogah was being transferred to Area 2 Headquarters in Annotto Bay, St. Mary.
<ICE-JA:W2C-011#65:3> The transfer, the report claimed, was because of information which

Fogah had <quote> " leaked" </quote> to the media. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#66:3> Speaking with the <it> Gleaner </it> yesterday, Fogah explained

the transfer.
<ICE-JA:W2C-011#67:3> He said his was one in a chain of transfers which was recommended by

the Commissioner to the Police Services Commission. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#68:3> He would be replacing Assistant Commissioner R. Roach presently

in charge of Area 2 Headquarters.
<ICE-JA:W2C-011#69:3> Roach, he said, would be taking command of the Mobile Reserve

Division and would be replacing Assistant Commissioner Fred Evering.
<ICE-JA:W2C-011#70:3> Evering is expected to be transferred to Area 3. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#71:3> Fogah, who presently commands the Services Division, said that

the transfers were only still recommendations, because he did not know if

that have been approved by the Service Commission. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#72:3> The Assistant Commissioner also said he was not relieved of his

position as was also reported.
<ICE-JA:W2C-011#73:3> He said he was asked to concentrate fully on the audit which is

expected to be completed within two months. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#74:3> Commenting on what caused further controversy during the issue

was when Deputy Commissioner Rudyard Hamilton spoke on radio and said that

none was being transferred.
<ICE-JA:W2C-011#75:3> He did not remember about the transfers, Fogah said. </p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#76:4> <h> Flood victim mourned </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#77:4> By Deon Green </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#78:4> <h> MORANT BAY; St. Thomas: </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#79:4> THE small Church of God of Prophecy in the remote district of

Hillside, St. Thomas, was filled to capacity last Saturday afternoon, for the

funeral service for young Gilbert Brown who died two weeks ago when flood

rains caused the walls of the room in which he was sleeping to collapse on

him. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#80:4> Among the mourners who crowded in the tiny wooden structure were

Gilbert's classmates from the Hillside Primary School.
<ICE-JA:W2C-011#81:4> In hushed voices they spoke about their friend whose remains laid in

a cream-velvet casket, dressed in black pants and white tuxedo shirt. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#82:4> Outside, the skies were overcast and the intermittent drizzle

served as stark reminders of how the nine-year-old died. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-011#83:4> Leaning against a tree with a solemn look on his face was Gilbert

Brown senior, the boy's father. </p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#1:1> <h> <bold> Incorporation of rainfall into modelling for optimal

water allocation on the Rio Cobre Irrigation Scheme, Jamaica </bold> </h>

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#2:1> <bold> ALWIN L. HALES </bold> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#3:1> <it> National Irrigation Commission, 14-20 Port Royal Street,

Kingston, Jamaica </it> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#4:1> <bold> MARTIN A. BURTON </bold> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#5:1> <it> Institute of Irrigation and Development Studies, University

of Southampton, Southampton SO17 1BJ, UK </it> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#6:1> <bold> Abstract </bold>
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#7:1> A computer model has been applied to the planning and operation of the

Rio Cobre irrigation system to determine optimal water allocation strategies,

especially during periods of limited water supply.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#8:1> The model allows for the effect of rainfall by using historical

rainfall data at prescribed probability levels to predict rainfall

contribution to irrigation over a given planning horizon.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#9:1> The rainfall information together with other operational parameters

such as evapotranspiration, crop growth data, water availability and system

capacity are used to determine ways of allocation of water so that the value

of crop production is optimized.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#10:1> The operation of the scheme was simulated over an entire season to

determine optimal water allocation period by period.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#11:1> Each period the actual rainfall and irrigation depth for the previous

was used to update the soil moisture content to the beginning of the current

period.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#12:1> Historical rainfall data was then used to predict rainfall inputs

over the planning horizon which may be from one to five periods.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#13:1> The optimal water allocation for the current period was then

determined.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#14:1> For comparison, the runs were repeated with varying levels of

predicted rainfall.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#15:1> The results show that considering the effect of rainfall in

irrigation scheduling can improve effective rainfall by reducing runoff

losses. </p> <O> text </O>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#16:1> <h> <bold> INTRODUCTION </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#17:1> Computer models are being increasingly applied to the management

of irrigation schemes at the main systems level.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#18:1> The INCA model ( Bird & Makin, 1992) facilitates the processing of

scheme data with its graphical database interface while the CAMSIS model (

Burton, 1992) allows the user to choose from several water allocation

policies.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#19:1> The computer model <it> Irrigation Management and Optimisation System

</it> ( IRMOS) was developed ( Hales, 1994) to facilitate optimal water

allocation in irrigation systems.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#20:1> In 1992 the IRMOS model was applied to the Rio Cobre irrigation

scheme, Jamaica to assess the scheme's actual performance and to evaluate

simulated performance under various operational conditions.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#21:1> The objectives of this paper are to show how rainfall was

incorporated into the IRMOS model and to demonstrate the impact of rainfall

prediction on various performance indicators in the scheduling of water

allocation on the Rio Cobre scheme ( Fig. 1) during the study period. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#22:1> <h> <bold> BACKGROUND </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#23:1> <h> <bold> The Rio Cobre scheme </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#24:1> The Rio Cobre Irrigation scheme is located in southern St

Catherine, Jamaica and has a command area of 12 000 ha.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#25:1> The water is diverted from the Rio Cobre River into a canal network

for distribution to over 400 users growing a variety of crops.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#26:1> Annual rainfall in the irrigated areas averages less than 600 mm, of

which 40% falls in the months of September and October.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#27:1> Figure 2 shows actual rainfall during 1992 and average rainfall for

the Innswood Station within the Rio Cobre scheme.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#28:1> The average class A Pan evaporation is 1865 mm year <sp> - 1 </sp>

with a peak of 7 mm day <sp> - 1 </sp> in July.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#29:1> The discharge in the river is such that the water that can be

diverted into the system is generally inadequate to meet the requirements of

the crops during some months of the growing season, even during a wet year.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#30:1> The scheme grows a variety of crops including sugarcane, pasture,

vegetables and freshwater fish.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#31:1> Sugarcane is by far the predominant crop accounting for 67% of the

cultivated area.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#32:1> The study period covers the calendar year 1 January  31 December

1992.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#33:1> The diversion structure on the river was breached in May 1991,

resulting in its capacity being reduced from 6 to 3 m <sp> 3 </sp> s <sp> - 1

</sp> .
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#34:1> Consequently, during the study period, the average crop area

irrigated by the scheme was only 4200 ha. </p> <O> picture </O> <O> caption

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#35:1> <h> <bold> The IRMOS model </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#36:1> The IRMOS model is a tool for computer-aided management and

optimal water allocation in an irrigation system.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#37:1> The model has two main modules, one mainly for data management and

the other for water management and scheduling.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#38:1> The model is designed to allow optimal water allocation at the main

systems level on schemes with one or more sources of water, several tertiary

and quaternary units and complex cropping patterns. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#39:1> The data management component of the model involves the

processing of infor <l> mation that describes the physical characteristics of

the scheme, climate history, seasonal information and periodic data.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#40:1> Crop coefficient and root depth for different development stages,

yield response information, production costs and unit price are stored in the

database for each crop.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#41:1> Seasonal data describe the season, period length, planned cropping

pattern, expected water supply and other information required for preseason

planning.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#42:1> Periodic data include planting, harvesting, water application,

rainfall and evaporation. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#43:1> A schematic layout of the Rio Cobre scheme ( Fig. 1) is typical

of a scheme that can be modelled by IRMOS.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#44:1> The scheme typically comprise a main and secondary canal network that

supplies water at off-takes to Water Management Units ( WMU).
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#45:1> Each WMU may contain tertiary canals, farms and fields.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#46:1> For the purpose of the model each WMU contains a number of basic

analytical units called a plot.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#47:1> A plot has a unique soil type and cropping pattern, so that at any

point in time the plot's crop coefficient, yield sensitivity factor, root

depth and status can be determined. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#48:1> The water management module uses the information in the database

to perform the analyses necessary for planning, operating and performance

assessment of an irrigation system.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#49:1> During a normal run for planning or operation, IRMOS generally

estimates available water supply, determines for each plot the crop water

requirement and the expected input from rainfall.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#50:1> It then proceeds to allocate available water according to user

selected water allocation policy.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#51:1> Two of the water allocation policies available in IRMOS are

performance orientated.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#52:1> One allocates water to minimize crop losses during a period and the

other to maximize net benefits over a planning horizon.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#53:1> Performance assessment involves the evaluation of various performance

indicators derived from data on the actual history of the scheme over a

specified number of consecutive operational periods.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#54:1> It also allows hindsight analyses by enabling the user to assess how

the scheme would have performed if it were operated differently, e.g. if

rainfall probability or water allocation policy were different. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#55:1> <h> <bold> Performance indicators </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#56:1> The following are the definitions of some of the performance

indicators that allows IRMOS to track the performance of an irrigation

scheme: </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#57:1> <bold> Unsatisfied Demand Ratio ( UDR) </bold>
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#58:1> The UDR is defined for any instance when the water allocated is less

than demand as the ratio of the shortfall to the demand.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#59:1> If there is more than one instance of water deficit then the UDR is

computed as the ratio of the sum of all deficits to the sum of all demands.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#60:1> <bold> Over-Supply Ratio ( OSR) </bold>
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#61:1> The OSR is defined for any instance when the volume of water

allocated is higher than demand as the ratio of the volume allocated in

excess of the demand to the total volume supplied.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#62:1> If there is more than one instance of water deficit then OSR is

computed as the ratio of the sum of all excess allocations to the sum of all

allocations. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#63:1> <bold> Relative evapotranspiration </bold>
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#64:1> The relative evapotranspiration of a crop is the ratio of actual

evapotranspiration to potential evapotranspiration for a prescribed period.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#65:1> A relative evapotranspiration of unity indicates that the crop

evaporated at its potential rate throughout the period. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#66:1> <bold> Relative yield </bold>
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#67:1> The relative yield of a crop is the ratio of actual yield to the

maximum yield expected under the growing conditions.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#68:1> Doorenbos & Kassam ( 1979) proposed the following linear relationship

between relative yield and relative evapo <l> transpiration for a given

growth period: </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#69:1> <it> Yr </it> = 1 - <it> Ky </it> ( 1 - <it> Er </it> ) </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#70:1> where <it> Yr </it> is the relative yield, <it> Ky </it> is yield

sensitivity index for the growth period, and <it> Er </it> is the relative

evapotranspiration. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#71:1> <bold> Specific yield </bold>
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#72:1> The specific yield is here defined as the crop production per unit

volume of water.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#73:1> In the IRMOS model the specific yield for a water management unit is

computed as the sum of the values of all crops harvested within the unit per

million cubic metres of water allocated to the unit. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#74:1> <bold> Relative profitability </bold>
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#75:1> The relative profitability is the ratio of net value of crop

harvested within a season to the potential net value obtainable at maximum

yield. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#76:1> <bold> Effective rainfall ratio </bold>
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#77:1> The effective rainfall ratio is the fraction of total rainfall that

contributes to crop evapotranspiration. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#78:1> <h> <bold> INCORPORATING RAINFALL INTO IRMOS </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#79:1> Rainfall data are used in the modelling process in estimating

crop irrigation demand and to compute actual crop evapotranspiration.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#80:1> In either case the rainfall and evaporation data must be stored in

the database prior to running the water management module.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#81:1> The historical rainfall data are analysed to determine the periodic

rainfall depths at various probability levels ( Sooriyarachi & Lea, 1990) and

stored in that form.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#82:1> The model estimates the irrigation demand using historical data when

planning the water allocation for a given period.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#83:1> This occurs in both the pre-season planning and the in-season

operational modes.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#84:1> The model computes the actual evapotranspiration in the process of

updating the soil moisture content using actual data over the previous

period. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#85:1> <h> <bold> Estimating irrigation demand </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#86:1> The water requirement of an individual crop is estimated by

applying the procedures outlined by Doorenbos & Pruitt ( 1977).
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#87:1> The irrigation demand at an off-take for a given period is the

minimum amount of water that the scheme must allocate to ensure that all

crops within the unit commanded by the offtake evapotranspire at their

potential rate.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#88:1> In computing the irrigation demand for a period the expected rainfall

depth is determined from historical rainfall data based on the dependable

rainfall probability.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#89:1> The rainfall probability is chosen by the user depending on whether

wet, dry or average conditions are expected during the period.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#90:1> The effective rainfall is then computed and applied to the crop's

root zone.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#91:1> Any part of the crop water requirement not met by the expected

effective rainfall is the irrigation demand. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#92:1> <h> <bold> Computing actual evapotranspiration </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#93:1> The actual evapotranspiration rate ( <it> Eta </it> ) measured in

mm day <sp> - 1 </sp> is estimated by modelling the water balance within the

root zone of the crop.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#94:1> The root zone is considered as a reservoir to which water is added

through irrigation or rainfall and removed by evaporation or percolation (

Rao, 1987).
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#95:1> The actual evapotranspiration rate is equal to the potential

evapotranspiration rate ( <it> ETm </it> ) when the soil water content is

above the readily available water ( <it> RAW </it> ) level, otherwise stress

occurs and the relative evapotranspiration rate is proportional to the

moisture content of the root zone. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#96:1> The soil moisture balance model in IRMOS determines the soil

moisture content in the root zone of a crop at the end of a period, given the

soil moisture content at the start of the period, the effective rainfall and

irrigation depth.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#97:1> The depth of water ( mm) in the root zone of a crop at the end of a

given period is determined generally from: </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#98:1> <it> W <sb> e </sb> </it> = <it> W </it> <sb> 0 </sb> <it> Dr

</it> + <it> I </it> + <it> G </it> + <it> Re </it> - <it> AET </it> - <it> L

</it> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#99:1> where <it> W </it> <sb> 0 </sb> is the moisture content of the

root zone ( mm m <sp> - 1 </sp> ) at the start of the period, and <it> Dr

</it> is the rooting depth ( m).
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#100:1> The symbols <it> I </it> , <it> G </it> , <it> Re </it> and <it> AET

</it> are applied irrigation depth, ground water contribution, effective

rainfall and actual evapotranspiration ( mm), respectively, and <it> L </it>

is the depth of water ( mm) lost to runoff or percolation. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-040#101:1> The application of the above equation in the IRMOS model is

illustrated in a flow chart as presented in Fig. 3.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#102:1> The model allows for a daily or periodic update of the soil water

content, depending on available data and level of precision required.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#103:1> At each update the irrigation depth and actual effective rainfall,

are applied to the crop's root zone.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#104:1> If the total depth of water exceeds the water holding capacity of

the soil then runoff or percolation losses occur and the effective rainfall

is further reduced.
<ICE-JA:W2A-040#105:1> If none of the rainfall is effective, then some of the irrigation

water is lost and the irrigation efficiency is further reduced. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#1:1> <h> <bold> The Blue Swallowtail and other Butterflies in Rozelle

</bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#2:1> by Audette J. A. Bailey, Department of Zoology, UWI, Mona. </p>

<O> six-photographs <O>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#3:1> Jamaica is the home of 120 species of butterflies including seven

swallowtails.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#4:1> Swallowtails are among the larger and more colourful butterflies

worldwide.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#5:1> They are so named because the hindwings have extended tips called

tails.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#6:1> The Blue Swallowtail ( <it> Eurytides marcellinus </it> ) and the

Giant Swallowtail ( <it> Papilio homerus </it> ) are found only in Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#7:1> The Natural History Society of Jamaica has adopted the Blue

Swallowtail as its logo. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#8:1> <h> <bold> EARLY OBSERVERS </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#9:1> <it> Eurytides </it> was first illustrated by Sir Hans Sloane in

1725.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#10:1> Sloane you may recall, was the physician of Duke Abermale, former

Governor of Jamaica and one of our naturalists in the 1700's. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#11:1> The Natural History Society started collecting information on the

Blue Swallowtail and other butterflies of Jamaica since its inception in the

1940's and these records are published in the Natural History Notes.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#12:1> Early observers of the Blue Swallowtail included Captain D. J. N.

Walker of Up Park Camp, students of Jamaica College, and C. B. Lewis of the

Institute of Jamaica. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#13:1> In the 1940's and 50's it was not unusual to see large numbers of

<it> Eurytides </it> flying in the vicinity of Jamaica College, Half Way Tree

and Long Mountain.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#14:1> In 1953, C.B. Lewis reported seeing thousands of butterflies flying

from east to west on May 31 through to June 2. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#15:1> <h> <bold> THE ONLY KNOWN BREEDING SITE </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#16:1> During the 60's and 70's, the Blue Swallowtail seemed to have

declined dramatically though few people took notice of it.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#17:1> In the early 1980's interest in the Blue Swallowtail was revived and

the only known breeding site in Rozelle, St. Thomas was visited by members of

the Society.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#18:1> The team was led by Dr. Thomas Farr who later published the

description of the fifth stage ( instar) caterpillar in the Natural History

Notes. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#19:1> Soon after this visit, members of the society discussed the need

for a detailed study of the ecology and biology of the butterfly and

consequently a proposal was written and possible funding sources examined.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#20:1> Negotiations were also held with the managers of the Coptic Farms in

St. Thomas on the preservation of the breeding site.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#21:1> The project was shelved due to lack of manpower and financial

resources needed to implement it. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#22:1> In November 1990 when Dr. Eric Garraway and myself began a

detailed study of the Giant Swallowtail, we also set out to gather

information on the life history of as many butterfly species as possible.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#23:1> Among them was the Blue Swallowtail.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#24:1> Time did not allow us to begin our search for the Blue Swallowtail

until June 1992. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#25:1> <h> <bold> A FIELDTRIP TO ROZELLE </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#26:1> On the morning of June 17, 1992 we departed for Rozelle.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#27:1> We were accompanied by Dr. Margaret Hodges, a participant in the

previous Natural History Society trip.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#28:1> Margaret remarked that she remembered very little  but throughout

the day what appeared to be unimportant information to her, proved otherwise.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#29:1> On arriving in Rozelle, we made our way up a dirt road that led

to the more forested areas.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#30:1> The team was halted by a flash of blue, as a butterfly swooped to the

ground.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#31:1> Thinking that this might be <it> Eurytides </it> , we parked the

vehicles and went in search of the butterfly.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#32:1> We soon found the bright blue and black butterfly perched in the

road.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#33:1> It was <it> Eurytides </it> ! </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#34:1> We drove up the road for a few more minutes before we decided to

continue on foot.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#35:1> As we hiked, we searched every tree that looked like the

caterpillar's food plant, Black Lancewood ( <it> Oxandra lanceolata </it> ) .

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#36:1> We also spent some time to observe other butterfly species: <it>

Anteos maerula maerula </it> was seen drinking from recent deposits of cow

dung ( at least twelve were observed at one site) and <it> Battus polydamus

jamaicensis </it> seemed to be enjoying the nectar of Orange Sage ( <it>

Lantana camara </it> ) .
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#37:1> <it> Mestra dorcas </it> , by far the most abundant of the species in

the area glided lazily in and out of the shrubs while <it> Marpesia eleucha

pellenis </it> hovered in the distance over shrubs and trees.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#38:1> Other butterflies encountered were <it> Papilio thersites </it> and

<it> Anaea troglodyta portia. </it> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#39:1> We hiked for what seemed like forever, until the road narrowed

into a track.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#40:1> We thought of abandoning the trip but on Margaret's persistence we

continued. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#41:1> At about noon, we arrived at what seemed to be a small farm.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#42:1> An area of the forest was sealed off by a gate made from barbed wire

and thin logs.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#43:1> We had encountered such barriers before in the Rio Grande Valley,

where small farmers seal off areas of the forest for cattle rearing, so we

assumed we could let ourselves in.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#44:1> Soon after that, we came across a stand of trees which looked

familiar to Margaret and our search of all branches began in earnest; <quote> 'at

eyelevel' </quote> according to Margaret. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#45:1> <h> <bold> BLUE SWALLOWTAIL <quote> 'AT EYE LEVEL' </quote>

</bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#46:1> Success came shortly after, when the stillness of the air was

interrupted by an excited voice announcing a caterpillar find.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#47:1> Margaret had found a <it> Eurytides </it> caterpillar and we were now

certain of the foodplant.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#48:1> With more adrenaline flowing through our veins we searched branch

after branch.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#49:1> Soon we became familiar with the location of the caterpillars which

were found resting singly, <quote> 'at eyelevel' </quote> , and on the upper

surfaces of leaves or stems. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#50:1> At the end of the search we had nine caterpillars with a range of

colours.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#51:1> We found out later that there are three colour forms of the

caterpillar; thus the caterpillar is showing colour polymorphism. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#52:1> Though we were pleased that we encountered these butterflies we

were still hoping to see large numbers of Blue Swallowtail recorded in the

1940's and 50's, but this was not to be.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#53:1> On our return trip we only saw two more <it> Eurytides </it> which

stopped at a flower to feed.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#54:1> One did not escape the net of the collector and was photographed and

released. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#55:1> Continuing our descent, we sighted <it> Dynamine egaea egaea

</it> , various <it> Eurema </it> species, some blues, <it> Dione vanillae

insularis, Pyrgus oileus, Adelpha abyla, </it> more <it> Mestra dorcas </it>

and numerous tortoise beetles and fiddler beetles ( sometimes called

policeman beetle ( <it> Exopthalmus </it> spp.).
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#56:1> As we neared the end of the dirt road we caught sight of pupa and

stopped to investigate.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#57:1> We discovered 21 pupae of this kind ( three different colour forms),

green caterpillars feeding on a legume, two pale purple caterpillars feeding

on <it> Passiflora perfoliata </it> ( of the Passion fruit family), and brown

iridescent caterpillars feeding on Blue Vervain ( <it> Stachytarpheta </it>

spp.).
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#58:1> We collected all pupae and caterpillars along with larval food plant.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#59:1> <h> <bold> BACK IN THE LAB </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#60:1> Exhausted, but satisfied with our find we departed Rozelle and

headed for Kingston.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#61:1> On returning to the laboratory, we placed the caterpillars and pupae

in plastic cages which contained moist paper towel and waited patiently for

metamorphosis to occur.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#62:1> The caterpillars were fed daily and the cages cleaned. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#63:1> Five of the <it> Eurytides marcellinus </it> emerged within 7-10

days after pupation but one did not emerge until four months later.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#64:1> This adult emerged after the pupa was placed in contact with the

moist paper towel. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#65:1> All twenty-one pupae died.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#66:1> One parasitic wasp of the genus <it> Spilochalcis </it> ( Family:

Chalcidae) emerged from each of twenty pupae collected and one pupa was

preyed on by an unknown predator.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#67:1> These pupae could have been those of a large yellow butterfly, <it>

Phoebis </it> spp. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#68:1> The butterflies produced from the brown iridescent, pale purple

and green caterpillars were <it> Junonia genoveva </it> ( Buckeye), <it>

Heliconius charitonius simulator </it> ( Zebra) and <it> Anteos maerula

maerula </it> respectively. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#69:1> <h> <bold> MORE FIELD VISITS NEEDED </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#70:1> For avid and prospective butterfly observers Rozelle provides a

wide variety of species which can be found in lowland areas.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#71:1> It is also less than an hour's drive from Kingston and interested

persons need only spend half a day on a collecting/ photographing trip.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#72:1> The road to the forest though not paved, was drivable until after the

heavy rains of May 1993.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#73:1> However, the trail to the forest is very good.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#74:1> The area is very hot and dry and collectors must equip themselves

with plenty water. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#75:1> We will continue to visit Rozelle as time allows but we hope that

those interested in learning more about Jamaican butterflies and other

insects will join us on future trips. </p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#76:2> <h> <bold> The Natural History of Termites of Jamaica </bold>

</h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#77:2> <bold> by Pamela Clarke, Department of Zoology, UWI, Mona </bold>

</p> <O> photograph </O>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#78:2> <h> <bold> INTRODUCTION </bold> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#79:2> Termites are distributed mainly in the tropics with species

inhabiting forests and structures associated with human habitation.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#80:2> This chronic association has inspired numerous studies of the biology

and control of termites.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#81:2> Intensive studies of Jamaica's termites commenced at the University

of the West Indies in 1986. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#82:2> The termites ( order Isoptera) consist of five families:

Mastotermitidae, Hodotermitidae, Rhinotermitidae, Kalotermitidae and

Termitidae.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#83:2> The first four families are collectively termed lower termites, while

species of the family Termitidae are referred to as higher or advanced

termites. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#84:2> <h> <bold> THE TERMITES OF JAMAICA </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#85:2> Termites are commonly referred to as white ants and in Jamaica,

they are colloquially called duck ants or chi-chi.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#86:2> This erroneous classification of termites as ants arises from their

superficial resemblance.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#87:2> Two simple diagnostic features that may be used to distinguish

termites are 1) their soft and generally white bodies and 2) the absence of a

<quote> " waist" </quote> ( constriction between the thorax and abdomen).

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#88:2> There are seventeen species of termites in Jamaica ( see Box).
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#89:2> In contrast to many other animal groups, none of these species are

endemic to the island but also occur in other regions.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#90:2> Jamaica's termites represent three of the five families: </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#91:2> Kalotermitidae are collectively called dry <l> wood termites.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#92:2> As the name implies, they inhabit wood which provides both shelter

and food.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#93:2> These species of termites are well-known to man because they

voraciously consume domestic and commercial items containing cellulose ( eg.

furniture and wooden frames of buildings).
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#94:2> <it> C. brevis </it> is the most common pest of building timber.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#95:2> The ingested wood particles are digested by flagellated protozoa (

specific to lower termites and their ancestors).
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#96:2> The excrement of frass is released as small, dry pellets, which

filters through the holes of the wood.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#97:2> These pellets are used as indicators of the presence of drywood

termites. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#98:2> Rhinotermitidae consist of the popular subterranean and damp-wood

termites.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#99:2> Subterranean termites build nests below the surface of the ground

from which they radiate in search of food.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#100:2> They are rarely seen, but their presence can be confirmed by their

trails that provide an indispensable link with the moist underground.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#101:2> The often complicated nests have many different parts and house

large populations.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#102:2> The parent nest is the original nest containing the reproductives.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#103:2> The subsidiary nests constructed after the completion of the parent

nest are usually designated for various functions such as brood chambers and

food reserves.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#104:2> Multi- regional nests with specialized sections offers many escape

routes and enhance the resilience of subterranean termites to insecticides.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#105:2> However, interaction between nests facilitates the spread of

introduced toxic substances. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#106:2> Termitidae include the arboreal termites which also construct

large, complex multi-regional nests with intricately designed walls.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#107:2> Nests are not restricted to trees but are built on walls and roofs

of buildings.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#108:2> These nests grow to very large sizes to accommodate the continuously

increasing colony.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#109:2> The most common species is <it> N. nigriceps </it> , which is found

in diverse habitats of Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#110:2> The behaviour and social system of the family Termitidae are more

complex and highly organized than in the lower termites; consequently, they

are capable of more efficient foraging, defence of nests and regulation of

colony size. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#111:2> <h> <bold> SOCIAL AND CASTE SYSTEM </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#112:2> All termites exhibit the highest form of sociality known in

animals; the system ( termed eusociality) is characterized by the presence of

a non-reproductive caste ( <quote> " workers" </quote> , <quote> " soldiers"

</quote> and other specialists), overlapping adult generations and

cooperative brood care.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#113:2> The non- reproductive individuals develop from fertilized eggs; this

is different from the social Hymenopterans ( bees, ants, social wasps) where

the workers develop from unfertilised eggs. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#114:2> The non-reproductive castes forgo reproduction of their own;

instead they stay in the parent nest and help their mother and father to rear

additional brothers and sisters.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#115:2> This is known as reproductive altruism. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-028#116:2> Nine different castes have been identified in termite colonies,

but the full complement never occur simultaneously in a single nest.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#117:2> Immature termites can be separated into two group depending upon the

presence of wings into apterous ( wingless) and brachypterous nymphs; these

two forms are also called larvae and nymphs, respectively.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#118:2> The remaining castes are referred to as adults because of their

potential for further development and are subdivided into sterile and

reproductive individuals.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#119:2> Both groups are morphologically and physiologically adapted for

specific functions.
<ICE-JA:W2B-028#120:2> Super-specialization of castes enables greater efficiency in the

execution of jobs and ultimately enhances the success of colonies. </p> </I>

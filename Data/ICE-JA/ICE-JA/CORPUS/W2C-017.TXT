<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-017#1:1> <h> Whopping phone bills likely </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-017#2:1> <h> ...In wake of JTC system failure </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-017#3:1> By Gordon Williams </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-017#4:1> UNSUSPECTING ICAS code customers could be starting at whopping

telephone bills within the next few weeks, following a breakdown in the

protection system at the Jamaica Telephone Company ( JTC) about two months

ago, and widespread illegal use of codes. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-017#5:1> The <it> Sunday Gleaner </it> understands that during an exercise

to upgrade certain systems at the JTC, the threshold system which protects

owners of the International Call Authorisation System ( ICAS) codes from

fraudulent code use, broke down.
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#6:1> As a result, the illegal use of some codes continued without

restriction for about two months. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-017#7:1> But customers with codes, which were used illegally during that

period, may not discover the rampant abuse of their codes until their bills,

which are prepared by computer, arrive within the next few weeks.
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#8:1> The cost amassed from those illegal calls won't be known before then.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-017#9:1> The threshold system allows ICAS customers to restrict the amount

of overseas calls made by that code over any specific period.
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#10:1> For example, a customer may request two overseas calls per day.
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#11:1> The code will not work if more than two calls are made for the day.
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#12:1> Customers would then have to call the JTC to have the code

reactivated.
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#13:1> But unsuspecting customers would not necessarily know they needed to

call the JTC if the threshold system was not in service. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-017#14:1> The JTC has about 130,000 telephone customers.
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#15:1> About 90,000 own ICAS codes.
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#16:1> The <it> Sunday Gleaner </it> could not ascertain how many of those

codes were abused during the breakdown.
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#17:1> But between 1,500 and 2,000 codes have been used illegally since

1989, according to the JTC. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-017#18:1> <h> Investigation </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-017#19:1> Weeks of investigation revealed that illegal use of ICAS codes

stepped up dramatically during recent months.
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#20:1> Codes were easily available on streets all over the island, but

mainly in the Corporate Area.
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#21:1> Some codes were passed on without charge, others sold for up to

$400. <ICE-JA:W2C-017#22:1> Persons were seen with several functioning codes at one time.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-017#23:1> But the JTC has not officially acknowledged the threshold system

breakdown.
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#24:1> When the <it> Sunday Gleaner </it> contacted the JTC on July 22,

public relations manager Errol Miller said the number of illegal overseas

calls had <quote> " dropped dramatically" </quote> , since ICAS was

introduced in August 1989, and that there had been <quote> " no significant

increase this year" </quote> in the illegal use of codes as compared to last

year. </p>

<p> <quote>
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#25:1> " There is nothing unusual," </quote> he said. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-017#26:1> The company also maintains that most problems with ICAS fraud

stem from customer negligence. </p>

<p> <quote>
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#27:1> " The problems we have experienced arise primarily from customers who

have been in some way or form negligent with ICAS codes," </quote> said Mark

Johnson, vice president of the JTC's Network Fraud Control. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-017#28:1> <h> Codes sold </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-017#29:1> Earlier this month, a high level police source told the <it>

Sunday Gleaner </it> that no special police unit was currently assigned to

investigate ICAS fraud.
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#30:1> However, he added that there were reports that codes were being sold

illegally, even outside the JTC office at Duke Street. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-017#31:1> But following persistent requests for additional information,

including a check with Mr. Johnson, the <it> Sunday Gleaner </it> was told by

the JTC to submit a written list of questions.
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#32:1> That offer was rejected. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-017#33:1> On Wednesday, Mr. Johnson arranged a meeting with the <it> Sunday

Gleaner </it> for 7:30 a.m. the following day.
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#34:1> During Thursday's meeting Mr. Johnson and JTC senior public relations

officer Ann Astwood answered several questions about the ICAS system on tape

and granted permission for a reporter to take notes.
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#35:1> Everald Edwards, another JTC public relations officer, was also

present at the meeting. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-017#36:1> When told about evidence of widespread illegal use of ICAS codes

Mr. Johnson said: <quote>
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#37:1> " I'm not going to dispute that.
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#38:1> I'd be foolish to dispute that." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-017#39:1> Mr. Johnson then requested that the tape be turned off.
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#40:1> That request was granted after Mr. Johnson and Mrs. Astwood promised

to offer explanations about the recent upsurge of illegal code use.
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#41:1> For the sake of accuracy on the part of the JTC, they said, the

explanation would be written and submitted to the Sunday Gleaner later that

day.
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#42:1> Mr. Johnson then proceeded to explain the breakdown in the JTC

system. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-017#43:1> However, no written explanation was received by the <it> Sunday

Gleaner </it> on Thursday.
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#44:1> After repeated calls to the offices of both Mrs. Astwood and Mr.

Johnson, between Thursday afternoon and Friday, a bearer delivered a letter

to the Gleaner office at 3:30 p.m. Friday. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-017#45:1> The letter did not include any information about the threshold

system breakdown, as promised by Mr. Johnson and Mrs. Astwood.
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#46:1> It did state, however, that <quote> " the availability of exposed

codes becomes especially pronounced during the summer months." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-017#47:1> The JTC introduced ICAS to combat widespread problems with clip

on calls ( illegal connections).
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#48:1> The company lost approximately

$70 million for illegal calls per year before ICAS.
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#49:1> Last year, the company lost approximately

$1,2 million.
<ICE-JA:W2C-017#50:1> In its statement on ICAS fraud, the JTC called ICAS an <quote> "

effective security system which continues to work in the manner for which it

was designed." </quote> </p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-017#59:2> St. William Grant Park, once a place where one used to go for

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-017#60:2> As the old man scraped at his chin with the dull blade, his eyes

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-017#104:2> In trees, on the walls near the depressing stench of the

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#1:1> <h> Director of the Institute of Jamaica 1950-1973 </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#2:1> Thomas H Farr and Elaine Fisher </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#3:1> <bold> C </bold> harles Bernard Lewis, Jr was born in Worcester,

<}> <-> Massachussetts </-> <+> Massachusetts </+> </}> in April of 1913.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#4:1> Bernard, as he was generally known, attended public schools in

Westchester, Pennsylvania and Providence, Rhode Island.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#5:1> In 1931, he entered Brown University, also in Providence, and

graduated in 1935 with a BA degree in Biology.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#6:1> During these years he also seems to have acquired a considerable

knowledge of geology.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#7:1> In addition, he was something of an athlete and took part in track

meets, his best event being the broad jump.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#8:1> He was considered to be of Olympic calibre but was unable to take part

in the trials for the United States Olympic team that went to Berlin in 1936.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#9:1> It was during his university years that Bernard met and became a

lifelong friend of Jesse Owens, the American sensation of those memorable

games.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#10:1> After Brown University, Bernard went on to become a graduate student

and instructor at John Hopkins University in Baltimore.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#11:1> There he was awarded a Rhodes Scholarship to Oxford where he studied

from 1936 to 1939. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#12:1> In 1938, he was a member of an Oxford University Biological

Expedi <l> tion to the Cayman Islands.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#13:1> The team did much collecting and several pub <l> lications were

produced as the result of their work.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#14:1> Bernard's contribution ( with Chapman Grant) was <it> The Herpetology

of the Cayman Islands </it> , published in 1940 by the Institute of Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#15:1> An even more important event for him that year was his marriage in

October to Lucille Bodden whom he had met in Grand Cayman. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#16:1> While visiting Jamaica in 1938, Bernard contacted the Board of

Gover <l> nors of the Institute of Jamaica and presented proposals for the

setting up of a Natural History Museum.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#17:1> He must have been delighted when the Board not only accepted his

proposals but also invited him to accept the post of Curator of the Museum.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#18:1> His salary was to be &pound; 500 per annum, supplemented by some

perquisites. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#19:1> September 1, 1939, the day Adolph Hitler sent his troops into

Poland, was the day Bernard took up his new post.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#20:1> Two days later, England and France declared war on Germany; the

Second World War had officially begun. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#21:1> The Institute of Jamaica already had the nucleus of a collection

of animals and plants.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#22:1> Bernard immediately set to work to improve it, a task in which he was

greatly aided by local school <l> teachers.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#23:1> However, as the war con <l> tinued, collecting equipment, petrol,

tyres and automobile parts became scarce or entirely lacking so that field

trips had to be curtailed.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#24:1> In the mean <l> time, in spite of material and manpower shortages,

work on the construction of the Natural History Building had begun.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#25:1> It was actually completed in 1945, before the war ended in August.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#26:1> With the coming of peace, filed trips were again possible.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#27:1> Bernard spent many hours in the field but his administrative duties

increased, especially after he was appointed Director of the Institute in

1950.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#28:1> This meant that he spent most of his time <quote> 'tied to his desk'

</quote> or in committee meetings. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#29:1> Bernard Lewis had a wide interest in zoology and he was also very

knowledgeable about botany and geology.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#30:1> He had been a good choice to revive the natural history work of the

Institute and his wider interests also made him a good choice for his new

post as Director.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#31:1> He entered with enthusiasm into historical and archeo <l> logical

projects.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#32:1> He had a natural interest in painting and he put a great deal of

effort into preparing the building on North Street which was to house the

Jamaica School of Art.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#33:1> He even became adept at restoring prints himself. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#34:1> The Institute of <}> <-> Jamaic </-> <+> Jamaica </+> </}>

expanded considerably during Bernard Lewis's years as Director.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#35:1> The Arawak Museum at White Marl, the Military Museum at Up Park Camp,

the Folk Museum in Spanish Town, the former African Museum at Devon House,

and the National Library on East Street, successor to the West India

Reference Library, were all developed as new Divisions of the Institute.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#36:1> Nor did he neglect the Institute's Junior Centres on East Street and

at Half-Way-Tree.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#37:1> He was very much involved in the original planning for the

development of a museum at Port Royal and was one of the key members of the

committee that planned the Zoo at Hope.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#38:1> In connection with the Zoo, he made a trip to Belize and Costa Rica

to obtain Central American animals which would add interest to the Zoo's

collection. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#39:1> Bernard Lewis was a prolific writer.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#40:1> He contributed articles to local newspapers, <smallcaps> Jamaica

Journal </smallcaps> and local Science and Historical Society publications.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#41:1> For a while he was editor of the <it> Natural History Notes </it> of

the Jamaican Natural History Society of which he was a member.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#42:1> He was also a member of the Geological Society, the Historical

Society, the Gosse Bird Club and the Library Association.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#43:1> He served on the government Historical Monu <l> ments Committee,

Wildlife Protection Committee, Scientific Research Coun <l> cil, Jamaica

National Trust and the Jamaica National Commission for UNESCO. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#44:1> Even when he was Director of the Institute, Bernard never

hesitated when heavy furniture, boxes and books needed to be moved.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#45:1> His strength and energy aroused comment, even when he was

middle-aged.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#46:1> In fact, his energy and keenness could be disconcerting to some of

his companions when after a full day of collecting natural history specimens

they found themselves still collecting at a light trap at one o'clock in the

morning in an area such as Portland Ridge. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#47:1> It came as a great surprise to Bernard to receive an OBE in 1957,

an honour of which he was justifiably proud and for which he received many

congratu <l> lations.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#48:1> Another well-deserved honour came in 1959 when the Institute of

Jamaica awarded him a Silver Mus <l> grave Medal in recognition of his

encouragement of Arts and Science in Jamaica. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#49:1> He went on retirement leave on August 1, 1973 after thirty-four

years of service to the Institute.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#50:1> In October, 1976, he suffered a severe stroke from which he never

fully recovered.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#51:1> He and his wife Lucille moved to Tampa, Florida, where he died as a

result of a fall in September 1992. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#52:1> Throughout his life, Bernard Lewis set an outstanding example of

a man totally dedicated to his work and to the mandate of the Institute of

Jamaica: <it> For the encouragement of Art, Science and Literature. </it>

</p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#53:2> <h> CORAL REEFS AND COASTAL POLLUTION </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#54:2> Judith Mendes </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#55:2> <bold> C </bold> oral reefs are tropical, shallow water,

limestone structures that support a wide variety of marine plants and

animals.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#56:2> Reefs are unique in that they are formed by some of the animals and

plants that inhabit them.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#57:2> The group that contributes the most to reef formation is the stony

corals. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#58:2> <h> What are Corals? </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#59:2> Corals are sessile ( not mobile) and because of this they were

for a long time assumed to be plants.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#60:2> The first person to propose that they were not plants was the French

naturalist Jean Andre Peyssonel in 1723.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#61:2> His ideas were ridiculed and then rejected by the French Academy of

Sciences.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#62:2> Peys <l> sonel was so discouraged by this that he gave up all

scientific work.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#63:2> Now, of course, we know that he was right: corals are animals. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#64:2> Corals are invertebrates that belong to the phylum Cnidaria, as

do the jellyfish and the closest living relatives of corals, the sea

anemones.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#65:2> They live in colonies made up of a collection of independent but

connected coral polyps which form a thin living skin over the limestone

skeleton.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#66:2> A coral polyp is basically a simple tube topped with a mouth

surrounded by a ring of tentacles [ see 4].
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#67:2> Each polyp sits in the coral skeleton in a small cup into which it

can contract in order to protect itself.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#68:2> Polyps are small but they do vary in size with species from a

millimeter to a few centimetres in diameter. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#69:2> A remarkable feature of corals, considering the small size of

individual coral polyps, is their ability to form massive limestone

structures.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#70:2> The most famous of these is the Great Barrier Reef which stretches

over two thousand kilometres parallel to the northeast coast of Australia.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#71:2> This ability to form permanent structures is made possible by the

symbiotic association of coral <O> three-photographs </O> <O> illustration

</O> and algae.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#72:2> The algae, known as zoo <l> xanthellae, live and photosynthesize

within the lining of the coral polyps.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#73:2> The process of photosynthesis enhances the rate at which corals can

precipitate calcium from the water around them for skeleton formation and

also provides carbon products for use by the coral as food.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#74:2> In return, the coral provides the algae with a safe environment in

which to live.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#75:2> So although it appears at first to be a simple immobile organism, the

coral is, in fact, a complex association of an animal and a plant that

together form a rock. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#76:2> In Jamaica there are forty-three species of reef-building coral.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#77:2> These vary greatly in size and shape from the massive Boulder Coral,

<it> Montastrea annulayis, </it> which commonly forms heads two metres in

diameter, to the tiny Golfball Coral, <it> Favia fragum, </it> with an

average size of 2.5 centimetres.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#78:2> There are also convoluted, grooved heads of Brain Coral, <it>

Diplotia, </it> and dense thickets of branching Elkhorn and Staghorn Coral,

<it> Acropora. </it>
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#79:2> Deeper on the reef there are the delicate flat plates of the Lettuce

Coral, <it> Agaricia. </it> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#80:2> <h> Functions of Coral Reefs </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#81:2> In addition to their great beauty, which is enjoyed by scuba

divers and snorkellers, reefs have several im <l> portant ecological

functions.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#82:2> They provide a home for a vast array of other plants and animals,

including economi <l> cally important fish and shellfish, the primary

resources of Jamaica's fishing industry.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#83:2> They are the source of sand for our beaches and also serve to protect

those beaches from wave action, and subsequent erosion, by breaking the force

of the waves before they reach the shore.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#84:2> Damage to a reef affects the reef itself, the fishing and

recreational activities that rely on the reef, as well as the beaches it

protects. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#85:2> <h> Corals and Pollution </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#86:2> Corals have a low tolerance for changes in environmental

conditions and, as a result, coral reef communities are extremely susceptible

to pollution.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#87:2> The most serious threats are deposits of sediment and excess

nutrients. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#88:2> Sediment in the water comes primarily from hillsides that have

been eroded as a result of poor farming practices and deforestation.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#89:2> The eroded soil is washed down by rivers to the sea.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#90:2> Sediment has two effects on coral.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#91:2> First, when suspended in the water, it blocks sunlight from the

corals, preventing the symbiotic algae from functioning.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#92:2> The second effect occurs when sediment settles on the coral itself,

smothering and killing the delicate polyps. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#93:2> Excess nutrients in the water, primarily nitrates and phosphates

from domestic sewage effluent, enhance the growth of plant life.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#94:2> This process is known as eutrophication.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#95:2> Large blooms in the water, of microscopic plants known as

phytoplankton have the same effect on coral as sediment suspended in the

water: they block out light.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#96:2> At the same time the rapid growth of thick mats of seaweed on the

reef has the same effect as sediment settling onto the coral: it smothers it.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#97:2> <h> The Example of Hellshire </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#98:2> The damaging effects of eutrophi <l> cation and increased

sediment load in the water can be seen just south of Kingston, where there is

a prevailing westerly surface current [ see 5].
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#99:2> The reefs off Half Moon Bay in Hellshire, down current from the mouth

of Kingston Harbour are shown in plate 6.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#100:2> The water is murky, the coral is dead and overgrown by the green

filament <l> ous algae, <it> Bryopsis </it> .
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#101:2> Plate 7 shows the contrasting reef at Windward Edge, just south of

<ICE-JA:W2B-023#102:2> The water is clear and there is an abundance of live coral and

<ICE-JA:W2B-023#103:2> With the reefs that protect them damaged, the beaches of Hellshire

have also suffered.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#104:2> For example, between 1984 and 1988, Half Moon Bay lost between

fifteen and twenty metres of beach [ Hendry 1989]. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-023#105:2> The damaging effect of pollution on the environment in this part

of Jamaica is not a new phenomenon. In a 1972 article in <smallcaps> Jamaica

Journal </smallcaps> on coastal water pollution, Barry Wade described in

detail the deteriorating state of Kingston Harbour.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#106:2> He wrote, <quote> 'It is of great concern that Jamaica's coral reefs

& should not be subjected to such pollution.' </quote>
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#107:2> Twenty-one years later, sewage effluent and industrial waste

continue to pour into, and out of, Kingston Harbour and Dr Wade's concern has

become a reality.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#108:2> The problem of pollution is not confined to Kingston Harbour.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#109:2> It has spread to the reefs of Hellshire and the south coast.
<ICE-JA:W2B-023#110:2> Where will it stop? </p> </I>

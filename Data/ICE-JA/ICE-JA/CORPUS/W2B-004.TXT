<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#1:1> <h> <bold> JOHN DUNKLEY </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#2:1> <h> <bold> An Analysis of Three Paintings </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#3:1> <bold> by Kay Anderson </bold> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#4:1> <it> The National Gallery of Jamaica has paid homage to John

Dunkley in an exhibition which opened on 10 December 1991, the hundredth

anniversary of Dunkley's birth.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#5:1> Apart from a few pieces by Dunkley, the exhibition consisted of works

by twenty-seven Jamaican artists each of whom feels some special relationship

with this original and very private artist.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#6:1> A book on Dunkley's life and works is to be published by the Gallery.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#7:1> Comparatively little is known of Dunkley's life.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#8:1> As a young man he went to work in Panama where he began to paint.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#9:1> Some of his years there were spent at sea as a sailor and he saw much

of the world.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#10:1> He returned to Jamaica in his thirties, married, settled down and

opened a barber shop.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#11:1> There he continued to paint his mysterious, enigmatic landscapes and

gardens inhabited by spiders, frogs, crabs and other small creatures.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#12:1> He also carved in wood, producing unusual pieces filled with energy

and, sometimes, humour.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#13:1> His work was recognized in Jamaica and abroad; he won a medal at the

1939 New York Work's Fair for <quote> 'notable contribution to the art of the

world' </quote> and four of his paintings were exhibited in London in 1946.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#14:1> John Dunkley died in 1947. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#15:1> Kay Anderson examines three of Dunkley's paintings and opens a

pathway into his interior landscape. </it> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#16:1> Almost all of John Dunkley's paintings are landscapes, but they

are the landscapes of his own imaginary world.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#17:1> These extraordinary fantasies of mood draw the viewer into paintings

where the flora is menacing, where long, lonely roads to the edge of beyond

pass dark, truncated trees and the way ahead seems to be barred by cobwebs.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#18:1> Strange creatures hide in corners and even the least menacing of the

paintings has a sombre tone. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#19:1> The three paintings examined here were selected because,

together, they represent most of Dunkley's range. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#20:1> <h> <bold> Bustamante - The Good Shepherd </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#21:1> <it> Mixed media on hardboard, 17" x 14 ˝" ( 42.5 x 36.25 cm).

</it> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#22:1> Dated about 1940, this is not a typical Dunkley, in that it is

not simply a landscape.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#23:1> The subject matter is that of the Good Shepherd feeding his sheep.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#24:1> The focus of the painting is a portrait of Bustamante, set slightly

to left of centre, the tall figure squeezed into the rectangle of the

painting, almost forming a semi-circle. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#25:1> Immediately behind the figure is a dark road bordered sharply by

a thrusting diagonal fence.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#26:1> In the fore <l> front beyond the edge of the fence, foliage peeps out

and to the left, locked deep within the picture plane, is a dark ominous

area.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#27:1> A lamb, apparently smiling, is lying on some sort of rock in the

forefront left, immediately behind Bustamante's legs. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#28:1> Bustamante appears to be feeding a flock of sheep which fill the

centre right front and deep space of the picture plane.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#29:1> The sheep at the back seem to be on a different level and are more

widely scattered but are moving in the direction of the central human figure.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#30:1> The foreground sheep are closely packed, almost forming a triangle

facing Bustamante.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#31:1> They have eyes only for him.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#32:1> In the background left, five sheep have strayed from the flock.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#33:1> One is halfway through the fence about to plunge into the abyss in

which the second is lost, while three others race headlong down the dark

road. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#34:1> A full moon, well lit at the top and dark underneath, rises above

the crest of the slope at the top right-hand corner of the picture.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#35:1> Slightly to the left of the moon, a white bird, its wings outspread,

flies in the direction of the central figure.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#36:1> At the lower right-hand side is an example of the heavy leafy motif

which is a hallmark of Dunkley's work. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#37:1> The almost monochromatic colour of the painting, relieved only by

spots of green and red, lends a sombre cast to the picture.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#38:1> Dunkley uses lines to shape the sharp diagonals of the fence, and to

outline the features of the model and the sheep as well as to delineate the

shape of the sheep and the form of the fence.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#39:1> Lines are used to define as well as to outline.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#40:1> The modelled areas suggest volume, define the shadows around the

figure and create the moon. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#41:1> Texture is also a hallmark of Dunkley's work and in this

painting, especially in the sheep, he experiments with various textures.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#42:1> They are used to differentiate between the <w> lambs' </w> wool, the

leaves, Bustamante's clothing, facial features and hair.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#43:1> The back <l> ground is more thinly painted, almost washed or scrubbed

of paint.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#44:1> No impasto here. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#45:1> Dunkley's gift for design is obvious in this work.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#46:1> The division of the canvas is classical in its proportions, the

figure cutting the canvas into two asymmetri <l> cal sections.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#47:1> The triangular shape of the flock of sheep strengthens the import <l>

ance of the main figure, which is itself curved to fit into the canvas.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#48:1> This stance reinforces the suggestion of caring on the part of the

shepherd as he appears attentive to the needs of his flock.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#49:1> A diagonal line zigzags behind Bustamante from the lower left-hand

corner to the top right-hand corner and carries the eye across the picture

plane so that it has to focus on the central character.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#50:1> This use of the zigzag is <}> <-> characteristics </-> <+>

characteristic </+> </}> of Dunkley's compo <l> sitional technique. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#51:1> In using a prominent political activist as the central character

in a painting entitled <it> The Good Shepherd </it> , Dunkley was making a

strong political statement, for the good shepherd is one who cares for his

sheep and keeps them safe from harm.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#52:1> Dunkley's daughter, Joyce Canaan, has informed us of Dunkley's

admiration for Bustamante, and this painting testifies to the regard in which

he held the politician.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#53:1> In fact, <O> photograph </O> for thousands of Jamaicans during this

period, Bustamante was seen as the emerging leader, shepherd, deliverer.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#54:1> The symbolism of the sheep running away in the background while

another leans over the edge of a crevasse at the bottom of which a fifth is

barely discernible, speaks of those sheep which have already been led astray

and will become lost.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#55:1> The white bird possibly symbolizes the dove of peace, again another

biblical allusion, and it too is headed in Bustamante's direction. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#56:1> This painting is remarkably self-contained.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#57:1> Everything which needs to be said is said.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#58:1> It operates on many levels as portrait, as religious allusion, as

political statement.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#59:1> It is at once literal and illusionary, real and surreal.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#60:1> It is valuable from the point of view of the social history as well

as in an artistic context.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#61:1> Beautifully designed, it presents a strong visual, artistic and

social statement giving clues as to the mental processes of the artist while

at the same time being valuable to the Jamaican public. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#62:1> <h> <bold> Back to Nature </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#63:1> <it> Mixed media on plywood 16" x 28" ( 40 x 70 cm) </it> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#64:1> This landscape is typical of Dunkley's work.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#65:1> The central plane of the painting is filled by a well defined,

heart-shaped flower bed in which different varieties of flowers grow.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#66:1> Left and right of this heart-shaped bed are two others, at slightly

different angles, in which are large flowers and typically Dunkley shrubs.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#67:1> On each side stands a huge ceramic Spanish jar ( still a popular

feature in the Jamaica of that period and a reminder of the years of Spanish

occupation).
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#68:1> From the jars grow equally large leafy plants leaning towards each

other across the picture plane.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#69:1> Immediately in front of the jars are what at first appear to be

lopped off tree trunks but on closer inspection they prove to be the ends of

wooden fences.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#70:1> The four rails are made from saplings and these, together with the

final upright post, are almost all that can be seen of each fence. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#71:1> At the very front of the picture plane, immediately in front of

the heart and defining it on either side, is a pathway which peters out in

the distance.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#72:1> There are bare footprints on this path, as if someone had walked

around the front of the picture and left his mark.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#73:1> In the background, flowers in beds spring on either side of the path

while trees can be discerned in the distant top quadrant of the painting.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#74:1> Peeping over the top of the rust-coloured trees is a strip of sky,

ominous in its tones. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#75:1> This painting is colourful by Dunkley's standards.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#76:1> Ochres, green, reds, rusts, yellows are blended into the sombre

blues, dull whites and blacks.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#77:1> As usual, white is used to highlight every detailed petal, sepal and

leaf and to illuminate the gateway in the distance through which the road

passes to nowhere and everywhere. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#78:1> Dunkley uses line to differentiate the various types of foliage

and to add definition to the shape of the heart-shaped flower bed, the

footprints, the fences and the Spanish jars.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#79:1> But volume is not forgotten as can be seen in the painting of the

body of the jars which are carefully modelled.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#80:1> Colour is used almost as chiaroscuro and lends itself to an

interpretation that something ominous, threatening, is about to take place in

this seemingly peaceful setting. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#81:1> As one is drawn into the picture plane the feeling of foreboding

increases as the darkness encloses, the road narrows, vision becomes limited

and the rust red tree tops outlining the darkly threatening skies call forth

the feeling of blood, of death, of infinity.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#82:1> Dunkley's painting, <it> Back to Nature </it> , can remind the viewer

of the Garden of Gethsemane, a place of prayer, with its portent of death and

the unknown lurking in its midst. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#83:1> The technical elements of composi <l> tion, form, and colour

enhance the subject matter and help to create a mood which makes this not

simply another landscape but a landscape of the surreal, almost a twilight

zone where one crosses from the seemingly innocuous into the macabre, the

unnatural. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#84:1> Yet for those who do not care to enter the garden, but simply

prefer to view it from outside, the painting is a straightforward statement

of the bounty of nature in all its lushness.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#85:1> This duality is part of the genius of Dunkley. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#86:1> <h> <bold> Jerboa </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#87:1> <it> Mixed media on ply wood 28" x 24" ( 70 x 60 cm) </it> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#88:1> Dunkley produced two Jerboa paintings.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#89:1> The one discussed is the larger of the two, painted in 1939.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#90:1> It shows a woodland scene with a distant landscape beyond.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#91:1> The picture is divided into five horizontal levels, with a sixth upon

which stands <quote> 'an imaginary animal' </quote> , a kangaroo-legged rat.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#92:1> He stands above a branch with seven leaves and a little triangle in

which three fishes swim.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#93:1> The composition is similar to that of another, <it> Girl Sitting on

Wall </it> , where the curve of a cut-stone wall emerges from the lower right

of the canvas to lead the viewer to the far left, then deeper into the upper

centre of the painting.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#94:1> This device takes the viewer through the painting, pausing to observe

details along the way but always to be caught back by the compelling curve.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#95:1> The powerful curved shape in <it> Jerboa </it> is formed by the trunk

of the tree rising from its roots in the front plane of the picture.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#96:1> Low on the left side of the tree is the branch on which the Jerboa

stands and midway along the trunk are five branches - four of them sprouting

leaves, and one a post-phallic symbol.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#97:1> However, the tree trunk does not curve back into the centre of the

picture but follows the left edge of the composition to disappear into the

mysterious depths of the canopy of large leaves stretching across the top of

the painting.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#98:1> From this canopy hang three spiders on long threads which lead the

eye down to the web at the lower centre.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#99:1> The three spiders comprise an isosceles triangle in the foreground

and mid-ground.
<ICE-JA:W2B-004#100:1> Both <it> Jerboa </it> and <it> Girl Sitting on Wall </it> are

inhabited by animals in the lower left-hand corner; the Jerboa in the Dunkley

creation of that name and in <it> Girl Sitting on Wall </it> , with a crab -

a symbol in some societies of the opposition of life and death. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-004#101:1> If this painting with its eerie spider web, and the three

waiting spiders, is discussing the entanglement or entrapment of life and

other life-related issues, it is an easy leap to imagine that the three

fishes are a symbol of the Trinity which governs life before, now, and in the

hereafter. </p> </I>

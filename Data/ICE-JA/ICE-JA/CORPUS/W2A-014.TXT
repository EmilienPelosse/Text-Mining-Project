<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#1:1> <h> Whither Caribbean Socialism? Grenada, Jamaica, and Guyana in

Perspective </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#2:1> <it> Carl Stone </it> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#3:1> SOCIALIST TRANSFORMATION has often been seen by the left intelli

<l> gentsia of the Caribbean as the optimal development path.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#4:1> That socialist vision has now been informed by various efforts within

the region to break out of the limiting confines of capitalist economic

management and to embrace varieties of socialist practice and policy.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#5:1> The experiences of Guyana in the 1970s and early 1980s, Jamaica in the

1970s, and Grenada between 1979 and 1983 are three notable efforts in this

direction.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#6:1> They provide, in different ways, interesting evidence on the limits

and potential of socialist initiatives in the Caribbean region. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#7:1> What is meant by socialism? For one thing, the concept overlaps

considerably different political economy structures; sharp controversy also

exists over whether the label appropriately describes regimes claim <l> ing

allegiance to the socialist ideology broadly defined.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#8:1> Several Marxist commentators, for example, have challenged the claims

to genuine so <l> cialist practice by the Manley regime in Jamaica in the

1970s and by Guyana under the late Forbes Burnham. <fnr> <sp> 1 </sp> </fnr>

<footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#9:1> 1. Clive Thomas in fact classifies these regimes as being state

capitalist rather than socialist.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#10:1> Clive Thomas, <quote> " From Colony to State Capitalism" </quote> (

Paramaribo, Suriname: Foundation in Arts and Sciences, 1982, mimeo.).

</footnote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#11:1> Socialism involves commitment to certain specific development ob

<l> jectives.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#12:1> These include large-scale redistribution of wealth, assets, or income

in favor of the majority classes and the assignment of a dominant role for

the state in economic management to further the life chances of the majority

and to reduce, control, or eliminate the power of capitalists.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#13:1> Socialist theory and practice diverge on the most effective means of

achieving these common objectives and on the accompanying political

structures necessary to activate socialist economic development. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#14:1> Social democrats seek to operate within the framework of

essentially capitalist economies, seeking to humanize them and reform them

through redistributive, populist, social service, and social welfare poli <l>

cies.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#15:1> More radical socialist commitments attempt to overturn the capital

<l> ist economy and replace it with a political economy controlled by the

state on behalf of the people.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#16:1> Theory and practice within both the reformist social democratic

approach and the revolutionary Marxist-Leninist approach diverge also on the

question of ownership of the means of production.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#17:1> Emphases vary also with respect to designs to transform the

productive capacity of an economy along socialist lines as against priorities

that concentrate on redistribution.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#18:1> Further confusion is added by the fact that socialist regimes often

embrace theories and articulate principles that diverge considerably from

their actual practice. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#19:1> Somewhere between the reformist social democratic regimes and the

Marxist- Leninist political economies are regimes that ultimately seek to

move toward the latter by gradual but decisive political and policy steps

that take them toward a so-called noncapitalist path. <fnr> <sp> 2 </fnr>

</sp> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#20:1> 2. See R. A. Ulyanovsky, <it> Socialism and the Newly Independent

Nations </it> ( Mos <l> cow: Progress Publishers, 1974). </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#21:1> This intermediary position has structural features that combine

capitalist elements with initiatives toward a state-managed socialist

economy. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#22:1> The promise of socialist development has also been associated

with the objective of reducing dependence on world capitalism and its domi

<l> nant system in the region ( the United Stated) as well as with the

inter-linked objectives of greater economic self-reliance, more decisive

locally controlled and inward-looking development policies, as well as

greater local political autonomy, dignity, and self-respect. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#23:1> Implicit in the socialist promise is the democratic claim that

socialist management of power is more responsive to majority interests, is

more accountable, and provides more extensive channels for mass political

participation.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#24:1> Strategies to deepen democracy or claims in that direction invariably

accompany efforts at socialist economic management.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#25:1> Social <l> ist management of power, it is claimed, is able to achieve

a greater deconcentration of political power. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#26:1> The interest in socialist transformation and its potential has

gener <l> ated considerable attention due to the deeply rooted economic and

social problems that remain unsolved in the English-speaking Caribbean under

capitalist economic management.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#27:1> These include high levels of open unemployment; extreme concentration

of income; distressing poverty levels among the bottom 40 percent of

household; extreme dependence on and vulnerability to trade, financial,

consumer, and fiscal trends within the world capitalist system; weak

production structures, depen <l> dent on one or two main sources of hard-

currency earnings; enormous idle capacity in agriculture; extensive foreign

ownership and export of capital; weak entrepreneurial traditions, centered

more on mercantile activity rather than on production; and massive outflows

of skills through emigration. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#28:1> Whether socialist economic management offers real solutions is an

issue that remains largely unexamined beyond the rhetorical claims and

assertions of the left and the equally strident counterclaims of

antisocialist adversaries.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#29:1> To confront this issue adequately requires some conceptual

clarification of the development assumptions of socialist thought and a

concrete specification of the precise nature of the root causes of Caribbean

economic underdevelopment. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#30:1> Because Marxist and social democratic notions of development

emerged as antitheses to capitalism within the core economies of the world

capitalist system, both assumed that capitalism had developed a productive

capability that socialist management could build on to achieve its class and

social objectives.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#31:1> The social democrats sought to harness the income base generated by

dynamic private entrepreneurs to redistribute some of it in the direction of

improving the quality of life of the poor through welfare state policies.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#32:1> Additionally, it was thought that the creative and productive

energies of private capital could be channeled more toward national

development by state regulation.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#33:1> Marx <l> ist development thought presumes that the task of revolution

lies in taking hold of the productive forces created by capitalism and using

them for the benefit of the majority classes rather than for the purpose of

capitalist profit generation.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#34:1> These essentially Western European as <l> sumptions have no basis

when applied to Third World regions like the Caribbean, where the priority

development task lies in building a strong and viable production base, as a

long history of retarded growth through colonialism, dependence, and

imperialism has left a legacy of an undevel <l> oped productive capacity.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#35:1> Two Marxist views on development in the Third World are more

relevant to the Caribbean situation.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#36:1> The first argues for a development alternative via socialism, which

would avoid the excess of materialism and acquisitiveness associated with

capitalism.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#37:1> This type of no frills development would seek to reorganize the

forces of production to meet the food, shelter, clothing, health,

educational, and other welfare needs of the majority classes. <fnr> <sp> 3

</sp> </fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#38:1> 3. North Korea and China ( under Mao) represent models of this type

of no-frills socialist development.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#39:1> Prescription of this path for the Caribbean is set out in George

Beckford and Michael Witter, <it> Small Garden, Bitter Weed: Struggle and

Change in Jamaica </it> ( Morant Bay, Jamaica: Maroon, 1980).
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#40:1> The basic- needs model of socialist development is comprehensively

analyzed in Carl Stone, <it> Profiles of Power in the Caribbean Basin </it> (

Philadelphia: ISHI, 1986). ch. 6. </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#41:1> The goal of mass affluence, or a high material level of living, is

portrayed as a capitalist design to promote overcon <l> sumption.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#42:1> Needs rather than wants would be satisfied, and equality assured.

Such an objective is realizable within the context of the produc <l> tive

capability of Caribbean economies.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#43:1> Its advantage over the present situation in the English-speaking

Caribbean is that it would reduce or eliminate much of the hard-core poverty

and unemployment among the bottom 40 percent of income earners.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#44:1> The disadvantage is that it would have nothing to offer to the top 60

percent of income earners  except a drastically reduced standard of living.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#45:1> The alternative Marxist view still clings to the idea of

socialism as capable of transforming the productive capacity of Third World

capital <l> ism and of beginning to bring the full fruits of modern

technology within the grasp of the world's working class. <fnr> <sp> 4 </sp>

</fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#46:1> 4. See for example Paul Baran, <it> The Political Economy of Growth

</it> ( New York: Monthly Review Press, 1957). </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#47:1> Here the vision of socialism coexisting with abundance and high

material standards of living is kept intact, although its realization can

only occur after the collapse of the world capitalist system.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#48:1> Underlying this view is a Leninist faith and optimism that the world

capitalist system is destined to collapse and the task of Third World

development is to prepare to partake in an alternative socialist development.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#49:1> Here the Prescription is for a develop <l> ment effort toward a

noncapitalist path.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#50:1> Success or failure cannot be judged in terms of whether incomes

increase or mass welfare improves.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#51:1> Rather, the key criterion is whether development is moved toward

socialism to ensure that these countries keep pace with the unfolding pattern

of inevitable world historical development from capitalism to socialism. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#52:1> Underlying this socialist view of development is the idea that

politics takes command of economic forces and guides them in a direction that

is either historically correct or designed to put them at the service of the

majority classes.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#53:1> In the Caribbean situation, it acquires currency as an escape route

from imperialism, class exploration, and dependency.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#54:1> Socialist development thought in the Third World and the Caribbean

focuses on the consolidation of power, the development, and a historically

correct view of world political alignments.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#55:1> The result is that an overloaded agenda of political concerns crowds

out detailed and technical economic policy questions. <fnr> <sp> 5 </sp>

</fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#56:1> 5. There is in fact not a single comprehensive work by a Marxist

theorist that deals with the technical issues of transformation to socialism

in the specific context of Caribbean economies.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#57:1> Clive Thomas, <it> Dependence and Transformation: The Economics of

the Transition to Socialism </it> ( New York: Monthly Review Press, 1984),

framework rather than addressing the specificities of the Caribbean context.

</footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#58:1> These relate to economic strategies and tactics; the nature of the

existing world capitalist system and how to maximize one's development policy

options within it; the level of existing productive capability and how to

advance it; how to go about obtaining develop <l> ment financing and to

improve one's position in the fields of technology and trade; and what forms

of economic planning are feasible, given the political capacity and

administrative resource capability.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#59:1> The result quite often is that conventional capitalist economic

strategies are borrowed and combined with socialist political strategies,

leading to development that is contradictory, internally inconsistent, and

confusing to both those who lead and those who follow. <fnr> <sp> 6 </sp>

</fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#60:1> 6. This was certainly a problem of the Manley regime in Jamaica.

</footnote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#61:1> Socialist development in the Caribbean can therefore be judged

according to a number of criteria.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#62:1> These include the following: </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#63:1> 1. Has it enhanced or improved the well-being and welfare of the

majority classes? </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#64:1> 2. Is it seeking to establish a reorganized basic-needs socialist

economy, or is it working within the capitalist market economy, albeit with

high levels of state regulation control or ownership? </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#65:1> 3. Is it Marxist or social democratic in ideological alignment?

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#66:1> 4. Is the accompanying political system liberal democratic or

based on the political prescriptions of the concapitalist path, which seeks

to establish political hegemony around a strong and dominant populist or

majoritarian political ideology? </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#67:1> 5. Are the development priorities biased toward consolidation of

power, biased toward economic transformation strategies, or are they balanced

evenly? </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#68:1> 6. How do the strategies of international alignment serve or

undermine the economic policy thrust, and what is optimal given existing

world economic realities? </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#69:1> 7. How far are the socialist goals ( economic or political)

compatible with the aspirations, values, and culture of Caribbean people?

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#70:1> 8. How fare are the economic and social objectives of socialist

practice likely to address the concrete or structural economic problems in

the region? </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#71:1> 9. To what extent are socialist politics consistent with the

accompanying economic policies? </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#72:1> In examining the attempts at socialist development in Grenada,

and Jamaica, and Guyana it will be useful to compare and evaluate these

regimes according to these criteria. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-014#73:1> There are essentially three basic structural problems in

English-speaking Caribbean countries.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#74:1> Firstly, as middle-income third World countries, they have

considerably raised their post-World War II living standards through

overdependence on mineral and primary products exports ( bauxite, oil, sugar,

bananas, etc.) to buoyant and lucrative core capitalist markets.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#75:1> Major downward shifts in prices and market demand for those exports

have meant drastic declines in the capacity to import.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#76:1> High dependence on imports ( due to small size and to a legacy of

economic dependence) makes the region extraordinarily vulnerable to adverse

price and supply changes in international commodity or con <l> sumer goods

markets.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#77:1> Third, import dependence combined with tech <l> nological dependence

mean that investment expansion to extend, en <l> large, and diversify

productive capacity require foreign investment, loans, credit, or aid to

bring such ( public or private) investment projects to fruition.
<ICE-JA:W2A-014#78:1> Policies to address these problems all require a capacity to mobilize

international or foreign resources or to penetrate overseas markets. </I>

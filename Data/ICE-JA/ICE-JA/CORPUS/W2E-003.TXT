<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#1:1> <h> The University's interface with the society </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#2:1> By Geof Brown </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#3:1> SIR Alister McIntyre, Vice Chancellor ( which is to say, working

head) of the University of the West Indies sent a strong new signal recently

on the future relationship of the University with the wider society.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#4:1> He let it be known that the intellectual, technical and technological

resources of the institution would become more available to industry,

organizations, and development entities.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#5:1> They will pay, of course, but the revenue will in turn assist the

development and expansion of the university itself.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#6:1> To a public often cynical about the extent of the University's

commitment to development as opposed to degree- manufacturing, the

pronouncement could hardly be more timely. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#7:1> <quote> " The University boasts the largest pool of specialized

and expert personnel as well as the greatest stock of research equipment and

other facilities within the Caribbean" </quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#8:1> So states a current report on the University's plans for the next

decade, in a very readable booklet titled 'The University of the West Indies

- Into the 21st Century'.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#9:1> Acknowledging that, <quote> " in order to survive and succeed the

Caribbean region must begin to build a pool of human resources in the areas

of science, technology, management and organization" </quote> the report

spells out a role and response for the University in these words: </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#10:1> <h> Human resources </h> </p>

<p> <quote>
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#11:1> " This development of human resources is the task that the University

will respond to in this decade...
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#12:1> Given the far reaching extent of our territorial responsibilities (

in) Jamaica, Trinidad, Barbados and eleven non-campus countries, the

University of the West is <bold> most suited </bold> ( my emphasis) to

respond creatively and purposefully to the challenges of human resource

development in the Caribbean" </quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#13:1> I can almost see the cynics relenting and the doubters cautiously

applauding.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#14:1> But if reservations remain, based on past experience of what the

report terms as the University being <quote> " acutely conscious of the need

to improve the efficiency of its own operations" </quote> , the following

commitment must be more reassuring. </p>

<p> <quote>
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#15:1> " There must be a continuing effort to enhance the relevance, quality

and usefulness of academic programmes. Information is the sustenance of a

growing society.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#16:1> Along this vein, the University sees it necessary not only to

encourage and facilitate more research in all aspects of the Caribbean but

also to assist in the dissemination of this information in a manner that is

<bold> practical </bold> and <bold> useful </bold> , ( my emphasis).
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#17:1> We plan to serve more effectively as a conduit for information and

knowledge both regionally and internationally" </quote> .</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#18:1> The preceding quotes underscore Vice Chancellor McIntyre's recent

announcement.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#19:1> The new commitment addresses a beef that a great many in the public

have about the too- traditional Ivory Tower-like role of U.W.I. in a

developing context.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#20:1> In contrast, the flexibility of response North American universities

with their Extension and Continuing Studies departments catering to a wide

clientele of motivated persons, is often cited against U.W.I. by frustrated

would-be students and by interested observers.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#21:1> Similarly innovations by British universities such as 'University of

the Air' or 'Open University' allowing working persons who cannot enter the

university walls to acquire academic qualifications or enhance scholarly

knowledge, are cited against a U.W.I. seen as too smugly trotting out degrees

in an uncreative, unimaginative fashion. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#22:1> It is refreshing that the report quoted here speaks so honestly

and candidly to the University's acknowledgement of its own inefficiencies

and shortcomings.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#23:1> What is of equal concern is the failure of the society to demand more

clearly and unequivocally the kind of university it wants.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#24:1> <quote> " War," </quote> it is said, <quote> " is too important to be

left to the generals." </quote>
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#25:1> Why is higher education not seen as too important to be left to the

intellectuals and academics? </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#26:1> <h> Personnel </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#27:1> If as the report states, the University boasts the largest pool

of specialized and export personnel as well as the greatest stock of research

equipment etc., in the Caribbean, why has the public not demanded fuller use

and integration of these resources with the needs and aspiration of the

society?
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#28:1> Why hasn't there been pressure up to now to imitate the flexibility

of response and the innovations of North American and British universities

where their served societies needs are not nearly as great as those of our

developing society? </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#29:1> When I last wrote on this topic, two persons corresponded with

very useful and practical suggestions for the University to become more

inclusive in its programmes and one leading service club invited me to

address them on the matter.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#30:1> I was disappointed.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#31:1> People ought to be speaking out; the public should be flooding the

U.W.I. with suggestions and demands ( yes demands) for appropriate responses

to their higher education needs.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#32:1> It is clear that Vice Chancellor McIntyre and the current U.W.I

leadership have set a climate for accepting such suggestions as are feasible

and workable, given their own pro- active moves and statements in the Report

cited. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#33:1> It is not enough that only a Rex Nettleford, Carl Stone or a Geof

Brown consistently appear in print or an Aggrey Brown on the air <?> - </?>

waves to join university academics to other resources informing or attempting

to help educate the society.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#34:1> It is signal and remarkable but not enough that an Extra-Mural

Studies ( now School of Continuing Studies) caters to thousands of

non-degree-seeking studies but is not yet permitted to prepare working adults

for degrees.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#35:1> It is useful and helpful but not enough that hundreds of

consultancies by university academics are quietly unseen like a <quote> "

light under a bushel" </quote> . </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#36:1> What will be <bold> practical </bold> and <bold> useful </bold>

is a partnership between a demanding public and a University which now states

( in the Report) that it is ready to meet the challenges of this decade

<quote> " in order to propel the Caribbean into the 21st Century." </quote>
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#37:1> Where are the takers? </p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#38:2> <h> Constitutional Reform </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#39:2> <h> Proposals for modification </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#40:2> By Lloyd Barnett </p>

<p> <h> ( Part V of a series) </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#41:2> NUMEROUS proposals have been made from time to time for

modification of the Constitution so as to effect improvements without

changing its basic structure.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#42:2> Some of these proposals are far-reaching in nature.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#43:2> Many are aimed at making effective the checks on executive power,

improving the efficiency of Parliament, and increasing the accountability of

Parliamentarians.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#44:2> In the Report of the Stone Committee which examined in detail the

performance, accountability and responsibilities of elected Parliamentarians

and made comprehensive recommendations, only three of its recommendations

entailed constitutional amendment for their implementation.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#45:2> These are changing to the presidential system, which we discussed in

Part III of this series; giving voters at the constituency level the power to

recall Members of Parliament who violate a prescribed code of conduct and/or

fail to perform their specified duties which we will examine in this article;

and requiring that Members of Parliament reside in or have a residence in the

constituencies they represent. </p>

<p> <h> Recall of Parliamentarians </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#46:2> The proposal to give electors the right to recall Members of

Parliament is theoretically attractive, but has serious practical and legal

problems.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#47:2> The majority of the Stone Committee supported it on the grounds that

<quote> " giving voters the right to recall Members of Parliament would have

a major impact in restoring to the public a sense that they as voters have

control over the politician.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#48:2> To give voters the right to recall non- performing Members of

Parliament would send a serious signal to politicians motivating them to take

their duties and responsibilities more seriously" </quote> . </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#49:2> The system of recall was first put forward by persons who

supported the Communist system.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#50:2> In the programme adopted by the Communist Party of the Soviet Union

in 1919, the theory of delegation is emphasized and provision is made for the

people to recall their delegates <quote> " in the most easy and accessible

manner" </quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#51:2> However, the experience of the Union of Soviet Socialist Republics

was that the devise was mainly used as an instrument for the purging of

deputies who lost favour with the party leadership.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#52:2> In France, this system was proposed by the Communist Party in the

discussion on a Constitution for the Fourth Republic but was rejected by all

other parties. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#53:2> When there are more than one Party in a country the existence of

this system is likely to cause constant electioneering, political instability

and possible chaos.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#54:2> The main reasons for strong dissatisfaction with the performance of

Members of Parliament is not their inaccessibility or lack of effort but the

low level of public and social services in the constituency.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#55:2> In many cases, this is aggravated by general public dissatisfaction

with the benefits being derived from the public sector or the general state

of the economy.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#56:2> It will therefore be difficult to divorce a particular Member of

Parliament from the state of the national economy and the performance of the

Government as a whole, or unfair to single him out for condemnation because

of Cabinet or ministerial attention by government.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#57:2> It will still be even less justifiable to apply the same standard in

the case of an Opposition Member of Parliament. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#58:2> The Minority Report gave the following cogent grounds why such a

system would cause insuperable difficulties: </p>

<p> <quote>
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#59:2> " ( 1) The use of recall petitions by party activists and local

government representatives seeking to replace the sitting MP; </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#60:2> ( 2) The use of the recall petitions to pursue factional disputes

among competing leaders in the constituency; </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#61:2> ( 3) Cumbersome and costly procedures governing the verification

of signatures on the petitions; </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#62:2> ( 4) The danger of an increase in by-elections and additional

costs in electoral administration occasioned by successful recall petitions

especially during the second term of governing parties when many MP's begin

to shrink their duties; </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#63:2> ( 5) The use of recall petitions by opposition candidates bent on

unseating incumbent MP's" </quote> . </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#64:2> The Majority felt that adequate safeguards and procedures could

be established to avoid frivolous and irresponsible recall petitions.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#65:2> These suggestions are: </p>

<p> <quote>
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#66:2> " ( 1) Requiring that recall petitions should be signed by 51 per

cent of registered voters in the constituency and that the recall petitions

be verified by the Returning Officer attached to the Electoral Office; </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#67:2> ( 2) That after a recall a petition has been verified the MP

would have a right to appeal to the Constituency Council made up of leaders

from civic and community organisations. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#68:2> The Revocation of an MP's election would only stand if the

Constituency Council agree that the petition is justified; </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#69:2> ( 3) That recall petitions would only be valid in cases where

MP's are accused of violating the MP code of conduct, are accused of failing

to perform the specified duties and responsibilities of MP's or are convicted

in the courts for crimes that bring the office of MP into disrepute.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#70:2> The Constituency Council should have the power to determine if the

recall petition meets these criteria" </quote> . </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#71:2> These suggestions are on their face quite impracticable.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#72:2> Bearing in mind our experience in the registration of voters system

it would be impossible for the Returning Officer to verify within a

reasonable time the identity and genuineness of the signatures of 51 per cent

of the registered voters which in all constituencies run into many thousands.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#73:2> It would also be very difficult to establish a precise code of

conduct or measure of unsatisfactory performance.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#74:2> If the rules of natural justice are maintained it would not be

possible to deny the Member of Parliament the right to challenge the Petition

in the Courts.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#75:2> If the system is put into operation at all it could condemn the

country to exist on a diet of by- elections, constant political in-fighting

and bickering, and bitter and violent rivalry on a continuous basis.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#76:2> It is surprising that this proposal should be put forward at the same

time as an attempt is being made to increase the independence of the back <?>

- </?> benchers in Parliament, since this system could make Members of

Parliament hostages of the Party machinery and political agitators. </p>

<p> <h> Reduction of the Parliamentary Term </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2E-003#77:2> It has been suggested that the life of each Parliament should be

reduced to a fixed term of say, four years.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#78:2> In practice, under the Parliamentary Cabinet System, Governments

often go to the polls before the expiration of their usual allotment of five

years and this has been the experience in Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2E-003#79:2> In many cases, the Political Executive loses touch with the people

after a period of time. </p> </I>

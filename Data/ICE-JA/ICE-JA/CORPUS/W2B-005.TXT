<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-005#4:1> <it> Seventy-five artists had work on display in the 1992 Annual

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-005#6:1> The artists could be classified as mainstream ( trained) and

intuitive ( self-taught), and their works covered an extensive array of art

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-005#7:1> Maybe the most outstanding aspect of this show was the wide

variety of themes presented, ranging from wide social issues through to

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-005#8:1> Nineteen ninety-two was notable for two significant events in the

history and culture of Jamaica: the recognition of Columbus's first voyage to

the Carib <l> bean and the archaeological find of valuable Taino sculptures

<ICE-JA:W2B-005#9:1> Karl <quote> 'Jerry' </quote> Craig's two mixed media pieces from the

<it> All That Glitters, El Dorado, Columbus </it> Series, individually titled

<it> Textures of New Seville </it> and <it> New Seville </it> , expressed in

highly textural compositions the Spanish <w> explorers' </w> quest for gold

in the Carib <l> bean and the Americas.
<ICE-JA:W2B-005#10:1> Craig used a variety of metallic tones of gold and brown with red and

blue injected to add richness and balance to the sparkle of the gold and the

<ICE-JA:W2B-005#11:1> Geometric shapes, mainly rectangular, sectionalized the paintings

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-005#12:1> The archaeological find was cele <l> brated by David Boxer in his

mixed-media triptych <it> Revival ( Taino Birdman) </it> made up of twelve

<ICE-JA:W2B-005#13:1> The panels in both works are fairly small, 20.5 x 26 cm and 25.5 x

<ICE-JA:W2B-005#16:1> <it> Tainototenlieder </it> is formed by adding two German words,

<it> toten </it> ( death) and <it> lieder </it> ( songs) to the word Taino,

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-005#17:1> In The <it> Seville Diptych </it> , Tina Matkovic-Spiro combines

both themes; the arrival of Christopher Columbus at Seville, St Ann and, to a

<ICE-JA:W2B-005#19:1> Whether or not all of these grew in Jamaica when Columbus arrived is

<ICE-JA:W2B-005#20:1> It is an idyllic landscape which is being presented here.
<ICE-JA:W2B-005#21:1> In the background, the coast glides behind the mountains and three

<ICE-JA:W2B-005#22:1> Little circular build <l> ings dot the landscape and a lone Arawak

<ICE-JA:W2B-005#23:1> An orange flame flickers above the highest point, the Arawak signal

fire at Blow Fire Hill which forewarns the Arawaks of their impending fate.
<ICE-JA:W2B-005#24:1> Measuring 92.5 x 427 cm, this diptych was by far the largest painting

in the show. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-005#31:1> Laura Facey Cooper, however, ex <l> plored the destruction of the

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-005#37:1> Social concerns were unearthed and solutions put forward by

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-005#50:1> Andrew Jefferson explored the very contemporary dance hall

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-005#56:1> Perhaps secondary to the variety of themes in this 1992 show was

<ICE-JA:W2B-005#60:1> One of Watson's strengths is his skill in capturing transparency, as

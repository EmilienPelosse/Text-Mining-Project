<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-006#1:1> <h> <bold> Imperial Policy and Colonial Sterling Balances 1943-56

<fnr> * </fnr> </bold> </h> </p> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#2:1> * I would like to thank the British Council and the Association of

Commonwealth Universities for the funding which made this research possible,

the Institute of Common <l> wealth Studies for the fellowship which

facilitated my research and Dr Robert Holland for his incisive comments on

the draft to this article. </footnote>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-006#4:1> Allister E. Hinds </bold> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-006#5:1> Between 1943 and 1956 Britain's strategy for maintaining the

sterling balances of its colonies was an important policy issue.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#6:1> These balances or sterling assets formed the external financial

reserves of the colonies and represented, ultimately, colonial claims upon

the resources of the UK itself.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#7:1> The principal components of the colonial sterling balances were

Colonial Government Funds, Currency Funds, Marketing Board Reserves <fnr>

<sp> 1 </sp> </fnr> and the reserves of commercial banks which were operating

in the colonies but not investing locally. <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#8:1> 1. The Government Funds consisted of the various central and local

government reserves and funds for which colonial governments acted as

bankers.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#9:1> The Currency Funds were made up of sterling or sterling securities,

equivalent to at least 100 per cent of the value of the local currency

issued.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#10:1> It also included the profits from commission charges and the interest

on investments made by the colonial currency authorities.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#11:1> The Marketing Board Reserves consisted of the surplus funds which the

various marketing boards had set aside for price stabilization, research and

development.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#12:1> The percentage of the total colonial sterling balance represented by

these each of its main components, varied according to the economic earnings

and the level of economic activities in the Colonies.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#13:1> For more details on the nature and the structure of the colonial

sterling balances see T236/3562, Memorandum on the Sterling Assets of the

British Colonies ( Col. No. 298), 18 Dec. 1953. </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#14:1> Colonial sterling reserves were, theoretically, assets which could

have been withdrawn by the colonies without restrictions. <fnr> <sp> 2 </sp>

</fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#15:1> 2. T236/3562.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#16:1> Memorandum on the Sterling Assets of the British Colonies, 2-9.

</footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#17:1> However, between 1943 and 1945 the contingencies of war made it

impossible for the British government to grant the colonies unrestricted

access to their own reserves. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-006#18:1> In the post-war period the question of granting the colonies unre

<l> gulated access to their sterling reserves posed a dilemma for the British

government.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#19:1> Britain could have reduced the burden of having to finance colonial

development by allowing the colonies to meet their development costs from

their sterling reserves.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#20:1> However, because of the devastation wrought by the war and the

instability which prevailed in the UK economy, especially between 1945 and

1952, Britain was not in a position to satisfy the demand for goods and

services which would have been generated by colonial expenditure of

accumulated sterling reserves.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#21:1> On the other hand, it could not have allowed the colonies to make

unlimited purchases from the Dollar Area, their principal alternative source,

because supplies had to be paid for in gold or dollars.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#22:1> The consequent drain on the gold and dollar reserves of the UK would

have added to the economic disequilibrium and further jeopardized the status

of the pound as an international currency.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#23:1> Finally, so long as its economy was weak it was advisable for Britain

to act promptly on the measures necessary to deal with the colonial balances.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#24:1> If it did not, subsequent increases in the size of the accumulated

colonial sterling reserves would have accentuated the threat posed by such an

<quote> 'overhang' </quote> of external claims on the UK economy. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-006#25:1> Britain's plight was exacerbated by its obligations under the

terms of the Anglo- American Loan Agreement of December 1945.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#26:1> According to the terms of this agreement, the UK was obliged to

restore the free convertibility of the pound sterling by abolishing all the

restrictions governing the conversion of pounds into other international

currencies by July 1947.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#27:1> It was also committed to the reduction of the overall size of its

total sterling liabilities, in other words, the sum total of all the sterling

or sterling securities held by Britain's creditors. <fnr> <sp> 3 </sp> </fnr>

<footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#28:1> 3. For details on the Anglo- American financial negotiations in the

war and post-war years see following: R. Gardner, <it> Sterling Dollar

Diplomacy in Current Perspective </it> ( new and expanded edition) ( New

York,1980); L.S. Pressnell, <it> External Economic Policy Since the War </it>

, Vol.1 ( London, 1986); R. Clarke, <it> Anglo-American Co-operation in War

and Peace </it> ( Oxford, 1982); and R.M. Hathaway, <it> The Ambiguous

Partnership: Britain and America 1944-47 </it> ( New York, 1981). </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#29:1> The Americans were determined to ensure that Britain reduced its

accumulated sterling liabilities, because they were poised to capitalize on

the escalation in the demand for goods and services that this would have

precipitated. <fnr> <sp> 4 </sp> </fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#30:1> 4. See CO 852/584/1/19037/16.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#31:1> Washington to Joint American Secretariat.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#32:1> Sever No.702, 26 April 1945. </footnote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-006#33:1> Britain's attempt to restore the free convertibility of the pound

produced a major economic crisis in 1947. <fnr> <sp> 5 </sp> </fnr>

<footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#34:1> 5. For the effects of this crisis on imperial policy see A. Hinds,

<quote> 'Sterling and Imperial Policy 1945-51' </quote> , <it> Journal of

Imperial and Commonwealth History </it> , 15, 2 ( 1987), 148-70. </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#35:1> After this had been overcome by re-establishing controls, its

economic stability was soon threatened by another crisis in 1949 and the

repercussions of the Korean War which began in 1950.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#36:1> The latter led to a boom in world commodity prices which facilitated

steep increases in colonial sterling assets between 1950 and 1952.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#37:1> Amid all of this, Britain was still expected to honour its commitment

to reduce the size of its accumulated sterling liabilities.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#38:1> These developments made the formulation of a policy to manage the

colonial sterling balances a particularly onerous task. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-006#39:1> This article examines the attempt made by the British government

to devise a policy to manage the expenditure of the sterling balances

accumulated by the colonies between 1943 and 1956 - a topic yet to receive

the detailed scholarly attention it deserves.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#40:1> Hitherto issues relevant to the general question of British policy

towards the expenditure of colonial sterling balances have been analysed by

three scholars: Judd Polk, D.J <+> . </+> Morgan and B.R <+> . </+>

Tomlinson. <fnr> <sp> 6 </sp> </fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#41:1> 6. See J. Polk, <it> Sterling </it> ( New York, 1956) 175-206; D.J

<+> . </+> Morgan, <it> The Official History of Colonial Development </it> (

London, 1980) II, 53-63 and III, 157-74; and B.R <+> . </+> Tomlinson,

<quote> 'Indo-British Relations in the Post-Colonial Era: The Sterling

Balances Negotiations 1947-49' </quote> , <it> Journal of Imperial and

Commonwealth History </it> , 13, 3 ( 1985), 142-62. </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#42:1> Polk's analysis of the sterling balances of the regional blocs which

comprised the British Empire is especially acute.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#43:1> However, while it is provocative with regard to the general factors

shaping British policy, Polk, writing in 1956, could do little to unravel the

complex debate within Whitehall.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#44:1> Morgan's work contains a lot of useful information.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#45:1> However, the author tended to narrate rather than interpret the

issues.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#46:1> In addition, he did not pay sufficient attention to the conditions in

the British and international economies which influenced the UK's policy

towards the colonial sterling balances. Tomlinson's work focuses on the

Indo-British sterling balances negotiations between 1947 and 1949.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#47:1> Despite his primary materials, Tomlinson gives readers only a limited

idea of some of the overall factors that shaped Britain's colonial sterling

balances policy.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#48:1> This article attempts to bolster the existing literature by analysing

the extent to which imperial policy towards the management of the colonial

sterling balances was determined by the state of the British economy.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#49:1> It also intends to show that the colonial development issue in

general, and colonial balances in particular, have to be seen in the context

of distinctive phases in the UK's post-war economic travail, rather than - as

is usual - a somewhat undifferentiated rendering of Britain's balance of

payments disarray.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#50:1> In so doing it is concerned only with the sterling assets of those

colonies which remained under British control throughout the period under

review. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-006#51:1> <h> I </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-006#52:1> Before the Second World War the total sterling liabilities of the

UK were about &pound;600 million, <fnr> <sp> 7 </sp> </fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#53:1> 7. A.N <+> . </+> Porter and A.J <+> . </+> Stockwell, <it> British

Imperial Policy and Decolonization 1938-1964 </it> , II ( London, 1989), 128.

Doc. 5 <quote> 'Sterling Balances' </quote> , note by the Chancellor of the

Exchequer, 20 Dec. 1951, CAB 129/48. </footnote> an estimated &pound;100-

&pound;150 million of which was owed to the colonies. <fnr> <sp> 8 </sp>

</fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#54:1> 8. T236/3351. <quote> 'The Treatment of the Colonial Sterling

Balances' </quote> , draft memorandum by O.L <+> . </+> Williams forwarded to

N.E <+> . </+> Young with a covering letter dated 4 May 1949.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#55:1> This memorandum was the Treasury's response to the Governor of the

Bank of England's request for a memorandum which examined the colonial

sterling balances, colonial currency reserves and the prospects for colonial

borrowing in London.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#56:1> See T236/3351, H. Wilson-Smith to Mr. Governor, 30 May 1949.

</footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#57:1> During the war years the acquisition of unrequited exports, inflated

prices and military expenditure by the British and Allied forces in the

colonies combined to produce a steady increase in the colonial sterling

assets held in London.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#58:1> Table 1 indicates the pattern of this growth.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#59:1> During this period the imperial government did not formulate any

policy to deal with colonial sterling assets beyond allowing them to

accumulate. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-006#60:1> The absence of policy did not preclude government officials from

considering ways in which the funds or reserves which comprised the balances

could be utilized.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#61:1> In May 1943 at a meeting to discuss the cost of living in Cyprus,

British government officials examined the feasibility of using part of the

colonial currency fund to finance the cost of colonial development. <fnr>

<sp> 9 </sp> </fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#62:1> 9. T236/4090. C.H.M. Wilcox to N.E. Young, 1 July 1943 <+> . </+>

</footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#63:1> This led Sydney Caine, the Colonial Office Financial Adviser, to

solicit the views of Lord Keynes ( an adviser to the Treasury) on the

imperial government's policy at this time of investing the increments to the

colonial currency fund in London, instead of spending it on current expenses

or on capital development in the Colonies. <fnr> <sp> 10 </sp> </fnr>

<footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#64:1> 10. T236/4090. S.Caine to Keynes, 14 May 1943. </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#65:1> Caine pointed to some of the benefits which might accrue to the

government if it adopted an alternative strategy and allowed the colonial

governments to spend a portion of their reserves on colonial development

projects.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#66:1> He contended that this <quote> 'would have precisely the same effect

on the Government's finances as if it raised a loan, i.e. the Government

would be drawing funds from the London market, and its net income would be

lower by the interest on those funds' </quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#67:1> He juxtaposed this with the government's policy of allowing colonial

governments to raise loans for colonial development on the London market,

while simultaneously investing funds for future income in London, through

their currency accounts.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#68:1> <quote> 'At first sight,' </quote> he argued, <quote> 'it looks as if

it might be better to simplify the business by using the currency funds

directly for the kind of expenditure which might otherwise by financed from

loans' </quote> . <fnr> <sp> 11 </sp> </fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#69:1> 11. Ibid. </footnote> </p> <O> caption </O> <O> table </O>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-006#70:1> Caine felt that it might have been possible to make some kind of

special arrangement with the currency authorities which would have

facilitated the short-term issue of Treasury bills, and thus allow colonial

governments to raise loans locally.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#71:1> Although he believed that there were alternatives to the British

government's policy for managing the colonial sterling balances, Caine did

not believe that the existing system needed major reforms.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#72:1> He thought that apart from considering the possibility of making some

kind of special arrangement to facilitate a local issue of the Treasury

bills, there was <quote> 'no great advantage in a change in the current

practice' </quote> <fnr> <sp> 12 </sp> </fnr> because the colonies were an

integral part of a much wider British financial system. <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#73:1> 12. Ibid. </footnote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-006#74:1> Keynes thought that Caine's analysis of the issue was complete

and convincing.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#75:1> He added that the existing practice for dealing with the colonial

currency reserve funds <quote> 'was designed, probably on purpose, to promote

a high degree of conservatism in development' </quote> . <fnr> <sp> 13 </sp>

</fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#76:1> 13. T236/4090.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#77:1> Keynes to Caine, 18 May 1943. </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#78:1> He claimed that a colony would be more reluctant to spend money on

development if it felt that it had to borrow rather than use its own funds.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#79:1> He added that, when the currency reserve was growing, to hold 100 per

cent reserve <quote> 'in a form equivalent to gold ( i.e <+> . </+> external

liquid assets) instead of having a proportionate fiduciary issue, tends to

lead to an underestimation of the financial strength of the Colony' </quote> .

<fnr> <sp> 14 </sp> </fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#80:1> 14. Ibid. </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#81:1> He thought there were alternatives to the excessive conservatism of

the present system, although he remained vague as to what they might be. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-006#82:1> This exchange between Caine and Keynes set the stage for the

debate which shaped imperial policy towards the management of colonial

sterling assets during the period under review.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#83:1> The main contributors to this debate were officials from the

Treasury, the Colonial Office and the Bank of England.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#84:1> Discussions focused upon what many officials believed was,

ultimately, the principal contradiction in the imperial government's strategy

relative to the colonial sterling balances: colonial development was being

financed out of scarce UK funds, while the colonies went on accumulating

sterling balances in the metropole. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-006#85:1> Between 1943 and 1945 the need for an alternative to the imperial

government's policy of simply allowing colonial balances to accumulate in

London was supported by most Treasury officials.
<ICE-JA:W2A-006#86:1> Notable among these was Ernest Rowe-Dutton, the Principal Assistant

Secretary. </I>

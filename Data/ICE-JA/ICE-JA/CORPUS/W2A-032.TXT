<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#2:1> G.C.LALOR, H. ROBOTHAM </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#4:1> ( Received January 4, 1994) </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#5:1> The Centre for Nuclear Sciences was established in 1984 with the

mandate to introduce Caribbean scientists to the application of nuclear

technology in multi-disciplinary studies, and to carry out research in areas

of national and regional importance.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#6:1> This paper describes the present facilities and the major programmes

being carried out at the Centre. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#7:1> The University of the West Indies, ( U.W.I.), is the only regional

University in the English-speaking Caribbean.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#8:1> It serves fourteen territories.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#9:1> The three U.W.I. campuses are in Jamaica, Trinidad & Tobago and

Barbados. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#10:1> The University, in its drive to develop science and technology in

the Caribbean and to provide a more effective service in the Region, has

pursued the concept of creating Centres of Excellence, each operating with a

specific theme; well equipped and funded, and operated by small but highly

trained specialised core staff.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#11:1> They are a resource not only for departments within the University,

but for private sector and government organisations. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#12:1> One such unit is the Centre for Nuclear Sciences ( CNS), which

was established in 1984 with the mandate to introduce Caribbean scientists to

the application of nuclear technology in multi-disciplinary studies, and to

carry out research in areas of national and regional importance.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#13:1> The Centre's present activities are divided into three main areas:

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#14:1> &black-square; the development of multi-element analytical

capabilities with the emphasis being on nuclear analytical techniques; </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#15:1> &black-square; radiation protection programmes; and </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#16:1> &black-square; national and university-based research, service

and training programmes. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#17:1> <h> <bold> The Centre for Nuclear Sciences </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#18:1> <h> <bold> Analytical Facilities <bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#19:1> The main analytical thrust is on multi-element analytical

techniques with the capability to routinely quantify a wide range of elements

at low detection limits in a variety of sample matrices. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#20:1> <h> <it> Neutron activation analysis ( NAA) </it> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#21:1> The Centre operates an Atomic Energy of Canada, Ltd. ( AECL)

SLOWPOKE-2 research reactor with a maximum rated flux of 10 <sp> 12 </sp> n

cm <sp> - 2 </sp> s <sp> - 1 </sp> for NAA.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#22:1> There are four Canberra and Ortec coaxial high purity Ge detector

systems and one Ortec HPGe low energy photon spectrometer ( LEPS) system

interfaced to either IBM compatibles or a DEC 380 computer.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#23:1> Spectra are analysed with EG&G Ortec software for DEC ( GELIGAM) and

IBM ( OMNIGAM), EPAA ( Kennedy, Ecole Polytechnique).
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#24:1> The IAEA gamma spectrometry software package GANAAS is now being

included in the laboratory as an option for spectrum analysis.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#25:1> The laboratory computers are networked, and in-house menu-driven user

interfaces are available to provide a choice of MCA emulation, spectrum

analysis and quantification software at each workstation. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#26:1> <h> <it> X-ray fluorescence analysis ( XRF) </it> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#27:1> A Siemens FK60-04 X-ray tube with a molybdenum anode and a

Kristalloflex high voltage generator as the primary source of X-rays is used.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#28:1> The system is configured for secondary target excitation usually with

zirconium as the secondary target. An EG&G Ortec SLP 10180 Si(Li) detector

with a FWHM of 180 eV at 5.9 keV and 0.025 mm Be window, and associated

spectroscopy electronics interfaced to a Canberra Series 35+ multi-channel

analyzer is used for spectrum collection.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#29:1> The IAEA QXAS software is used for spectrum analysis. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#30:1> <h> <it> Ion chromatography ( IC) </it> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#31:1> A Dionex 4500i ion chromatography unit with an automatic sample

changer is used.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#32:1> There are two detector systems: </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#33:1> &black-square; a pulsed electrochemical detector PED for the

determination of anions such as nitrate, chloride, sulphate, etc. and cations

such as ammonium, potassium, sodium, calcium, etc. by conductivity

measurements. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#34:1> &black-square; a variable wavelength detector VDM-II with a

microlitre volume quartz cell and fibre optics for light transfer.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#35:1> This is presently used for the determination of transition elements

by spectrophotometry after complexation with 4-(2- pyridylazo)resorcinol.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#36:1> <h> <bold> Electronics Workshop </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#37:1> The electronics laboratory provides the Centre with the

capability to maintain and repair much of the instrumentation within the

facility.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#38:1> The laboratory is also involved in the development of peripheral

systems associated with nuclear spectrometry measurements.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#39:1> For example, a computer controlled random access storage system has

been designed and built for storage and retrieval of 180 samples from

individually coded sites.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#40:1> The pneumatic system of the storage site is connected to the

SLOWPOKE-II irradiation pneumatic unit so that samples can be transferred

directly from the reactor to the storage areas.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#41:1> An onboard computer keeps a database of site status.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#42:1> The prototype was developed under a research contract from the IAEA.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#43:1> <h> <bold> Radiation Protection Programmes </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#44:1> The Centre has operated a pilot facility for the monitoring of

occupational exposure to radiation using thermoluminescence dosimetry since

1984.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#45:1> The funds for the equipment - a Vinten Universal Toledo 654D TLD

Reader, 623 <sp> 90 </sp> Sr irradiator and 6221E annealing unit - were

provided by the International Atomic Energy Agency ( IAEA).
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#46:1> During this phase, about 150 radiation workers from hospitals,

laboratories and industries were monitored.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#47:1> The success of this programme lead to the expansion of the project in

1992 to create a national facility for monitoring on an island-wide basis.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#48:1> This is being funded by the IAEA, U.W.I. and the Government of

Jamaica. The IAEA funded the purchase of a Harshaw 6600 automated TLD reader

with a built in <sp> 90 </sp> Sr irradiation station.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#49:1> Dose evaluation is done with a TLD Glow Curve deconvolution programme

and Harshaw REMS ( radiation evaluation and management system) software.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#50:1> The possibility of extending the service within the Caribbean Region

is also being explored. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#51:1> <h> <bold> Applications and National Projects </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#52:1> <h> <it> Development of GRASS GIS </it> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#53:1> The Centre is involved in several national programmes in which

very large SQL compliant relational databases are generated, stored and

manipulated on a MicroVax minicomputer.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#54:1> Much of the data being compiled have been obtained through the

Centre's research projects and are geographically referenced, and represent

information on the geochemistry of the environment and on human and livestock

health parameters in Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#55:1> In order to effectively manage this data and to allow easy public

sector access to the information, a Geographical Information System ( GIS) is

being developed.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#56:1> The system uses GRASS ( i.e. Geographical Resources Analysis Support

System) software running on a Sunsparc station 1+ as the graphical interface

with the Vax SQL database. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#57:1> <h> <it> Projects </it> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#58:1> The Centre's major programmes have involved collaboration with

both international and local organisations including the British Geological

Survey ( BGS), the Geological Survey of Jamaica ( GSJ), the Natural Resource

& Conservation Agency of Jamaica ( NRCA), the Scientific Research Council of

Jamaica ( SRC), and the Underground Water Authority ( UWA) of Jamaica. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#59:1> The major projects to date include: </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#60:1> i) <it> Orientation studies for regional geochemical surveys in

the Caribbean </it> <sp> 1,2,3 </sp> - CNS, SRC and BGS. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#61:1> The first phase was carried out in Jamaica and was based on six

sites representative of a wide range of geological and environmental

conditions occurring in the island.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#62:1> It identified an integrated sampling and analytical procedure to be

used systematically to produce geochemical maps of the island.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#63:1> The programme is to be extended to the Caribbean islands in 1993.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#64:1> ii) <it> Gold exploration studies </it> - GSJ. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#65:1> The <quote> " in-pool irradiation site" </quote> <sp> 4 </sp> was

installed just outside the aluminium container of the reactor so that 36

samples can be irradiated at a time, at a maximum flux of approx. 2 x 10 <sp>

11 </sp> n cm <sp> - 2 </sp> s <sp> - 1 </sp> .
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#66:1> This site is being used for the irradiation of sediments, soils and

heavy metal concentrates primarily for the determination of gold.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#67:1> This is part of a CIDA funded programme, <quote> " A Metallic Mineral

Survey of Jamaica." </quote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#68:1> Some 80 samples are analysed per week. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#69:1> iii) <it> The geochemistry of Jamaican soils </it> - CNS staff.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#70:1> Soil samples ( A, B, and C horizons) were collected from nearly

three hundred sites across the island.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#71:1> They were analysed by NAA for twenty-three elements to determine the

typical concentration ranges of elements and to identify areas of high or low

concentrations which would require more detailed sampling.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#72:1> Colour-coded maps showing the regional variation in the background

elemental concentrations across the island, are being prepared. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#73:1> iv) <it> A study of the quality and hydrogeochemistry of ground

and surface waters in Jamaica and island-wide ambient air quality

measurements </it> - CNS staff and collaborators <+> . </+> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#74:1> These studies are part of an Inter-American Development Bank (

IDB) funded programme to set up an environmental monitoring strategy for

Jamaica with the Centre as the principal executing agency. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#75:1> The project is being carried out in collaboration with various

Government agencies including the UWA, the NRCA.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#76:1> Background concentrations of trace elements and various pollutants

are being established in water, sediments and air particulates, and areas

with high levels are being targeted for follow-up studies. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#77:1> vi) <it> Radiometric surveys of Jamaica </it> <sp> 5 </sp> - CNS

staff. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#78:1> Total gamma radiation levels were measured with a vehicle mounted

detector traversing some 3000 km of roads throughout Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#79:1> These data and data on the radiation levels in Jamaican homes are

being mapped and used to determine average exposure rates to the Jamaican

population.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#80:1> Further measurements are elucidating the contribution of the uranium

and thorium daughters and potassium-40 to the gamma radiation levels. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#81:1> <h> <it> Training </it> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#82:1> The Centre has also provided training and facilities for

research, graduate and undergraduate teaching programmes within the

university community.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#83:1> Research and graduate projects have usually been done in

collaboration with the CNS's staff and have included: </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#84:1> i) <it> Studies of trace metals in stream sediments of the

Wagwater Basin </it> <sp> 6 </sp> - MPhil degree ( 1988) in Geology. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#85:1> Some 60 stream sediments from the Wagwater Basin which shows

evidence of mineralisation, were analysed for 19 elements by NAA and 5

elements by atomic absorption spectrometry. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#86:1> ii) <it> Trace metals in marine sediments and plants </it> -

MPhil degree ( 1987) in Chemistry </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#87:1> This work was part of a multi-disciplinary study of the coastal

ecosystems of the Hellshire Bay, Jamaica and involved research on the coral

reefs, beaches, coastal wetlands and the chemical composition of the sea

sediments.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#88:1> Some 70 samples of aquatic plants and sediments were analysed by NAA.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#89:1> iii) <it> Atmospheric dust in Kingston </it> - MPhil degree (

1992) in Chemistry. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#90:1> The levels and types of dust around Kingston were studied to

determine the level of dust pollution in the city and to assess the extent to

which the power station at Hunt's Bay contributes to it.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#91:1> Dustfall jars and air sampling pumps were used for sample collection.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#92:1> Trace element content was determined using NAA. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#93:1> iv) <it> Elemental concentrations in the Negril Morass, Jamaica

</it> <sp> 7 </sp> - MPhil degree ( 1990) in Chemistry. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#94:1> Six sites were sampled in duplicate at three depths and analysed

for twenty-five elements as a baseline study on the elemental concentrations

in the Negril Peat Morass. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#95:1> v) <it> Lichen as environmental monitors </it> <sp> 8 </sp> - PhD

degree ( 1987) in Botany. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#96:1> Lichens were used as environmental indicators for certain major,

minor and trace elements.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#97:1> Two species of lichen were transplanted into locations around

industrial plants and analysed after various periods of exposure for 17

elements by NAA. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#98:1> vi) <it> Geophagia studies </it> <sp> 9 </sp> - joint study with

Tropical Metabolism Research Unit ( TMRU). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#99:1> The use of chemical markers as indicators of geophagia was

investigated.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#100:1> Five volunteers each ingested capsules of three common soils in

Jamaica and a pre-ingestion and all post-ingestion faecal samples were

collected.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#101:1> These were analysed by NAA for 14 elements and 5 of the latter

selected for possible candidates as markers. </p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#107:2> <h> <bold> 1. Introduction </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#108:2> The processing of bauxite into alumina by the Bayer process is

one of the major industrial operations on the island of Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#109:2> When all four of the existing plants are in operation, a total of

almost three million tonnes of alumina is extracted for export annually,

leaving behind about fifteen million tonnes of slurry-like residue.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#110:2> This waste, which is known locally as red mud because of its

characteristic reddish-brown col <l> our and slimy consistency, is usually

discharged into natural or artificially created valleys.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#111:2> This method of disposal, practised by the industry for the past

three decades, has left behind several large red mud lakes which are, at

best, unsightly features of the landscape in certain parts of the island.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#112:2> More significantly how <l> ever, these lakes are potentially major

sources of pollution for the surrounding environment.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#113:2> The situ <l> ation is described graphically by Bell [ 1]. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-032#114:2> These factors have been influential in directing re <l> search

efforts towards finding some application which would result in large scale

use of the mud, and so at least slow down the creation of new lakes.
<ICE-JA:W2A-032#115:2> The invest <l> igation of its potential use as a building material

is one such effort. </I>

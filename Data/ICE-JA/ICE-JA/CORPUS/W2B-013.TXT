<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#1:1> <bold> IV </bold> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#2:1> <h> <bold> The Gulf Crisis </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#3:1> <h> <bold> <it> The Annexation of Kuwait </bold> </it> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#4:1> The dramatic and far-reaching events in Eastern and Western Europe

are of such magnitude and have such considerable implications that they would

have been enough to keep the world, and Jamaica along with it, fully occupied

for some time in analysis and speculation in sheer curiosity.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#5:1> But there was more to come. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#6:1> Even in this age of surprises when almost every day some new

development occurs, the invasion and annex <l> ation of Kuwait by Iraq stood

out, drawing much of the attention of the world away from Europe.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#7:1> Iraq's actions brought the danger of a Middle East conflagration with

repercussions far beyond that region.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#8:1> In addition, the seizure of Kuwait and its oil-fields resulted in a

steep rise in the price of oil, and this delivered a shock to the global

economy, putting Jamaica and a great many other countries into economic

disarray. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#9:1> As a result of the invasion, the United States of America, along

with a number of other countries, built up a large military presence in the

Gulf area, particularly in Saudi Arabia.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#10:1> The situation has been unique by reason of the fact that the Soviet

Union has stood shoulder to shoulder with the United States and other Western

countries in their determination to oppose Saddam Hussein, the President of

Iraq.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#11:1> Had such an event taken place just a few short years ago, the

situation would have been very different indeed.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#12:1> As a long-standing friend of Iraq and in an adversarial position vis

&agrave; vis the United States and others in respect of Middle Eastern

affairs, the Soviet Union could well have been on the other side.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#13:1> In 1990, however, for the first time in the postwar years, the two

superpowers joined to form a solid front in opposition to an aggressor. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#14:1> The Soviet Union voted positively on the Security Council

resolutions, but has sought to play a diplomatic role designed to achieve a

peaceful settlement while at the same time not mincing words about its

opposition to the action of Iraq and its determination to see the reversal of

the take-over of Kuwait. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#15:1> <h> <bold> <it> The New World Order </it> </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#16:1> The united stance of the Soviet Union and the United States of

America in this matter led President Bush to make the most far-reaching

statements.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#17:1> In an address to Congress in September 1990, he spoke of the

emergence of <quote> 'a new partnership of nations which stood aligned

against Iraq's occupation of Kuwait'. </quote>
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#18:1> He continued: <quote> 'We stand at a unique and extraordinary moment.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#19:1> The crisis in the Persian Gulf, as grave as it is, is a rare

opportunity to move towards an historic period of cooperation.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#20:1> Out of these troubled times, a new world order can emerge; a new era

 freer from the threat of terror, stronger in the pursuit of justice, and

more secure in the quest for peace.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#21:1> An era in which the nations of the world, east and west, north and

south, can prosper and live in harmony.' </quote>
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#22:1> He spoke further of <quote> 'a world quite different from the one we

have known.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#23:1> A world where the rule of law supplants the rule of the jungle.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#24:1> A world in which nations recognise the shared responsibility for

freedom and justice.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#25:1> A world where the strong respect the rights of the weak.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#26:1> This is the vision I shared with President Gorbachev in Helsinki.'

</quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#27:1> This extraordinary statement demonstrates the impact of the

developments which have been taking place, embracing those in Europe  both

East and West  and in the Gulf. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#28:1> In the months following Iraq's invasion of Kuwait the world

turned to the United Nations.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#29:1> The Security Council held a large number of meetings on this crisis

and approved more than twelve resolutions condemning the action of Iraq,

calling for its withdrawal from Kuwait, opening the way to the use of force

by the United States and other countries. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#30:1> In the face of the forces opposing him, Saddam Hussein held the

large number of Westerners in Iraq and Kuwait hostage, in order to use them

as a human shield against attack.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#31:1> This caused great distress and anger.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#32:1> Their sudden release later stunned the world. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#33:1> As the coalition countries built up forces in the Gulf area a

procession of prominent personalities, some current, some former leaders,

travelled to Baghdad in search of a peaceful solution to the crisis, or in

hope of obtaining the release of some of the hostages. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#34:1> Then, on 16 January 1991, after the expiration of the deadline

set by the UN Security Council for the withdrawal of Iraq from Kuwait, the

allied forces went to war  the first major conflict of the post Cold War

era. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#35:1> <h> <bold> <it> Impact on the World in General </it> </bold> </h>

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#36:1> I can only give here a brief summary of some of the considerable

implications of this war.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#37:1> For the world to be brought back to such a juncture has been a matter

of deep disappointment, the more so since the hope for peace had been

considerably boosted by the ending of the Cold War. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#38:1> This explains the persistence of the peace movement, and the

demonstrations against war which have been held, particularly in the Western

World. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#39:1> The vast financial and other resources being con <l> sumed by the

war represent a bitter irony as the world seeks to muster the will and the

resources to tackle more purposefully the problem of growing poverty, the

critical situation of Africa, the suffering of tens of millions of children,

the dangers posed by the continuing degrada <l> tion of the environment, and

the spread of <smallcaps> AIDS </smallcaps> across the globe. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#40:1> Developing countries are the main losers by way of this diversion

of resources.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#41:1> Moreover, there has been talk of a Marshall type plan for the

reconstruction of the Gulf region at the end of hostilities. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#42:1> The world faces an escalation of what are labelled <quote> 'terrorist'

</quote> incidents inspired, it is assumed, by the call by Saddam Hussein for

attacks on the interests of the members of the coalition.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#43:1> As a result, extraordinary security measures have had to be

instituted by these countries.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#44:1> The truth is that war can no longer be defined in conventional terms.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#45:1> The weak will seek their own ways of striking back no matter what the

judgement may be concerning those methods. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#46:1> There is the sobering fact that weapons and other devices sold to

Iraq by Western states and the USSR might well be used against the coalition

forces.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#47:1> These sales amounted to an estimated US$6 billion between 1983 and

1988. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#48:1> The technological sophistication of the weapons and other devices

being used in the conflict has led to it being characterised as the <quote> 'Nintendo

war' </quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#49:1> Related to this is the US call for restrictions, which it is hoped

would also be applied by its allies, on the export of chemical and biological

materials and missile technologies to Third World countries.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#50:1> The fear of proliferation of nuclear and biological weapons,

including missiles, is very real.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#51:1> It was also reported in the <it> New York Times </it> , of 21 January

1991 that some experts now warn that establishing new restrictions on

so-called North-South trade between industrial and developing countries would

heighten mistrust of the West among emerging coun <l> tries.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#52:1> The restrictions would apply to some dual-purpose technologies with

both military and commercial appli <l> cations, for example, computer systems

and software, and technologies for increasing agricultural yields.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#53:1> In February 1991, Prime minister Brian Mulroney of Canada called for

a global summit to stem the flow of high technology arms and weapons of mass

destruction to countries such as Iraq.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#54:1> He plans to propose this to the UN Secretary General and to ask for

further follow-up meetings. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#55:1> In addition to all this, deep concern has been express <l> ed

about the possibility of a major ecological disaster resulting from use of

chemical or nuclear weapons or by way of damage to oil installations.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#56:1> In fact, a major oil spill has occurred, with each side accusing the

other of being responsible. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#57:1> <h> <bold> <it> Impact on Foreign Workers </it> </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#58:1> The opportunities offered by the presence of oil in the Gulf area

meant that, over a long period, hundreds of thousands of people arrived from

Asia, Europe and elsewhere  including skilled workers and professionals.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#59:1> In some instances the immigrant populations seemed likely to

outnumber the host citizens.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#60:1> A long- term problem was taking shape, in any event, centring around

the civil and political rights of these workers and their families.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#61:1> But their sudden departure as a result of the Iraqi invasion of

Kuwait created a major refugee problem, entailed much suffering and also left

great nervousness and sensitivity.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#62:1> People from Pakistan, Bangladesh, the Philippines, Vietnam and other

developing countries, as well as from the West, rushed to escape as the

prospect of war loomed, and as the Iraqi forces took over, literally crushing

Kuwait.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#63:1> How many would wish to return when things calm down is a matter for

speculation.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#64:1> The attractions are great, but so are the dangers.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#65:1> And, in general, a consciousness of the instability of the region

will remain. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#66:1> Most of the refugees lost their employment and their property,

while their families abroad lost the massive remittances sent to them by the

estimated half a million workers.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#67:1> The home countries now face the terrible prob <l> lem of receiving

and resettling these workers.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#68:1> Most of these countries already have critical unemployment problems

and will be faced with serious difficulties with the return flow. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#69:1> We can imagine what would happen to Jamaica if large numbers of

past emigrants were suddenly forced to return.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#70:1> Indeed, the return of Jamaicans in the 1930s, the years of the great

depression in the United States, is regarded as being one of the factors in

the upheavals of that period. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#71:1> <h> <bold> <it> The UN and the Crisis </it> </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#72:1> The UN was regarded in the earlier stages of the Gulf crisis as

having come back to the centre stage, especially in respect of the area of

international peace and security.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#73:1> The organisation was seen by the US, the Soviet Union and others as

showing its ability to occupy that position in world affairs for which it was

originally intended. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#74:1> But as the war has proceeded there has been growing doubt in some

quarters.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#75:1> Feelings have been expressed that the UN was coerced into giving a

licence to the US and its allies to wage unlimited war on Iraq or that the

attacks have gone beyond what was implied or intended in the Council's

resolution. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#76:1> These feelings have been sharpened by the effects of the heavy

bombardment by allied forces and the seeming reluctance on the part of the

Council to meet, once the war had started.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#77:1> When it did meet the session, at the insistence of the US and others,

was a closed one. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#78:1> In considering these views, and there are of course many who

support the UN in this situation, it is well to recognise that that

organisation is not an independent entity, capable of making its own

decisions but an intergovernmental body in which decisions are made by its

members.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#79:1> The Security Council's resolutions were approved by a majority of the

fifteen members of that body.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#80:1> But, of course, this is not to deny the possibilities of the exercise

of influence or of pressure on members. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#81:1> <h> <bold> <it> The West and the War </it> </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#82:1> While the coalition partners in the Western world have in general

maintained their solidarity and their support, there are signs of uneasiness.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#83:1> These have become more evident as the intensive bombing of Iraq has

continued and as news reports speak increasingly of civilian casualties.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#84:1> The launching of the ground assault by the allied forces would lead

to increased casualties adding to the concern in the countries of the

alliance and giving greater impetus to the anti-war movement. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-013#85:1> It appears that the position of the European Com <l> munity has

been significantly affected by the conflict.
<ICE-JA:W2B-013#86:1> A sharp comment on this was made in the New York Times of 25 January

1991 by Craig Whitney who stated that the Gulf war has halted the momentum

toward political unity in Europe, breathed new life into the special

relationship between the United States and Britain, and raised widespread

concern about pacifism in Germany. </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-025#25:2> <h> <bold> Parks protecting Caribbean marine environment </bold>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-025#26:2> <bold> PRESERVATION OF the marine environment is essential for

countries whose economies revolve around the marketing of their immediate

coastal resources. </bold> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-025#27:2> Almost all Caribbean territories sell packages of sun, sand and

sea to tourists from the temperate climes, offering them a chance to <quote>

" get away from it all" </quote> , and sit down and relax.
<ICE-JA:W2B-025#28:2> Those countries with fine white sand beaches and healthy, accessible

coral reefs gen <l> erally have more marketable prod <l> ucts than those who

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-025#29:2> More and more, these countries are beginning to realise that the

to protect these valuable nat <l> ural resources. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-025#31:2> <h> <bold> DEVELOPED </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-025#32:2> Two such parks are the St. John National Park, located in the

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-025#33:2> The U.S. Virgin Islands consists of three main islands; St.

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-025#35:2> St. John has a population of approximately 4,500 persons, and

<ICE-JA:W2B-025#36:2> The St. John National Park is part of the United States National

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-025#59:3> <h> From the Minister of the Public Service and the Environment

</h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-025#60:3> AS we observe World Environment Day and the start of

Environmental Awareness Week for 1993, all Jamaicans should be deeply

concerned at the signs of environmental stress, and the serious deterioration

of local and international environmental Awareness Week, <quote> " Poverty

and the Environment" </quote> . </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-025#61:3> Unless we address and solve problems related to sound physical

planning and appropriate environmental management measures, and attain

consensus on sustainable development, the well-being of both present and

future generations cannot be assured.
<ICE-JA:W2B-025#62:3> Any delay in taking action to stop the continuous environmental

deterioration, may result in permanent and irreversible damage to the fragile

ecological integrity of this <quote> " land of wood and water." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-025#63:3> The current threat to the environment, which is becoming a

concern of all citizens, sectors, and indeed all mankind, stems essentially

from lack of understanding, and past neglect, in the management of our

natural resources.
<ICE-JA:W2B-025#64:3> The Jamaican environment has been degraded by decades of poor

agricultural practices, industrial pollution, inadequate and unsafe disposal

of sewage, inappropriate use of hillside lands , and non-sustainable

practices in agriculture, fishery and forestry. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-025#65:3> Developing countries such as the island of Jamaica also face

distinct environmental problems arising from size, scale, poverty and

population pressure. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-025#66:3> To achieve sustainable development, economic growth is a

compelling necessity.
<ICE-JA:W2B-025#67:3> However, sustainable development implies the incorporation of

environmental concerns into economic planning and policies at personal,

community, and national levels, as well as within firms and commercial

entities. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-025#68:3> The designation of June 5 as World Environment Day followed the

1972 Environmental Conference in Stockholm.
<ICE-JA:W2B-025#69:3> This year also marks the first anniversary of the Rio <quote> " Earth

Summit" </quote> , at which over one hundred Heads of State made important

commitments related to the protection of the Climate, the Earth's

Biodiversity and, indeed, led to the acceptance and endorsement of a global

plan of environmental action, known as <quote> " AGENDA 21" </quote> to take

us into the 21st Century without continuing the environmentally inappropriate

actions of the past and present. </p>

<P> <ICE-JA:W2B-025#70:3> We in Jamaica marked on June 5, the second anniversary of the

passing of the Natural Resources Conservation Authority ( NRCA) Act of 1991,

and the establishment of the NRCA on June 5, 1991. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-025#71:3> The Natural Resources Conservation Authority has a very wide

mandate, covering environmental education, coastal zone management, watershed

management, pollution control, conservation of protected areas and species,

waste management and national parks policy.
<ICE-JA:W2B-025#72:3> It is still in the process of establishing regulations,

organisational arrangements, enforcements for appropriate liaison with

government and with the private sector, community groups and non-governmental

organisations are being given particular attention. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-025#73:3> Already, the Natural Resources Conservation Authority has

implemented new arrangements for monitoring environmental impacts, and

ensuring community participation in the review of Environmental Assessments

and Audits.
<ICE-JA:W2B-025#74:3> It is in the process of implementing Pollution Reduction measures

involving both governmental and private sector polluters.
<ICE-JA:W2B-025#75:3> It intends to move beyond the present regime of voluntary compliance

with environmental standards, to a regime of mandatory compliance by early

1994.
<ICE-JA:W2B-025#76:3> The necessary studies required to form the basis of clean-up of

specific damaged ecosystems such as the Kingston Harbour, are well underway,

and the public will see the long-term measures to reverse the damage done

over the several decades of neglect being put in place in a very systematic

and methodical manner.
<ICE-JA:W2B-025#77:3> The measures for improvement in environmental management capacity

will not only involve the NRCA but, of necessity, must also involve

improvements in the capacity of related agencies in the public service, such

as the Town Planning Department, the Environmental Control Division, and key

resource management agencies, such as those responsible for Fisheries,

Forestry and Water Resource Management. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-025#78:3> Finally, the government is committed to the participation and

involvement of individuals and Community-Based Organisations ( CBO's) in the

environmental thrust, since it is the individual whose environmental ethics,

and attitudes, who will ultimately secure the <quote> " sustainable

development" </quote> culture and future toward which we aspire.
<ICE-JA:W2B-025#79:3> The recently established Environmental Foundation of Jamaica, based

on an intergovernmental agreement between the United States and Jamaican

Governments, and the Canadian-sponsored Green Fund, now in the final stages

of being established, will provide resources in the form of grants and

technical support, to allow community-based and non-governmental

organisations to execute meaningful environmental projects. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-025#80:3> Our Prime Minister, in his speech at Rio last June pointed out

that <quote> " it is in our hands that the solutions to environmental

problems rest&" </quote>
<ICE-JA:W2B-025#81:3> Let us continue to work together on finding solutions to these

problems, in our communities, at work, and in all aspects of our national

life, to ensure that we preserve our environment for future generations to

enjoy. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-025#86:4> <h> <bold> Planting </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-025#87:4> THE potato is best propa <l> gated by whole tubers ( seeds).

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-025#92:4> Rows should be 81-91 cms ( 32-36 inches) apart and plants should

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-025#93:4> <h> <bold> Fertility Requirements </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-025#94:4> The Irish Potato needs lots of nitrogen and potash.

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-025#97:4> At high rates of Potash ( K20) application i.e. above 168 kg/ha (

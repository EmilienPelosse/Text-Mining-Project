<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-018#1:1> <h> <bold> THE CHOICE OF TEACHING AS A CAREER </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-018#2:1> Hyacinth Evans </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-018#3:1> <quote> <it> " I thought this was one of the most important ways

in which I could make a vital contribution to my country." </it> </quote>

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-018#4:1> <quote> <it> " I love to work with people and to help people and

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-018#7:1> How does one choose an occupation in our society?
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#8:1> The answer to this question is not always straight-forward.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#9:1> Obviously individual inter <l> ests and aptitudes will play a

significant part in one's decision.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#10:1> But the attractiveness of the work or occupation is also important.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#11:1> The access to information <}> <-> abut </-> <+> about </+> </}>

occupations, and specific circumstances which facilitate or inhibit entry to

a particular occupation at a particular time need also to be considered.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#12:1> Individual or personal factors thus interact with social, economic

and circumstantial ones in one's choice of an occupation. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-018#13:1> Ginzburg et al ( 1951) in their attempt to develop what they

called a comprehensive theory of occupational choice identified some key

variables which operate in the choice process.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#14:1> They concluded that choice is a developmental process, or a series of

decisions over a period of years.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#15:1> It is very often influenced by significant persons in one's life -

e.g., parents, teachers, peers - and involves subjective consider <l> ations

as well as <quote> " awareness of external reality" </quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#16:1> The range of choice also influences the process which can be depicted

in stages, with fantasy choices being made at an early age ( about 11 years),

tentative choices made at about age 17, and more realistic choices being made

later after exposure to the work itself. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-018#17:1> Ginzburg et al developed a general theory without reference to

any specific occupation.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#18:1> Their conclusions were based on case studies of a cross section of

young American adults.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#19:1> The theory was shaped by the responses of upper class young men, with

women and lower income young men treated as variations.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#20:1> The dynamics of decision making may operate differently in specific

occupations and in differ <l> ent social and cultural contexts.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#21:1> In such cases some variables may be more salient in decision making

than others. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-018#22:1> Teaching as an occupation has some special features which may

make the choice process unique.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#23:1> For example, most if not all persons in a society become aware of

teaching as an occupation by virtue of their compulsory attendance at school.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#24:1> If an awareness of the occupation is an important factor as Ginzburg

et al contend, then teaching is a special occupation.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#25:1> In addition, teaching appeals to that desire to correct and improve,

which Stephens ( 1969) claims the human race developed as a means of survival

and which can in some individuals develop into a sense of mission.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#26:1> In his evolutionary account of the origins of basic pedagogical

tendencies, Stephens argues that human beings have survived because of these

deeply ingrained impulses which are lived out naturally in classrooms and

families.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#27:1> Thirdly, by virtue of an extended exposure to teachers and teaching,

students may come to perceive the occupation as having certain qualities

which they consider attractive.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#28:1> For example, the teacher may conceivably be seen as one who exercises

power, authority or control over others, or one who is kind and loving, or

one who imparts knowledge to others.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#29:1> Since the teacher is a significant other in <w> students' </w> lives,

he or she is capable of exercising indirect influence on one's early

identification with or even choice of teaching as an occupation.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#30:1> Indeed, students may view becoming a teacher as one way of becoming

like a " significant other" in their lives. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-018#31:1> Research on the sociolization of teachers suggests that these

early experiences are very powerful and enduring, and often do influence <w>

students' </w> decisions to become a teacher ( Lortie 1975, Goodlad 1984).
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#32:1> Research also suggests that new recruits to teaching view teaching as

easy and requiring little specialized learning ( Book, Byers and Freeman,

1983).
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#33:1> The teacher's nurturing, interpersonal and caring qualities, which no

doubt have great effect on them as students, become salient in their

conception of teaching ( Weinstein 1989).
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#34:1> Though this line of research in socialization seeks to identify

sources of dispositions, models of teaching and other pedagogical tendencies,

we can deduce from this research some special features of teaching which

serve to attract the young. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-018#35:1> The dynamics of occupational choice in particular social/cul <l>

tural contexts invite special examination.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#36:1> In non-industrialized coun <l> tries for example, there may exist a

narrow range of choices with respect to the number of occupations that exist.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#37:1> The individual in these countries may have a narrow range of choice

because of lack of information or because of the absence of the financial or

educational qualifications which provide access to the particular occupation.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#38:1> In such cases, early influences and decisions taken at what Ginzburg

et al call the fantasy stage may have greater salience than in other

countries in which the individual has a wide range of choice, or where

occupational counselling is available.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#39:1> And factors which ease one's entry to an occupation such as

availability of scholarships or free tuition for the required training, may

exercise a strong influence on one's occupational choice. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-018#40:1> <h> <bold> THE CASE OF JAMAICA </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-018#41:1> Jamaica is an interesting country for the study of the choice of

teaching as an occupation.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#42:1> Up to the 1960s, and even about a decade after independence in 1962,

the commercial sector grew slowly and new categories of jobs were established

infrequently.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#43:1> Colonialist attitudes restricted the access of the majority of black

Jamaicans - male and female - to jobs in the commercial and tourism sectors.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#44:1> Many of the relatively small percentage of students who graduated

from the elementary school ( the school for the masses), or who obtained the

then third Jamaica Local Examination, entered teaching.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#45:1> Teaching was regarded as an attractive occupation for both men and

women alike.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#46:1> In small rural communities and urban centres, the teacher and the

minister were the respectable leaders in the community. ( Goulbourne 1988 p.

73-6). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-018#47:1> Blocked access to alternative occupations and the high status of

the teaching profession at that time only partly explain the importance of

teaching as an occupational choice then.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#48:1> Access to a secondary education was even more limited than it is now,

with only about 2% of the graduates of the elementary school being admitted

to secondary high schools in 1948 ( King, cited in Evans 1991).
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#49:1> Conse <l> quently, the teacher colleges were often used as a means of

gaining a secondary education by the sons and daughters of the black popula

<l> tion.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#50:1> Teacher colleges came to be known as the black man's high school.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-018#51:1> Since the 1970s there has been an expansion of occupations in the

commercial and tourism sectors.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#52:1> New jobs in computer programming, personnel administration and

training have been created.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#53:1> At the same time, this expansion has created additional openings in

areas such as banking and insurance.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#54:1> These new positions have acquired a certain status, associated with

what is <quote> 'modern' </quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#55:1> They provide attractive alternatives to teaching as a career for

graduates of secondary educa <l> tion or of the teachers colleges.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#56:1> The status of teaching has also declined partly because of its

unfavourable comparison with these new occupa <l> tions, but also because of

what many see as changes in student characteristics and a deterioration in

working conditions.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#57:1> This change has affected teacher recruitment, retention and

satisfaction in other countries ( e.g., Schlecty and Vance 1983,

Feiman-Nemser and Floden 1986).
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#58:1> In Jamaica, limited research ( Coke 1990) and anecdotal reports

indicate that teachers are disenchanted with <w> students' </w> behaviour,

lack of <quote> 'discipline' </quote> and their learning and achievement.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#59:1> Harsh conditions and at times the absence of equipment and resources,

especially in the all-age and primary schools, have reduced the

attractiveness and the status of teaching.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#60:1> Above all, however, <w> teachers' </w> salaries have not kept pace

with the rapidly rising cost of living.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#61:1> All these factors have operated to make teaching a less automatic

choice than it was twenty or thirty years ago. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-018#62:1> The consequence of this altered situation has been a recent

reduction in the number of applicants to the teachers colleges and the flight

of experienced teachers from the profession.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#63:1> For although there was an increased demand for teachers during the

1960s as a result of the government's policy of expanding educational

opportunities, there has since 1980 been a drastic reduction in the number of

candidates admitted to colleges.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#64:1> For example, in 1979, 1,628 students were admitted to eight colleges.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#65:1> This figure was reduced to 728 in 1981, when higher qualifications

for entry were introduced, increased to 965 in 1985 and later reduced to 640

in 1990 ( JBTE Annual Statistics, 1979, 1981, 1985, 1990.)
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#66:1> ( Data for four of the colleges have been omitted because these

colleges were either established after 1979 or ceased functioning prior to

1990.) </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-018#67:1> However, the teachers colleges continue to provide an alterna <l>

tive route to a tertiary education.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#68:1> The teachers college programmes are used as the equivalent of grades

12 and 13 of the traditional high school, and graduates gain automatic

matriculation to the University of the West Indies.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#69:1> For the candidate who wants to gain admission to the University, the

teachers college may be seen as an attractive alternative to grades 12 and 13

of the high school, since the failure rate in the A'level exams sat at the

end of grade 13 is very high.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#70:1> Thus many young secondary level school graduates have a strong

incentive for attending the teachers college, though it is not clear that

they all intend to become teachers. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-018#71:1> <h> <bold> RATIONALE AND PURPOSE OF THE STUDY </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-018#72:1> Given this background, it became important to discover why those

who enter teaching decide to do so.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#73:1> What from among the many factors cited by Ginzburg et al was most

influential?
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#74:1> Since <quote> " every &dotted-line; decision &dotted-line; is a

combination of internal and external factors" </quote> , ( Ginzburg et al p.

16) and since in Jamaica there are internal and external factors that can be

considered equally powerful, which ones exercised the greatest influence?

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-018#75:1> We sought to identify these reasons and influences through

open-ended items included in a questionnaire developed as part of a larger

study which examined the impact of the teacher education programme of the

Jamaican teachers colleges. <fnr> <sp> 1 </sp> </fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#76:1> 1. The study of the impact of the teacher education programme was

conceived by Professors Lilian Katz of the University of Illinois - Urbana

and Jim Raths of the University of Delaware who also helped in the

development of the questionnaire.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#77:1> Data were collected and analyzed by the author and Dr. Monica Brown

of the University of the West Indies.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#78:1> The contribution of these persons is gratefully acknowledged.

</footnote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-018#79:1> <h> <bold> RESEARCH DESIGN </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-018#80:1> This study combined ethnographic and survey research methods.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#81:1> The sample consisted of all the 1989 graduates of ten of eleven

teachers colleges and departments in Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#82:1> The 21-item questionnaire, which sought to examine the impact of the

teacher education programme, included questions related to problems

experienced, the use of knowledge learned in college, and the college courses

which were most useful.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#83:1> The questionnaire was mailed to the graduates during the second

semester of the 1989-90 academic year while they were or should have been in

their first year of teaching. <fnr> <sp> 2 </sp> </fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#84:1> <ICE-JA:W2A-018:2. Many of those who graduate from the t eachers colleges do not in

fact enter teaching.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#85:1> However, the percentage of those who don't is unknown. </footnote>

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-018#86:1> The number of these graduates in 1989 was 270.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#87:1> Twenty of the respondents from four of the fourteen parishes were

chosen at random for indepth interviewing.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#88:1> During the interviews, additional probes were used where necessary.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#89:1> The two items included in the questionnaire and on the interview

schedule which pertain to choice of teaching were: </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-018#90:1> 1. What is the main reason why you chose to become a teacher?

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-018#91:1> 2. What other reasons made you decide to become a teacher? </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-018#92:1> These two questions, it was felt, would elicit the primary and

second <l> ary reasons which prompted them to make this occupational choice.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-018#93:1> A total of 108 first year teachers or 40.0% of the sample

responded to the questionnaire.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#94:1> Responses were analyzed for each question and coded according to the

categories and themes which emerged.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#95:1> Categories were combined and refined during analysis until a

manageable number of categories was evident.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#96:1> The number and percentages of those whose responses fell within a

category were then calculated.
<ICE-JA:W2A-018#97:1> A similar method was used in the analysis of the interview data. </p>

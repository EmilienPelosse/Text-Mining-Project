<ICE-JA:W2C-004#1:1> <h> CLASS without teachers </h>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#2:1> WITH only three months left for this year's sitting of Overseas

examinations, students at CLASS day school at the Mona Campus, UWI, say they

are without teachers. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#3:1> CLASS day school for private students is operated by the institute

of Management Science at the Mona Campus, University of the West Indies (

UWI). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#4:1> Some students who sat idly in a class at the school on Tuesday

told the <it> Gleaner </it> that since last term they have been without

teachers for some CXC and GCE subjects for which they have already been

registered to sit in June. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#5:1> The problem, they said, began when some <w> teachers' </w> salary

cheques bounced because the institution was experiencing financial

difficulties.
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#6:1> The problem got worse in January when the teachers, who are primarily

students at the University, sat their exams thus leaving the CLASS students

on their own. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#7:1> However, Executive Director of IMS, Winston Adams denied the

allegations. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#8:1> According to a past teacher who taught at CLASS for three months

last term, in October several teachers received <quote> " bounced"</quote>

cheques as salary payments, due to internal financial problems at the

institution. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#9:1> Consequently, those teachers have not returned to teach at the

institution, the teacher said. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#10:1> Classrooms at the institution on Tuesday were empty except for

several students who were doing work on their own. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#11:1> <quote> " It is really sickening that I come here wasting the day

waiting to have class and the teacher does not turn up," </quote> said one

17-year-old student. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#12:1> Another student said, <quote> " I just got a GCE maths teacher

this morning after the first one left last term, because of <quote> "

bounced" </quote> cheques payments." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#13:1> The students said they were especially worried about sitting some

of the subjects, such as Mathematics, without the help of a teacher. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#14:1> Michelle, another student, said she had requested a refund from

IMS in order to attend another day class, but was told that she was not

entitled to a refund after November. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#15:1> <h> Financial problems </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#16:1> Mr. Adams said, however, that IMS, a business institution, was

not having financial problems but that the CLASS programme which was started

in 1991 was having financial problems in terms of the administration of the

institution. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#17:1> He explained that CLASS, a university undergraduate/postgraduate

programme, was designed to assist private candidates who wished to sit

overseas GCE and CXC subjects. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#18:1> He said CLASS has operated since 1984 as a small project

providing a means of livelihood for university students.
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#19:1> However, due to an increase in the scope of the programme IMS

introduced the day programme at CLASS in 1991.
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#20:1> CLASS is the acronym for Council for Literature, Arts and Science

Students. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#21:1> The problem with the administration of the programme, which is

made up of post and undergraduate students of the UWI started that same year,

he said. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#22:1> He explained that the problem arose when six computers leased at

$110,000 were stolen from the CLASS office at the Mona Campus.
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#23:1> In an effort to settle the debt, IMS took money from the CLASS <w>

teachers' </w> salary pool. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#24:1> <quote> " In order to pay the teachers we had to negotiate with

the bank to go into an overdraft.
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#25:1> They therefore got payment late and this <}> <-> occurred </-> <+>

occurred </+> </}> on two occasions," </quote> Mr. Adams said. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#26:1> He denied the allegations that teachers were paid with " bounced"

cheques, saying that only two or three <w> teachers' </w> cheques were

returned by the bank, as the overdraft was still pending during October when

the next payment was made.
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#27:1> But since October the institution has not had a financial problem, he

said. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#28:1> He said there were no classes without teachers during the last

term but since the start of the year teachers have failed to attend classes

because of semester exams. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#29:1> Mr. Adams said corrective measures to have make-up classes at a

later date were in place and this was conveyed to the students. </p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#30:2> <h> Over 50,000 students to sit CE Exams today </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#31:2> By Mark Dawes </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#32:2> Today more than 50,000 students will sit the annual Common

Entrance Examinations ( CEE). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#33:2> The CEE has, over the years, come under severe criticism with

some even calling for its abolition.
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#34:2> One of the views advanced in the debate is that scholarship winners

perform poorly in their first year of high school, because they are not

sufficiently prepared. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#35:2> The <it> Gleaner </it> sought the views of a number of educators.

They were asked to comment on the question: <quote> " Are you satisfied with

the standard of the Common Entrance Exam?" </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#36:2> George Abrahams, a noted teacher who has been preparing students

for the Common Entrance for many years, gave an emphatic <quote> " No"

</quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#37:2> He contends that in many primary level schools, <quote> " the

standard is too low." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#38:2> <quote> " At least a half of those who are given free places are

not adequately prepared for high school, as the standard in many primary and

prep schools were too low," </quote> he said. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#39:2> In addition, Mr. Abrahams said that the exam was itself too easy,

and where there was a difficult question, it was designed primarily to select

the winners of Government scholarships. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#40:2> Mr. Abrahams, who tutors students at his Chesterfield Avenue home

in Kingston, has 70 schools represented in his classes. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#41:2> University of the West Indies professor, Errol Miller, said

studies show that the Mental Ability section of the exam does not contribute

to the selection process for a free place.
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#42:2> Mental Ability, he said, was in some instances a duplication of the

Maths and English which are separate subject areas in the exam.
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#43:2> To enhance the exam, Prof. Miller suggested that that section be

dropped replaced by Science. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#44:2> Prof. Miller added that <quote> " in a real sense the standard

does not matter. It ( the CEE) is an exam set to select students for high

school, not to measure educational standard." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#45:2> Marcia Stewart, principal of St. Hugh's High School for Girls,

acknowledged deficiencies in the CEE, but nevertheless pointed out that it is

perhaps the best selection method that the country has at this time.
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#46:2> She explained that each academic year a number of students enter the

first form unprepared for high school work.
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#47:2> This weakness, she said, was particularly evident in English as two

of the eight first form classes at the school usually concentrated on

remedial classes in English. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#48:2> Coleridge Barnett, principal of Wolmer's Boy's School, noted that

much of the free place winners entering the school were in need of remedial

classes, particularly in English Language. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#49:2> Hyacinth Bennett, principal of Hydel Preparatory School, was

uncertain where the blame should lie, for the poor performance of free place

holders.
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#50:2> She suggested that a study be done to determine the deficiencies in

the system. </p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#51:3> <h> KRC Restoration plan coming soon </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#52:3> THE Downtown Kingston Development Plan, being spearheaded by the

Kingston Restoration Company ( KRC) to restore the nation's capital to its

former glory, will be ready by the end of March. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#53:3> In its research to devise the plan, the Strategic Planning Group

( SPG) uncovered eight major social and economic issues that are of concern

to the community.
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#54:3> These were disclosed by Dr. Don Hanson of the SPG Tuesday night when

he presented proposals for the development to architects at the PCJ

Auditorium. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#55:3> Noting that the issues were of concern to all sectors of the

society, Dr. Hanson identified security as being at the top of the list.
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#56:3> He said the need for social order <quote> " raised the topic of the

importance of existing laws and the ability and will to govern." </quote>

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#57:3> The second major issue is <quote> " poverty." </quote>
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#58:3> He said <quote> " poverty is of major concern among those who have

been fortunate enough to succeed as well as those who are suffering from the

depth of poverty that we find in inner city conditions." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#59:3> The lack of employment opportunities is also significant.
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#60:3> <quote> " Joblessness," </quote> he noted, was widespread, with

present employment statistics characterising inner city populations in

Kingston and other major cities across the world.
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#61:3> Such factors, he said, create additional problems of pollution and

sanitation, stigma and discrimination. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#62:3> Dr. Hanson said the research also underlined the need for

learning opportunities, especially when it is established that annually

24,000 young Jamaicans, <quote> " those most educated, those most ambitious,

those most advantaged, emigrate to other countries permanently." </quote>
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#63:3> He underscored the resultant economic loss. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#64:3> Also on the list is what Dr. Hanson termed the <quote> " whole

concept of ghettoisation" </quote> of the city, which <quote> " particularly

applies to a ghettoised economy," one in which the community does not profit

from the revenues of the businesses and the activities within its own

district." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#65:3> The Downtown Kingston Development Plan was conceptualised

mid-1992 when the KRC entered into a joint venture with the Urban Development

Corporation ( UDC), the Town Planning Department ( TPD), the Kingston and St.

Andrew Corporation ( KSAC), the Jamaica Chamber of Commerce and the Town and

Country Planning Authority ( TCPA).
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#66:3> The joint venture was created to analyse the issues of a decaying

downtown Kingston and reverse the social and economic consequence it has

produced. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#67:3> It has been noted that the city, with its rich and diverse

heritage, has decayed and is now <quote> " a divided city - a city held

hostage by fear and poverty." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#68:3> The study being undertaken is seen by the architects as <quote> "

a plan of action structured to produce results." </quote>
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#69:3> The effort will involve a broad cross-section of Kingston's

community. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#70:3> The primary issues being investigated and developed into policy

recommendations are: community development and housing; economic development

and tourism; land use and urban design; transportation, infrastructure and

public service, and implementation strategy. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#71:3> The master plan will be structured into three five-year phases,

each identifying and recommending projects, the cost and beneficial

consequences. </p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#72:4> <h> Fair Competition Act soon for Jamaica </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#73:4> TRADE Administrator Phillip Paulwell has said that Jamaica could

have its Fair Competition Act passed into law by the end of next month. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#74:4> The bill is to be presented to Parliament shortly. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#75:4> Mr. Paulwell was speaking at a forum organized by the

newly-formed Consumer Lobby Group recently. Other presenters included

attorneys-at-law, Monica Ladd, Derrick McKoy and Lord Anthony Gifford. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#76:4> The Trade Administrator said that the proposed legislation seeks

to discourage price fixing, double-ticketing, misleading advertising and

abuse of dominant market position. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#77:4> The bill, he said, would not outlaw business mergers and

monopolies, but rather it sought to treat them in relation to the use of such

status. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#78:4> It also makes provision for the creation of a Fair Trading

Commission, and is designed to take action against companies that misuse

their market dominance.
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#79:4> However, the Commission <quote> " will deal favourably with any

monopoly which operates fairly and for the common good, within the limits of

the law." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#80:4> Miss Ladd, in her presentation, expressed the view that <quote> "

What the Act should do is to outlaw an abuse of the consumer, and go ahead

and define that. But it should not say an abuse by a dominant company, as

opposed to an abuse by another company." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#81:4> <h> Objections </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#82:4> She said that as the bill now stands there was insufficient

definition of <quote> " dominant market position" </quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#83:4> Further, dominance in a market is not easy to establish as there was

not enough data available to show what was the market share of various

companies. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#84:4> Miss Ladd also expressed her objections to provisions in the law

that disallowed mergers which are potentially threatening as <quote> " the

premise that basically the bigger you are the 'badder' you get," </quote> was

unsatisfactory. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#85:4> Some powers which the bill has given to the Minister of Commerce,

she said, should properly rest with the Commission.
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#86:4> Miss Ladd added that the Commission, rather than the Minister, should

be determinant of what a <quote> " good agreement" </quote> was between

companies as furnished under Section 3 of the Act. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#87:4> She pointed out that there was a public misconception that the

Act would lower prices.
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#88:4> This is not so, she said, as <quote> " it is designed to allow people

to sell at whatever price they deemed fit.
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#89:4> So, if someone wants to raise their prices sky high, the Act in

principle encourages them to that on the theory that consumers won't buy and

they will be driven out of business." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-004#90:4> Lord Anthony Gifford complained that though the Commission was

empowered to investigate instances of unfair competition, <quote> " its

powers to take action after it had investigated are very

limited&dotted-line;.
<ICE-JA:W2C-004#91:4> It has no teeth." </quote> </p> </I>

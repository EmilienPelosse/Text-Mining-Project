<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-019#3:1> Part of my concern in writing about Walter Rodney <fnr> ( 1)

</fnr> is to develop a political dialogue with my students in Caribbean

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-019#9:1> I intend to revisit Rodney's political activism in 1968 within the

context of a pre-existing social movement in Jamaican urban communities of

the poor that were very heavily influenced by Rastafarianism.
<ICE-JA:W2A-019#10:1> Rodney's expulsion by the Jamaican government was based on fears that

his interaction with the leading figures at the grass-roots could lead to the

emergence of a radical political ideology taking hold among them and that

instability would ensue if such a political movement became an oppositional

force threatening the political system of independent Jamaica that was barely

six years old.
<ICE-JA:W2A-019#11:1> The possibility of a link between the intellec <l> tuals and the

masses seemed to be a source of paranoia in the ruling Jamaica Labour Party,

but so far no hard evidence of Rodney trying to overthrow the Jamaican

government has emerged.
<ICE-JA:W2A-019#12:1> What emerges is hostility by the political elite to black nationalism

coupled with cold war anti-communism, and nar <l> row-minded Jamaican

nationalism which portrayed Rodney as a foreigner from Guyana who was

interfering in national politics.
<ICE-JA:W2A-019#13:1> Rodney symbolised these fears and insecurities within the politi <l>

cal elite.
<ICE-JA:W2A-019#14:1> The latter feared more than anything else that Rodney could give some

coherence to the disparate expressions of resis <l> tance to the political

status quo and for them it was a question of either squashing that type of

criticism or in the case of the People's National Party, then in

parliamentary opposition, co-opting and subordinating it politically.
<ICE-JA:W2A-019#15:1> The Rodney ban in 1968 saw the playing out of these fears. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-019#16:1> <h> <bold> LIFESTYLE AND POLITICS </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-019#17:1> Rodney came to embody the aspirations of politically aware young

economically and politically.
<ICE-JA:W2A-019#18:1> People saw in Rodney qualities they admired.
<ICE-JA:W2A-019#19:1> He was well-educated, well-informed, articulate, yet different from

<ICE-JA:W2A-019#20:1> The clues to Rodney's appeal may be found not simply in the status

<ICE-JA:W2A-019#21:1> He was not an intellectual posturer, neither did he use his immense

knowledge of Africa <fnr> ( 2) </fnr> and the Caribbean to show off his

of colonial and bour <l> geois historians. <footnote>

Publications.
<ICE-JA:W2A-019#23:1> Less well-known are his book-length essays, Rodney ( 1975a, 1975b) "

<ICE-JA:W2A-019#28:1> To them he became Brother Wally.

<ICE-JA:W2A-019#30:1> Moreover the appeal was also based on the extent to which he embodied

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-019#31:1> Rodney <fnr> ( 3) </fnr> spent eight and a half months lecturing

<ICE-JA:W2A-019#35:1> What's going on in this place?" </quote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-019#36:1> They greeted each other warmly and as I was then passing on the way

<ICE-JA:W2A-019#37:1> Winston Davis had a reputation as an outstanding Spanish teacher and

<ICE-JA:W2A-019#38:1> He was also doing research on Cuban literature under the

<ICE-JA:W2A-019#39:1> Because of his visit to Cuba and his activities in the Young

<ICE-JA:W2A-019#40:1> The mid- sixties had seen him making decisions that resulted in his

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-019#42:1> Political attraction to or repulsion from a personality is often

<ICE-JA:W2A-019#43:1> The fact that his education had not alienated him made a big

<ICE-JA:W2A-019#44:1> Rodney was of average height and had the look of a 1960's black

<ICE-JA:W2A-019#45:1> He wore dashikis, had an Afro hair style and a slight beard.
<ICE-JA:W2A-019#46:1> Pat, his wife, wore a low haircut and dressed in African print wraps.

<ICE-JA:W2A-019#50:1> She trav <l> elled with him whenever she had time off from her job as

<ICE-JA:W2A-019#51:1> Pat was very fascinated by Jamaica, how beautiful it was.
<ICE-JA:W2A-019#52:1> But Walter cautioned her, <quote> " there are two sides to Jamaica

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-019#54:1> <quote> Because Walter took me down to Trench Town and I met a

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-019#58:1> He was a well-educated black man who was very comfortable in a

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-019#61:1> His decision to live off campus was deliberate as he loathed what

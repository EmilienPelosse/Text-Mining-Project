<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-032#1:1> <h> <bold> MOTORING IN JAMAICA </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-032#2:1> <h> <typeface> The Early Years </typeface> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-032#3:1> <it> Dr W J Hanna </it> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-032#4:1> <it> <bold> A </bold> lthough the motorcar is today an essential

mode of transport in Jamaica, it is impossible to ascertain the exact date on

which the first one arrived in the island.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#5:1> According to an Editorial published in the Daily Gleaner of January

29, 1906, </it> </p>

<p> <X> <quote> the first motor car that reached our shores was owned by Mr L

D. Baker, Junior, at Port Antonio, in 1900.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X6:1> Mr. E Nuttall, the well known Kingston solicitor followed Mr. Baker

and in the Winter of 1903 imported a steam car locomobile.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X7:1> Of the owners of <}> <-> gasolene</-><+>gasoline</+></}> cars, Dr.

Henderson was the first in the Spring of 1904 with a Duryea soon followed by

Mr. Griffiths of Black River with a fourteen horse power Orleans

car.</X></quote></p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-032#X8:1> By 1906 there were about a dozen cars in the island and they must

really have been novelties amongst the traditional <quote> 'horse and

buggies' </quote> then in use for everyday transport. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-032#X9:1> Early motoring was regarded as a sport, not anything to be taken

seriously.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X10:1> Opinion was divided as to whether it would amount to anything, or

remain just a fad.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X11:1> Given the trials faced by the early Jamaican motorist, it is indeed

a wonder that the motorcar became a popular and essential means of transport.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-032#X12:1> To help foster this new phenomenon, the Jamaica Motor Union had

been formed in 1905 to encourage safe automobilism, improve road conditions,

fight against laws which would restrict the growth of automobilism, encourage

safe driving and provide information to the motoring public.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X13:1> The President was Dr G.C. Henderson and the Secretary was Mr E.

Nuttall. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-032#X14:1> Newspapers of the day served to publish both the views of early

<quote> 'automobilists', </quote> ( as they called themselves) as well as of

those not so inclined to this new mode of transport.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X15:1> Editorials usually sought to highlight both points of view but

seemed to accept that cars had arrived to stay.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X16:1> The activities of the Legislative Council were also reported in the

papers, so it is possible to get an idea of the then Government's viewpoint

on this new and evolving phenomenon.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X17:1> By 1908, road guides of the island, complete with maps and motoring

tips, were being published for both tourists and locals alike.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X18:1> From all these sources, it is possible to gain a sense of early

motoring in Jamaica as well as the difficulties and problems faced by the

motorists. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-032#X19:1> <h> <bold> Early Conflicts and Anti-motoring Sentiments </bold>

</h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-032#X20:1> There were conflicts between the new automobilists and those who

travelled by more established forms of road transport from the beginning.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X21:1> An example of this is contained in a letter, signed with a

nom-de-plume of <quote> 'Caution' </quote> which appeared in the <it> Gleaner

</it> on February 14, 1905.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X22:1> It reported an account of an encounter between a buggy and motorcar

on a country road: </p>

<p> <X>
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X23:1> <quote> The Editor,</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-032#X24:1> Sir, On one of our country roads recently a serious accident

might have occurred, because of the careless driving of a motor car, the

driver of which without sound of horn or whistle, rushed round a corner and

frightened the horses attached to a buggy which was being driven by a lady.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X25:1> Fortunately the only injury was to the pole of the buggy, but there

might have been loss of life or limb.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X26:1> Of course, any expenditure incurred for repairs may be recovered

from the transgressor.</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-032#X27:1> We are pleased to welcome cordially all visitors and tourists,

but we do expect that they will give all necessary attention to the rights of

the road.</X></quote></p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-032#X28:1> Approximately one week later, a scathing reply to <quote> 'Caution's'

</quote> letter appeared, written by Mr H.W. Griffiths of Hodges, Black

River, in which he claimed to be the driver of the car involved in the

incident.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X29:1> His version follows: </p>

<p> <X>
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X30:1> <quote> On my way from St. Elizabeth to Trelawny, by way of Ramble,

at the latter place there are many turnings.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X31:1> Before reaching each one my horn was loudly sounded.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X32:1> No doubt the rattle of the buggy must have prevented the driver from

either hearing the horn or the noise of the engines working.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X33:1> On rounding one of these turnings but not fast ( otherwise I should

not have been able to pull up in time) as the turning happened to be a sharp

one, I came across the buggy mentioned after I was well round the corner, and

on my proper side, as the car pulled up in the left-hand watercourse; the

buggy was still proceeding not actually on the wrong side of the road, but a

little more than in the middle, so much so that I could not have passed and

pulled up fully thirty- five feet from where I first stopped; the driver of

the buggy moved then on to her left side and I commenced to proceed, but when

I got about twenty feet from the buggy, the pony on the off-side became

fractious.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X34:1> I at once stopped the car, and also the engines entirely, and waited

for the buggy to pass.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X35:1> As the ponies were being driven forward again, the off-side one

suddenly backed, but only for a yard or so, as the other pony was pulling

forward; this sudden jerk bent the table of the buggy...</X></quote></p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-032#X36:1> As a result of such confrontations, an anti-motorcar lobby came

into being in Jamaica and tried to fight the oncoming change, sometimes going

to extremes to present their point of view.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X37:1> This was reflected in a letter written in 1906 by a visitor to the

island, suggesting that Jamaica be kept a motorcar-free zone or that the tax

on <}> <-> gasolene </-> <+> gasoline </+> </}> and cars be made so high as

to prohibit their use here: </p>

<p> <X>
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X38:1> <quote> The great majority of people from the U.S.A: who come to

Jamaica are quiet, sensible people who bring tired nerves and while

recuperating in your invigorating climate want to enjoy its beautiful scenery

without taking their lives in their hands every mile while they are doing so.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X39:1> Five of every such visitors would be driven away for every one

automobilist who would come if automobiles were common in Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X40:1> Let there be one paradise free from the evil...</quote></X></p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-032#X41:1> <h> <bold> Early <quote> 'Roadblocks' </quote> to Motoring

</bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-032#X42:1> From the early motorist's point of view, everything seemed to

conspire against the success of motoring in Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X43:1> There was the price of gas, with its exorbitant <quote> 'gas tax'

</quote> , a lack of spare parts and trained mechanics, and bad roads in many

areas.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X44:1> The tax on petroleum products was a particularly strong issue raised

by motorists.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X45:1> An editorial in the <it> Gleaner </it> of January 29, 1906, outlined

the issue: </p>

<p> <X>
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X46:1> <quote> The one great obstacle in the development of motoring in

Jamaica is the enormous import duty to which all the products of petroleum

are liable.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X47:1> The <}> <-> gasolene</-><+>gasoline</+></}> used in explosion motors

( <it> author's note: internal combustion engines</it>) costs anything from

sixpence to eightpence per gallon in addition to which there is a duty of

sevenpence halfpenny which just doubles the cost.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X48:1> When it is further remembered that an owner is called upon to pay a

license of 5 shillings for driving and 10 shillings for registration in

addition to the ordinary wheel tax it will be seen that motoring is somewhat

of an expensive luxury.</quote></X></p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-032#X49:1> At the heart of the matter was the Government's refusal to lower

the duty on <}> <-> gasolene </-> <+> gasoline </+> </}> as it was once

thought that it would be more popular than kerosene as fuel for lamps in the

home.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X50:1> Although the Government knew this not to be so and, in fact, derived

only about &pound; 200 annually from this tax, they still refused to lower it

at that time. </p> <O> illustration </O>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-032#X51:1> A lack of motorcar supplies and a regular supply of gasoline

also plagued early motorists.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X52:1> This was illustrated by one of Dr Henderson's letters to the <it>

Gleaner </it> on October 21, 1905: </p>

<p> <X>
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X53:1> <quote> As there are altogether less than a dozen motor vehicles,

including cars, tricars and motor cycles, in Jamaica, there is not sufficient

demand for regular establishments for the supply of petrol, lubricants and

parts.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X54:1> A small amount of gasoline is imported by some hardware dealers and

others, but car owners, as a rule, import their own petrol in iron drums of

ten to 100 American gallons ( equal eight to eighty English), and they are

always ready to assist one another, or a brother motorist visiting the

island, in case of need.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X55:1> As passenger steamers by insurance regulations are forbidden to

carry petrol, it can only come down once a month in cargo boats and the cost

with freight, duty etc., works out at about 2s 6d to ls 9d per English

gallon.</quote></X></p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-032#X56:1> <h> <bold> Development of Road Etiquette </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-032#X57:1> One of the earliest references to road etiquette was found in a

letter by Mr Griffiths in the <it> Gleaner </it> of February 22, 1905.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X58:1> In it he offered advice to other motorists and drivers of buggies,

should they find themselves in a position of conflict: </p>

<p> <X>
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X59:1> <quote> Should any driver meet a motor car, who is handling a

fractious animal, all he has to do is put up his hand of warning: the

motorist is not to know an animal is fractious unless he is warned, and then

he must stop his car, and I feel sure that certain motorists would render all

possible assistance should it be required.</quote></X></p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-032#X60:1> In an attempt to draw attention to the problem motorists faced

in sharing the road with other users, Dr George Henderson, as President of

the Jamaica Motor Union, wrote yet another letter to the <it> Daily Gleaner

</it> on March 8, 1906.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X61:1> In it he made a plea for himself and fellow <quote> 'automobilists'

</quote> that the Legislature do something about other road users more

delinquent than motorists were: </p>

<p> <X>
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X62:1> <quote> 'Road Hogs'</quote> there always have been and will always

be, and the automobilist is not the only offender in that class.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X63:1> Pedestrians, riders, cart and buggy drivers as well who insist upon

monopolising more than their due share of the road and obstructing,

endangering by neglecting to lead or exercise proper control of their

animals, and refusing or neglecting to make way for traffic meeting or

overtaking them, all form species of that genius, and equally show an entire

disregard of the <quote> 'rule of the road'.</quote></p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-032#X64:1> Pioneers have always to undergo an extra amount of trouble and

encounter opposition, and while I do not complain unduly of the restrictions

the Legislature has seen fit to place upon motorists in return for granting

them the privilege to use the roads, I am in hopes that in the future a sense

of equity may yet induce it to turn the attention of its officers to the

pedestrian or donkey drivers who walk four or six abreast in the main roads,

driving their animals in front of them, instead of leading, the small

children often seen in charge(!) of carts drawn by mules or horses which they

are entirely unable to control, the deaf or sleeping driver whose trap

meanders along the centre of the roadway who requires almost a giant cracker

to rouse him to consciousness...</quote></X></p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-032#X65:1> Sharing the road with other forms of vehicular traffic meant

that the motorcar needed to have some special means of identification to warn

the pedestrian of the type of machine which was in close proximity.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X66:1> To this end, the Motor Car Law <O> illustration </O> <O> photograph

</O> <O> caption </O> 1905, Section 10, specified that, <quote> 'The person

in charge of a motor car shall carry attached thereto a horn or other

instrument capable of giving audible and sufficient warning of the approach

or position of the motor car.' </quote>
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X67:1> However, by 1908 every form of wheeled traffic was sporting a horn.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X68:1> The potential for danger arising from this was obvious as Dr

Henderson pointed out in a letter of March 26, 1908: </p>

<p> <X>
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X69:1> <quote> If imitation is the sincerest form of flattery, motorists

should be proud of the way in which their signal has <quote> 'caught

on'</quote> and been appropriated by users of other vehicles.</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-032#X70:1> There is another side to the question: the safety of the public.
<ICE-JA:W2B-032#X71:1> Normal road users regulate their pace and movements relative to

vehicles overtaking them by the sound of signals heard by them, and if the

same signal is used by carriages at 6-8 miles per hour, cycles at 8-12 and

motors at 16-20, confusion is likely to occur, and an error of judgement on

their part, or on that of the motorist, for whose use, under penalty, the

horn was originally prescribed, may easily result in serious or fatal

accident.</quote></X></p></I>

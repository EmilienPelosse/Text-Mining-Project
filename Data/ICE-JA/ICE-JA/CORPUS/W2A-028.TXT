<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#6:1> ABSTRACT
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#7:1> The encapsulation of a widely used organophosphorus pesticide,

Diazinon ( o,o,-diethyl o-[6-methyl-2-(1-methylethyl)-4-pyrimidinyl]

phosphorothioate, in the form of Basudin was successfully achieved in three

starch matrices  a calcium adduct, and two starch borate complexes.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#8:1> Of the latter, one ( borate-1) was prepared by using NaOH to

solubilize the starch and the other ( borate-2) was prepared by solubilizing

the starch with boiling boric acid solution.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#9:1> The borate-1 encapsulation procedure was used for three starches 

corn, cassava, and dasheen which are all produced locally.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#10:1> The percent recovery was in the order  dasheen ( 100%) &gt; cassava

( 96%) &gt; corn ( 91%).
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#11:1> However, the percent encapsulation was in the reverse order  dasheen

( 94%) &lt; cassava ( 96%) &lt; corn ( 97%). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#12:1> <h> <bold> INTRODUCTION </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#13:1> Slow release technology has been developed in the drug industry

<fnr> <sp> 1 </sp> </fnr> since 1960 in order to combat the side effects

caused by administering high doses of conventional drugs and at the same time

to maintain an effective level of drugs in the body. <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#14:1> 1. Lewis, D. H.; Cowsar, D. H. Controlled Release Pesticides. Harbert

B. ed., American Chemical Society, Washington D.C., USA, 1977, 1 </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#15:1> Applying the same principle, attempts have been made <fnr> <sp> 2

</sp> </fnr> during the last decade to develop suitable controlled release

pesticides for use in the soil and on foliage to reduce ( 1) pesticide

application rates ( 2) pesticide levels in the environment and ( 3) pesticide

toxicity and to extend pesticide residual activity. <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#16:1> 2. Baker, R.W.; Lonsdale, H. K. <it> Chem. Tech., 1975, 5, 668. </it>

</footnote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#17:1> There are three main advantages of using con <l> trolled release

formulations: ( i) reduced possibility of over or underdosing ( ii) much less

pesticide can be used for the same period of activity as a conven <l>

tionally applied pesticide and ( iii) maintenance of an effective

concentration of pesticide for a prolonged period.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#18:1> The first advantage can be illustrated by Figure 1 where the

concentration of an active agent has been plotted as a function of time after

delivery in a conventional manner.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#19:1> Shortly after the agent is applied the concentration rises rapidly to

a maximum and then falls as the agent is metabolized or degraded.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#20:1> Two important concentration levels are to be noticed in this figure 

the effective concentration which is the minimum concentration necessary to

produce the desired effect and the toxic concentra <l> tion above which side

effects occur.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#21:1> It is important, therefore, to maintain a concentration of pesticide

between the effective and toxic levels.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#22:1> Controlled release formulation is an ideal method of achieving this

concentration level. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#28:1> Encapsulation processes can be divided into three categories:

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#29:1> 1) Phase Separation  in this process micro-capsules are formed

by emulsifying or dispersing the core material in an immiscible continuous

phase in which the wall material <O> graph </O> <O> caption </O> <O> graph

</O> <O> caption </O> dissolves and then separates from the continuous phase,

depositing around the core particles </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#30:1> 2) Interfacial Reaction  microcapsules are formed by emulsifying

or dispersing the core material in an immiscible phase and then an

interfacial polymerization reaction results at the surface of the core

particles </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#31:1> 3) Physical Methods  wall material and core particles are

physically brought together and wall material flows around the core particles

to form capsules.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#32:1> Matrix polymerization in which core material is embedded in a polymer

matrix during capsule formation falls in this category.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#33:1> This method of encapsulation was used in this study. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#34:1> It should be noted that when a particular method of encapsulation

is chosen one of the factors that must be considered is the end use of the

formulation and thus the desired permeability of the membrane.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#35:1> Some of the parameters that affect capsular wall permeability and how

that can be manipulated are discussed in the literature. <fnr> <sp> 4 </sp>

</fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#36:1> 4. Scer, H. B. Controlled Release Pesticides. American Chemical

Society, Washington D. C., U.S.A., 1974. </footnote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#37:1> Microencapsulation techniques have been used to prepare

spherically-shaped reservoir devices for controlled release of pesticides.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#38:1> The most frequently used procedures include phase separation,

coacerva <l> tion, spray coating, solvent evaporation from emul <l> sions and

interfacial polymerisation.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#39:1> The choice of method depends mainly on the characteristics of the

pesticide and the release-controlling membrane.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#40:1> Common polymeric wall materials include polyamides, polyesters,

polyureas, cellulose and gelatin.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#41:1> The most notable of the currently marketed microencapsulated products

is the Penncap-M for <l> mulation developed by the Penwalt Corporation, <fnr>

<sp> 5,6 </sp> </fnr> in which Methyl Parathion is encapsulated in the

polyamide wall material. <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#42:1> 5. Koeslater, R. C.; Ivy, E. E. Proc. Controlled Release Pesticides.

University of Akron, Ohio, U.S.A.; 1974. </footnote> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#43:1> 6. Koeslater, R. C. Proc. Controlled Release Pesticides. University

of Akron, Ohio, U.S.A., 1976. </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#44:1> This product exhibits decreased toxicity and longer activity.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#45:1> In addition to these delivery systems which operate by diffusion or

leaching mechanisms, other formulations which rely on erosion and diffusion

have been reported. <fnr> <sp> 7 </sp> </fnr> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#46:1> 7. Sinclair, R. G. <it> Enviro. Sc. & Tech. </it> , 1977, 7, 955.

</footnote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#47:1> The most important factor to be considered when selecting a

coating material is the chemical inertness of the material towards the active

in <l> gredient.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#48:1> A number of polymeric materials are used as encapsulation matrices

for controlled release.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#49:1> It is necessary, in order to facilitate sustained release, that these

materials are fairly resistant to degradation by both biological and

environmental factors.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#50:1> It is known that if an inert material is added to the soil it aids

aeration, but there is also the potential for ecological damage if the

material is accumulated over time.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#51:1> Most synthetic polymers are resistant to microbial degradation and

show limited suscep <l> tibility to environmental degradation.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#52:1> Natural polymers are therefore a better choice since they are

biodegradable and this therefore eliminates the prob <l> lem cited above.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#53:1> The use of starch as a natural polymer in the slow release of

<ICE-JA:W2A-028#54:1> 8. Shasha, B. S.; Doane, W. M.; Russel, C. R. <it> Poly. Sc. Polym.

Lett. </it> , 1976, 14, 417. </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#55:1> In that study a starch xanthate matrix which has lipophilic but water

insoluble properties was used to encapsulate a variety of pesticides.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#56:1> Subsequently, the same group of workers used other starch matrices

viz, a calcium starch adduct and borate complex. <fnr> <sp> 9,10 </sp> </fnr>

<footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#57:1> 9. Shasha, B. S.; Trimnell, D.; Otey, F. H. <it> J. Polym. Sc. </it>

, 1981, 19, 1981. </footnote> <footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#58:1> 10. Trimnell, D.; Shasha, B. S.; Wing, R. E.; Otey, F. H. <it> J.

Appl. Polym. Sc. </it> , 1982, 27, 3919. </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#59:1> Encapsulation with steam injected ( jet cooked) starch <fnr> <sp> 11

workers. <fnr> <sp> 12,13 </sp> </fnr> <footnote>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#63:1> The following is a report of the investigation on the possibility

of encapsulating pesticides in locally available cassava starch using

modified literature methods applicable for Pearle corn starch. <fnr> <sp>

8-10 </sp> </fnr> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#64:1> <h> <bold> EXPERIMENTAL </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#65:1> All chemicals used were reagent grade.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#66:1> All or <l> ganic solvents and water used were distilled before use.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#67:1> Commercially available K-brand corn starch was used.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#68:1> Some of the cassava starch was bought in a local market and some was

extracted from cassava in the laboratory.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#69:1> Diazinon 60 EC used for encap <l> sulation was obtained from Grace

Agrochemicals, Kingston, Jamaica. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#70:1> <h> <bold> Starch Extraction </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#71:1> 1.65 kg of cassava obtained from a local market were peeled,

cubed and crushed in a blender.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#72:1> The resulting slurry was strained through a fine mesh cloth.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#73:1> The residue was washed with water and strained again.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#74:1> This was repeated until the filtrate was pale white.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#75:1> The filtrate was left to settle.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#76:1> When the suspended starch had settled the supernatant liquid was

poured off and the residue was left to dry.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#77:1> 390 g of starch which was slightly off-white in colour were obtained.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#78:1> <h> <bold> Pesticide Encapsulation </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#79:1> A. Starch-Xanthide method  this method was essentially adopted

from the published procedure. <fnr> <sp> 8 </sp> </fnr>
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#80:1> The encapsulated pesticide was filtered on a 60 mesh and washed with

water and pressed dry.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#81:1> The semi-dried mass was forced through a 15 mesh grid and left to dry

to yield 70 g of product. </p>

<p> B. Starch-Calcuim Chloride Method  Shasha's published procedure <fnr>

<sp> 8 </sp> </fnr> was followed to prepare the product.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#82:1> The encapsulated product was washed thoroughly with water and then

pressed dry.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#83:1> The semi-dried mass was forced through a 16 mesh grid and left to

dry. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#84:1> C. Starch-Borate Method 1  22.5 g of starch, 10 cm <sp> 3 </sp>

of emulsifiable concentrate formulation of Diazinon and 35 cm <sp> 3 </sp> of

water were placed in a blender and mixed.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#85:1> 25 cm <sp> 3 </sp> of a 6% NaOH solution were added to gel the starch

and mixing was continued.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#86:1> 2.5 g of boric acid were then added and mixed until a uniform rubbery

mass was obtained.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#87:1> 11.25 g starch were then added and mixing continued until

non-adhering particles were formed.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#88:1> The particles were then forced through a 16 mesh grid and left to air

dry to yield 34 g of product. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#89:1> D. Starch-Borate Method 2  10 cm <sp> 3 </sp> of the

emulsifiable concentrated formulation of Diazinon Chlorpyrifos were added to

11.25 g starch and thoroughly mixed in a blender.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#90:1> 15 cm <sp> 3 </sp> of boiling boric acid solution ( 1.25 g boric acid

in 15 cm <sp> 3 </sp> water) were added.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#91:1> The mixture was blended again until a uniform gel formed.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#92:1> The mixture was poured onto plates in thin layers and left to dry.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#93:1> The dried films were then broken up in a blender. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#94:1> Alternatively, boric was added to a slurry of starch in unheated

water.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#95:1> The mixture was then heated to gel approximately at 80 &degree; C.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#96:1> 1-10 cm <sp> 3 </sp> of an emulsifiable concentration formulation of

Diazinon were added, followed by mixing.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#97:1> The mixture was poured onto plates in thin layers and left to dry.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#98:1> Water volume was varied from 15 to 100 cm <sp> 3 </sp> and boric acid

mass was varied from 1.5  5 g. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#99:1> E. Cooked Starch  10 cm <sp> 3 </sp> of an emulsifiable

concentration formulation of Diazinon were added to 11.25 g starch in a

blender and mixed.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#100:1> 30 cm <sp> 3 </sp> of boiling water were added to the mixture and

the latter blended.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#101:1> The gelled slurry was poured onto plated in thin layers to dry.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#102:1> The dry films were then broken up in a blender. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#103:1> <h> <bold> Analysis for Recovered Pesticide and Percent

Encapsulation </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#104:1> A. Calcium Adduct and Starch  Borate 1  approximately 0.1 g of

the encapsulated product was weighed out accurately in 100 cm <sp> 3 </sp>

conical flasks.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#105:1> About 20 cm <sp> 3 </sp> of water were added and the product left to

soften for about 15 min.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#106:1> The flasks were then heated for about 10 min.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#107:1> At this stage the complexes were completely dissolved.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#108:1> After cooling, the solutions were extracted with three 30 cm <sp> 3

</sp> portions of 15% dichloromethane/hexane solution.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#109:1> The extracts were dried and transferred to volumetric flasks.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#110:1> The contents were analysed for the pesticide using a gas

chromatograph fitted with a flame photometric detector.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#111:1> This analysis gave the total amount of pesticide present,

encapsulated and unencapsulated ( on the surface).
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#112:1> The unencapsulated pesticide was determined by washing the granules

with a suitable solvent, collecting the washing and analysing the latter by

gas chromatography. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#113:1> B. Starch  Xanthide  approximately 0.1 g of the encapsulated

product was weighed out accurately and crushed in an electrically operated

mortar and pestle in the presence of approximately 50 cm <sp> 3 </sp> of

hexane.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#114:1> The solvent was filtered from the powder and collected.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#115:1> The powder was then washed with approximately three 10 cm <sp> 3

</sp> portions of hexane and the washings combined with the filtrate, dried,

placed in a 100 cm <sp> 3 </sp> volumetric flask made up to the mark and then

analysed by gas chromato <l> graphy. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#116:1> C. Starch  Borate 2 and Cooked Starch  approximately 0.05 g of

the encapsulated product was placed in a 100 cm <sp> 3 </sp> conical flask

and about 30 cm <sp> 3 </sp> was added. <X>
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#X117:1> This was left for about 3 hrs. to soften the matrix, and then

homogenized.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#X118:1> The homogenized mixture was then extracted with three, 30 cm <sp>

3</sp> portions of 15% dichloromethane/hexane solution.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#X119:1> The extract was then analysed for the pesticide using a gas

chromatograph fitted with a flame photometric detector.</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#X120:1> <h> Particle Size Separation</h></p>

<p> The granules were separated into four particle size ranges: &gt; 8, 8/16,

16/30 and &gt; 30 by sifting the granules through meshes of appropriate size

range.</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#X121:1> <h> Swellability Determination</h></p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#X122:1> About 0.5 g of the encapsulated product was placed in a 5 cm

<sp> 3</sp> measuring cylinder and the volume noted.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#X123:1> Water was placed in the cylinder and the mixture left for about 3

hours.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#X124:1> The volume of the granules was then noted.</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#X125:1> <h> pH Measurement</h></p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-028#X126:1> About 1 g of the granules was placed in 100 cm <sp> 3</sp>

water and left to soak for about one hour.
<ICE-JA:W2A-028#X127:1> The pH of the water was then measured by a digital pH meter.</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#1:1> <h> UWI students to wait three more months for news on fee hike

</h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#2:1> Students attending the University of the West Indies ( UWI) will

have to wait for nearly another three months before knowing what percentage

of the economic cost of their education they will have to pay, as a special

meeting of the University Council held on Tuesday deferred taking action on

the matter until the annual meeting of the Council on April 16, 1993. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#3:1> This was disclosed yesterday by the Joint Guild of Undergraduates

of the UWI at a press conference held at the <w> Students' </w> Union on the

Mona Campus.
<ICE-JA:W2C-009#4:1> The press conference was held to update the media on the proposed

tuition fee increases, and the Joint Guild was represented by the guild

presidents of the Mona, St Augustine and Cave Hill Campuses. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#5:1> President of the Mona Guild of Undergraduates, Derrick Wilson,

said that the students of the UWI are prepared in principle to pay more for

their education, but certain main issues had to be resolved.
<ICE-JA:W2C-009#6:1> He said these issues are: how much must students be required to pay,

and that this would depend on affordability; if the fees should be paid in

lump sum or over a period of time, and what mechanisms are there to ensure

that the students can access loan funding, thus ensuring that students from

the lower socio-economic groupings are not prevented from entering the

University because of their inability to pay. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#7:1> Mr. Wilson said that after numerous consultations with students, a

more detailed examination of the financial position of the University, and a

realistic assessment of the budgetary constraints of the governments of the

region, the Guild had come up with certain proposals. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#8:1> These include the introduction of the new fee structure on a

phased basis, which would see undergraduate students at Mona paying 5 percent

of their economic cost in 1993- 94, 7.5 percent in 1994-95 and 10 percent in

1995-96; a proposal that students at St. Augustine be asked to pay a flat

rate of TT$3,800 per student, irrespective of faculty; that undergraduate

students from non <?> - </?> campus territories be asked to pay 33 1/3

percent of their economic cost, and that postgraduates be asked to pay 30

percent and not 50 percent as was originally proposed. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#9:1> Mr. Wilson said further that the Joint Guild has been able to get

the University to understand that an up front payment of 15 percent would be

burdensome and impossible for many students.
<ICE-JA:W2C-009#10:1> The University and the governments of the region were therefore

willing to have the <quote> " phased in" </quote> approach rather than the

lump sum payment.
<ICE-JA:W2C-009#11:1> He said they have been able to convince the University and

governments also that a proper loan mechanism should be in place before there

can be any increase. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#12:1> The Mona Guild president indicated that they as well as

administration of the UWI would like the issue to be resolved as quickly as

possible, so that students planning to enter the institution next year can be

aware from as early as possible what financial preparation they need to make.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#13:1> Also speaking at the conference were the guild presidents of Cave

Hill, Delano Franklyn, and St. Augustine, Kiran Singh.
<ICE-JA:W2C-009#14:1> Elaborating on the loan mechanism that they wanted to see in place,

Mr. Franklyn said this was one of the most crucial and important factors that

have been part of their discussions since September 1991.
<ICE-JA:W2C-009#15:1> He said that both of the campuses in Jamaica and Trinidad have been

having favourable discussions with the commercial sectors and to a very large

extent with the governments. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#16:1> They have therefore come to a level of agreement that the

commercial sector would be willing to provide <}> <-> substantials </-> <+>

substantial </+> </}> sums to help students.
<ICE-JA:W2C-009#17:1> The Barbados government had already taken a decision that their

students would not be required to pay the increased fees. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#18:1> He said the area of difficulty was on the question of who would

stand as guarantors for the loans, the level of interest payment, and over

what period of time would students be allowed to access such loans.
<ICE-JA:W2C-009#19:1> Mr. Franklyn said these issues would have to be addressed and the

details ironed out before there could be any agreement. </p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#20:2> <h> House passes Copyright Act </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#21:2> The 1992 Copyright Act was passed unanimously in Parliament

yesterday. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#22:2> Prime Minister P.J. Patterson, commenting on the Act referred to

it as a <quote> " major innovative legislation, which commanded the unanimous

support of the entire House." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#23:2> Leader of the Opposition, Edward Seaga, in welcoming the passage

of the Act noted that Jamaica now conforms to any of several international

conventions which, he said, allow creative works to be protected. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#24:2> Mr. Seaga, in making his contribution to the debate, focussed on

the development of the music industry from its early stages of ska and rock

steady, through reggae which, with the likes of Marley, took Jamaica onto the

international stage, to its current form, dance hall. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#25:2> He chose the music industry, he said, not to the exclusion of

other intellectual pursuits needing copyright protection, but because it was

from here that the cry for protection was greatest and also because this area

truly showed the creative force of the Jamaican people. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#26:2> He questioned however, the limiting of the period for which the

intellectual copyright endures.
<ICE-JA:W2C-009#27:2> Although the extension to 50 years from the previous 30 years was an

improvement he said, it was less than the 70 years given in other countries.
<ICE-JA:W2C-009#28:2> He felt the issue should be discussed in international fora

concerning giving the same tenure of ownership to intellectual property as

well as to physical.
<ICE-JA:W2C-009#29:2> In addition, he noted, this would be to the advantage of the poor,

the less skilled, those of limited academic qualification, from whom a wealth

of creativity came, as they would be able to bequeath their creations to

their successors, without termination. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#30:2> He also suggested that the <quote> " public domain" </quote> such

as folklore traditions should be protected by mechanisms which ensure that

those who profit from it should pay less fees for its usage. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#31:2> He also asked why there could not be the adoption of a system of

actual registration for works rather than the performance itself.
<ICE-JA:W2C-009#32:2> This, like the birth certificate, he said, could have many uses. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#33:2> On the subject of increased piracy of works with the advanced

technology, he said that although there was no way to prevent it, artistes

could benefit from a mechanism where the state collected duties on imported

video equipment, the funds being made available for the general use of the

artistes. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#34:2> In closing, he suggested to the Prime Minister that in

recognition of the achievements of our musicians, thought be given to an

institute of popular music, for the training of artistes, their exposition to

their rights, the collection of copies of every recording, the training of

the artistes in the very legislation being passed, and the creation of a

mechanism to give artistes the respect due them. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#35:2> Senator Carl Rattray, in responding, traced the previous attempts

to enact and pass the copyright law, saying that this bill was one of the

longest in preparation in modern Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2C-009#36:2> He said it was interesting for him that moral rights were included in

the legislation.
<ICE-JA:W2C-009#37:2> He briefly outlined some of the provisions of the legislation, noting

that parliamentarians would be happy that they would be free to quote works

in the performance of their duties. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#38:2> He ended by saying that the utility of the legislation would be

judged by its impact on our culture, and that our creators would be the

monitors of it, prompting any necessary changes. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#39:2> The Prime Minister, in his contribution, said that the passage of

the Act did not mean the end to the discussion of some of the difficult

areas, but that it had come to the stage where it was more prudent to pass

it, in its present form. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#40:2> Answering some of Mr. Seaga's concerns, with regards to the

question of those creations in the <quote> " public domain" </quote> , he

said that <quote> " though he agreed with the protection of these works, this

would not be effected via the Act, but possibly, by separate legislation,

<foreign> sui generis </foreign> , being a type proposed." </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#41:2> With regards to the suggestion to register artistes, he was very

anxious to avoid a bureaucratic and cumbersome system.
<ICE-JA:W2C-009#42:2> He said that without registration, creators of works were afforded

protection as long as they performed the work for the first time. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#43:2> Mr. Patterson next spoke of the promotion of the entertainment

industry which he recognized as one of the fastest growing and great earners

of foreign exchange and said that steps had been taken to upgrade and provide

for the better legal and economic grounds to encourage artistes to give of

their skills and talents. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#44:2> The Prime Minister ended by thanking those who had helped the

legislation to become a reality.
<ICE-JA:W2C-009#45:2> He added that once the Act was passed, there would be the need to

embark upon a process of widespread dissemination and public education so

that everybody would know their rights and responsibilities.
<ICE-JA:W2C-009#46:2> The Prime Minister then moved successful motions for a second and

third reading of the Bill. </p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#47:3> <h> Floods hit St. Thomas </h>

<p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#48:3> <h> &bullet; Student drowns; over 60 seek government assistance

</h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#49:3> BY MARCIA BENNETT </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#50:3> HEAVY and continuous rains which lashed the eastern end of the

island from Monday until early yesterday morning, and which resulted in

extensive flooding and landslides, has left one person dead and a trail of

destruction in St. Thomas.
<ICE-JA:W2C-009#51:3> Several houses were badly damaged, household effects, livestock and

produce lost and three rivers overflowed their banks, wreaking havoc on

nearby communities. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#52:3> Areas hardest hit by the disaster were Hillside, Golden Grove,

Bath, Sunning Hill, Duhaney Pen, Springfield and Seaforth, all in the western

section of the parish, where many families were washed out of their homes and

lost items of clothing and household effects. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#53:3> Ten-year-old Gilbert Brown of Hillside district was one of six

children who were swept out of their home by rising waters on Wednesday

night.
<ICE-JA:W2C-009#54:3> When the Jamaica Herald visited the home, the child's griefstricken

grandfather, Joseph McDuffus told our news team that at about 11:30, he was

awoken by <quote> " an explosion" </quote> and water, rising to about four

feet gushed into the four-bedroom house. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#55:3> He said the children were carried out of the house by the water

and five were found later, pinned against a mango tree in the pouring rain.
<ICE-JA:W2C-009#56:3> A search was made for Gilbert and some time after, his hand was seen

sticking out of the mud.
<ICE-JA:W2C-009#57:3> Family members had to dig him out, but he was already dead.
<ICE-JA:W2C-009#58:3> Gilbert's father who shares his name, was too overcome with grief to

speak with the Herald reporter. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#59:3> Angry Hillside residents are blaming Forestry Development

Industry Corporation for the damage and loss of life.
<ICE-JA:W2C-009#60:3> They say the company cut down trees which would have acted as a

retainer for the water, in order to build a road through the hills.
<ICE-JA:W2C-009#61:3> This view was echoed by St. Thomas Member of Parliament Errol

Anderson who held FIDCo totally responsible for the cutting down of trees

which he said was in contravention of nature. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#62:3> <quote> " This is nothing short of a devastation" </quote> , Mr.

Anderson said.
<ICE-JA:W2C-009#63:3> He said the disaster was greater than that which occurred during

adversely affected. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#64:3> PNP caretaker Fenton Ferguson also issued an appeal for grants

from the Ministry of Agriculture to help small farmers who had suffered

losses and said the need for assistance was <quote> " urgent" </quote> . </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#65:3> A resident of another hard-hit area, Duhaney Pen, Alexander

Housen, told the Herald that his house and shop were destroyed and that he

<ICE-JA:W2C-009#66:3> He said this was the third time that he has been affected by heavy

rains.
<ICE-JA:W2C-009#67:3> Another man reportedly lost 600 chickens when his coop was washed out

<p> <ICE-JA:W2C-009#68:3> It was a similar experience for the Golden Grove community and

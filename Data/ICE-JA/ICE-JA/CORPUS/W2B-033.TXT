<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-033#1:1> <h> Cable TV: will Jamaica be ready for the competition? </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-033#2:1> <bold> by COURTNEY JACKSON </bold> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-033#3:1> THE CURRENT public discussions concerning the granting of a cable

television licence to one or other of the existing, or a new television

station, in the context of the Government's economic liberalisation policies,

appears so far to have a limited perspective on this important question. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-033#4:1> There are relevant telecommunication technologies and policy

issues emerging internationally , which should inform such a debate.
<ICE-JA:W2B-033#5:1> A missing element which should be central to these considerations is

the role of the Jamaica Telephone Company ( JTC) and TOJ in the future of the

national telecommunication system. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-033#6:1> Telecommunication systems in North America and Europe have evolved

from telephony in the 1870s, to radio, to broadcast TV, then to cable-TV or

CATV and finally to data communications to facilitate the computer

revolution.
<ICE-JA:W2B-033#7:1> The small copper wires which run from the local exchange to the

customer's house or business to provide telephone services were not suitable

for TV and high volume computer data because the transmission channel was too

small. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-033#8:1> To make the benefits of the newly developed communication and

computer technologies widely available, it was necessary to establish new

facilities, such as packet-switching data networks by telephone companies,

and an entirely new network of coaxial cable for TV distribution by CATV

companies which were, for the most part, different from those doing

broadcast-TV, such as JBC TV. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-033#9:1> Since the late 1970s, a branch of the United Nations  the

International Telecommunications Union, working through the International

Consultative Committee for Telephone and Telegraph, one of its arms  in

cooperation with the International Standards Organisation and all the major

telecommunications and computer companies worldwide, has been working on

standards for the development and implementation of a more rational and

efficient system for the delivery of all the services above, both at their

basic and enhanced quality levels, such as plain telephone service,

electronic yellow pages, colour tax, picturephone, high definition

television, etc. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-033#10:1> The technological bases for this effort are the established world

capacities for the high volume production of very large-scale integrated

electronic circuits, wide transmission channels through optical fibre cables,

and dramatic price reductions for computing and telephone exchange equipment.

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-033#11:1> <h> <bold> Rates, methods </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-033#12:1> One of the first results of this continuing collaboration is the

promulgation of standards for two new categories of telephone service called

narrowband and broadband Integrated Services Digital Networks.
<ICE-JA:W2B-033#13:1> The rates and methods of implementation by companies and governments

will depend on such considerations as maximizing the use of the existing

costly copper cable system; ensuring that the interests of subscribers are

protected both in monopoly and competitive conditions; optimising the use of

scarce foreign exchange; balancing of private versus national interests, and

others. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-033#14:1> Narrowband ISDN can be transmitted on the existing copper outside

plant but CATV cannot, so there is need for a new wide-channel transmission

infrastructure for CATV specifically, and broadband ISDN generally. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-033#15:1> Will it be coaxial cable or optical fibres in the subscriber

access network?
<ICE-JA:W2B-033#16:1> CATV companies abroad are finding it cost-effective to use fibre up

to the headend distribution point to which many customers are connected, and,

similarly telephone companies use <}> <-> fiber </-> <+> fibre </+> </}> for

trunk links between exchanges. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-033#17:1> From the viewpoint of telephone companies, fibre of the local

loop, through existing ducts or as an overlay, has to justify itself  in

terms of capital, operational and maintenance costs  against the expansion

of the in-place copper-using concentrators, multiplexors and pair-gain

devices, on a plain telephone service revenue basis  the overwhelmingly

dominant revenue source. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-033#18:1> Because the local loop does not generally extend beyond five

miles ( perhaps considerably less in Jamaica) and the economies of fibre are

obtained over much longer distances, fibre-to-the-home has proved to be three

times as costly as copper; and fibre-to-the-terminal distributor supplying

many subscribers, one and one-fifth as costly. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-033#19:1> These are data reported from field trials conducted since 1986 in

Europe and North America for <quote> 'active' </quote> local loop fibre

systems involving components which convert between the light and electrical

elements not only at the exchange and customer premises, but also in between.
<ICE-JA:W2B-033#20:1> Laboratory scale results are now being reported on new <quote> 'passive'

</quote> loop-fibre technologies which require conversions only at the loop

extremities and operate with considerably reduced costs compared to active

systems. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-033#21:1> Some important questions are: </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-033#22:1> - Whether a new coax network is more economical in the long run

on a CATV revenue base, with the additional advantage of providing

competition in the telecommunication marketplace, versus fibre on a CATV,

voice and data revenue base; should the telephone company be allowed to

provide CATV service in addition to what it presently does; or should it be a

common carrier for whomever wants to lease its transmission facilities 

fibre or coax?
<ICE-JA:W2B-033#23:1> Should JTC be encouraged to invest in local access fibre, despite the

relatively high cost, but extend the service only to business customers whose

service demand will allow for break-even operation, at a minimum? </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-033#24:1> The explosion in the range of telecommunication service over the

last two decades, and the promise of even more in the immediate future, has

awoken many businesses and countries to the competitive advantages which can

accrue from the creative exploitation of this resource.
<ICE-JA:W2B-033#25:1> Surely, Jamaica ought to strive to be ready for the international

competition as we approach the 21st century. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-033#26:1> <it> - Courtney Jackson is a former executive engineering analyst

with the Jamaica Telephone Company.
<ICE-JA:W2B-033#27:1> He is currently a PhD. student in industrial and systems engineering

at the University of Florida, U.S.A. </it> </p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-033#65:3> <h> <bold> The rehabilitation of Kingston Harbour </bold> </h>

</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-033#66:3> <bold> THE Kingston Harbour is the major physio-geographical

factor that contributed to Kingston being the largest inhabited city in

Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2B-033#67:3> It provided the principal port for commerce and trade and related

industrial and residential development.
<ICE-JA:W2B-033#68:3> However, the seemingly inevitable by-product of civilization, wastes,

has gradually had a negative effect. </bold> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-033#69:3> Industrial and commercial development have produced a variety of

waste that have contaminated the soil, water and air.
<ICE-JA:W2B-033#70:3> Solid waste from these developments have cluttered the gullies that

subsequently wash into the harbour.
<ICE-JA:W2B-033#71:3> Poor agricultural practices have also caused contamination of the

Harbour due to the gradual erosion of valuable top soil.
<ICE-JA:W2B-033#72:3> The use of fertilizers and pesticides in an effort to improve

productivity has also had the undesirable effect of contamination of surface

and groundwater flows to the Harbour. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-033#73:3> Several recent studies have not yet resulted in the

implementation of programmes geared toward halting the pollution loads to the

Harbour.
<ICE-JA:W2B-033#74:3> While a number of proposals has been aired on various approaches of

physically cleaning the Harbour, the strategy will have to be one that

tackles the problem at the source. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-033#75:3> Two basic approaches to the problem of the eutrophication of the

Harbour have been considered.
<ICE-JA:W2B-033#76:3> By seawater exchange through pumping from outside the Harbour or by

altering the Harbour configuration or alternatively, by dramatically reducing

pollution to the Harbour. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2B-033#77:3> The strategy of waste reduction is a proven means of curtailing

the deterioration of environmental conditions and while it is not without

cost and will have to be a long-term approach, it reduces the risk associated

with major civil works and inherently has economic benefits not directly

linked to Harbour rehabilitation. </p> </I>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-024#6:1> ABSTRACT <space>
<ICE-JA:W2A-024#7:1> Statistics and periodicities in the April-May-June rainfall were

investigated.
<ICE-JA:W2A-024#8:1> The rainfall was more intensive in May than in April and June.
<ICE-JA:W2A-024#9:1> April rainfall had longer periodicities than June, with May falling in

between.
<ICE-JA:W2A-024#10:1> Some of the April periodicities can be correlated to the solar

activity during the westerly phase of the Quasi-Biennial Oscillation ( QBO)

at 15 mb.
<ICE-JA:W2A-024#11:1> A partial explanation is offered in terms of the coincidence of cold

fronts and increased solar radiation.
<ICE-JA:W2A-024#12:1> The possible association of one of the May periodicities with the

occurrence of El Ni&ntilde;o was investigated and an association between

heavy rainfall ( over 300 mm) in May and the occurrence of El Ni&ntilde;o was

found.
<ICE-JA:W2A-024#13:1> Other periodicities are discussed.
<ICE-JA:W2A-024#14:1> The April showers of 1993 are explained in terms of abnormally cold

weather in North America and the May rainfall of 1993 is associated with a

mature phase of the El Ni&ntilde;o. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-024#15:1> <h> <bold> INTRODUCTION </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-024#16:1> The April-May showers of 1993 were ab <l> normally heavy.
<ICE-JA:W2A-024#17:1> The islandwide precipitation averaged over 200 mm and 400 mm for the

respective months.
<ICE-JA:W2A-024#18:1> The above numbers are based on a sample of data since the final 1993

<ICE-JA:W2A-024#19:1> Many questions were asked as to the cause, such as, <quote> " Were

above, although inferences can be made.
<ICE-JA:W2A-024#21:1> The paper will look specifically at some statistics of the April-May

rainfall, search for periodicities and correlations and attempt to give some

physical explanations and point out the need for research using general

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-024#46:1> <h> <bold> CORRELATIONS </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-024#47:1> The power spectra for April, May and June are composed of many

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-024#49:1> The Quasi-Biennial Oscillation ( QBO) has a period of

approximately 26 months or 2.1 years.
<ICE-JA:W2A-024#50:1> The June data has a strong 2.1 year component, while the April data

fall and QBO and this would have to be inves <l> tigated further.
<ICE-JA:W2A-024#52:1> It will be shown below that the longer periods in the April data

could be related to the 11 year solar cycle through the phase of the QBO in

such a way that the 11 year period of the solar activity will not be

explicitly evi <l> dent. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-024#94:1> <h> <bold> EXPLANATION </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-024#95:1> A hypothesis can be put forward to partially explain the positive

correlation between rain <l> fall and solar activity during the westerly

phase of the QBO.
<ICE-JA:W2A-024#96:1> The April rainfall can be ex <l> plained in terms of precipitation

resulting from the advance of cold fronts from the north over land ( Jamaica)

which is being heated by solar radiation, which is in that part of its cycle

where the intensity of radiation has nearly peaked <fnr> <sp> 7 </sp> </fnr> .

<footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-024#97:1> 7. National Meteorological Service Climate Branch.
<ICE-JA:W2A-024#98:1> An overview of the Climate of Jamaica, 1986. </footnote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-024#99:1> Figure 7 shows the monthly variation of global radiation over Jamaica

with the radiation start <l> ing to peak in March. <X>
<ICE-JA:W2A-024#X100:1> Thus April showers are caused by the coincidental occurrence of

cold fronts and increased solar radiation over land.
<ICE-JA:W2A-024#X101:1> The cold air moves in under the warm air over land and forces moist

air upward.
<ICE-JA:W2A-024#X102:1> The resulting uplifting of moist air leads to the formation of

cumulonimbus clouds and eventual precipita<l>tion.
<ICE-JA:W2A-024#X103:1> The effect in March is not as pronounced, perhaps because of the

time lag required for solar heating to take effect.</p>

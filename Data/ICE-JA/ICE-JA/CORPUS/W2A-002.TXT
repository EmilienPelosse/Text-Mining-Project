<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-002#2:1> <h> <bold> Derek Walcott  A Personal Memoir </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-002#3:1> Derek Walcott, The Nobel Laureate for Literature, 1992, was born,

along with his twin brother Roderick, on January 23, 1930, in St Lucia in the

old British West Indies.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#4:1> His parents were Alix Walcott, a respected school teacher, and her

husband Warwick Walcott, of the Public Works Department.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#5:1> Warwick died when the twins were one year old, leaving Alix the

daunting task of bringing up the twins and their sister Pamela.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#6:1> Despite the early death of Warwick he, who had been himself a painter

and producer of plays, was to have a great influence on the artistic career

of Derek. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-002#7:1> Because I was one of the main readers on Henry Swanzy's BBC's <it>

Caribbean Voices </it> I was fortunate to come into contact with Walcott's

work as early as 1949, and I first met him in 1951 at the Colony Club,

Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#8:1> He was with an old school mate of mine, Tony Steer, who, Walcott tells

me, later appointed himself a bishop of his own church somewhere in Central

America. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-002#9:1> Walcott was then an undergraduate at the University College of the

West Indies, Mona.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#10:1> He was reading ( or as we would have said in those days of colonial

innocence, studying) the old combined honours degree: English, French, Latin.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#11:1> I was still a lecturer at the London University Institute of

Education, and had returned to Jamaica, where I had been born in 1920, partly

to look at the new University College.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#12:1> Later, in 1953, when I joined UCWI, as one of the few West Indian

members of staff, Walcott was still a student. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-002#13:1> I can remember Derek's smile when, on that first meeting with

Steer, I quoted the first of his verses which stuck in my mind: <it> ...nursing

neurosis like a potted plant... </it>
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#14:1> He did not smile easily in the early days at UCWI, and alas,

sometimes gave the impression that he was in fact nursing neurosis. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-002#15:1> My recollection is that I had heard vaguely of Walcott in 1946

from James ( Sonny) Rodway on the crowded SS Jamaica Producer which was

taking a whole batch of us, from the Caribbean, to pursue university  mainly

postgraduate  studies all over the U.K.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#16:1> Rodway was from a dis <l> tinguished Guyanese family but he had moved

to St Lucia in the colonial education service to be an inspector of schools.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#17:1> One of Walcott's earliest works is dedicated to him  because, says

the inscription quoting Catullus,
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#18:1> <quote> 'You did not take my poems to be mere mist' </quote> -

<foreign> <it> ...namque tu solebas/ Meas esse aliquid putare nugas... </it>

</foreign> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-002#19:1> Much to my surprise Rodway knew the work of George Campbell of

Jamaica, whom he then  1946  considered to be the leading Caribbean poet.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#20:1> ( This came out as we were discussing some poems I had recently

published.)
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#21:1> I was surprised because at the time I hardly knew of St Lucia ( its

capital, Castries, had been raided during the war and heavily shelled); I

certainly did not know of its poets.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#22:1> And although George was the leading nationalist poet of Jamaica, and

a prot&eacute;g&eacute; of Edna and Norman Manley, I might not have known

much about his work except for the fact that we had been students together at

the Jesuit High School, St George's College in Kingston.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#23:1> George's work was also well known to another St Lucian, the painter

and amateur anthropologist, Harry Simmons.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#24:1> And through Harry, Walcott was more influenced by Campbell's work

than he perhaps realised.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#25:1> This can be seen in Walcott's <it> Another Life </it> where Simmons

reads to Walcott Campbell's well known poem <it> Holy </it> : <quote> 'Holy

be the white head of a Negro...' </quote> and where in fact Walcott so took

over the theme of <quote> 'Holy be...' </quote> that the subtitle of my

forthcoming book on his poetry is <quote> 'from exorcism to benediction'

</quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#26:1> For his concerns moved from those of cursing the vile things of his

early experience to that of blessing the good things.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#27:1> The theme of <it> Holy be </it> runs through the whole of <it>

Another Life </it> . </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-002#28:1> One of the other bits of his verse which I could have quoted at

him in the Colony Club was <quote> 'You in the castle of your skin/ I the

swineherd...' </quote>
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#29:1> The persona in that poem feels that he has been rejected by his

<quote> 'Princess' </quote> because he is clearly too dark, and she is

pictured as safe in her <quote> 'fine castle' </quote> , looking down on him

and banishing him to the pens of the swine, a mere swineherd.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#30:1> There is little doubt that in his early days Walcott was much

concerned in his poetry with themes of colour, discrimination and rejection,

of being on the periphery <}> <-> , </-> <+> . </+> </}>
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#31:1> He seemed also in his personal relations and <it> aura </it> to have

been prickly, morose and deeply worried.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#32:1> The wife of a colonial official once said about him, as a young man,

that she knew no one who so strongly gave the impression of being disturbed

and bleak.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#33:1> When I relayed this remark to a scholar from Trinidad he said <quote>

'She ought to know all about that, and the reasons for it  is she not from

St Lucia?' </quote> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-002#34:1> One of his remarkable achievements  most suitable to a Nobel

Laureate  had undoubtedly been his movement from exorcism to benediction.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#35:1> He had much to reject in the time and place of his birth, but he has

been able to move from the necessary rejection of <quote> 'the works and

pomps of Satan' </quote> to the acceptance of, and the building on, all that

was positive: his great gifts, his family's nurture, the <quote> 'good

colonial education' </quote> which he received, the care of such people as

Harry Simmons and the Irish brother who introduced him to Joyce and Yeats and

Irish ballads. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-002#36:1> Few people could have moved as he did from his early feelings of

rejec <l> tion to his wonderful taking over, in <it> Another life </it> , of

Francois Villon's <quote> 'I have swallowed all my hates' </quote> . </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-002#37:1> From the culture of St Lucia into which Walcott was born another

Nobel Laureate also came: the late Sir Arthur Lewis, the economist.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#38:1> And one of Walcott's close friends and colleagues of his early days,

Dunstan St Omer, is an outstanding painter, as was Harry Simmons in a smaller

fashion.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#39:1> This island country, of 180 square miles, also accounts for an

outstanding musical genius, Luther Francois, and outstanding entrepreneurs

such as the late Sir Garnett Gordon, and many writers. In fact the cultural

life of the place, despite poverty and prejudice, is a cause for wonder. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-002#40:1> The cultural factors which first would have made any gifted

person such as Walcott uncomfortable, dissatisfied and rebellious, and which

he was later, with the touch of a true alchemist, to transmute to gold, are

alluded to by the shabine, the main character in, and narrator of Walcott's

<it> The Schooner Flight </it> : </p> <X>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-002#X41:1> <quote> I'm just a red nigger who love the sea,</p>

<p> I had a sound colonial education,</p>

<p> I have Dutch, nigger and English in me,</p>

<p> and either I'm nobody, or I'm a nation...</quote></p></X>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-002#X42:1> Walcott's mother was his connection with the Dutch, through the

tiny island of Sint Maartin. His father's father was from Warwickshire in

England.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#X43:1> Two of his grand parents were black; two white  an expression of

the cultural mixture of his island home.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#X44:1> St Lucia itself had been battled for by the French and English for

some two centuries: in the days of sail it controlled a wind route direct to

the Main.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#X45:1> Fourteen times it changed hands between England and France.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#X46:1> Just off its shores, at the battle of the Saintes ( 1793), the fate

of France in North America and India was decided by Rodney's victory over De

Grasse  this is one of the starting points of Walcott's remarkable <it>

terza rima </it> novel/ poem <it> Omeros </it> . </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-002#X47:1> So while the population was genetically African with a fair

sprinkling of Carib and European, the cultures which were brewing together

were Trans-Atlantic African, French ( Pre and Post-Revolutionary) British (

main <l> ly through schooling and the English language it taught), heavily

Roman Catholic, with a small but staunch Methodist presence.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#X48:1> Both Walcott and Lewis came from Methodist families.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#X49:1> By the time Walcott was growing up, English was the language of the

schools and civil service; French Creole the language of the countryside and

the streets; French of the Creole families who had escaped from the

Revolutionary islands, and a Creole of English was spreading.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#X50:1> Of this <foreign> <it> m&eacute; lange de tous </it> </foreign> your

ethnic cleanser, white or black, Catholic or Protestant, Muslim or

rationalist, would have despaired. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-002#X51:1> One of the marks of Walcott's greatness, it seems to me, is that

while many of us would have done our best to deny the <quote> 'mixture'

</quote> generated in our culture through history and European geopolitics,

and a few of us would have merely accepted it, Walcott used it and the

experience that it brought, to make poetry of the highest quality.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#X52:1> In doing this he has also enriched that language which first came

from England  that glorious and growing language which so many, who should

know better, are now trying to persuade us, especially in the <quote> 'United

Kingdom' </quote> , stopped developing, even perhaps died, with Shakespeare

and the King James translation of the Bible!
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#X53:1> He has also clearly shown by his art how the influence of the

languages brought by brutalised slaves from Africa has been giving birth to

new extensions of language throughout the Caribbean. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-002#X54:1> But Walcott's greatness lies not only in his feeling for the

whole of his spiritual/ physical/ intellectual environment, and his ability

to work through all of it, but also in his dedication to work  the dictum of

Horace's about using the <foreign> <it> labor limae </it> </foreign> , he

took whole-heartedly to his poetry. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-002#X55:1> But poetry is neither mainly a matter of concerns or of hard

work; it is hewn, or in a few cases comes as if by magic, from a particular

nexus of language and music.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#X56:1> It combines a creative insight into tradition with an ability to

experiment, to extend the confines into other regions. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-002#X57:1> So one must refer  one has not space to do more than that  to

Walcott's magical ability with language and his ability to handle many verse

forms, and to extend them  whether sonnet or terza rima or quatrain.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#X58:1> Note in <it> Tales of the Islands </it> his extension of the sonnet

form and of end rhyme into internal patterns which create new richness in the

use of assonance.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#X59:1> From early on he revealed an ability to create meaning through the

combination of varieties of not only registers but languages.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#X60:1> In <it> Schooner Flight </it> Shabine, who is not only of mixed race

and culture, but a Creole speaker, narrates the whole of the poem in an idiom

not only suitable to creating verisimilitude but to communicating in a

special way certain meanings not easily communicated in a monolingual display

of <quote> the STANDARD LANGUAGE' </quote> . </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-002#X61:1> In one of his early poems he refers to <quote> 'fishing the

twilight for alternate voices his own voice' </quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#X62:1> And many a learned critic shouted <it> pastiche </it> as his

youthful exuberance disclosed too easily his vast reading of both English and

French poets.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#X63:1> Soon he found his <quote> own voice' </quote> .
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#X64:1> But certainly that is always at most half the battle.
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#X65:1> He also had some thing to say  something meaningful, not only to

Nazareth but to Rome, Nairobi and New York  even to London, which is always

a bit narky about him  after all he has deserted not only the negative

understatement London so loves but also the Empire, and lives half the year

in AMERICA! ( the other half he spends in the Caribbean).
<ICE-JA:W2A-002#X66:1> Further, his great friends tend to be foreigners who do not

understand that the only done thing, in showing that one is really <quote>

'top drawer' </quote> , is to pretend that one has nothing to say and is

indeed rather boring! </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-002#X67:1> In one sense his medium can be seen as flowing against his

<quote> message' </quote> , for while the former appears to grow more and

more transparent, the latter never forgets, or attempts to hide, the fact

that it comes from a complicated situation  a situation as multicultural and

<quote> 'impure' </quote> as it is possible to imagine. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-002#X68:1> I have referred above, by implication, in quoting the fleeing

Shabine, to the cultural variety of Trinidad, his native land. </I>

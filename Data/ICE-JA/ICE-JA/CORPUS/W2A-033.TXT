<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#4:1> ABSTRACT
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#5:1> The solar drying of red mud, as is now practised at the Ewarton

alumina plant of Alcan Jamaica Company, is basically a very simple process.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#6:1> The mud is spread out and allowed to dry under the hot tropical sun.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#7:1> Though simple, the process had to be neither over nor underdesigned in

terms of the size of drying area necessary to treat the volume of mud

effluent from the plant.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#8:1> The principles underlying the spread <l> ing and drying of the mud had

to be well understood and quantified to avoid mistakes in design which could

be costly and harmful to the environment. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#9:1> Of the many scientific aspects of this project, which had to be

studied, two are dealt with in some detail in this paper  the rate of

evaporation and the influence the rheology of the mud has on its pumping to

the drying site and its spreading thereon. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#10:1> <h> <bold> INTRODUCTION </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#11:1> Red mud is the common name for the major waste material from

alumina plants.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#12:1> It is the part of bauxite which is insoluble in the caustic soda used

in alumina plants to extract the valuable part, alumina hydrate, from the

bauxite. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#13:1> The main constituents of red mud are the oxides of iron, silicon,

titanium, and aluminium.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#14:1> Nearly all of it is of very small particle size.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#15:1> As it comes from the alumina plant it is in the form of a slurry,

i.e. the particles are suspended in water.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#16:1> The water contains a small concentration of the caustic soda used in

the plant, together with a smaller concentration of sodium carbonate and

sodium aluminate.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#17:1> It is these solutes which, though only slightly toxic, are a threat

to water supplies and make the handling of the waste an important part of the

process.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#18:1> Without them, the solids in the red mud slurry would ( after drying)

be very similar in properties to the bauxite from which it came. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#19:1> Extensive efforts made, worldwide, to utilize red mud as a raw

material for a useful product have not yet yielded a process which can absorb

a significant proportion of its vast output.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#20:1> Consequently, safe dis <l> posal of the major part of the red mud

produced is now, and is likely to be for some time, a major con <l>

sideration in the process. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#21:1> In earlier systems of red mud disposal the red mud slurry came

direct from the last stage of mud washing thickeners in the alumina plant,

and conse <l> quently was a fairly fluid slurry.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#22:1> On reaching the <quote> " mud pond" </quote> this slurry would

separate quickly into two phases  the liquid phase, which was a dilute

solution of the caustic liquor circulating in the alumina plant, and a

semi-solid phase, which was a much more viscous slurry than that coming from

the plant.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#23:1> If the mud pond was sealed, the liquor phase could be recycled to the

alumina plant, thereby saving the chemical content of the liquor ( sodium

hydroxide, sodium carbonate, sodium aluminate, and traces of other chemicals)

and eliminating the chance of these chemicals contaminating the en <l>

vironment  particularly its water supplies.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#24:1> If the pond was not sealed, either naturally or by a manufactured

seal, most of the liquor would seep into the ground and possibly contaminate

water sup <l> plies if local conditions allowed.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#25:1> The chemicals in <l> volved are not very toxic, especially when

highly diluted by rain water as they often are, but they can give the water

an unpleasant taste and put it beyond the sodium concentration limits

normally regarded as acceptable for potable water supplies. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#26:1> Whether or not the pond is sealed, the semi-solid phase continues

to lose liquid at an ever decreasing rate over several years, and so becomes

more and more solid with time.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#27:1> In the case of Jamaican red mud ( which is very much finer in

particle size than red mud from other bauxites) the mud slurry comes from the

plant at around 20% solids concentration, then quickly loses liquid until it

is about 35% solids.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#28:1> Thereafter, it loses liquid slower and slower until it reaches around

50% solids.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#29:1> This process is shown diagramatically in Figure 1.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#30:1> A useful study of the dynamics of these red mud ponds was carried out

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#31:1> The earlier mud disposal system suffered from loss of chemicals

and risk of environmental pollu <l> tion if the pond was not sealed, and

suffered from very high mud disposal cost if it was sealed ( the high cost

being due to the cost of sealing and the frequen <l> cy of having to build

new sealed ponds as each one lasted only a few years before becoming

completely filled). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#32:1> An improvement on the mud disposal system described above was the

so-called <quote> " dry stacking" </quote> method which originated in Germany

and was described by Schepers and Haerter. <sp> 2 </sp>
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#33:1> More recently Pohland and Tielens <sp> 3 </sp> reviewed the method.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#34:1> It was adopted by several alumina plants in the 1960s and 1970s.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#35:1> A similar method for other mineral tailings was first reported by

Robinsky. <sp> 4,5 </sp>
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#36:1> The essence of this method is that red mud slurry would be dewatered

( i.e. solids concentration increased) suf <l> ficiently before leaving the

alumina plant that it would not immediately separate into two phases.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#37:1> This is usually done by vacuum filtering the washed mud slurry in the

plant.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#38:1> This produced a filter <quote> " cake" </quote> which can be pumped

to mud ponds by high pressure pumps ( usually the cake has to be liquified by

violent agitation and addition of a small amount of water before it can be

pumped). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#39:1> Besides having less liquid to lose ( and thereby reducing

pollution risk if the pond is not sealed), dewatering and thickening red mud

slurry to the ex <l> tent that high pressure pumps are needed to transport it

gives it the additional advantage that it does not flow readily.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#40:1> It therefore piles up to form a low angle <quote> " stack" </quote>

having a gradient, after it comes to rest, of between 2% and 10% ( between 2

and 10 units verti <l> cally to 100 units horizontally) depending on how

<quote> " thick" </quote> the mud slurry has been made.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#41:1> The ad <l> vantage of this stack angle is that rain water runs

readily off the stack rather than re-wetting the mud as happens with the

earlier method.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#42:1> Though it takes a long time, red mud stacks will dry out and become

hard, whereas most mud ponds will never dry out. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#43:1> The advantages of drying red mud as part of its scheme for

disposal make the cost and effort of doing so worthwhile.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#44:1> Dried red mud takes up much less space.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#45:1> A 75% by weight slurry occupies a little less than half the volume

occupied by a 50% by weight slurry.

<ICE-JA:W2A-033#47:1> Note that very little shrinkage occurs after 75% solids. </p> <O>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#48:1> <h> <bold> STACKING AND SOLAR DRYING </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#49:1> Though a stack of red mud will dry out eventual <l> ly, reaching

dryness can take a long time, especially if the initial water content is as

high, as it is bound to be if the mud comes from Jamaican bauxite. (
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#50:1> Jamaican red mud which is fluid enough to be pumped, has a moisture

content of 70-75% whereas pumpable muds from other bauxites typically con <l>

tain around 50% of water.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#51:1> The water content of Jamaican mud, per tonne of dry solids, can

therefore be three times that of other red muds). </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#52:1> Solar evaporation rate in Jamaica is high com <l> pared with most

other countries where red mud is produced but the high tonnage of water to be

evaporated ( around 4500 tonnes/day for each Alcan alumina plant) meant that

evaporation rate had to be optimized in order to keep the land area used

reasonable.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#53:1> The scientific part of the mud disposal project therefore had to do

with finding and optimiz <l> ing the factors which affect the relationship

between areas used for drying and the quantity of mud dried thereon. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#54:1> This paper is concerned mainly with the scien <l> tific aspects

of solar drying of red mud slurry.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#55:1> Never <l> theless, in an operation carried out on a scale dictated by

the red mud output of a modern day alumina plant ( 1000-2000 tonnes/day solid

basis, 4000-10000 ton <l> nes/day wet basis), scientific aspects could not be

en <l> tirely divorced from economic considerations i.e. cost of disposal had

to lie within the range affordable in relation to the market price for the

end product  aluminium.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#56:1> Hence elegant methods of intensifica <l> tion of solar radiation such

as lens or mirror con <l> centration of radiation falling on a larger area,

or automatic angling of the receiving surface to keep it perpendicular to the

sun's rays, had to be excluded.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#57:1> Likewise, double handling of the mud would have been uneconomic;

consequently, the mud had to be dried in the same location it was to remain

after drying.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#58:1> Economic factors limited the number of vari <l> ables which could be

manipulated in striving towards the best solution for disposal of the red

mud. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#59:1> The two major scientific aspects of solar drying dealt with in

this paper are:- </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#60:1> a) How drying rate is affected by evaporation rate and </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#61:1> b) How the rheology of the mud affects the evaporation rate via

the depth of mud layer being dried. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#62:1> <h> <bold> DRYING RATE </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#63:1> The solar drying of any wet, solid material in <l> volves

exposing a large area of the material to direct solar radiation in order to

provide latent heat for evaporation of surface water.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#64:1> As the surface water evaporates into the surrounding air, it is

replaced by more water from the interior of the mass. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#65:1> It is possible to estimate evaporation rates by an <quote> "

energy balance" </quote> method which assumes that all radiation, less a

proportion calculated to be lost by convection, conduction and re-radiation,

results in evaporation.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#66:1> Such a method is the one proposd by Penman. <sp> 6 </sp>
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#67:1> As this method needs a lot of equipment, and more direct measurements

of evaporation rate were available, it was not used in the present study.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#68:1> It was interesting, however, to check whether the magnitude of solar

radiation in Jamaica was suffi <l> cient to provide the latent heat. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#69:1> For mud at a concentration of 25% solids, 2.67 tonnes have to be

evaporated from every tonne of solid to dry it to 75% solids.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#70:1> Consequently, to dry 1500 tonnes/day, dry basis ( the mud output of

the Ewarton plant at full production), on the 200 acres ( 80 hectares) site

available near the plant, would re <l> quire an average evaporation rate of

5mm/day. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#71:1> If we assume an evaporation rate of 5mm/day, 5kg of water will be

evaporated off each square meter.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#72:1> The latent heat needed for this evaporation would be 2850 kg.

cals/day.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#73:1> This converts to 138 watts average over the 24 hours, but as most of

this comes from solar radiation, the average during 10 sunshine hours needs

to be 331 watt/m <sp> 2 </sp> . </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#74:1> When compared with the measured <quote> " mean net incoming

radiation" </quote> of 420 watts/m <sp> 2 </sp> at Kirkvine, reported by Wagh

<sp> 7 </sp> , it appears that there is more than sufficient solar radiation

to provide for 5 mm/day evaporation.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#75:1> Wagh's average was taken from read <l> ings taken hourly between 0730

and 1730 at Batter <l> sea Pond, near Kirkvine Works, and they showed that

there is very little insolation outside these hours. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#76:1> Extrapolation from the data of Langhaar ( 1953) to Jamaica's

latitude of 18 &degree; N gives maximum 24 hour averages ( i.e. for perfectly

clear skies) of 130 BTU/(hr) ( sp.ft) in mid summer to 90 BTU in mid winter.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#77:1> These convert to 410 and 284 watts/m <sp> 2 </sp> over 24 hours, or

984 and 687 over 10 sunshine hours.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#78:1> As these figures are for cloudless skies, and the averages due to

cloud and rain are 50-60% ( say 55%) of the cloudless averages in the Eastern

United States <sp> 8 </sp> we could expect actual monthly 10- hour averages

in Jamaica to be from 540 watt/m <sp> 2 </sp> ( mid summer) to 375 watts in

mid winter.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#79:1> These figures agree well with the 420 watts reported by Wagh.
<ICE-JA:W2A-033#80:1> The conclusion reached is that solar radiation in Jamaica is

sufficient to provide the evaporation required, providing it im <l> pinges on

a wet mud surface. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-033#81:1> <h> <bold> Mass Transport Theory </bold> </h> </p>

<ICE-JA:W2A-033#84:1> The relationship between these two factors controls the evaporation

<p> <ICE-JA:W1B-026#38:3> <h> <bold> Lord Chancellor, Appeal Court president not the

same</bold></h></p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W1B-026#39:3> THE EDITOR, Sir:</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W1B-026#40:3> The Right Honourable Prime Minister's letter to the Leader of the

Opposition pub<l>lished in <bold> The Gleaner</bold> of May 19, 1993,

proposing the ap<l>pointment of the Honourable Carl Rattray to the position

of President of the Court of Ap<l>peal contains a grave and mis<l>leading

deception, to wit:</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W1B-026#41:3> The analogy of the Pres<l>ident of the Court of Appeal to the

Lord Chancellor of En<l>gland.</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W1B-026#42:3> The Lord Chancellor's posi<l>tion is today essentially that of an

administrator, akin to a Minister of Justice.
<ICE-JA:W1B-026#43:3> His office is the core of the administra<l>tion of Justice generally.
<ICE-JA:W1B-026#44:3> His tasks include recommending to the Queen, High Court Judges,

Circuit Judges, Stipen<l>dry Magistrates, recommends Queen's Counsels,

Masters and Registrars of various kinds and even Justices of the Peace.
<ICE-JA:W1B-026#45:3> Lord Chancellor Gardiner once referred to all this as <quote> a

scandalous amount of judicial patronage.</quote></p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W1B-026#46:3> The position of Lord Chan<l>cellor does however, carry with it by

convention the priv<l>ilege of sitting on the Appel<l>late Committee of both

the House of Lords and the Privy Council, a privilege extended to

ex-chancellors also.
<ICE-JA:W1B-026#47:3> Howev<l>er, his role as a Judge has been somewhat diminished and he

seldom sits in this capacity.
<ICE-JA:W1B-026#48:3> The present Chancellor, Lord MacKay of Glasfern, sat on only five of

the approximately sixty-two cases reported in the All England Law Reports

from the House of Lords in 1991.</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W1B-026#49:3> It is of interest to note further, that Lord Gardiner seldom sat

as a judge while between 1971 and 1964 Lords Elwyn-Jones and Hailsham sat on

only twenty-nine of the five hundred and forty-two cases heard in the House

of Lords.
<ICE-JA:W1B-026#50:3> Thus, the position of Lord Chancellor is an aberration, a creature of

convention and custom in a country without a written constitution.
<ICE-JA:W1B-026#51:3> He has no equivalent in Jamaica.
<ICE-JA:W1B-026#52:3> If a comparison is sought within the English system the Presi<l>dent

of the Court of Appeal would have to be compared with the Lord Chief Justice

who sits as President of the Criminal Division of the Court of Appeal or the

Master of the Rolls who sits as President of the Civil Division.
<ICE-JA:W1B-026#53:3> The ap<l>pointment of a politician or ex-politician to any of these

positions is absolutely unheard of.</p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W1B-026#54:3> In our jurisdiction, we have a written Constitution, a

cardi<l>nal feature of which is the separation of power.
<ICE-JA:W1B-026#55:3> In other words, there is a distinct line between functions exercised

by the Judiciary and those exer<l>cised by the Political Executive which

ought not to be trans<l>gressed.</p>

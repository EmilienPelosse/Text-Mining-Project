<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-035#5:1> <bold> Abstract </bold> -
<ICE-JA:W2A-035#6:1> Stochastic simulation and forecast models of hourly average speeds are

presented in this paper.
<ICE-JA:W2A-035#7:1> The models take into account several basic features of wind speed data

including autocorrelation, non-Gaussian distribution, and diurnal

nonstationarity.
<ICE-JA:W2A-035#8:1> The positive correlation between consecutive wind speed observations

is taken into account by fitting an autoregressive moving average ( ARMA)

process to wind speed data which were transformed to make their distribution

approximately Gaussian and standardized to remove diurnal nonstationarity.
<ICE-JA:W2A-035#9:1> Based on three years of data, separate models were obtained for the

months of June, July, and August.
<ICE-JA:W2A-035#10:1> Tests were performed to verify the adequacy of the models and

comparisons were made between generated and real series and between

forecasted and real series to check if the wind speed behaviour was

faithfully reproduced.
<ICE-JA:W2A-035#11:1> Good results were obtained. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-035#12:1> <h> <bold> 1. Introduction </bold> </h> </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-035#13:1> The peak demand for power in Jamaica during any typical day

occurs in the early afternoon ( private com <l> munication, Jamaica Public

Service Co. Ltd.).
<ICE-JA:W2A-035#14:1> Since the wind speed in Jamaica also peaks in the early af <l>

ternoon, it is possible that wind energy conversion sys <l> tems ( WECS)

could be used as alternate back-up sup <l> plies instead of conventional

back-up plants if the winds are favourable.
<ICE-JA:W2A-035#15:1> These conventional plants require ap <l> proximately a 15-minute

start-up time; and if WECS are to be used as alternate sources of back-up

power to the national grid, a statistical model is necessary to forecast wind

power in advance.
<ICE-JA:W2A-035#16:1> Such a forecast would allow a load dispatcher at the Public Utility

to deter <l> mine how much back-up power could be expected from WECS a few

hours in advance and advise accordingly.
<ICE-JA:W2A-035#17:1> Since wind power is a function of wind speed, forecasts of power

generally are derived from forecasts of speed.
<ICE-JA:W2A-035#18:1> Methods of converting wind speed to wind power are described

extensively in the literature ( e.g. [ 1] and [ 2]) and will not be discussed

here. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-035#19:1> Models to forecast wind speeds were developed by using data from

Norman Manley International Airport, which is situated in the vicinity of

Kingston.
<ICE-JA:W2A-035#20:1> Data from this site were chosen because of their reliability and

continuity and also because a previous study [ 3] in <l> dicated that the

area in the vicinity of Manley was a good site for WECS.
<ICE-JA:W2A-035#21:1> The models were also used to simulate winds speeds at Manley since a

methodology to develop reliable models to simulate wind speeds is required as

the data at the other potential WECS sites are of short duration ( e.g.;

Flagaman, Discovery Bay, and Pimento Hill) and a long sequence of data is nec

<l> essary in reliability and load matching studies. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-035#22:1> Many previous studies such as those by Chou and Corotis [ 4], Goh

and Nathan [ 5], McNerney and Veers [ 6], and Kirchhoff <it> et al. </it> [

7] have attempted to incorporate autocorrelation into wind speed models.
<ICE-JA:W2A-035#23:1> The approaches taken in some of these studies have been based on very

specific assumptions about the sta <l> tistical characteristics of wind speed

data.
<ICE-JA:W2A-035#24:1> Moreover, some of the studies have neglected to consider impor <l>

tant properties of such data, including the non-Gaus <l> sian shape and

nonstationarity of the wind speed.
<ICE-JA:W2A-035#25:1> The Chou and Corotis model has been found not to gen <l> erate enough

persistency in simulated data [ 8].
<ICE-JA:W2A-035#26:1> The work of McNerney and Veers used a Markov method for simulating

non-Gaussian wind speed time series, with a Rayleigh distribution as an

example.
<ICE-JA:W2A-035#27:1> The work of Kirchhoff <it> et al. </it> employed a Markov chain model

for generating time series for 15-minute average wind speeds and for rapidly

sampled turbulent wind speeds. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-035#28:1> To take into account autocorrelation, Blanchard <it> et al. </it>

[ 9] and Brown <it> et al. </it> [ 2] employed a class of para <l> metric

time series models called autoregressive moving average processes ( ARMA)

made popular by Box and Jenkins [ 10].
<ICE-JA:W2A-035#29:1> However, the previous studies did not use several years of wind speed

data for a given month to develop a model for that month.
<ICE-JA:W2A-035#30:1> If more than one year of data for a given month is used, the

statistical calculations would be more complicated, requiring a revision of

the formulae.
<ICE-JA:W2A-035#31:1> The primary advantage of including more than one year of data in the

model development is the increased reliability of the estimates of the model

parameter.
<ICE-JA:W2A-035#32:1> In particular, a wind speed model based on many years of June data

should be more representative of future June speeds than a model based on a

single year of June data. </p>

<p> <ICE-JA:W2A-035#33:1> The modelling methodology described here, consists of first

fitting ARMA process of various orders to hourly average wind speed ( HAWS)

data which have been transformed to make their distribution approximately

Gaussian and standardized to remove diurnal nonsta <l> tionarity.
<ICE-JA:W2A-035#34:1> Then a model selection criterion is employed to select the most

appropriate ARMA process.
<ICE-JA:W2A-035#35:1> Seasonal nonstationarity is removed by fitting a separate model for

each month. </p> <O> caption </O> <O> table </O>
